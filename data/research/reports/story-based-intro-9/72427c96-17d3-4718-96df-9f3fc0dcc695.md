LLM-assisted brainstorming in team-based knowledge-work: effects on psychological safety, contribution equity, and collaboration quality; identifying interaction patterns that maximize positive outcomes

Executive summary
- Across a growing set of studies, AI-assisted brainstorming in teams tends to influence three core social-technical outcomes: perceived psychological safety, distribution of contributions (contribution equity), and collaboration quality. Evidence synthesized from CHI-like lab studies, HCI governance work, and broader teamwork literature points to several patterns that maximize positive outcomes: mixed-initiative interactions where humans and AI share responsibility; active, autonomous AI participation with safeguards to avoid dominance; transparent AI explanations and provenance; explicit attribution of AI contributions; and governance/logical structuring of AI involvement (e.g., prompts, turn-taking, and role delineation) (He et al., 2024; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Patil et al., 2023; Gomez et al., 2024; Allen et al., 1999). 
- Quantitative results are heterogeneous and context-specific; several studies report qualitative or design-pattern findings rather than broad, generalizable effect sizes for psychological safety, equity, or collaboration quality in real-world teams (e.g., CHIWORK 2024; ICCC 2024; ECIS 2024; ECIS 2025; IJ HCI 2025; Patil et al., 2023). Where numbers exist, they point to AI-assisted ideation increasing idea quantity and diversity in some settings but also raising concerns about dominance, attribution, or overreliance in others (e.g., Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Patil et al., 2023). 
- Actionable design guidance emerges from the corpus: emphasize mixed-initiative patterns with transparent AI reasoning; implement explicit attribution and private collaboration spaces; provide group-level governance and ethics considerations; and design measures to monitor safety, equity, and collaboration quality over time (Zhou & Chen, 2025; Gomez et al., 2024; Seeber et al., 2020; Patil et al., 2023; He et al., 2024).

1) Introduction and scope
- Research question: In team-based knowledge-work, how does LLM-assisted brainstorming affect perceived psychological safety, contribution equity, and collaboration quality, and which interaction patterns maximize positive outcomes?
- Rationale: A robust body of work on human–AI collaboration indicates that AI can augment ideation and problem solving when design patterns carefully balance augmentation with human agency, transparency, and governance. Psychological safety (the climate that enables risk-taking and speaking up) is a key driver of learning and collaboration quality, and its magnitude interacts with team learning, contribution equity, and downstream performance (Edmondson, 1999; Patil et al., 2023). Interaction patterns—patterns of AI initiative, explanations, and turn-taking—shape these outcomes in predictable ways, though context matters (Gomez et al., 2024; Allen et al., 1999; Horvitz, 1999; Amershi et al., 2019).
- Evidence base (highlights and proxies):
  - CHIWORK 2024: Group Ideation with an LLM in a Virtual Canvas (He et al., 2024). Qualitative and design-focused; reports perceived benefits (AI as facilitator and perspective augmenter) and risks (loss of human perspectives, reputational harms) with emphasis on mixed-initiative interfaces and design tensions around agent representation and engagement; no full numeric outcomes published in the excerpt (He et al., 2024).
  - ICCC 2024 (Muller, Houde, et al.): Six sessions with 18 participants; AI as a fourth participant; AI increased ideation output; mixed-initiative patterns yielded higher total ideas and some AI-originated ideas reached final status; AI in proactive mode boosted participation but could dominate if not controlled; significance tests reported for action-type sequences (χ2; p-values) but no global safety/equity metrics reported in the excerpt (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24).
  - ECIS 2024 Short Paper (Memmert, 2024): 25 professionals across teams; two-by-two design manipulating GLM suggestion completeness and origin; data indicate cognitive effects like cognitive stimulation and social loafing in group effects; no direct safety/equity metrics; early AI exposure showed lasting effects on originality/diversity (Memmert, 2024 ECIS).
  - 2025–2026 literature and syntheses (Patil et al.; Jarrahi et al.; Zhou & Chen; Gomez et al.; Seeber et al.): Point to robust relationships between psychological safety, learning, and performance; highlight the importance of transparency, metacognition, and governance; provide interaction-pattern taxonomies and measurement frameworks for HAI collaboration.
- What counts as "positive outcomes" in this report:
  - Increased collaboration quality (trust, coordination, shared mental models), higher quality and quantity of ideas, equitable participation, and sustained psychological safety in AI-enabled brainstorming contexts.

2) Evidence synthesis by outcome domain

2.1 Perceived psychological safety
- Direct measurements of psychological safety in team-based LLM brainstorming are sparse in the core sources. However:
  - Patil et al. (2023) find that psychological safety correlates positively with team learning and productivity; learning behavior mediates the safety–performance link, suggesting that fostering safety should be a design objective in AI-enabled brainstorming to unlock learning and performance benefits.
  - CHIWORK 2024 (He et al., 2024) discusses safety in terms of risks and social friction around AI participation and ownership; design patterns that promote inclusion and reduce power imbalances (mixed-initiative patterns, attenuated AI dominance) are implied as safety-enhancing.
  - Psychological safety is reinforced by transparency, uncertainty signaling, and provenance in AI outputs (Kim, 2023; See also related governance/ethics lines in Seeber et al., 2020; Gomez et al., 2024).
  - Jarrahi et al. (2025) show AI augmentations in information practices can reduce cognitive load and improve collaboration, but caution that misalignment or over-trust can reduce perceived safety if AI outputs are not interpretable or controllable.
- Practical interpretation: To maximize safety in LLM brainstorming, design for explicit and visible AI rationales, uncertainty signals, and an easily overrideable AI presence; ensure there are private spaces and attribution controls to avoid reputational risk and social friction.

Inline citations: (Patil et al., 2023); (He et al., 2024); (Kim, 2023); (Jarrahi et al., 2025); (Seeber et al., 2020); (Gomez et al., 2024).

2.2 Contribution equity
- Evidence patterns:
  - Baltà-Salvador et al. (2025) report in an undergraduate design context that AI-assisted brainstorming increases idea quantity and originality but also shows semantic convergence and a tendency for AI-originated ideas to be less elaborated; humans remain core in evaluation, refinement, and final selection. This points to potential equity concerns unless prompts and role allocations are designed to surface diverse inputs from all participants.
  - Muller et al. (2024 ICCC 24) show AI-originated ideas can reach final status, but humans still drive the critical evaluation and finalization processes; a significant share of final ideas come from human input, though AI-originated ideas contribute meaningfully in joint generation cycles. This pattern signals mixed equity in practice and underscores the need for explicit attribution and equal participation mechanisms.
  - The broader Design/HAI literature emphasizes the need for attribution policies, transparent logs, prompt librarians, and fine-grained control over AI contributions to ensure equitable recognition and opportunity across team members (41, 58; See Gomez et al., 2024; Zhou & Chen, 2025; Nixon et al., 2024).
- Practical interpretation: To maximize equity, build structured turn-taking, ensure AI prompts surface inputs from quieter members, track contribution lineage (who originated ideas vs who refined or evaluated), and implement transparent attribution that credits both human and AI contributions where appropriate.

Inline citations: (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025); (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24); (Gomez et al., 2024); (Zhou & Chen, 2025); (Nixon et al., 2024); (Allen et al., 1999).

2.3 Collaboration quality
- Evidence patterns:
  - Across several sources, AI-assisted brainstorming tends to improve idea generation quantity and, in some contexts, the diversity of concepts, while potentially reducing elaboration or introducing convergence in phrasing (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Memmert, 2024 ECIS).
  - The presence of AI as an active teammate (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24) with a properly tuned balance of initiative and human oversight improves synthesis, coherence, and integration of ideas in multi-person groups.
  - The literature emphasizes that governance, transparency, shared mental models, and adaptive scaffolds (GX framework; group-centered AI) correlate with higher collaboration quality by strengthening social transparency, accountability, and ethics in group work (GX: Beyond Individual UX; See also 5, 6;  GX references in the context page).
- Practical interpretation: To maximize collaboration quality, design for shared mental models, mixed-initiative governance, and explicit justification/traceability of AI outputs; ensure AI contributions are integrated via transparent processes and human-mediated evaluation.

Inline citations: (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025); (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24); (Patil et al., 2023); (Gomez et al., 2024); (Zhou & Chen, 2025).

3) Interaction patterns that maximize positive outcomes (patterns across sources)
- Mixed-initiative collaboration with shared control:
  - Pattern: AI proactively contributes ideas and structure, but humans retain control and can modulate AI involvement; ensure safe, reversible escalation of AI initiative (He et al., 2024; Seeber et al., 2020; Allen et al., 1999; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
  - Rationale: Enables creative augmentation while preserving human oversight and reducing dominance risks (He et al., 2024; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24).
- Transparent explanations and provenance:
  - Pattern: AI outputs are accompanied by concise explanations, sources, and confidence signals; enable traceability of idea origins (Kim, 2023; Gomez et al., 2024; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
- Explicit attribution and ownership:
  - Pattern: Clear policies for attribution of AI contributions; logs of AI/human inputs and edits; shared responsibility and accountability for outcomes (Nixon et al., 2024; Seeber et al., 2020; Allen et al., 1999).
- Structured participation and turn-taking:
  - Pattern: Rotating or equal-turn prompts; prompts designed to solicit input from quieter participants; explicit prompts to critique and expand AI-generated ideas (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Muller et al., 2024; Memmert, 2024 ECIS).
- Private collaboration spaces and content ownership:
  - Pattern: Private digital spaces to test ideas and hold sensitive ideation; ownership policies to mitigate reputational risk and balance collaboration dynamics (He et al., 2024; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Nixon et al., 2024).
- Phase-aware/co-adaptive interaction:
  - Pattern: Align AI collaboration with phases of ideation (generate, evaluate, refine, synthesize) to maintain momentum and ensure exploration is balanced with critical evaluation (Patchwork patterns; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
- Governance and ethics-led design:
  - Pattern: Group-level ethics, governance, and accountability frameworks; explicit risk-mitigation and bias-control mechanisms; end-user participation in surfacing harms (Seeber et al., 2020; Nixon et al., 2024; Jensen & colleagues in governance lines; ND-IBM 2025 proposals).

Inline citations: (He et al., 2024); (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24); (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025); (Memmert, 2024 ECIS); (Nixon et al., 2024); (Gomez et al., 2024); (Allen et al., 1999); (Patchwork design patterns 4.x in the Patchwork account); (Zhou & Chen, 2025).

4) Quantitative considerations: metrics and study designs to guide future work
- Suggested metrics to capture outcomes (to translate the qualitative signals into evidence for your study):
  - Perceived psychological safety: validated scales (e.g., Edmondson’s team safety scale) administered pre-session, mid-session, and post-session; complemented by observational measures of voice, participation, and response latency (O’Donovan et al., 2020).
  - Contribution equity: distribution of idea generation, prompts, refinements, and final decisions by participant; fairness indices or Gini coefficients across participants; attribution logs for AI vs human inputs (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24).
  - Collaboration quality: multi-item scales for coordination, trust, and perceived efficacy; practical indicators such as time-to-decision, rate of consensus formation, and artifact quality; also consider “collaboration patterns” metrics from the HA I taxonomy (Gomez et al., 2024).
  - Interaction patterns: frequency and type of AI contributions (Provide–Options, Display–Information, Predict/Propose, etc.), AI prominence level, turn-taking equality, and whether mixed-initiative modes were used (Gomez et al., 2024; Allen et al., 1999; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
- Experimental designs to validate patterns:
  - Within-subjects designs comparing fully human, mixed-initiative (balanced autonomy), and AI-dominant conditions; multi-task experiments across diverse team types (functional, cross-functional) and task domains (design, planning, ideation).
  - Mixed-methods studies combining surveys, behavioral logs, and qualitative debriefs to triangulate safety, equity, and collaboration outcomes (O’Donovan et al., 2020; Patil et al., 2023; Jarrahi et al., 2025).
  - Longitudinal field studies to test durability of effects (Patil et al., 2023; Latessa et al., 2023), including cross-cultural comparisons (Patil et al., 2023; Mogård et al., 2022).

Inline citations: (Edmondson, 1999); (Patil et al., 2023); (O’Donovan et al., 2020); (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025); (Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24); (Gomez et al., 2024); (Jarrahi et al., 2025); (Seeber et al., 2020); (Banerjee et al., 2024; ND-IBM 2025 proposals as governance patterns).

5) Gaps, caveats, and research opportunities
- Direct empirical measurements of psychological safety, contribution equity, and collaboration quality in real-world team settings with LLM-assisted brainstorming remain sparse and context-specific; many studies provide design patterns, qualitative insights, or domain-specific experiments rather than broad causal estimates (He et al., 2024; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Memmert, 2024 ECIS).
- The field would benefit from standardized metrics and cross-domain replications to establish effect sizes and generalizable interaction patterns; a taxonomy of interaction patterns (Gomez et al., 2024; Seeber et al., 2020) should be operationalized into experimental manipulations for rigorous testing.
- Cross-domain governance and ethics patterns (ND-IBM 2025; Nixon et al., 2024) provide a critical lens; integrating ethics/governance metrics with safety/equity/collaboration measures will be essential for a mature evidence base.

6) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Design principles to maximize positive outcomes:
  - Promote mixed-initiative collaboration with calibrated AI initiative and robust human oversight; ensure the AI’s role supports rather than supplants human input; include veto/override mechanisms (He et al., 2024; Allen et al., 1999; Horvitz, 1999; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
  - Build provenance and explanation into AI outputs; provide confidence metrics and source links to foster trust and safer collaboration (Kim, 2023; Gomez et al., 2024; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
  - Establish explicit attribution policies and logs; implement prompts to surface input from diverse participants; provide private collaboration spaces to protect sensitive ideation (He et al., 2024; Muller et al., 2024 ICCC 24; Nixon et al., 2024).
  - Introduce governance-oriented design patterns and ethical checks; ensure compliance with organizational norms and societal considerations; enable multi-stakeholder feedback loops (ND-IBM 2025 proposals; Seeber et al., 2020; Nixon et al., 2024).
  - Use longitudinal, mixed-method evaluation to assess safety, equity, and collaboration quality in AI-enabled brainstorming; publish effect sizes and cross-context replications to advance the field (Patil et al., 2023; Mogård et al., 2022; Jarrahi et al., 2025).
- Research roadmap:
  - Phase 1: establish a baseline with a standard mixed-initiative design and explicit measurement of safety/equity/quality; compare against all-human and AI-dominant baselines.
  - Phase 2: explore phase-aware AI interventions (initiation, critique, synthesis) and track changes in psychological safety and collaboration quality over time.
  - Phase 3: test governance and attribution models in cross-domain teams (engineering, design, health, etc.) to derive normative guidance for post-AI collaboration.
  - Phase 4: scale to cross-cultural teams; test cross-cultural moderators on safety and equity outcomes.
- Practical deployment:
  - Start with an advisory or mixed-initiative mode, emphasizing transparency and human-in-the-loop governance.
  - Provide equal-participation prompts and rotation in ideation tasks; track participation to ensure equity.
  - Implement private spaces for sensitive ideation; maintain logs for accountability and attribution.
  - Establish post-session reflection and learning loops to consolidate safety, equity, and collaboration improvements.

Appendix: measurement suggestions and implementation checklist
- Safety metrics:
  - Pre/post session Edmondson psychological safety scale; observer-based PS indicators (voice, deflections, learning orientation) per O’Donovan et al. 2020.
  - Observational coding of “voice” versus “defensive voice” and “silence” during AI-assisted brainstorming.
- Equity metrics:
  - Participation counts (ideas contributed, edits, critiques) by participant; attribution records for AI contributions; equity indices (Gini or Theil) across participants.
- Collaboration quality:
  - Trust, coordination efficiency, perceived alignment; multi-item scales; time-to-decision and iteration quality.
- Interaction-pattern logging:
  - Frequency of AI contributions; type of contribution; AI prominence; turn-taking balance; whether AI outputs included explanations; provenance of ideas.
