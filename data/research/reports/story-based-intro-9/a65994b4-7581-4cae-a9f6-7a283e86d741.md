Active Recall after an LLM-assisted study session: transfer, calibration, and conditions for strongest effects

Executive summary
- Retrieval practice after learning typically yields stronger long-term retention and often enhances transfer to related formats, relative to passive review or rereading. Classic meta-analytic syntheses place transfer effects in the medium range (around d ≈ 0.40 to g ≈ 0.50 for applied transfer in classroom-like settings) and show robust near-transfer benefits across materials, retention intervals, and test formats [Rowland, 2014; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021].
- Transfer-appropriate processing (TAP) predicts the largest transfer benefits when the retrieval demands align with the target task’s processing requirements; when formats or cognitive demands diverge, transfer gains can shrink (Morris, Bransford, & Franks, 1977).
- Direct evidence linking active recall after LLM-assisted study to transfer/calibration is still sparse. Emerging work on AI-assisted retrieval (e.g., AI-generated prompts, feedback, and adaptive prompting) suggests plausible mechanisms for enhancing retrieval benefits, but rigorous, repeated demonstrations in authentic LLM-enabled learning contexts remain needed [Yusof, 2025; Indran et al., 2023; Zuckerman et al., 2023; Kasneci et al., 2023; Steiss et al., 2024].
- When transfer to truly novel tasks is the goal, the strongest gains are observed when prompts demand reconstruction or problem solving, when learning materials promote underlying principle extraction, and when transfer tasks share cognitive demands with the training material (Opitz & Kubik, 2024; Barenberg et al., 2021; Brunyé et al., 2020). Far transfer remains more challenging and less reliable than near transfer across domains [Opitz & Kubik, 2024; Barenberg et al., 2021; Brunyé et al., 2020; Pan & Rickard, 2018].
- Actionable implications for authors designing LLM-assisted learning experiments: embed retrieval-practice steps after LLM-guided study blocks, design prompts that align with target transfer tasks, incorporate immediate or delayed, informative feedback, and explicitly measure both transfer to novel tasks and calibration (confidence-accuracy) across near and far transfer contexts.

1. Introduction and research question
Research question: Does active recall after an LLM-assisted study session improve transfer and calibration to novel tasks versus passive review or re-reading, and under what conditions is the effect strongest?

Rationale
- The robust literature on retrieval practice (active recall) shows consistent advantages for long-term retention relative to passive rereading, including gains in transfer to related formats and novel problems under appropriate conditions [Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a; Rowland, 2014; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021].
- TAP highlights that the transfer benefits of retrieval practice are greatest when the retrieval demands align with the cognitive operations required by the transfer task (Morris, Bransford, & Franks, 1977).
- LLM-assisted (AI-enhanced) study workflows plausibly enable scalable retrieval practice, but direct experimental evidence specific to LLM-enabled transfer and calibration is still developing. Early studies indicate that AI-generated prompts and feedback can yield learning gains and could shape transfer trajectories if prompts are carefully designed and feedback is informative and aligned with learning goals [Yusof, 2025; Indran et al., 2023; Zuckerman et al., 2023; Kasneci et al., 2023; Steiss et al., 2024].

2. Evidence landscape: core findings relevant to transfer, calibration, and LLM contexts
2.1 Core findings on retrieval practice and transfer (non-LLM-specific)
- Core effect (retrieval vs rereading): Retrieval practice strengthens learning beyond re-reading, with effect sizes typically in the small-to-moderate to moderate range depending on materials and test types (d ≈ 0.31–1.26 in foundational demonstrations; g ≈ 0.50 in broader meta-analytic synthesis) [Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a; Rowland, 2014].
- Transfer magnitude: Transfer of test-enhanced learning (i.e., transfer to novel contexts or test formats) tends to be medium (d ≈ 0.40) overall, with larger effects (g ≈ 0.58) when transfer is across formats (e.g., base prompts to final tests in different formats) and substantial heterogeneity across materials and tasks [Pan & Rickard, 2018; Barenberg et al., 2021].
- Content and domain generalization: Benefits extend to complex materials and applied domains (e.g., physiology, clinical case material). Moderators such as prior knowledge often show limited robust moderation, with effects persisting across learners and contexts in many domains [Agarwal et al., 2021; de Lima & Buratto, 2024; Buchin & Mulligan, 2023; Cogliano et al., 2019].
- Moderators and boundary conditions:
  - Initial retrieval success: Gains increase with higher initial recall success, with a notable uptick (e.g., beyond ~75% initial recall) predicting stronger transfer/retention [Rowland, 2014].
  - Spacing vs massing: Spaced retrieval yields stronger, more durable learning than massed retrieval [Karpicke & Roediger, 2007; Rowland, 2014].
  - Feedback: Retrieval with corrective feedback tends to bolster learning more than retrieval without feedback, particularly in education contexts; feedback’s quality and timing are critical moderators [Ariel & Karpicke, 2018; Roediger et al., 2011].
  - Material complexity: For highly complex or interdependent material, effects vary; highly integrated material tends to show stronger transfer, whereas poorly integrated material can attenuate benefits [Endres et al., 2024; Kriechbaum & Bäuml, 2024; van Gogh & Sweller, 2015].
- Theoretical accounts:
  - Transfer-appropriate processing (Morris et al., 1977) explains why matching retrieval demands to final-task demands yields strongest transfer.
  - Elaborative retrieval and contextual reinstatement accounts help describe how retrieval fosters richer representations and potential generalization.

2.2 Direct evidence on LLM-assisted or AI-assisted contexts (where available)
- AI-assisted retrieval practice (preliminary evidence):
  - Yusof (2025) reports a retrospective cohort study in a large education course where ChatGPT-generated retrieval-practice questions with feedback yielded higher final-exam performance than cohorts without AI-assisted retrieval practice (64 students per group; significant group difference via Welch’s t-test). This suggests that AI-assisted retrieval can replicate or extend the benefits of retrieval practice in an AI-enabled context, though the study is not a randomized trial and does not directly measure transfer or calibration to novel tasks [Yusof, 2025].
  - Broader AI-enabled learning reviews emphasize that retrieval prompts, feedback quality, and the design of AI-assisted tutoring systems influence outcomes; hybrid human–AI workflows often outperform fully autonomous AI tutoring in terms of instruction quality, alignment with learning objectives, and calibration of responses (e.g., expert curation enhances outcomes) [Indran et al., 2023; Zuckerman et al., 2023; Kasneci et al., 2023; Steiss et al., 2024; Bauer et al., 2025].
- Mechanisms and design considerations:
  - Retrieval prompts that require generation and elaboration, feedback that guides error correction, and spaced retrieval support calUp to maintain accountability and calibrate self-assessment. These interfaces and practices align with retrieval-practice theory and TAP, suggesting plausible transfer/calibration benefits in AI-enabled contexts if prompts are well-designed and feedback is informative [Fiorella & Mayer, 2016; Roediger & Butler, 2011; Karpicke, 2017].

2.3 Evidence gaps and boundary conditions
- Directly measured transfer to genuinely novel tasks in AI-enabled contexts remains rare; most AI-related studies focus on learning outcomes (retention, comprehension) within or near the trained domain. There is a need for rigorous randomized, cross-domain transfer experiments in LLM-enabled learning designs with explicit calibration measures [Yusof, 2025; Indran et al., 2023; Steiss et al., 2024].
- The effect sizes for AI-assisted retrieval-based transfer remain to be established with large samples and across multiple domains; current literature emphasizes that breakouts across formats, depth of processing, and the alignment between prompts and transfer tasks are critical determinants of success.

3. Evidence synthesis for LLM-assisted learning specific to transfer/calibration
3.1 Direct LLM-enabled retrieval-practice evidence
- Yusof (2025) reports that ChatGPT-assisted retrieval practice improved final-exam performance in a large undergraduate course (n ≈ 64 per group); this aligns with retrieval-practice theory but does not directly address transfer to novel tasks or calibration metrics. It provides a foundational signal that AI-generated prompts and feedback can sustain or boost learning gains in AI-enabled workflows.
- The literature on AI-assisted feedback quality shows that prompt design, feedback specificity, and alignment with learning objectives are critical; high-quality prompts can yield feedback with explanatory depth and targeted guidance, which could amplify retrieval-based benefits in AI-enabled contexts [Jacobsen & Weber, 2023].
- The broader AI-enabled education literature emphasizes the potential for human-in-the-loop designs and RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) pipelines to support retrieval prompts, with cautions about overreliance on AI, fairness, and calibration of AI outputs [Córdova-Esparza, 2025].

3.2 Inference for transfer/calibration in LLM-enabled study
- Given TAP and transfer research, if LLM-assisted retrieval prompts replicate retrieval practice with task-relevant cues, transfer (near) to novel formats within the same domain is plausible, with larger gains for well-aligned prompts and well-structured feedback.
- Far transfer in AI-enabled settings remains more uncertain; tasks that require structural generalization or rule induction are the most promising targets for far transfer, but direct evidence from LLM-enabled contexts is currently limited.
- Calibration outcomes (confidence-accuracy) have been observed in broader retrieval-practice literature, particularly in elaborated or constructive retrieval designs (Endres et al., 2024). Whether AI-assisted retrieval prompts enhance calibration in a similar way remains to be rigorously tested.

4. Moderators and boundary conditions for strongest effects in LLM-enabled contexts
- Alignment of retrieval prompts with transfer tasks (TAP): retrieval prompts should reflect the cognitive demands of the target novel tasks (e.g., problem solving, scenario-based reasoning) to maximize transfer, particularly for far transfer [Morris et al., 1977; Barenberg et al., 2021; Opitz & Kubik, 2024].
- Prompt quality and prompt-design framework: high-quality prompts yield more accurate, explanatory, and actionable feedback; use modular design (context, question, format, examples) and CLEAR principles to maximize learning outcomes [Jacobsen & Weber, 2023].
- Feedback quality and timing: informative feedback that highlights correct reasoning and provides guidance improves learning; in AI contexts, feature alignment with learning objectives and domain specificity enhances transfer/calibration, particularly in hybrid human–AI workflows [Ariel & Karpicke, 2018; Jacobsen & Weber, 2023; Steiss et al., 2024].
- Learning material properties: meaningful, semantically structured content and tasks with clear underlying principles are more conducive to transfer (near and some forms of far transfer) than unguided or superficially structured content [Opitz & Kubik, 2024; Endres et al., 2024; Brunyé et al., 2019].
- Learner characteristics: prior knowledge, achievement, and intrinsic interest interact with retrieval practice; some evidence suggests limited robust moderation by prior knowledge, but more research is needed in AI-enabled settings [de Lima & Buratto, 2024; Cogliano et al., 2019; Schwerter et al., 2025 preprint].
- Task complexity and domain: simpler, well-structured materials more likely yield near-transfer gains; highly complex tasks may require additional scaffolds (rule induction, guided abstraction) to realize transfer, including far transfer [Endres et al., 2024; Kriechbaum & Bäuml, 2024; van Gogh & Sweller, 2015; Pan & Rickard, 2018].

5. Practical guidelines for authors and practitioners (actionable insights)
- Design and implement LLM-assisted retrieval-practice workflows with explicit transfer aims
  - After LLM-guided study blocks, include structured retrieval prompts that require both recall and application to novel contexts (near and far transfer prompts). Align prompt content with the target task types you expect learners to face.
  - Use a mix of retrieval formats (short-answer, scenario-based prompts, applied problems) to encourage both recall and transfer-relevant processing.
  - Mirror final-task demands: ensure retrieval practice cues resemble the cognitive demands of the transfer tasks (Morris et al., 1977). When possible, test with near tasks first, then progressively introduce related but more novel tasks to probe transfer gradient.
- Emphasize high-quality AI prompts and feedback
  - Use modular prompt design (context, question, format, examples) and the CLEAR framework (concise, logical, explicit, adaptive, reflective) to maximize feedback usefulness and alignment with learning goals [Jacobsen & Weber, 2023].
  - Prefer expert-validated or strongly curated prompts to reduce misalignment between AI feedback and learning objectives.
- Optimize spacing and testing
  - Incorporate spaced retrieval opportunities across days/weeks with deliberate delays to harness delayed retrieval benefits and support transfer calibration (spacing effects; Rowland, 2014; Pan & Rickard, 2018).
  - Include transfer-relevant tests that vary in format and context (e.g., near vs far transfer probes) and measure both performance and calibration (confidence judgments).
- Calibrate for content complexity
  - For moderately complex, semantically structured content, expect robust transfer in near contexts; for highly complex or ill-structured content, add scaffolds, mediating prompts, and guided elaboration to improve the possibility of transfer.
- Measure calibration and transfer explicitly
  - Include calibration measures (e.g., confidence-accuracy judgments, metacognitive judgments about novelty/difficulty) and transfer probes across tasks that share core elements but differ in surface features.
  - Report effect sizes for transfer (near and far) and calibration metrics, including confidence intervals, to enable cross-study comparisons with established benchmarks (d ≈ 0.40; g ≈ 0.50; see meta-analytic syntheses).
- Research design considerations
  - Use randomized or quasi-experimental designs with adequate power (20–40 participants per cell as a rough guideline from meta-analytic considerations) and pre-registration to mitigate biases (Agarwal et al., 2021; Rowland, 2014).
  - Include moderation analyses for initial retrieval success, task difficulty, and prior knowledge to identify boundary conditions and potential equity implications (Schwerter et al., 2025 preprint; de Lima & Buratto, 2024; Cogliano et al., 2019).
- Ethical and practical considerations
  - Ensure prompt design preserves accuracy, avoids hallucinations in the AI output, and respects privacy and data-use constraints.
  - Be transparent about AI-assisted methods, including prompt design, feedback mechanisms, and instruction alignment with learning objectives.

6. Gaps and future research directions
- Direct evidence for transfer/calibration in LLM-assisted learning is still in its infancy. Future work should prioritize:
  - Randomized controlled trials of retrieval-practice after AI-assisted study, measuring near and far transfer across domains.
  - Calibration outcomes (confidence judgments, metacognitive accuracy) in AI-assisted retrieval contexts.
  - Systematic examination of prompt design quality, feedback strategies, and human-in-the-loop vs fully automated AI tutoring in relation to transfer and calibration.
  - Cross-domain replication across domains (STEM, health professions, language, social sciences) to estimate generalizability.
  - Longitudinal studies to determine durability of transfer gains in AI-enabled learning environments.

7. Conclusions
- The cumulative evidence from retrieval-practice literature indicates that active recall after learning generally improves retention and can enhance transfer to novel formats, especially when retrieval demands align with target tasks (transfer-appropriate processing) and when feedback is well-timed and informative.
- In AI-enabled contexts, LLM-assisted retrieval practice shows promise, with early evidence (e.g., Yusof, 2025) suggesting improved exam performance, but robust, domain-spanning evidence on transfer to genuinely novel tasks and calibration remains limited. The strongest gains in transfer likely arise under conditions of close alignment between training prompts and transfer tasks, high-quality prompts and feedback, spaced retrieval, and consideration of content complexity.
- For researchers and practitioners, the practical implication is to build LLM-assisted retrieval-practice workflows that emphasize retrieval-aligned prompts, elaborative or problem-solving prompts when transfer is the goal, spacing, and calibrated feedback, while explicitly measuring near and far transfer and calibration outcomes.
