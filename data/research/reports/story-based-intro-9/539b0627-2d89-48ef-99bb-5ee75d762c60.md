# report.md

Design Features for Robust Long-Run Skill Development with Repeated LLM Use

Executive summary
- Across diverse studies of AI-enabled learning, three design features repeatedly emerge as central to durable skill development when practitioners engage with LLMs over weeks to months: (1) deliberate-practice scaffolds that structure progressive, goal-directed practice with explicit feedback; (2) variable prompts that expose learners to diverse representations, contexts, and problem framings to foster transfer; and (3) ongoing feedback loops that couple AI outputs with human validation, reflection, and iterative refinement. The strongest evidence points to integrated, human-guided practice architectures that pair scaffolds with real-world tasks and feedback loops to cultivate durable skill formation and taste for high-quality outputs (Wang, 2025; Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025). Where evidence exists, effect-like signals include accelerated skill acquisition, improved transfer to new contexts, enhanced metacognitive engagement, and sustained engagement over multi-week periods (Shen et al., 2025; Ma & Chen, 2025; Fisher & Frey, 2023). Causal inferences about long-run durability remain strongest when designs embed structured practice with ongoing reflection and domain grounding rather than tool-centric, one-off tool use (Fisher & Frey, 2023; Ma & Chen, 2025).

- The convergent logic across sources is that the durability of skill development rests on the interaction of pedagogy, domain grounding, and tool design: deliberate-practice frameworks provide the scaffolding; prompt variation elevates transfer and reduces overfitting to a single prompt style; and feedback loops—especially those that couple AI outputs with human interpretation and real-world validation—anchor practice in authentic domains and sustain motivation and self-regulation (Hattie & Timperley; Shute; Deci & Ryan). Grounding design choices in theory (e.g., SDT, ZPD, retrieval practice) improves the likelihood that durable taste formation accompanies skill gains when LLMs are used across weeks or months (Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025; Deci & Ryan, 2012; Vygotsky, 1978).

- Actionable implications for authors and practitioners include (a) building deliberate-practice spine with scaffolded task progression and reflection prompts; (b) composing a library of varied prompts and prompt-patterns to support transfer across contexts; (c) implementing sustained feedback loops that combine AI-provided diagnostics with human interpretation and real-world validation; (d) grounding AI-assisted practice in domain-relevant curricula and performance goals; and (e) planning longitudinal evaluation that tracks durability, transfer, and taste formation, not only immediate outputs.

1) Research question and scope
- Research question: Which design features (deliberate practice scaffolds, variable prompts, ongoing feedback loops) most robustly support long-run skill development when practitioners repeatedly use LLMs across weeks or months?
- Scope: Synthesize evidence from multiple lines of work—scaffolding and deliberate practice in AI-augmented learning; prompt engineering and variability; ongoing feedback loops with AI and human-in-the-loop designs; cross-domain demonstrations of durable skill formation and taste formation; and theory-grounded mechanisms (SDT, ZPD, retrieval practice, spaced repetition). The literature cites concrete demonstrations of durable skill formation under integrated LLM-enabled practice when scaffolds are combined with reflection/real-world validation and domain grounding (Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025; Fisher & Frey, 2023). Evidence quality varies by domain and study design; longitudinal, cross-domain causal data remain an area for further study (Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025; Fisher & Frey, 2023).

2) Core design features and evidence (organized by feature)

2.1 Deliberate-practice scaffolds
- What it means in AI-enabled contexts:
  - Structured, progressive practice tasks with explicit skill targets and feedback loops; scaffolds are designed to push practitioners toward higher-level thinking and real-world performance through repeated cycles of practice and reflection (Wang, 2025; Fisher & Frey, 2023; Sidorkin, 2025).
  - Examples include MPs/mini-presentations aligned to culminating deliverables in business-education contexts; scaffolded branding tasks; task decompositions with explicit milestones; and teacher scaffolding that contextualizes AI outputs (Wang, 2025).
- Evidence base:
  - Meta-analytic findings indicate scaffolding yields meaningful learning gains (average effect size around 0.53 to 0.58) across online learning contexts, with larger effects when scaffolds are distributed, adaptive, and thoughtfully faded rather than front-loaded (Fisher & Frey, 2023). Durability improves when practice is spaced and involves retrieval-based engagement; over-scaffolding can hamper long-run independence if not faded appropriately (Fisher & Frey, 2023).
  - Long-run durability is strengthened when deliberate-practice scaffolds are paired with prompts that elicit self-regulation, planning, and reflection, and when these scaffolds are embedded in a theory-aligned design (Shen et al., 2025; Hattie & Timperley; Shute).
  - Real-world grounding matters: deliberate practice anchored in domain-relevant tasks and workplace practice is more likely to yield durable skill formation than generic, tool-centric tasks (Ma & Chen, 2025).
- Mechanisms and design implications:
  - Scaffolds provide a structured progression that can be maintained across weeks; fading strategies (most-to-least or least-to-most) should be planned from the outset, with explicit indicators for when to reduce support (Fisher & Frey, 2023).
  - Practical implication: design a multi-phase practice spine with milestones, reflection prompts after each module, retrieval practice, and a planned fade toward autonomous domain work.

2.2 Variable prompts
- What it means in practice:
  - Prompt variation—zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought (CoT), role prompts, instruction prompting, and automatic prompt generation techniques—supports robust practice across contexts by reducing prompt-specific learning and expanding representational coverage (Choi et al., 2025; Palavalli & Santolucito, 2024; Paleyes et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024; Lin, 2024; Marvin et al., 2024).
  - Evidence signals:
    - In prompting-focused work, ROT prompting (a particular variant) with GPT-4 Web yielded high output consistency and strong recommendations; variability in model responsiveness suggests that no single prompt works across all models, emphasizing the need for prompt libraries and model-aware design (Wang et al., 2024).
    - Prompt perturbations (keyboard typos, synonyms, paraphrase) used to test robustness show that even small prompt changes can produce large output differences; evaluating prompt robustness helps identify resilient configurations for long-run practice (PalEyes et al., 2025).
    - Case studies show that scaffolded prompt design, combined with prompt-ensembles, can significantly improve outputs with limited labeled data (Guo et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2022).
- Evidence and limitations:
  - Evidence for the direct impact of variable prompts on long-run durability (weeks/months) is indirect; much of the robust evidence concerns short-run performance, reliability, and model-specific responses. However, the logic is that systematic prompt variation supports transfer by training learners to recognize multiple prompts and contexts, a property conducive to durable skill formation when integrated with deliberate practice and feedback loops (Choi et al., 2025; Palavalli & Santolucito, 2024; PalEyes et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2024).
- Design implications:
  - Build a diverse prompt library with multiple variants per task family; incorporate prompt-ensembles and model-aware selection to stabilize output quality across weeks.
  - Pair variable prompts with explicit justification prompts and reflective prompts to anchor transfer to long-run practice, rather than relying on any single prompt form.
  - Plan for prompt auditing across models and tasks to prevent brittleness when model updates occur.

2.3 Ongoing feedback loops
- What it means in practice:
  - Ongoing feedback loops involve real-time, iterative feedback on AI outputs, with human-in-the-loop interpretation, error diagnosis, and dynamic adaptation of prompts and tasks based on learner performance and domain grounding (Shen et al., 2025; Tarun et al., 2025; Lin, 2024; Thamma, 2025).
  - In HITL RL frameworks, feedback loops include explicit human judgments, rewards, and policy updates, with explainability and governance integrated along the cycle (Retzlaff et al., 2024; Lin, 2024; Nazir & Banerjee, 2025; Meng et al., 2025).
- Evidence and syntheses:
  - Large syntheses indicate that continuous feedback loops—particularly when combined with domain grounding and alignment strategies like RLHF and instruction tuning—yield improvements in accuracy, reliability, and transfer potential; they can mitigate hallucinations and misalignment but require careful governance and safety checks (Ouyang et al., 2022; Packowski et al., 2024; Singhal et al., 2023a; Thamma, 2025; Tarun et al., 2025).
  - Meta-analytic evidence suggests that AIFB systems (AI-assisted feedback) show generally positive effects on learner outcomes and satisfaction, especially when feedback targets task/process/self-regulation and is delivered over sustained practice periods (Shen et al., 2025; Ma & Chen, 2025).
- Design implications:
  - Build weekly or biweekly feedback cadences with just-in-time explanations, actionable next steps, and reflective prompts; ensure feedback is interpretable and linked to performance criteria.
  - Integrate human oversight for high-stakes or domain-critical work, while enabling scalable AI-driven feedback in lower-stakes contexts; design governance mechanisms for transparency and safety.
  - Include retrieval-grounding content in feedback loops (RAG) to surface relevant sources and avoid hallucinations; support evidence trails for accountability and transfer.

3) Cross-cutting theory-driven mechanisms
- Self-Determination Theory (SDT) and motivation
  - Durable skill development is stronger when practice aligns with SDT needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Motivation mediates long-run engagement and mastery, so designs should incorporate autonomy-supportive structures, clear progression, and collaborative elements (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Noels et al., 2019; Ma & Chen, 2025).
- Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) and scaffolding
  - AI-enabled scaffolding that calibrates challenge to the learner’s current capabilities (ZPD) and gradually withdraws supports fosters independence and mastery over time (Vygotsky, 1978; Sidorkin, 2025; Teixeira de Melo et al., 2025).
- Retrieval practice and spacing
  - Spaced retrieval prompts and retrieval-based learning support durable retention; combining retrieval practice with LLM-assisted practice is a promising direction for long-run skill formation (Pyc & Rawson, 2009; Delaney et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2024).
- Grounding and provenance
  - Grounding AI outputs with retrieval (RAG) and provenance reduces hallucinations and supports robust skill development by tying outputs to reliable sources (Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020a; Singhal et al., 2023a; Ke et al., 2025a).
- Complementarity of social and material scaffolds
  - Evidence on fading distributed scaffolds shows that durability of learning improves when material scaffolds are complemented by adaptive social scaffolds (teacher mediation) and scaffolds fade in a calibrated way. Purely static scaffolds with no adaptive social support degrade performance as learners gain independence (Martin et al., 2019).

4) Quantitative anchors and representative findings (select highlights)
- Scaffolding effectiveness across online learning: meta-analytic effects of scaffolding around 0.53–0.58; distributed scaffolding and fade strategies improve durability; heavy front-end scaffolds can reduce long-run benefits; spacing and retrieval practices strengthen durability (Fisher & Frey, 2023).
- Proactive GenAI agents with embedded scaffolds yield durable gains in domain comprehension in higher education contexts; ongoing, personalized feedback reduces cognitive load and supports sustained learning across weeks (Yan et al., 2025).
- LLM-based RAG and alignment strategies in healthcare yield high accuracy and reduced hallucinations in high-stakes tasks; ongoing alignment reduces drift and improves long-run performance (Ke et al., 2025a; Singhal et al., 2023a; Amugongo et al., 2025a).
- Prompt engineering and variability studies show that model outputs depend critically on prompt design; ROT prompts often outperform alternatives in particular contexts, though model dependence necessitates model-aware design and cross-model testing (Wang et al., 2024; Paleyes et al., 2025).
- Long-run skill durability is best supported by integrated systems combining deliberate-practice scaffolds, prompt variability, RAG-grounding, RLHF orientation, and domain grounding—rather than any single feature alone (Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025; Tarun et al., 2025).

5) Limitations and caveats
- Many findings are domain-specific (education, healthcare, software developer tasks, business contexts) and often involve short-to-medium time horizons. Direct evidence for long-run durability across weeks or months in real-world knowledge-work contexts remains limited and warrants rigorous longitudinal studies (Ma & Chen, 2025; Shen et al., 2025; Ke et al., 2025a).
- Variability in AI models, tool architectures, data grounding, and human oversight complicates cross-study generalization. Model updates, retrieval back-end changes, and interface differences can shift the effectiveness of design features (Wang et al., 2024; Thamma, 2025).
- Ethics, safety, and equity considerations must be integrated into long-run HITL designs; biases in human-in-the-loop processes or in LLM judgments can propagate or distort learning trajectories if not properly guarded (Qian et al., 2025/2026; Tarun et al., 2025).

6) Actionable design heuristics (condensed)
- Heuristic 1: Build deliberate-practice spine
  - Structure weeks-months of progressive tasks with explicit performance criteria, retrieval practice, and reflective prompts; plan fade strategies from the outset.
  - Ground tasks in domain-specific curricula to ensure transfer and relevance; pair with domain-grounded feedback loops.
- Heuristic 2: Develop a robust, model-aware prompt library
  - Create a library of varied prompts per task family (zero-shot, few-shot, CoT/TOT, role prompts) and a prompting workflow that selects prompts based on model, task, and learner state.
  - Use prompt ensembles and retrieval grounding to stabilize outputs across weeks and model updates.
- Heuristic 3: Implement sustained feedback loops
  - Combine AI-provided diagnostic feedback with human interpretation, reflection prompts, and iterative content adaptation; schedule regular feedback cadences (weekly micro-feedback, monthly reviews) with just-in-time explanations.
- Heuristic 4: Ground practice in real-world tasks and performance metrics
  - Align practice tasks with real-world work outputs, domain performance criteria, and long-run competence indicators (transfer tasks, durable skill measures, metacognitive growth).
- Heuristic 5: Plan longitudinal evaluation
  - Design longitudinal studies with pre/post/retention assessments and transfer tasks; report effect sizes and confidence intervals; include qualitative insights to capture taste formation and motivational trajectories.
- Heuristic 6: Include governance, safety, and fairness
  - Integrate model transparency, bias audits, and safety guardrails; maintain audit trails of interventions and outcomes to support accountability and sustained trust.
- Heuristic 7: Account for context and heterogeneity
  - Adapt scaffolds and prompts to domain, culture, and learner profiles; include social scaffolds and mentorship to support autonomous practice in varied work contexts.
- Heuristic 8: Manage cognitive load and attention
  - Avoid overwhelming learners with excessive prompts; use concise, well-structured prompts interleaved with reflection prompts; design for focused, single-task sessions when possible.
- Heuristic 9: Sustainability and cost-consciousness
  - Consider cost implications of long-running LLM usage; build efficient prompting pipelines and caching strategies; reuse prompts and prompts-history to reduce overhead.

7) Implementation blueprint (high-level, adaptable)
- Phase 1: Baseline and grounding
  - Define domain competencies and long-run skill targets; construct a domain-grounded prompt library; set up a domain-aligned scoring rubric and a small-scale pilot to calibrate prompts.
- Phase 2: Deliberate-practice spine and variability
  - Implement weekly practice blocks with progression; deploy varied prompts per task; anchor prompts in domain context; integrate retrieval-grounded content.
- Phase 3: Feedback loops and domain grounding
  - Establish weekly micro-feedback and monthly reflective reviews; connect AI feedback to real-world outputs; incorporate expert validation for high-stakes domains; enable just-in-time adaptation.
- Phase 4: Long-run evaluation and fade
  - Introduce fading of scaffolds as mastery indicators emerge; measure durability via retests and transfer tasks; examine taste formation through metacognitive measures and qualitative feedback.
- Phase 5: Scaling and governance
  - Expand to diverse domains; implement governance and fairness checks; maintain transparency and auditability; monitor for equity and safety concerns.

Notes on scope, generalizability, and interpretation
- The evidence base includes diverse domains (education, healthcare, programming, business education, EFL, psychology, and real-world work settings). While the convergent view supports three core design features for durable skill development, a single study is rarely sufficient to claim universal durability across all domains and time horizons. Longitudinal, cross-domain studies with consistent metrics are needed to confirm generalizability and quantify durable gains.
- Where quantitative effect sizes are available (e.g., effect sizes around 0.53–0.58 for scaffolding; ~0.4–0.8 in some prompting studies), designers should treat them as indicative rather than universal; context, domain, learner characteristics, model/version, and grounding quality mediate outcomes.
- Ethical, safety, and equity considerations are recurrently prominent: in HITL RL, RAG, and grounding designs, consider bias, privacy, and access to AI tools as integral to design and evaluation.

Actionable takeaways for authors and practitioners
- Build a robust, theory-grounded design space that combines deliberate-practice scaffolds, variable prompts, and ongoing feedback loops into an integrated LLM-enabled workflow.
- Ground AI outputs in domain-specific curricula and performance goals, and ensure accessible, interpretable feedback with explicit justification and rationale.
- Implement a multi-layer evaluation framework that includes human-in-the-loop assessments, automated checks, and evidence-grounded metrics across weeks/months, with clear data pipelines for logging, auditing, and iterative improvement.
- Plan for model updates and versioning; maintain model- and prompt-history aware deployment to minimize brittleness and sustain learning trajectories in dynamic LLM ecosystems.
- Design for scalability and equity: ensure scaffolds are accessible to diverse learners and do not exacerbate inequities; provide adaptive supports to learners with varying initial skill levels.
