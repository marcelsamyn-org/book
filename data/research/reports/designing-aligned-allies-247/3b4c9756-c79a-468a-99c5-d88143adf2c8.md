# report.md

Can constraint-based workflows (pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, and limited iteration cycles) reduce over-trust and mode-collapse in decision-making without sacrificing speed, in a randomized within-subjects design?

Abstract
- This report synthesizes evidence across studies that bear on constraint-based workflows for AI-assisted decision-making (pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, and bounded iterations). It discusses whether such constraints can deter reliance on AI shortcuts and preserve disciplined reasoning while maintaining speed, and it outlines a concrete randomized within-subjects design to quantify effects on over-trust, mode-collapse, and throughput.
- The available empirical work provides partial support: constraint-based designs can improve throughput of curated outputs and perceived usefulness, without clear evidence on causal reductions in over-trust or mode-collapse across domains. A representative randomized within-subjects study (Kang, 2024) reports that a constraint-based treatment increased clipping/referencing throughput and expert-perceived usefulness relative to baselines, with mixed-speed implications (Kang, 2024) [Kang, 2024].
- Across broader literatures, evidence about trust calibration, explanations, and governance mechanisms suggests constraint-based prompts and checks may help deter shortcuts and sustain disciplined reasoning, but findings are domain-specific and often qualitative or indirect with respect to speed (e.g., Vereshchak et al., 2021; Majumdar, 2025) [Vereshchak et al., 2021; Majumdar, 2025].
- This report presents a rigorous study design to test the core question, anchored in speed–quality and trust literature, and closes with actionable recommendations for researchers and practitioners.

1) Introduction and research question framing
- Core idea: Constraint-based workflows impose governance-like boundaries on AI-assisted decision making by (a) pre-specifying how prompts are formulated, (b) requiring mandatory checks prior to accepting AI outputs, and (c) limiting the number of iterative AI interactions per decision. The aim is to deter AI shortcuts and preserve disciplined reasoning while maintaining decision velocity.
- Why it matters: Rapid AI-enabled decision-making can amplify errors if outputs are accepted unchecked (speed can outpace verification). Constraint-based workflows are designed to slow down and channel human reasoning at critical junctures, while preserving the benefits of AI acceleration for data processing and synthesis (Majumdar, 2025) [Majumdar, 2025].
- Evidence base (high level): 
  - Constraint-based features (pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, limited iterations) map onto governance principles such as HITL, structured verification, and auditing. They are discussed as components of a broader strategy to deter AI shortcuts and uphold disciplined reasoning in fast decision environments [Majumdar, 2025; NIST AI RMF 1.0; Vereschak et al., 2021].
  - Several studies show that constraint-like prompts, structured interfaces, and gating can enhance throughput of structured outputs and perceived usefulness, but the literature does not consistently provide randomized within-subject causal evidence that directly links these constraints to reductions in over-trust or mode-collapse across domains or to precise speed metrics (Kang, 2024; Naiseh et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2024) [Kang, 2024; Naiseh et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2024].
- Research question as stated: Can constraint-based workflows (pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, and limited iteration cycles) reduce over-trust and mode-collapse in decision-making without sacrificing speed, in a randomized within-subjects design?

2) Synthesis of relevant evidence (domains, designs, and outcomes)
- Vibe coding and iterative workflows (qualitative chassis for constraint ideas)
  - Vibe coding studies show that iterative prompting, rapid verification, and selective manual edits can maintain fast cycles while preserving human oversight. They emphasize that trust is dynamic and context-dependent, with structured prompts and explicit checks acting as guardrails against AI shortcuts (Sarkar & Drosos, 2025) [Sarkar & Drosos, 2025].
  - Design implication: a constraint-based workflow embedding high-level objectives with explicit technical constraints and mandatory checks could anchor disciplined reasoning while preserving speed through a rapid verification loop (Sarkar & Drosos, 2025) [Sarkar & Drosos, 2025].
- Quantitative within-subject evidence for constraint-based workflows (SYNERGI)
  - Kang (2024) reports a randomized within-subjects design comparing a constraint-based treatment vs Baseline across a scholarly synthesis task (SYNERGI-like interface). Key results:
    - Threads created: Treatment μ = 5.2 vs Baseline μ = 4.9; not significant (p ≈ 0.8) [Kang, 2024].
    - Clips collected: Treatment μ = 9.9 vs Baseline μ = 4.9; significant increase under Treatment (p = 0.006) [Kang, 2024].
    - References saved: Treatment μ = 20.4 vs Baseline μ = 7.9; significant increase under Treatment (p = 0.03) [Kang, 2024].
    - Expert-rated usefulness: Treatment M = 5.6 vs Baseline M = 3.0 and Chat-GPT4 M = 4.0; Treatment significantly higher (Kang, 2024) [Kang, 2024].
    - Goodness of context: Treatment M = 5.5 vs Baseline M = 3.2 and Chat-GPT4 M = 2.2; Treatment significantly higher (Kang, 2024) [Kang, 2024].
  - Interpretation: a constraint-based workflow improved structured outputs and perceived usefulness, while not showing a significant difference in thread creation rate (which may reflect interface design and task structure rather than speed per se). The increases in clips/references suggest more efficient import of system-generated outputs in a constrained workflow (Kang, 2024) [Kang, 2024].
- Trust calibration and explanations
  - Vereschak, Bailly, Caramiaux (CSCW 2021) emphasize the need for explicit trust definitions, standardized experimental protocols, and theory-aligned trust measures. They argue that lack of explicit trust constructs undermines cross-study synthesis. This supports using a well-specified trust framework when testing constraint-based workflows (Vereschak et al., 2021) [Vereschak, Bailly, Caramiaux, 2021].
  - Naiseh et al. (2022) show that XAI class and fit with user constraints influence trust calibration in clinical decision support. Explanations can both aid and impair calibration, depending on class and interface alignment with job constraints, suggesting constraint-based workflows should pair prompts/checks with calibrated explanations to avoid unintended over-trust (Naiseh et al., 2022) [Naiseh et al., 2022].
- Speed–quality trade-offs and governance
  - Majumdar (2025) frames speed–quality as a governance problem: high-speed AI decisions threaten accuracy, fairness, robustness, and ethical alignment; mitigation requires an integrated socio-technical approach (XAI, FAML, HITL, robust validation, auditing). This provides a theoretical basis for constraint-based workflows as operational governance levers that can be tested in randomized within-subject designs (Majumdar, 2025) [Majumdar, 2025].
  - NIST AI RMF 1.0 frames governance (GOVERN), measurement (MEASURE), mapping (MAP), and management (MANAGE) as core functions for trustworthy AI. Constraint-based workflows can be instantiated as governance artifacts (prompts, checks, iteration caps) within the MAP/MEASURE/MANAGE flows (NIST AI RMF 1.0) [NIST AI RMF 1.0].
- Mode collapse and RLHF concerns
  - Studies on RLHF-conditioned models show reduced output diversity and deterministic tendencies (mode collapse-like behavior) and highly concentrated token predictions, which support the motivation for constraint-based workflows to counteract shortcutting and maintain broader reasoning spaces. While not an experiment on governance per se, these findings motivate the testing of constraint-based prompts and checks to preserve diversity in reasoning pathways (Gomez et al., 2023; “Mysterious mode collapse” type discussions) [Gomez et al., 2023; RLHF mode-collapse literature].
- Evidence gaps to address in a randomized within-subjects test
  - Direct evidence linking constraint-based prompts/mandatory checks/limited iterations to reductions in over-trust and mode-collapse, while preserving speed, is sparse across domains. The strongest within-subject data come from studies like Kang (2024) in structured academic synthesis tasks. Naiseh et al. (2022) provide trust calibration insights in clinical settings but do not test constraint-based workflows in a randomized within-subject design.

3) Gaps and design considerations for a direct test
- Gaps to fill with a dedicated randomized within-subjects study:
  - Direct causal evidence that pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, and limited iterations reduce over-trust and mode-collapse across decision domains.
  - Quantified speed metrics (response times, time-to-decision) under each constraint condition, with drift-diffusion modeling to interpret speed–accuracy dynamics.
  - Generalizability across domains (clinical, finance, engineering, etc.) and task difficulty levels.
- Design principles drawn from the literature:
  - Use explicit trust definitions and standardized trust measures (Vereschak et al., 2021; Li et al., 2026) to quantify calibration and reliance.
  - Pair constraint-based prompts with calibrated explanations and transparent governance signals to sustain appropriate trust (Naiseh et al., 2022; Zerilli et al., 2022).
  - Incorporate HITL-like gating and auditability (NIST RMF; Majumdar, 2025) to ensure accountability and traceability of constrained workflows.

4) Proposed randomized within-subjects design (conceptual blueprint)
- Experimental design
  - Within-subjects, counterbalanced design where each participant completes decision tasks under multiple constraint conditions:
    - C1: Baseline unconstrained AI workflow (no pre-specified prompts; no mandatory checks; open iteration).
    - C2: Pre-specified prompts only.
    - C3: Mandatory checks only.
    - C4: Limited iterations only.
    - C5: All three constraints applied (full constraint-based workflow).
- Task domain
  - A diverse set of decision tasks with measurable ground truth (e.g., risk assessment, planning, or classification tasks) that are amenable to speed–accuracy measurement and trust calibration.
- Outcome measures
  - Speed: time-to-decision, time per iteration.
  - Decision quality: accuracy against ground truth or expert benchmark.
  - Trust calibration: validated scales (TIAS/S-TIAS or domain-adapted trust measures) and calibration curves.
  - Over-trust proxies: AI acceptance without justification, reliance on AI when evidence is weak.
  - Mode-collapse proxies: diversity of reasoning paths, prompts used, and justification depth; AI output diversity metrics.
  - Process governance: incidence of mandatory checks completed, prompts followed, iteration caps adhered to; HITL interventions if applicable.
- Analytic plan
  - Linear mixed-effects models with Condition as a fixed effect and Subject as a random effect; task difficulty as covariate.
  - Drift-diffusion modeling to extract threshold (a) and drift rate (v) per condition, testing for a shift toward higher thresholds under constrained conditions.
  - Pre-registered hypotheses about reductions in over-trust and mode-collapse and about speed changes under constraint conditions.
- Power considerations
  - A priori power analyses to determine sample size, accounting for within-subject correlations and expected effect sizes from prior constraint-based studies (e.g., Kang, 2024; Naiseh et al., 2022).
- Pre-registration
  - Pre-register hypotheses, task set, constraint specifications, and analysis plan to ensure replicability and minimize p-hacking.
- Practicalities
  - Ensure explainable outputs and transparent prompts to enable auditability and trust calibration measurement (Vereschak et al., 2021; Naiseh et al., 2022).
  - Include domain-appropriate caution regarding generalizability and domain-specific risk.

5) Expected outcomes and interpretation (based on the synthesis)
- If constraint-based workflows reduce over-trust and mode-collapse with only modest speed costs (or even speed gains due to reduced downstream verification), this would support constraint-based governance as a viable design principle for disciplined AI decision-making.
- If speed penalties are substantial, but accuracy/trust calibration improves, the design may still be valuable for high-stakes contexts; optimization would focus on reducing overhead in checks and prompts.
- The interpretation should hinge on speed–quality trade-offs (DDM-inspired) and the governance framework around trust, explainability, and auditability (Majumdar, 2025; Vereschak et al., 2021; NIST RMF 1.0).

6) Limitations and caveats
- No single study to-date provides a universal answer; the field shows domain-specific results and mixed outcomes regarding trust calibration, explanation effects, and constraint efficacy.
- A carefully designed within-subjects experiment is needed to establish causal effects and generalize beyond a specific task or domain.
- Implementation fidelity matters: prompt templates, checks, and iteration caps must be well-designed and validated; otherwise, constraints may backfire or degrade user experience.

7) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Design and implement constraint-based workflow prototypes as governance artifacts, not just software features:
  - Pre-specified prompts that anchor objectives and constraints.
  - Mandatory checks that require justification and cross-validation.
  - Bounded iteration cycles to prevent prompt-chasing and maintain discipline.
- Measure trust calibration rigorously:
  - Use validated scales (TIAS/S-TIAS) or domain-adapted trust measures; assess calibration via confidence–accuracy relationships.
  - Track over-trust and mode-collapse proxies (e.g., reliance on AI without adequate justification, reduced diversity in reasoning steps).
- Integrate governance signals:
  - Provide audit trails, justification logs, and explainability outputs that support traceability and accountability.
  - Consider HITL gating where high-stakes decisions trigger human intervention and review.
- Domain and context considerations:
  - Start with domains where risk of over-trust and mode-collapse is high (healthcare, finance, safety-critical planning) and where governance signals are feasible to implement.
- Reporting and replication:
  - Pre-register the study protocol; publish prompts, checks, and iteration-limits as part of the research artifact.
  - Report effect sizes, confidence intervals, and model fits for speed, accuracy, and trust calibration.

Notes and caveats
- The evidence summarized here draws from a corpus of studies with varying methodologies and domains. While some studies provide direct randomized within-subject data on constraint-like interfaces (e.g., Kang, 2024; Naiseh et al., 2022), others provide qualitative or theory-driven insights on trust, explanations, and governance (Majumdar, 2025; Vereshchak et al., 2021; NIST RMF 1.0). There is no universal, domain-agnostic answer yet; the proposed randomized within-subjects design offers a concrete path to quantify the core question across relevant decision domains.

Concluding synthesis
- Constraint-based workflows—comprising pre-specified prompts, mandatory checks, and limited iterations—are conceptually aligned with governance approaches designed to deter AI shortcutting and preserve disciplined reasoning under speed pressure. The strongest direct within-subject empirical evidence to date comes from targeted studies (e.g., Kang, 2024) showing improved structured outputs and perceived usefulness under constraint-based designs, with variable implications for speed. Trust calibration literature underscores the importance of explicit trust constructs, calibrated explanations, and auditability. A rigorously pre-registered randomized within-subjects study, with drift-diffusion-informed analysis, is warranted to determine causality and quantify speed–quality–trust trade-offs for constraint-based workflows across decision domains.
