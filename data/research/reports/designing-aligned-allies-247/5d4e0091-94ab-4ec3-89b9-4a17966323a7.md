Title: Do active-recall prompts inserted after AI-assisted tasks improve long-run transfer to new domains? A synthesis and proposed field-design

Executive summary
- Across AI-enabled learning research, retrieval-practice–oriented, reflective prompts are well-supported to bolster durable learning and transfer in many contexts, but direct evidence for post-task active-recall prompts after AI-assisted tasks in field settings with a 3-month transfer follow-up is sparse or absent in the available literature (Létourneau et al., 2025; Pane et al., as cited; Corral & Carpenter, 2025).
- Robust meta-analytic and experimental work consistently shows: retrieval practice improves long-term retention and facilitates transfer to novel tasks; however, effect sizes vary by domain, design, feedback, spacing, and task difficulty (Rowland, 2014; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a).
- Field evidence specific to AI-enabled workflows and explicit 3-month cross-domain transfer is limited. Notable nearby findings include: CTAI long-run gains in high-school algebra after sustained ITS use (Pane et al., as cited), and mixed results in post-task reflective prompts in metacognition-focused work (Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025). Several studies report transfer effects only over short delays or within constrained domains (Manee et al., 2024; Corral & Carpenter, 2025; Pickering et al., 2021).
- Gaps identified: (a) direct, randomized field experiments testing active-recall prompts after AI-assisted tasks with a 3-month cross-domain transfer endpoint; (b) explicit measurement of transfer to genuinely novel domains; (c) consistent reporting of effect sizes for transfer under AI-enabled reflective prompts.
- Actionable design principle: to robustly test the question, implement a multi-arm field trial with a 3-month follow-up that isolates post-task active-recall prompts, includes cross-domain transfer tasks, and uses rigorous pre-registration and power analysis.

1) Context and research question
- Core question: Do active-recall prompts inserted after AI-assisted tasks (e.g., prompts asking learners to explain what they learned and how to apply it) improve long-run transfer to new domains, measured over a 3-month follow-up in a field experiment?
- Rationale: Reflective, retrieval-based prompts may strengthen durable learning and domain-general transfer when integrated into AI-enabled workflows. This aligns with retrieval-practice theory, transfer-appropriate processing, spacing, and feedback literature, but direct field evidence in AI-enabled tasks with a 3-month transfer endpoint is not yet established in the accessible literature (Rowland, 2014; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021).

2) Evidence landscape: what we know about active recall, AI-enabled prompts, and transfer

2.1 Retrieval-practice and transfer in education (non-AI-specific)
- Core finding: Retrieval practice strengthens long-term retention and supports transfer to novel problems, more consistently than restudy under many conditions (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a; Rowland, 2014).
- Meta-analytic norms:
  - General testing effect (across domains): g ≈ 0.50 (Rowland, 2014).
  - Transfer effects for retrieval practice: d ≈ 0.40 (Pan & Rickard, 2018).
  - Real-world classroom contexts: g ≈ 0.50 (Yang et al., 2021).
- Mechanisms and moderators:
  - Feedback after retrieval enhances gains (Roediger et al., 2011).
  - Transfer-appropriate processing: alignment between retrieval tasks and later transfer demands is critical (Morris, Bransford, Franks, 1977).
  - Benefits robust across learner differences in many studies, though context and material type matter (de Lima & Buratto, 2024; Cogliano et al., 2019).
- Transfer within complex material and domain breadth: retrieval benefits appear across complex, health-content and applied domains, not limited to simple recall (Dobson et al., 2018; J. L. Dobson et al., 2019; Kriechbaum & Bäuml, 2024).

Inline references: Roediger & Karpicke 2006a; Rowland 2014; Pan & Rickard 2018; Yang et al. 2021; Morris et al. 1977; de Lima & Buratto 2024; Cogliano et al. 2019; Dobson et al. 2018; etc.

2.2 AI-enabled prompts, reflective practice, and learning durability
- Metacognitive prompts and reflective work:
  - Large-scale reflective-prompt tests show mixed short-term versus long-term effects and moderated transfer by prior achievement (Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025).
  - Reflective prompts can increase metacognitive SRL activities and alter engagement, but immediate learning gains are not always observed; transfer effects are moderated by learner characteristics (prior performance) (Pape & Thomas, 2025).
- AI-assisted reflective writing and metacognition:
  - Reflective writing tools (e.g., Reflectium) show positive effects on performance and perceived learning experiences in controlled contexts, though results are not uniformly positive, and long-run transfer data are limited (Neshaei et al., 2025).
- AI-tutoring and reflective prompts in K-12/CBLE contexts:
  - Systematic reviews note positive effects of AI-driven ITS features (immediate feedback, adaptivity) on learning, with durability more likely in longer, well-supported deployments; but direct testing of post-task reflective prompts and 3-month cross-domain transfer remains sparse (Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Field-transfer evidence gaps:
  - Some field studies (e.g., CTAI) show durable algebra gains after sustained ITS use, but domain-specific durability does not automatically generalize to cross-domain transfer; evidence for cross-domain 3-month transfer in AI-enabled workflows is not yet established (Pane et al., as cited; Létourneau et al., 2025).

Inline references: Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025; Neshaei et al., 2025; Létourneau et al., 2025; Pane et al., as cited.

2.3 Laboratory and cross-domain transfer exemplars relevant to mechanisms
- Pickering et al. (2021) show transfer within an integrated event: retrieval can yield transfer to related, untested associations when material is highly integrated; boundary conditions include material integration strength and pattern completion mechanisms. This supports the plausibility that post-task active recall could promote cross-domain transfer when prompts elicit integrated representations (within-event transfer; pattern completion) [Pickering et al., 2021].
- Neuro/ERP and memory-updating studies show retrieval practice alters neural representations in MPFC/LPFC and can strengthen target memory and reduce intrusion, suggesting possible neural-level mechanisms for durable transfer in AI-enabled tasks that require updating knowledge representations (Ye et al., 2020). Caveat: not AI-task field data and not 3-month cross-domain transfer.

Inline references: Pickering et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2020.

2.4 Representative field and design considerations
- Large-scale ITS field trials show durable learning in some contexts (CTAI in high schools; approx +0.20 SD in year 2) but weaker or non-significant in other contexts (Pane et al., as cited). This emphasizes context sensitivity and the need for sustained, theory-aligned design for durable learning and potential transfer (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Post-task reflective prompts have demonstrated metacognitive benefits in several contexts, yet not consistently linked to 3-month cross-domain transfer in AI-enabled field settings (Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025).

Inline references: Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025; Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025.

3) Gaps and what is not yet demonstrated
- Direct evidence for post-task active-recall prompts after AI-assisted tasks in field experiments with a 3-month cross-domain transfer endpoint is not available in the cited sources.
- The strongest near-term evidence for durable transfer via retrieval practice comes from non-AI contexts or within-domain durable gains (e.g., CTAI algebra) rather than explicit cross-domain transfer measured after AI-assisted prompts over months.
- Meta-analytic and experimental work consistently supports retrieval-based learning, but design specifics that maximize cross-domain transfer in AI-enabled workflows (prompt timing, prompt content, feedback quality, domain mismatch handling) remain under-specified in field settings with a 3-month horizon.

4) Implications for study design (recommended framework for a 3-month field experiment)

4.1 Conceptual model
- Active-recall post-task prompts after AI-assisted tasks can foster durable memory representations and cross-domain transfer by:
  - Eliciting retrieval of core concepts and transfer-relevant reasoning.
  - Providing feedback that guides error correction and elaboration.
  - Leveraging spacing and interleaving across prompts to promote durable encoding.
  - Aligning retrieval prompts with transfer-appropriate processing demands (Morris et al., 1977).

4.2 Experimental design (field trial)
- Population and setting: professionals or students engaged in AI-enabled workflows (e.g., coding tasks with AI assistants, data-science notebooks with AI copilots, or classroom contexts with AI-powered tutors).
- Design: randomized controlled trial with three arms:
  - Arm A (Treatment): AI-assisted tasks followed by post-task active-recall prompts asking learners to explain key concepts and specify how to apply them in a new domain; prompts include brief targeted feedback.
  - Arm B (Active-control): AI-assisted tasks followed by post-task generic prompts or non-transfer-focused prompts (e.g., summarize the task) or no prompts.
  - Arm C (No post-task prompts): AI-assisted tasks with no post-task reflective prompts.
- Intervention duration: several weeks to allow learning consolidation; plan for a 3-month follow-up.
- Transfer tasks: design cross-domain transfer probes that require applying learned concepts to a genuinely new domain, not just a re-framing of the practiced domain.
- Outcome measures:
  - Primary: performance on cross-domain transfer tasks at 3 months (primary endpoint).
  - Secondary: delayed retention of core concepts in practiced domain, transfer-task depth (explanation quality, transfer plan quality), and transfer-probe accuracy.
  - Tertiary: process data (prompt engagement metrics, time-on-task, prompt depth), and learner attitudes toward AI-assisted prompts.
- Covariates and moderators: prior knowledge in target domains, domain similarity, motivation, and self-efficacy; consider including balance for work-task complexity and AI-tool familiarity.
- Analysis plan:
  - Mixed-effects regression modeling to account for clustering (classrooms, teams, or cohorts).
  - Pre-registered primary analyses with effect-size reporting (Cohen’s d or Hedges g for transfer outcomes).
  - Mediation analyses to test whether quality of explanations or transfer-oriented reasoning mediates observed transfer gains.
- Power considerations:
  - Based on retrieval-practice literature, target a medium effect size (g ≈ 0.40–0.50) with sufficient power (80% or higher); plan for attrition across a 3-month follow-up and use a pre-registered power analysis to determine sample size (Agarwal et al., 2021; Rowland, 2014).

4.3 Practical design guidelines
- Prompt design:
  - Use a structured post-task prompt ladder: retrieve core concepts; articulate one to three transferable applications; provide a concrete transfer plan; reflect on potential obstacles.
  - Include explicit transfer prompts that map to novel contexts and require justification of transfer decisions.
  - Provide targeted, immediate feedback after each retrieval attempt to guide elaboration and error correction.
- Spacing and interleaving:
  - Implement spacing across weeks (e.g., 1–3–7–14 day pattern) to optimize long-term retention and transfer; interleave prompts across related domains to test generalization (Rowland; Roediger & Karpicke; spacing literature).
- AI-system considerations:
  - Ensure prompts are transparent, attributable, and come with quality controls to avoid misplaced trust in AI outputs.
  - Log prompts, responses, and feedback to enable traceability and post-hoc analysis of the retrieval-practice process.
- Ethics and equity:
  - Monitor for potential biases in AI feedback and ensure accessibility across diverse learner populations.

5) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- If your goal is durable, cross-domain transfer in AI-enabled workflows, prioritize retrieval-based prompts after AI tasks and couple them with explicit transfer tasks at a 3-month follow-up.
- Ground the design in the retrieval-practice literature (retrieval with feedback, spacing, interleaving) and in transfer science (transfer-appropriate processing) to maximize the probability of durable, domain-general learning.
- Use rigorous field-design standards: randomization, pre-registration, multi-domain transfer tasks, and transparent reporting of effect sizes and confidence intervals.
- Collect process data to illuminate mechanisms: measure depth of retrieval, quality of explanations, and the extent to which learners connect practiced content to new domains.

6) Limitations and caveats
- The field evidence directly addressing 3-month cross-domain transfer for post-task active-recall prompts in AI-enabled tasks is not yet established; most sources discuss either short-term transfer, within-domain durability, or reflective prompts in non-AI contexts (Engelmann et al., 2021; Pape & Thomas, 2025; Corral & Carpenter, 2025; Manee et al., 2024; Pane et al., Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Effects may be contingent on task type, domain similarity, learner prior knowledge, feedback quality, and the quality of the AI prompts themselves.
- Ethical and practical concerns with AI-enabled prompts (data privacy, fairness, cognitive load) should be monitored and mitigated.

9) Conclusion
- The literature robustly supports retrieval-practice–driven durability and transfer in diverse educational contexts, and provides a credible theoretical basis for using post-task active-recall prompts after AI-assisted tasks. However, direct field evidence for a 3-month cross-domain transfer outcome in AI-enabled workflows remains sparse. A rigorously designed field experiment—randomized, multi-domain, with a 3-month follow-up and explicit transfer tasks—will be needed to answer the research question definitively.
- Proposed study designs, as outlined, should emphasize: (a) explicit post-task active-recall prompts; (b) transfer-focused assessment at 3 months; (c) robust sample sizes and pre-registered analyses; (d) careful control of prompt design quality, feedback, spacing, and domain similarity.
