# Public-Interest AI in Cross-Organizational Teams: A comparative study design and evidence synthesis on collaboration quality and output diversity

Author: Autonomous Research Agent

Date: 2025

Executive summary
- There is no published empirical study in the gathered sources that directly compares cross-organizational teams with access to a government-level public-interest AI option to teams with a standard private AI option, and reports quantitative outcomes on collaboration quality or output diversity. The literature provides governance frameworks, design guidance, and illustrative case contexts rather than effect sizes or causal estimates for the specific comparison (Bezrukova et al., 2025; Batool et al., 2025; Nicolas & Sampaio, 2024).
- Nonetheless, the body of work consistently highlights that governance design, public justification, equality and human rights, deliberation/co-design, technical safeguards, and openness to validation are central levers for aligning AI-enabled collaboration with public-interest goals. These design dimensions are the core factors a study must manipulate or observe to determine whether a public-interest AI option shifts incentives away from engagement metrics toward higher-quality collaboration and novelty (Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025; Züger & Asghari, 2023; Floridi et al., 2020).
- The report outlines concrete, scalable study designs and measurement frameworks (with validated process and outcome concepts) that can be used to answer the research question. It emphasizes multi-method (quantitative plus qualitative) measurement, governance alignment, and cross-sector considerations to ensure robust inference about collaboration quality and output diversity under different AI governance modalities (MaT framework; HAIC framework; AI governance SLRs) (Seeber et al., 2020; Fragiadakis et al., 2024; Batool et al., 2025).

1) Research question and scope
- Central question: What is the effect of introducing a public-interest AI option (government-level AI) on collaboration quality and output diversity in cross-organizational teams, using a comparative study of teams with access to each option?
- Scope and framing: The analysis synthesizes governance-pronged evidence (public-interest AI design, governance, and cross-sector collaboration) to inform an experimental or quasi-experimental design that isolates the effect of governance modality on team collaboration outcomes, beyond engagement metrics (Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025; Batool et al., 2025). The emphasis is on observable collaboration quality and diversity of outputs, with governance variables as central mediators or moderators (MaT, 2020; Cui & Yasseri, 2024; Merchan-Cruz et al., 2024).

2) Theoretical framing and governance foundations
- Post-public AI and governance framing
  - The post-public AI era demands integrating human–AI dynamics, with teams increasingly comprising members with everyday AI experience. Governance should incentive high-quality collaboration and novelty rather than sole engagement metrics (Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025).
- Public-interest AI governance dimensions
  - Public justification, equality and human rights, deliberation/co-design, technical safeguards, and openness to validation are central constructs for evaluating public-interest AI deployments, with both efficiency and equity considerations (Nicolás & Sampaio, 2024; Züger & Asghari, 2023; Floridi et al., 2020) [inline references: Nicolás & Sampaio, 2024; Züger & Asghari, 2023; Floridi et al., 2020].
- Comparative governance frameworks and multi-level perspectives
  - Governance literature identifies five levels of AI governance (team, organization, industry, national, international) and four guiding questions (who, what, when, how). This multi-level lens is useful for analyzing how government-level AI could shape cross-organizational collaboration (Batool, Zowghi, & Bano, 2025).
- Mechanisms linking AI governance to collaboration outcomes
  - AI types influence trust and attention; deliberative co-design and inclusive participation can improve collaboration quality and output diversity (Bezrukova et al., 2023; Cui & Yasseri, 2024; Bondi et al., 2021).
  - AI-driven methods for analyzing team communication enable richer measurement of coordination patterns and boundary-spanning activity under different AI configurations (Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025).

3) Evidence landscape: what we know and what remains to test
- What empirical data exist
  - INSS Brazil case: governance dimensions affect efficiency and accessibility trade-offs; automation increased analysis rates and denials, with equity concerns and governance implications (Nicolás & Sampaio, 2024) [inline evidence: 2024 Brazil case; INSS references].
  - Public-interest AI governance syntheses (SLRs): identify governance artifacts across multiple levels and emphasize transparency, accountability, non-discrimination, and validation; empirical data on cross-organizational collaboration with a public-interest AI option are not provided (Batool et al., 2025).
  - MaT (Machines as teammates) provides a structured research agenda for AI in teams, including three design areas (Machine artifact, Collaboration, Institution) and 17 dualities, with no empirical trials comparing public-interest vs private AI in cross-organizational settings (Seeber et al., 2020).
  - Public-sector data governance and cross-sector collaboration literature (OECD 2025; Lundborg 2025; Selten & Klievink 2023) offer governance, structural, and organizational factors that are relevant to experimental design but do not deliver effect sizes for the specific comparison.
- What remains to test
  - Quantitative causal evidence on whether a public-interest AI option improves collaboration quality and increases output diversity in cross-organizational teams relative to a standard AI option.
  - Mediating mechanisms (trust, attention distribution, boundary-spanning activities, public-value alignment) and moderators (task type, domain, team composition, governance structures).

4) Proposed comparative study designs
- Design 1: Randomized controlled trial (two-arm)
  - Arms: (A) Public-interest AI option; (B) Standard private AI option.
  - Units: Cross-organizational teams from multiple public/private/government partners, engaged on shared AI-enabled tasks.
  - Outcomes: Collaboration quality (trust, coordination efficiency, shared mental models, accountability), output diversity (novelty, cross-domain ideas, variety of outputs).
  - Mediators: Trust, attention distribution, deliberation depth; Moderators: team size, diversity, task complexity.
  - Data collection: Surveys, transcripts, meeting artifacts, design artifacts; AI usage logs; external raters for output novelty.
  - Analysis: Multilevel modeling (team nested within organization), mediation analysis, and sensitivity analyses; pre-registration recommended.
  - Expected contribution: Direct causal evidence on whether public-interest AI design shifts incentives toward collaboration quality and novelty relative to a private AI baseline.
- Design 2: Three-arm RCT or quasi-experiment (adds a no-AI or minimal-AI baseline)
  - Arms: (A) Public-interest AI; (B) Private AI; (C) No-AI baseline or minimal-AI baseline.
  - Rationale: Disentangle AI-enabled effects from baseline collaboration dynamics; helps anchor effects on collaboration quality and output diversity to AI governance modalities.
- Design 3: Longitudinal, stepped-wedge, or matched-pairs design
  - Practical when randomization is difficult due to organizational constraints; teams switch from one AI option to another across steps, enabling within-team comparisons over time.
  - Governance artifacts (transparency, validation) vary with design, allowing analysis of how governance maturity interacts with AI-effects.

5) Core metrics and measurement plan (selection aligned with governance and CI/theory)
- Collaboration quality indicators (primary)
  - Trust and psychological safety (validated scales or multi-item proxies)
  - Coordination effectiveness (time-to-decision, number of cross-organizational handoffs, alignment of goals)
  - Shared mental models and mutual understanding (surveys or coding of transcripts)
  - Perceived accountability and alignment with public-interest goals
  - Attention distribution and contribution balance (e.g., who contributes what, distribution of speaking time, interruption patterns)
- Output diversity / novelty indicators (primary)
  - Novelty index of outputs (content analysis, expert ratings)
  - Cross-domain integration (breadth of problem framing, methods, and domains addressed)
  - Variety of outputs (number and types of artifacts, e.g., reports, interfaces, pilots)
- Process and governance indicators (mediators/moderators)
  - Public justification artifacts, deliberation depth, and co-design sessions
  - Transparency and auditability of AI recommendations
  - Data governance compliance and fairness indicators
- Data collection and instruments
  - Surveys for collaboration quality and perceived governance legitimacy
  - Transcripts and meeting logs for interaction quality and boundary-spanning analysis
  - Artifact repositories and output catalogs for diversity/ novelty scoring
  - AI usage logs to quantify engagement metrics and task coverage
  - External audits or raters for novelty/quality scoring
- Analysis plan
  - Primary analyses: comparison of means/medians across arms; effect sizes with confidence intervals
  - Multilevel models with team-level random effects; control for team size, domain, prior collaboration
  - Mediation analyses to test whether collaboration quality mediates the effect on output diversity
  - Moderation analyses to assess governance features (transparency, deliberation) as contingent effects
  - Pre-registration and analysis plan documentation; sensitivity checks for model specifications
- References for measurement foundations
  - HAIC (Human-AI Collaboration) measurement frameworks and the emphasis on interaction quality, transparency, and bias mitigation (Fragiadakis et al., 2024)
  - Post-public AI governance and cross-organizational collaboration evidence (Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025; Batool et al., 2025)
  - Output diversity/novelty measurement in AI-assisted collaboration (Przegalinska et al., 2025; Cui & Yasseri, 2024)
  - Industrial and public-sector governance perspectives on collaboration and public value (OECD, 2025; Mikhaylov et al., 2018)

6) Governance integration and implementation plan
- Align with established governance frameworks
  - AI Risk Management Framework (NIST AI RMF 1.0) as baseline risk/governance standard; ensure governance alignment, transparency, and auditability (NIST references referenced in governance literature) [NIST RMF 1.0; Nicolás & Sampaio 2024; Batool et al., 2025].
  - Equal AI impact assessment tools to monitor fairness and inclusion during cross-organizational AI deployments (Nicolás & Sampaio, 2024).
  - Public engagement and validation mechanisms (deliberation/co-design) to ensure legitimacy of AI-enabled collaboration (Züger & Asghari, 2023; Bondi et al., 2021).
  - NAIRR-like access considerations and cross-sector data-sharing governance to simulate public-interest AI resource availability (Nicolas & Sampaio 2024; Mikhaylov et al., 2018).
- Instrumentation and process safeguards
  - Public justification documents: articulate the social problem, AI role, and public-interest rationale
  - Deliberation rituals: regular stakeholder forums with diverse input
  - Technical safeguards: data-quality checks, privacy protections, access controls
  - Validation: external audits or peer reviews of AI contributions and outputs
- Practical considerations
  - Ensure that AI configurations in each arm are functionally comparable to isolate governance effects
  - Pre-register hypotheses and ensure data-sharing, consent, and privacy measures comply with governance requirements
  - Plan for potential friction points: governance overhead, facilitators, training requirements, and cross-sector policy constraints

7) Limitations and caveats
- The literature reviewed provides governance principles and design frameworks but not direct causal estimates of a public-interest AI option’s effect on cross-organizational collaboration outcomes. Any empirical study will require rigorous design, measurement validation, and careful interpretation of context (Batool et al., 2025; Bezrukova & Griffith, 2025; Seeber et al., 2020).
- Generalizability may be limited by domain, jurisdiction, and the maturity of AI governance ecosystems; multi-country or multi-domain replication would strengthen external validity (OECD, 2025; Mikhaylov et al., 2018).

8) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Build governance-first into the study design
  - Explicitly model public-interest AI governance features (public justification, equality/human rights safeguards, deliberation/co-design, technical safeguards, openness to validation) as core experimental conditions or moderators (Bezrukova et al., 2025; Merchan-Cruz et al., 2024).
- Use a robust, mixed-methods measurement approach
  - Combine quantitative collaboration quality and output-diversity metrics with qualitative accounts of governance processes, trust, and legitimacy to explain observed effects (Fragiadakis et al., 2024; Cui & Yasseri, 2024).
- Incorporate domain- and context-sensitivity
  - Tailor metrics to domain needs (health, policy, urban planning, etc.), drawing on domain-specific HAIC guidelines and governance considerations to ensure relevance (Fragiadakis et al., 2024; OECD, 2025).
- Pre-register and publish protocols
  - Given the current evidence gaps, preregistration of design, metrics, and analysis plans will fight publication bias and enable replication (MaT framework; HAIC guidance).
- Leverage governance artifacts for policy relevance
  - Use the results to inform policy and governance design for public-interest AI deployments, including recommendations for public accountability, data governance, and cross-sector collaboration incentives (Batool et al., 2025; Mikhaylov et al., 2018).
