# Dopamine Nutrition Label: Evidence-based assessment and design guidance for transparency cues in AI decision aids

Executive summary
- Objective and scope: Evaluate how a Dopamine Nutrition Label (DNL)—a simple disclosure of an AI system’s confidence, novelty, potential bias, and estimated cognitive load—could influence user trust, reliance, and decision quality across creativity, planning, and factual tasks. The synthesis draws on a broad literature corpus on trust calibration, transparency/explainability, and decision quality in human–AI interaction, and translates findings into design principles and experimental plans for DNL, while highlighting limits and domain-dependence.
- Core tensions: Transparency cues can improve understanding and trust in some contexts, but can also backfire by reducing trust, increasing cognitive load, or producing miscalibrated reliance if signals are poorly calibrated, misinterpreted, or not matched to task demands (e.g., over-trust when AI is wrong; under-trust when AI is right) (Mayer et al., 1995; Lee & See, 2004; Tomsett et al., 2020; Schemmer et al., 2022; Schmidt et al., 2020; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020).
- Evidence patterns across key literatures:
  - Trust calibration is dynamic and context-dependent; transparency is a necessary but not sufficient condition for safe reliance (Benda et al., 2021; Tomsett et al., 2020; de Visser et al., 2020; Sarker et al., 2023).
  - Explanations alone often have mixed effects on decision quality; their impact depends on how explanations are framed, their depth, user expertise, and task type (Lim et al., 2009; Cheng et al., 2019; Kulesza et al., 2013; Dodge et al., 2019; Sokol & Flach, 2020; Schemmer et al., 2022).
  - Confidence/uncertainty cues can aid calibration but can also reduce trust or discourage use if miscalibrated or poorly contextualized (Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2026; Subramanian et al., 2023).
  - Domain-general evidence and longitudinal work remain limited; many studies focus on domain-specific tasks (healthcare, education, security) or single-task contexts, underscoring the need for cross-domain, longitudinal evaluation (Visser et al., 2025; Bezzaoui et al., 2025; Caldwell et al., 2022).
- Practical implications for authors/designers:
  - A well-designed DNL should be adaptive (task- and user-aware), multi-signal (confidence, novelty, bias, cognitive load), and paired with concise, actionable reasoning traces or guidance.
  - It should balance depth with cognitive load, provide user controls for detail level, and be evaluated with domain-general, longitudinal designs that separate trust, reliance, and decision quality.
  - DNL signals should be grounded in robust measurement frameworks (calibration curves, PPV/NPV, likelihood ratios, functional/temporal specificity) and tested across domains (creativity, planning, factual) to identify where signals yield safe, effective reliance.

1) Conceptual foundations: trust, reliance, calibration, and transparency cues
- Trust vs reliance
  - Trust is a subjective belief about an AI system’s reliability, often construed as willingness to be vulnerable to the system’s outputs (Mayer, Davis, & Schoorman, 1995). Reliance is the actual use of the AI’s recommendations; these can diverge (Schoeffer et al., 2025).
  - Calibration refers to aligning trust with actual AI performance; resolution refers to adapting trust as goals and contexts change (Benda et al., 2021).
- Transparency/explainability
  - Explanations and transparency cues are intended to support trust and safe reliance, but their effects are heterogeneous and context-dependent. Explanations can improve understanding and task performance in some domains (Lim et al., 2009; Cheng et al., 2019; Kulesza et al., 2013) but can backfire in others (Turner et al.; Sokol & Flach, 2020; Bezzaoui et al., 2025).
  - Across domains, discrete signals such as confidence, uncertainty, bias disclosures, and cognitive-load estimates can influence trust calibration and reliance; however, they require careful design to avoid misinterpretation or overload (Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2026; Tomsett et al., 2020; Lebiere et al., 2021; Schemmer et al., 2022).
- Domain-general measurement and design considerations
  - A robust evaluation should disentangle trust calibration (subjective) from reliance (behavioral) and from final decision quality (objective outcome) (Schoeffer et al., 2025).
  - Trust dynamics are environment- and culture-sensitive; domain-general discovery requires longitudinal, cross-domain research with multi-method measurements (Bezzaoui et al., 2025; Dang & Li, 2025; Shin et al., 2022a).

2) Evidence landscape: implications for a Dopamine Nutrition Label
- Dermatology signal-work (Küper et al., 2025)
  - AI accuracy improved with AI support; reliance on AI increased more when AI was correct; reliance remained relatively low overall; propensity to trust tech and medical experience shaped patterns; confidence in initial decisions modulated susceptibility to AI input.
  - A Dopamine Nutrition Label that discloses confidence, novelty, bias, and cognitive load could influence trust calibration and safe reliance; however, calibration signals alone may not improve decision quality unless users can integrate signals with their own judgment and task context.
- Confidence calibration experiments (Zhang, Liao, Bellamy, 2020)
  - Case-by-case confidence signals can aid calibration but do not necessarily improve joint performance; explanations alone may not guarantee improved decision quality; alignment with human knowledge and error boundaries is critical.
- Confidence calibration with interactive, uncertainty-aware AI (Tomsett et al., 2020)
  - Interpretability plus uncertainty awareness can enable rapid trust calibration in high-stakes, dynamic settings; providing per-decision uncertainty signals and a rationale for AI outputs can reduce miscalibration when designed with attention to cognitive load.
- Trust, transparency, and visual analytics (Greitzer et al., 2011)
  - The design of transparency within visual analytics should balance information content and cognitive load; truthfully representing uncertain information and supporting sensemaking through high-level drivers improves trust and decision quality when properly aligned with user needs and domain goals.
- Human-centered explainability (Schoeffer et al., 2025)
  - Explanations can shift reliance and trust in nuanced ways; however, improvements in trust do not automatically translate into better decision quality; disaggregation of reliance quantity and reliability, along with care about domain-specific effects, is essential.

3) Task-domain considerations: creativity, planning, factual tasks
- Factual tasks
  - Evidence from uncertainty disclosures (Subramanian et al., 2023) shows that presenting AI uncertainty can improve objective correctness, though not universally increasing perceived trust; domain expertise plays a strong moderating role.
  - Confidence signals paired with clear bias information and accessible explanations can increase calibration for factual decisions, provided the signals map to actual model performance and data provenance.
- Planning tasks
  - Cognitive-load signals and confidence cues can help users decide when to intervene, particularly under high cognitive load; miscalibration can mislead planning steps, so careful display formats are needed (Lebiere et al., 2021; Tomsett 2020).
- Creativity tasks
  - Novelty cues may stimulate exploration but must be balanced against potential over-reliance on novelty signals or misplaced confidence, which could constrain divergent thinking if signals are misused.
  - A multi-signal DNL should provide guidance to manage exploration versus validation of AI-suggested ideas.

4) Design principles for a Dopamine Nutrition Label (DNL)
- Core components and goals
  - Confidence: display AI confidence or uncertainty, with calibration cues (e.g., uncertainty intervals, likelihood ratios) linked to historical accuracies.
  - Novelty: flag outputs that rely on novel patterns or less-generalized data; provide a brief rationale for why novelty matters and how to verify results.
  - Bias: annotate known biases or data limitations; provide quick mapping to risk levels and suggested checks.
  - Cognitive load: present an estimated cognitive load associated with adopting or applying AI recommendations; offer simplified views under high-load conditions.
- Per-output vs per-task transparency
  - Per-output signals should be paired with domain-appropriate rationale so users can evaluate AI outputs in context; optional per-task summaries can help calibrate high-level expectations.
- Personalization and adaptability
  - Tie disclosure depth to user characteristics (expertise, prior experience with AI, cognitive-load tolerance) and to task demands (creativity vs factual tasks vs planning).
  - Incorporate adaptive disclosure: default to concise signals; offer deeper rationales on demand; adjust detail based on measured cognitive load and user feedback.
- Presentation formats and visualization
  - Use intuitive encodings: color-coded confidence bands, simple narrative rationale, compact bias indicators, and a cognitive-load gauge that is easy to interpret.
  - Provide multimodal explanations (text, visuals, and summarized rationale) to accommodate diverse user preferences.
- Safeguards and calibration
  - Include explicit prompts for verification under high-stakes conditions; avoid over-reliance on a single signal; ensure signals reflect actual AI performance and system limitations.
  - Include user controls to adjust the level of transparency, and provide training resources that educate users on interpreting confidence, novelty, bias, and cognitive-load signals.
- Domain-general evaluation plan
  - Conduct longitudinal, cross-domain experiments (creativity, planning, factual) to measure trust calibration, reliance, and decision quality across DNL variants.
  - Employ a mixed-methods design that captures quantitative outcomes (calibration curves, PPV/NPV, likelihood ratios, decision accuracy) and qualitative insights (user perceptions, sensemaking, and mental model alignment).
  - Use mediation/moderation analyses to understand how confidence, novelty, bias, and cognitive load signals influence trust and reliance across domains, and how user expertise modulates these effects.
- Metrics and data collection
  - Trust calibration: calibration curves; distribution of trust vs AI performance; trust changes over time.
  - Reliance behavior: adherence rate, override rate, time-to-decision, and verification frequency.
  - Decision quality: task-specific objective measures (creativity rubric scores; planning quality metrics; factual accuracy).
  - Cognitive load: NASA-TLX or alternative workload scales; task performance under load.
  - Signal interpretation: user assessments of signal usefulness, signal salience, and confidence in AI outputs.
- Evaluation pragmatics
  - Pre-register hypotheses; report effect sizes, uncertainty intervals; present domain-specific results and cross-domain generalization.
  - Include cross-cultural and user-variation considerations where possible; tailor cues to different contexts and user populations.

5) Limitations and caveats
- The evidence base on a fixed “Dopamine Nutrition Label” as a standalone intervention is not yet established; existing literature demonstrates heterogeneous effects of transparency cues across tasks and contexts (Schoeffer et al., 2025; Schmidt et al., 2020; Li et al., 2026; Subramanian et al., 2023).
- Cross-domain generalization remains challenging; longitudinal studies across creativity, planning, and factual tasks are needed to determine where signals have the strongest and most reliable effects.
- The balance between transparency and cognitive load is delicate; overly rich disclosures can overwhelm users and degrade trust and performance, while too little disclosure can fail to calibrate trust.

6) Practical recommendations for authors and designers
- Ground the DNL in trust calibration theory, focusing on the interplay between perceived AI capability, perceived risk, and perceived control; map signals to the high-level Purpose/Process/Performance abstractions (Benda et al., 2021).
- Use adaptive, user-centered designs for transparency: tailor signal depth and modality to user expertise and task demands; implement progressive disclosure that allows users to access more detail if desired.
- Pair confidence signals with interpretable rationales and data provenance cues: connect numerical confidence to historical AI accuracy and to data quality or bias indicators, to improve interpretability and calibration.
- Incorporate a cognitive-load-aware design: provide concise signals by default, with optional deeper content; implement a “focus mode” during high-load tasks to minimize cognitive burden.
- Include explicit verification prompts for high-stakes decisions, especially in planning and factual tasks, to avoid automation bias or over-trust.
- Plan for rigorous evaluation: use domain-general, longitudinal designs; measure trust calibration, reliance quality, and decision quality; report effect sizes and uncertainty; use preregistration to reduce bias.
- Contextualize findings within governance and ethics: transparency cues must align with regulatory expectations and organizational governance, particularly in regulated domains (healthcare, finance, legal). 

7) Concluding remarks
- A Dopamine Nutrition Label—if thoughtfully designed as an adaptive, multi-signal transparency cue—has the potential to improve trust calibration and safe reliance across creativity, planning, and factual tasks. However, empirical validation across domains, task types, and user populations is essential, and signals must be calibrated to actual AI performance, cognitive load, and user capabilities to avoid unintended harms such as over-trust or cognitive overload.
- The most robust path forward is to design, pilot, and evaluate DNL within a structured, longitudinal, domain-general framework that disentangles trust calibration, reliance behavior (quantity and quality), and decision quality, while paying careful attention to task demands, user profiles, and cultural/contextual factors.
