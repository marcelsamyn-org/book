# report.md

Title: Assessing Sustained AI-Assisted Coaching for 6–12 Months: Impact on Skill Formation, Autonomy, and Dependency Risk

Authors: [Your Name], Research Team

Date: 2025

Executive summary
- Across domains, sustained AI coaching can improve targeted outcomes (e.g., goal attainment) and engagement in the short to medium term, but there is limited direct evidence about long-run autonomous skill formation, independence, or resilience when AI is unavailable. The best-available controlled data come from 6–10–month trials showing AI coaching matching human coaching on goal attainment, with little to no measurement of independence or AI-off performance (Terblanche et al., 2022; Abusamaan et al., 2024). 
- Systematic reviews consistently highlight a gap: no standardized, longitudinal evidence on skill formation and resilience to AI unavailability during sustained coaching, along with heterogeneous engagement metrics that complicate cross-study synthesis (Loughnane et al., 2025; Joksimović et al., 2023).
- Conceptual and design-oriented work consistently recommends: (a) explicit AI-off or fading phases to assess dependency risk and autonomy; (b) standardized dose/engagement metrics; (c) inclusion of domain-general and domain-specific skill measures; (d) human-in-the-loop safeguards to preserve autonomous judgment (DOE guidance; Akbar 2025; Khlaif 2025; Bauer et al. 2025; Akbar 2025; DOE 2023).
- Actionable implication: to answer the 6–12 month question, implement a rigorously designed longitudinal RCT (or multi-arm quasi-experiment) with AI-on and AI-off conditions, standardized engagement metrics, and explicit autonomy/dependency outcomes. A hybrid AI–human coaching arm can be included to balance scalability with depth of expertise while monitoring dependency risk.

1) Motivation and research question
- Core question: Over 6–12 months, how does sustained AI-assisted coaching affect (a) skill formation, (b) independence/autonomy, and (c) susceptibility to dependency (e.g., performance when AI is unavailable) compared with a control group without ongoing AI coaching?
- Rationale: The convergence of evidence suggests sustained AI coaching can accelerate certain proximal outcomes, but the longitudinal impact on autonomous expertise and resilience to AI downtime remains unclear. Longitudinal data with explicit AI-off conditions are needed to determine whether AI coaching strengthens autonomous skills or fosters dependency (Terblanche et al., 2022; Loughnane et al., 2025; Joksimović et al., 2023).

2) Synthesis of relevant evidence (high-level)
- AI coaching vs control on sustained outcomes
  - Terblanche et al. (2022) show AI coaching can increase goal attainment over ~10 months; AI is as effective as human coaching on that endpoint by study end. However, independence, autonomous judgment, and AI-unavailability resilience were not measured. This implies potential for skill formation in a goal-directed sense but does not demonstrate durable autonomy under AI withdrawal.
  - Abusamaan et al. (2024) provide a 12-month protocol comparing AI-assisted coaching to human coaching in a digital Diabetes Prevention Program. Primary endpoint is a CDC diabetes-risk-reduction benchmark at 12 months, with secondary endpoints including engagement and cost-effectiveness. No explicit AI-off condition or autonomy/dependency metrics are specified, limiting inferences about independence or AI-unavailability resilience from the protocol alone.
- Engagement, dose, and standardization
  - Loughnane et al. (2025) report feasibility/acceptability of AI, human, and hybrid coaching in digital health interventions, with positive engagement and lifestyle outcomes but substantial heterogeneity in engagement metrics and delivery. They highlight the need for standardized engagement metrics to enable cross-study comparisons and longer-term synthesis.
  - Standardization gaps (engagement metrics, dose, modality definitions) impede drawing conclusions about long-term autonomy and dependency risks across AI coaching implementations.
- Domain-general vs domain-specific considerations
  - Joksimović et al. (2023) identify that AI supports complex problem solving across cognitive, metacognitive, social, and affective dimensions, with domain-general and domain-specific effects. They emphasize the scarcity of longitudinal data on sustained coaching and independence, indicating a need to study domain-general vs domain-specific trajectories under sustained AI coaching.
- Autonomy and dependency frameworks
  - Khlaif et al. (2025) synthesize AI-dependency literature in education, highlighting drivers of dependency and potential negative consequences for autonomy; they propose I-P ACE and Self-Determination Theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness) as lenses for understanding AI-dependency dynamics, underscoring the need for autonomy-supportive design.
  - DOE (U.S. Department of Education, 2023) emphasizes human-in-the-loop governance, explainability, formative assessment, and guardrails to preserve learner autonomy in AI-enabled education.

3) Gaps and implications for your study design
- Direct long-term (6–12 months) evidence on independent skill formation and resilience to AI downtime is sparse. No single study provides a robust causal estimate of independence or dependency risk under sustained AI coaching across domains (Terblanche et al., 2022; Loughnane et al., 2025; Joksimović et al., 2023; Khlaif et al., 2025).
- There is no consensus on standardized engagement-dose metrics, complicating cross-study inference about how AI coaching dose relates to autonomy outcomes.
- The most compelling path to address the question is a longitudinal design with:
  - An AI-on group (sustained AI coaching)
  - A control group (no ongoing AI coaching)
  - Optional hybrid group (AI + human coaching, to monitor potential dependency dynamics)
  - An AI-off or fade-phase condition to explicitly test resilience and autonomous performance without AI support
  - Multiple domains (or at least multi-task components within a domain) to test domain-general vs domain-specific effects
  - Explicit, validated autonomy/independence measures and objective AI-off performance tasks
  - Pre-registered outcomes and analytic plan
- Practical and ethical considerations: ensure human-in-the-loop safeguards, explainability, data privacy, and deconfounding with standard engagement metrics; consider a mixed-methods approach (quantitative trajectories plus qualitative insights into autonomy, reliance, and decision-making).

4) Proposed study design (6–12 months)
- Design type: Longitudinal randomized controlled trial (or multi-arm quasi-experiment) with AI-on, AI-off, control, and optional hybrid arms.
- Population: Adults or professionals requiring skill development in a domain with clear, measurable competencies; ensure diverse participants to enhance generalizability. If domain-specific, select a domain with transferable outcomes (e.g., clinical decision support, complex problem solving, algorithmic/engineering tasks) and include a non-domain transfer task if possible.
- Arms (3–4 arms):
  - Arm A: Sustained AI coaching (AI-on) for 6–12 months
  - Arm B: Control (no ongoing AI coaching; standard non-AI coaching or minimal AI onboarding)
  - Arm C (optional): Hybrid AI+Human coaching to balance scalability with depth (monitor AI dependency risk)
  - Arm D (AI-off phase): AI-on for initial months, followed by a defined AI-off period to test resilience and independence
- Intervention specifics:
  - AI coaching features should include pedagogically sound scaffolding, metacognitive prompts, and explicit fading plans for autonomous practice
  - If using a hybrid arm, ensure human coach oversight and transitions to autonomous practice
  - Incorporate explicit AI-off test windows (e.g., monthly or quarterly) to assess performance without AI prompts
- Outcomes (primary):
  - Skill formation: domain-specific competencies, transfer to novel tasks, time-to-proficiency, and maintenance of skills after AI-off
  - Independence: autonomous planning, decision-making quality without AI prompts, self-regulated learning measures
  - Dependency risk: performance drop during AI-off windows, resilience and recovery after AI reintroduction, and reliance indices (e.g., proportion of tasks completed with AI assistance)
- Secondary outcomes:
  - Engagement dose: standardized metrics (dose, frequency, duration, modality) and adherence
  - Cognitive load and working memory demands during AI-enabled and AI-free phases
  - Satisfaction, perceived autonomy, trust in AI, and perceived ability to override AI
  - Cost-effectiveness and scalability (especially for AI-on vs hybrid)
- Measures and instruments:
  - Domain-relevant skill assessments (validated tests or performance tasks)
  - Independent performance tasks without AI (AI-off tests)
  - Self-regulation/autonomy scales (e.g., validated SRL measures)
  - Dependency metrics (self-report scales, usage logs, observed behavior)
  - Transfer tasks across domains or contexts to examine domain-general applicability
  - Qualitative interviews or think-aloud protocols to understand autonomy development and perceived dependency
- Data analysis plan:
  - Mixed-effects models to capture longitudinal trajectories and group differences
  - Growth-curve analyses to model skill formation and autonomy trajectories
  - Difference-in-differences (pre/post AI-off) to isolate AI-off effects
  - Moderation analyses for baseline expertise, AI literacy, task complexity, and modality (AI-only vs hybrid)
  - Pre-registration and preregistered analysis plan to enable replication and cross-study synthesis
- Safety, ethics, and governance:
  - Human-in-the-loop guardrails, explainability requirements, and override mechanisms
  - Data privacy protections and informed consent for AI-off testing
  - Regular safety reviews and escalation protocols (especially in health, medical, or safety-critical domains)

5) Actionable insights and recommendations for authors
- Use a fading/AI-off testing protocol:
  - Implement scheduled AI-off windows to quantify autonomous performance and resilience; quantify performance difference between AI-on and AI-off conditions
- Emphasize autonomy-supportive design:
  - Include explicit prompts for learners to justify AI recommendations, encourage independent problem-solving, and require autonomous outputs at defined milestones
- Domain-general vs domain-specific assessment:
  - Include tasks designed to test general problem-solving strategies in addition to domain-specific skills to identify domain-general autonomy gains
- Standardize engagement metrics:
  - Develop a core, cross-study engagement-dose metric (frequency, duration, modality mix, dialog depth) to enable cross-study synthesis and meta-analytic work
- Hybrid coaching considerations:
  - If including a hybrid arm, study how the human coach can scaffold autonomy and mitigate over-reliance on AI, with explicit tapering protocols
- Reporting and preregistration:
  - Pre-register hypotheses (e.g., AI-off performance as primary autonomy indicator; AI-driven skill formation as a secondary outcome)
  - Publish effect sizes with CIs for primary and secondary outcomes; share data to facilitate future meta-analyses
- Ethical and equity considerations:
  - Monitor for differential access to AI, privacy risks, and potential exacerbation of inequities; ensure accessibility and cultural adaptation as needed
- Practical implications:
  - Provide guidance on when AI coaching is most efficacious, where human coaching should supplement AI, and how fading strategies can maintain autonomous skills while still delivering benefits of AI

7) Practical implications for researchers and practitioners
- If your goal is to determine whether sustained AI coaching strengthens autonomous skill formation, a rigorous longitudinal design is essential. The recommended approach is to implement a fading AI strategy with explicit AI-off assessments and autonomy measures, ideally across multiple domains to gauge domain-general vs domain-specific effects.
- Hybrid models can offer scalability while preserving depth of expertise, provided there are deliberate safeguards to prevent excessive dependency and to promote autonomous problem solving.
- Standardization of engagement and dose metrics is critical to enable cross-study synthesis and accurate interpretation of how AI coaching dose relates to skill formation and independence.
- Ethical and safety considerations should be embedded from the design phase, with clear guardrails and human oversight to safeguard learner autonomy and wellbeing.

- Data availability and preregistration details
