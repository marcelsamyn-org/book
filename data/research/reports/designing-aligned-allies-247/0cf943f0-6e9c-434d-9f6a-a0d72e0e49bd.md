# report.md

Title: Aligned Ally vs. Engagement-Optimized AI in a Four-Week Field Experiment for Professional Knowledge Work

Authors: Research Design Team

Date: 2025

Executive summary
- This report outlines a comprehensive, evidence-based plan to conduct a two-arm, randomized controlled field experiment in professional knowledge-work settings over a four-week task loop. The goal is to compare an aligned-ally AI interface that enforces deliberate friction (e.g., mandatory reflection prompts, constrained auto-complete choices) with a conventional engagement-optimized AI, and to evaluate productivity (output quantity), quality (expert-rated accuracy/novelty), and learning depth across the four-week horizon.
- The design draws on robust field evidence from large-scale AI augmentation studies (notably the BCG/HBS field experiments) that demonstrate substantial productivity and quality gains when AI is used in frontier tasks, along with boundary conditions (frontier vs. outside-frontier performance) and patterns of human–AI collaboration (Centaurs vs. Cyborgs) [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].
- It also triangulates insights from related work on AI-driven productivity, learning depth, and deliberate-friction design principles (e.g., reflection prompts, audit trails, and data governance) across diverse knowledge-work contexts [Noy & Zhang, 2023; CHI 2025 findings; CAQ 2024; Stackpole 2024; Van Quaquebeke et al., 2025].
- Actionable deliverables include: a detailed experimental protocol, a concrete metric and analysis plan, data governance and ethics considerations, and a set of design guidelines for practitioners and researchers aiming to implement friction-enabled AI assistance in four-week field loops.

1) Problem statement and research question
- Core question: In a randomized controlled field experiment within professional knowledge-work settings, does an aligned-ally AI interface that enforces deliberate friction (e.g., mandatory reflection prompts, constrained auto-complete choices) improve learning depth and agency without sacrificing near-term productivity relative to a conventional engagement-optimized AI after a four-week task loop?
- Rationale in brief: Deliberate friction is posited to enhance cognitive engagement, self-regulated learning, and accountability, potentially yielding deeper understanding and more durable skills without large near-term productivity losses. The empirical literature provides evidence of productivity and quality benefits from AI augmentation in frontier tasks and identifies boundary conditions (frontier vs. outside-frontier) that inform friction design and task allocation (see section 4) [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].

2) Evidence-informed benchmarking to anchor design
- Frontier vs. outside-frontier effects and overall gains from AI augmentation
  - Frontier tasks: AI augmentation yields meaningful productivity, speed, and quality improvements (e.g., +12.2% tasks completed, +25.1% faster, +40%+ quality) with larger gains for lower performers; this establishes a credible baseline for where friction design may be most impactful when aligned with AI strengths [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].
  - Outside-frontier tasks: AI augmentation can reduce performance due to misalignment with current AI capabilities; friction design should therefore include task-framing, gating, and validation to mitigate risk (boundary awareness) [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].
- Learning depth, cognitive effort, and critical thinking in AI-assisted work
  - CHI 2025 work on AI and critical thinking demonstrates that GenAI can reduce perceived cognitive effort but also induce risk of overreliance; deliberate friction and verification protocols can counteract this and promote metacognition and responsible use, especially for experienced workers [Noy & Zhang, 2023; Lee et al., 2025]. Inline: [CHi 2025; Noy & Zhang 2023; Lee et al., 2025].
- Governance, auditability, and data-prior considerations
  - The CAQ (Auditing in the Age of Generative AI, 2024) emphasizes explainability, audit trails, and governance to support reliable AI adoption in important professional tasks; these considerations map closely to friction-enabled alignment features that promote transparency and traceability [CAQ, 2024]. Inline: [CAQ, 2024].
- Practical design patterns for friction-enabled interfaces
  - Deliberate friction concepts (e.g., mandatory reflection prompts, constrained choice sets, audit trails) are widely discussed as design levers to promote agency and learning while maintaining accountability; field-tested empirical validation in four weeks remains an open area, making this trial particularly informative [Yang et al., 2024; Stackpole 2024; Van Quaquebeke et al., 2025].
- Key takeaway for design
  - A friction-enforced aligned-ally interface has plausible benefits for learning depth and agency, but must be calibrated to avoid meaningful near-term productivity losses, particularly on tasks outside the AI frontier. A careful four-week field trial with frontier-aware task allocation and robust measurement can illuminate these dynamics.

3) Target population and setting
- Population: Professional knowledge workers in knowledge-intensive domains (e.g., consultants, analysts, policy or regulatory professionals, technical writers) who regularly produce analytical documents, decision-support material, or complex syntheses.
- Setting: Real-world organizational work environments in which AI augmentation is embedded into routine workflows (document drafting, analysis, reporting, and synthesis tasks) over a four-week period.
- Rationale: Large-field evidence shows AI gains in frontier tasks with professional knowledge workers, with notable heterogeneity by baseline performance and task type, making this population appropriate for testing friction-enabled interfaces at scale [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].

4) Experimental design and randomization
- Design: Randomized controlled field experiment with two arms
  - Arm A: Aligned ally friction interface (deliberate friction)
    - Features:
      - Mandatory reflection prompts after key task stages (e.g., after drafting, after evaluating AI suggestions, before accepting AI output)
      - Constrained auto-complete choices (curated option sets; user must justify deviations)
      - Structured prompts requiring justification and explicit rationale for edits or insertions
      - Audit-trail logging of decisions and reflections for traceability
    - Optional enhancements: lightweight “AI-aided questioning” prompts to challenge AI suggestions without overwhelming the user.
  - Arm B: Conventional engagement-optimized AI
    - Features:
      - Free-form AI assistance with minimal friction prompts
      - Broad auto-complete/suggestion surface
      - No mandatory reflection prompts or justification requirements
      - Standard logging for usage (but no enforced audit trail for reflection)
- Randomization unit and sample size
  - Randomization unit: Individual knowledge workers (or small teams if collaboration is integral to the tasks) to balance power and manage contamination risk.
  - Stratification: By domain area, baseline productivity, prior AI familiarity, and task complexity distribution.
  - Sample size: A priori power analysis is recommended. Based on prior large-field results (e.g., 758 participants in a frontier-oriented study) and to accommodate a four-week repeated-measures design, target N ≈ 250–350 participants per arm (adjusted for anticipated attrition and weekly return rates), which supports detecting medium effect sizes in productivity, learning depth, and quality with mixed-effects modeling. See Section 7 for analysis details.
- Task loop and duration
  - Four weekly task blocks with standardized task types (e.g., drafting, analysis, synthesis, reporting) ensuring overlap in domain-relevant skills and AI interactions.
  - Frontier labeling: Each task is categorized as frontier-friendly (AI capabilities likely to assist well) or outside frontier (AI capabilities unlikely to support reliably). The distribution should be balanced across arms to examine frontier effects on productivity, quality, and learning depth.
- Outcomes and endpoints
  - Primary outcomes (pre-registered):
    - Productivity: Output quantity per week (e.g., number of deliverables produced, word-count or document units), and per-task throughput.
    - Quality: Expert-rated accuracy and novelty using standardized rubrics; inter-rater reliability targets (e.g., ICC > 0.75).
    - Learning depth: Composite index derived from reflection-quality metrics, transfer tasks, and domain knowledge assessments (pre/post) or weekly metacognitive probes; retention/transfer tests post-intervention if feasible.
  - Secondary outcomes:
    - AI adoption/usage intensity: rate of AI suggestion usage, reflection-prompt engagement, adherence to constraints.
    - Perceived cognitive load, task enjoyment, and perceived autonomy/trust in AI outputs.
    - Task-level friction indicators: reflection prompt completion rate, justification length, and time spent on reflective phases.
  - Mediators and moderators:
    - Friction engagement depth (quality of reflections, justification detail)
    - Frontier vs. outside-frontier task performance
    - Baseline expertise and prior AI familiarity
- Instrumentation and data collection
  - System logs: Detailed AI prompts used, selections accepted, time spent per task, revisions, and reflection prompts completed.
  - Output artifacts: Deliverables produced per task, with versioning metadata.
  - Expert panel: Independent raters blind to arm assignment evaluate quality and novelty; ensure robust rubric with multiple anchors and calibration sessions.
  - Knowledge assessments: Pre-task baseline and post-task learning assessments; optional transfer tasks to gauge learning depth beyond week 4.
  - Process measures: Audit-trail data, reflection-depth coding (qualitative rubric), and cognitive-load proxies via brief in-task surveys.
- Analysis plan (statistical approach)
  - Primary analysis: Mixed-effects models with random intercepts for participant (and potentially for teams) and fixed effects for arm, week, and arm × week interaction.
  - Secondary analyses: Mediation analysis to test whether reflection-depth mediates learning depth and any indirect effects on productivity/quality; moderator analysis for frontier status and baseline proficiency.
  - Intention-to-treat (ITT) and per-protocol analyses to assess adherence impact; sensitivity analyses to assess outliers and task-type differences.
  - Pre-registration: All primary and secondary endpoints, models, and plan for handling missing data should be preregistered to improve credibility and reproducibility.
- Validity threats and mitigation
  - Ecological validity: Conduct in real workplaces with randomized assignment and standardized task sets; use frontier classification to ensure tasks align with AI strengths.
  - Adherence and attrition: Build onboarding, ongoing support, and lightweight friction calibration to reduce disengagement; implement adaptive friction controls if necessary.
  - Learning depth measurement: Use validated rubrics and multi-method indicators (reflections, transfer tests) to avoid conflating superficial compliance with genuine learning.
  - Rater bias: Implement blinded expert ratings; rotate raters and provide calibration sessions to ensure reliability.
  - Data privacy and ethics: Obtain IRB approval, ensure informed consent, minimize data exposure, and use encryption and de-identification where possible; maintain audit trails with access controls.

5) Inline citations and evidence mapping
- Frontier effect and productivity/quality gains in frontier tasks
  - Dell’Acqua et al., 2023: frontier tasks yield +12.2% more tasks, +25.1% faster delivery, and >40% higher quality; large heterogeneity by baseline performance; outside-frontier tasks may degrade performance (19 percentage-point drop) [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023]. Inline: [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023].
- Friction design and learning depth insights in related work
  - CHI 2025: GenAI reduces perceived cognitive effort but can reduce critical thinking unless mitigated by verification strategies and friction-based guidelines; deliberate friction may support learning depth and autonomy in experienced users [Lee et al., 2025; Noy & Zhang, 2023]. Inline: [CHi 2025; Noy & Zhang 2023].
- Governance and auditability in AI-enabled professional work
  - CAQ (Auditing in the Age of Generative AI, 2024): emphasize explainability, audit trails, governance, and data-quality considerations; aligns with friction design to promote transparency and accountability [CAQ, 2024]. Inline: [CAQ, 2024].
- Additional design considerations for friction interfaces
  - Stackpole (2024): medium friction (e.g., targeted friction prompts) may improve accuracy without significant throughput penalties; heavy friction can raise cognitive load. This informs friction calibration choices for the four-week trial [Stackpole, 2024]. Inline: [Stackpole, 2024].
- Related frontier/learning frameworks
  - Van Quaquebeke et al. (2025) and System 0 design principles provide a theoretical scaffold for friction-based designs that balance agentic transparency, learning depth, and productivity; these may guide the development of the friction prompts and audit-ability features [System 0; Van Quaquebeke et al., 2025].

6) Actionable design guidelines for practitioners and researchers
- Implement friction with purpose
  - Use mandatory reflection prompts after critical task steps to induce deliberate processing and self-regulated learning.
  - Constrain the AI’s auto-complete suggestions to a curated set, requiring justification for deviations to encourage deliberate decision-making.
  - Maintain an auditable decision trail that records the rationale for AI-supported actions, enabling accountability and learning extraction.
- Frontier-aware task allocation
  - Classify tasks as frontier-friendly or outside-frontier to decide where friction should be engaged most intensively (e.g., frontier tasks receive stronger reflection prompts and stricter justification rules; outside-frontier tasks receive stronger human oversight and gating).
- Learning-depth centric measurement
  - Include pre/post knowledge tests and transfer tasks to quantify learning depth, in addition to standard productivity and quality metrics.
  - Use reflection-depth indicators (richness and specificity of post-task reflections, justification quality) as primary mediators for learning depth.
- Reliability, privacy, and governance
  - Build robust data governance: data provenance, versioning, access control, and privacy-preserving handling of reflections and prompt logs.
  - Ensure explainability: require AI to provide rationale for decisions and enable users to request clarifications.
- Sample sizing and statistical power
  - A four-week field trial with two arms and four weekly observations benefits from around 200–300 participants per arm to detect medium effects in productivity and learning depth in mixed-effects frameworks; adjust for expected attrition and cluster effects if teams are the randomization units.
- Practical onboarding and ramp-up
  - Provide targeted onboarding that explains friction rationale and the learning objectives; consider a short baseline period for participants to acclimate to friction prompts before formal data collection begins.
- Ethical and governance considerations
  - Ensure IRB approvals, consent processes, and data-use policies; maintain transparency with participants about what is being measured, how data will be used, and how AI assistance may influence outcomes.

7) Appendix: Implementation blueprint (high-level)
- Timeline
  - Week -2 to Week 0: Preparation and baseline assessments; materials, rubrics, and friction prompts finalized; pilot friction calibration with a small cohort.
  - Week 1–Week 4: Full four-week trial with weekly task loops; data collection ongoing; weekly debriefs to monitor workload and satisfaction; interim safety/quality checks as needed.
  - Week 5: Debrief and final assessments; analysis begins; reporting and dissemination planning.
- Materials and tooling
  - Aligned ally friction interface prototype: reflection prompts, constrained autocomplete, audit-trail capture, and a front-end UI for justifications.
  - Control interface: standard AI prompts, unconstrained suggestions.
  - Rubrics: detailed quality (accuracy, relevance, novelty) and learning depth rubrics; reflection-depth coding guide.
  - Data governance toolkit: consent forms, data access controls, audit logs, data-minimization guidelines.
- Governance and ethics
  - Institutional approvals; data privacy review; legal/compliance checks; transparency plans for participants.

Notes and caveats
- This report synthesizes diverse bodies of evidence to propose a rigorous four-week field experiment testing a friction-enforced aligned-ally AI interface vs a conventional engagement-optimized AI. While there is strong evidence that AI augmentation can boost productivity and quality in frontier tasks, there is less direct empirical evidence for deliberate-friction interfaces in four-week professional field loops. The planned design integrates frontier-aware task allocation, robust measurement of learning depth, and governance/ethics scaffolds to generate actionable, generalizable results.
