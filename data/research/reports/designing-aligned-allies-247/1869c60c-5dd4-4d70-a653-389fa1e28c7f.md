# Report: Assessing Output Diversity Sampling via Concurrent A/B Evaluation in Real-World Tasks

Artifact: report.md

Executive summary
- Objective: Determine whether enforcing output diversity sampling—presenting a small set of diverse options with explicit contrastive explanations—increases originality and calibration without compromising factual accuracy, using a concurrent A/B design on real work tasks.
- Core rationale: The literature identifies risks of homogenization in LLM outputs and emphasizes multi-faceted evaluation (calibration, factual accuracy, user trust) across phases and tasks. QUEST provides a structured human-evaluation scaffold; rigorous calibration and factuality metrics (BLEU/ROUGE/HELM) supplement human judgments; human evaluators remain critical in domains like healthcare and professional work (Tam et al., 2024; Papineni et al., 2002; Lin, 2004; Liang et al., 2022).
- Design blueprint: Leverage QUEST principles to plan, implement adjudication, and score, while embedding a concurrent A/B comparison: Arm A (baseline conventional output) vs Arm B (diverse output set with explicit contrastive explanations). Assess originality, calibration, factual accuracy, and user satisfaction using a mix of objective metrics and human ratings.
- Actionable outcomes: Provide a concrete protocol for implementing the study, specify metrics (origin­ality, calibration, factual accuracy, user satisfaction), describe the statistical analysis plan (mixed-effects models with pre-registered hypotheses), and lay out risk-mitigation strategies (adjudication rounds, inter-rater reliability checks, factual validation).
- Limitations: Direct empirical evidence for “output diversity sampling” as a standalone intervention in real work tasks is sparse in the cited sources; the study design must operationalize originality and calibration explicitly and guard against degradation in factual accuracy.

1) Introduction and problem framing
- Research question: Does enforcing output diversity sampling (a small set of diverse options with explicit contrastive explanations) increase originality and calibration without harming factual accuracy, when evaluated via a concurrent A/B design on real work tasks?
- Why it matters: Output homogenization and mode collapse threaten long-tail usefulness and trust. A diversity-enabled approach could broaden solution spaces (originality) and improve users’ calibration of confidence, provided factual accuracy is maintained (Tam et al., 2024; Bender et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2024).
- Foundational frameworks and metrics draw from:
  - QUEST (Tam et al., 2024): Planning, Implementation/Adjudication, Scoring/Review; dimensions include Quality of Information, Understanding and Reasoning, Expression Style and Persona, Safety/Harm, Trust and Confidence.
  - Evaluation approaches for factuality and calibration: BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), HELM (Liang et al., 2022); traditional accuracy-based metrics (Tam et al., 2024).
  - Human evaluation emphasis: In healthcare and professional domains, human evaluation is crucial for utility, safety, and accuracy; automated metrics alone are insufficient (Tam et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Awasthi et al., 2023).
- Gaps identified for this study:
  - Direct empirical evidence on “output diversity sampling” as a discrete intervention in concurrent A/B designs is not reported in the core sources; the present study fills this gap by implementing a structured protocol grounded in established evaluation practices.

2) Conceptual grounding and metrics
- Output diversity sampling and contrastive explanations
  - Conceptual aim: Surface a small set of diverse options, each with explicit contrastive rationales that enable users to compare alternatives and identify trade-offs.
  - Potential benefits: Increased perceived originality, richer user deliberation, and improved understanding of model reasoning, if explanations are well-grounded and non-harmful.
  - Risks: Potential degradation of factual accuracy or increased cognitive load if contrasts are poorly designed or misaligned with domain facts.
- Originality and calibration as evaluation targets
  - Originality: Proxies include diversity of outputs across candidates, cross-task novelty, and human judgments of novelty/usefulness. Some literature suggests that diversity strategies can improve exploration and reduce homogenization, but concrete, multi-task evidence remains limited (e.g., AmbER, MOCHA, NoveltyBench lines; Chen, 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Awasthi et al., 2023).
  - Calibration: Alignment between confidence signals and actual correctness; reliability diagrams, Brier scores, and more advanced, multiclass calibration measures (ECE, MCE, cwECE, ACE) are recommended for multi-output settings (Virk et al., 2025; Naeini et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2017; Kull et al., 2019; Nixon et al., 2019).
- Human-in-the-loop and adjudication
  - Use QUEST’s three-phase workflow to structure tasks, sampling, adjudication, and scoring, ensuring training and adjudication of evaluators, and measuring inter-rater reliability (kappa/ICC).

Inline citations used in this section: (Tam et al., 2024); (Papineni et al., 2002); (Lin, 2004); (Liang et al., 2022); (Wei et al., 2024); (Park et al., 2024); (Yuan et al., 2024); (Awasthi et al., 2023); (Chen, 2023); (Kiyasseh et al., 2024); (Baladram, 2025); (Virk et al., 2025).

3) Study design: concurrent A/B in real work tasks
- Population and tasks
  - Real work tasks that require decision support, synthesis, or analytic reasoning (e.g., professional writing, research planning, domain-analytic tasks) to ensure ecological validity.
  - Task diversity: Include a mix of fact-based, analytic, and creative tasks to test domain-generalizable effects.
- Experimental arms
  - Arm A (Baseline): Standard AI output generation with a single best answer or a typical top-k that lacks explicit contrastive explanations.
  - Arm B (Diversity Sampling): Output diversity sampling with a small set (e.g., 3–5) of diverse options, each accompanied by explicit contrastive explanations comparing options and identifying trade-offs.
- Concurrency and randomization
  - Randomly assign tasks or users to Arm A or Arm B to obtain unbiased estimates; block by task type or domain to reduce confounding.
- Task iterations and adjudication
  - Use a multi-phase QUEST-adjacent adjudication approach: initial evaluator scoring (Phase 1: Planning/Planning), implementation/adjudication (Phase 2), and scoring review (Phase 3) with adjudication rounds to resolve disagreements.
- Outcomes and metrics (primary, secondary)
  - Primary outcomes:
    - Originality: objective (distinct output diversity across options) and human judgments of novelty/usefulness.
    - Calibration: per-arm calibration signals (ECE, MCE, Brier Score, reliability diagrams) and calibration-in-the-large; if feasible, classwise calibration or top-k calibration.
    - Factual accuracy: expert adjudication on factual correctness for selected outputs; fact-checking against ground-truth or trusted sources.
  - Secondary outcomes:
    - User satisfaction and perceived usefulness/trust in explanations.
    - Decision quality and time-to-decision.
    - Inter-rater reliability (kappa/ICC) for human judgments; adjudication rounds to resolve disagreements.
- Statistical analysis plan
  - Primary analysis: linear mixed-effects models or generalized linear mixed models with random effects for task and evaluator, fixed effects for arm (A vs B), task type, and potential interactions.
  - Effect size reporting: Cohen’s d for continuous outcomes; odds ratios for binary outcomes; 95% CIs.
  - Calibration analysis: compare calibration curves and compute Brier, ECE/MCE; consider cwECE/ACE for multiclass settings if many options are used.
  - Multiple comparisons: apply corrections (e.g., Holm-Bonferroni) if evaluating multiple correlated outcomes.
- Pre-registration, preregistration artifacts, and reporting
  - Pre-register hypotheses, task set, evaluation rubrics, and statistical analysis plan; commit to sharing methods and aggregated results to promote reproducibility.
- Ethical and governance considerations
  - Ensure transparency about the diversity mechanism, provide opt-out or explainable controls for users, and maintain data privacy and informed consent where applicable.
  - Monitor for biases or unintended harms introduced by explicit diversity prompts or contrastive explanations.

4) Measurement instruments and concrete metrics
- Originality/Novelty
  - Output diversity metrics: lexical/semantic diversity across options (distinct-N, embedding-based distances), plus human-rated novelty/usefulness for each option.
  - Cross-task novelty: assess if diversity in one task correlates with novelty in related tasks.
  - If feasible, cross-task recombination indicators (e.g., mix of strategies across tasks).
- Calibration
  - Calibration metrics: ECE, MCE, Brier Score; reliability diagrams per arm; multiclass calibration measures if each option has a probability-like score.
  - Top-k calibration: if presenting multiple options, assess calibration across the set (Top-k lens, GECE or other context-specific lenses if applicable).
- Factual accuracy
  - Ground-truth adjudication: expert validation of factual statements across outputs.
  - Automated checks where appropriate (cross-checks against external sources, citation verification, or domain knowledge checks).
- User experience
  - Satisfaction, perceived usefulness, trust, cognitive load; Likert scales; post-task interviews or open-ended feedback.
- Adjudication quality
  - Inter-rater reliability and adjudication round outcomes; time spent adjudicating.

5) Practical considerations and risk mitigation
- Evaluator training and harmonization
  - Provide evaluator training on scoring rubrics to reduce variance; pre-train on a subset of tasks to stabilize scoring.
- Adjudication throughput
  - Plan for adjudication rounds with a manageable number of disagreements; set thresholds for acceptable disagreement and escalation criteria.
- Task- and domain-specific considerations
  - Some domains may favor more or less diversity; stratify analyses by domain to identify domain-specific effects.
- Computational and UI constraints
  - Presentations of multiple options should be user-friendly; avoid cognitive overload by constraining the number of options and ensuring clear, concise contrastive explanations.

6) Actionable insights and recommendations for the author
- Grounding in established frameworks
  - Use QUEST to structure planning, implementation/adjudication, and scoring; align Phase activities with concurrent A/B design (Tam et al., 2024).
  - Map outcomes to core dimensions: originality (Expression Style/Persona and novelty assessments), calibration (Understanding/Reasoning and Trust), factual accuracy (Quality of Information), and user satisfaction (perceived usefulness).
- Metrics and data collection plan
  - Adopt a multi-metric approach: originality (diversity metrics + human novelty judgments), calibration (ECE/Brier/MCE; reliability curves), factual accuracy (expert adjudication), and user satisfaction.
  - Include objective task performance and time-to-decision as secondary outcomes.
- Design choices for Arm B (diversity)
  - Use 3–5 diverse options per task with explicit contrastive explanations for each option.
  - Ensure explanations are concise, grounded in domain facts, and structured to facilitate comparison (contrastive reasoning; non-probabilistic language where possible).
- Adjudication and reliability
  - Use adjudication rounds to resolve differences; monitor inter-rater reliability; adjust scoring rubrics as needed to improve consistency.
- Reproducibility and governance
  - Pre-register the protocol; publish aggregated results and methodology; consider open data and code sharing to enable replication and cross-domain comparison.
- Risk management
  - Monitor for degradation in factual accuracy; incorporate fact-check gates and source verification where possible; ensure contrasts do not mislead or misrepresent evidence.
- Timeline and milestones
  - Phase I: Study design and evaluator training; Phase II: Pilot with a subset of tasks; Phase III: Full deployment; Phase IV: Adjudication, analysis, and reporting.
- Reporting format
  - Provide a results matrix with per-arm statistics for originality, calibration, factual accuracy, and user satisfaction; include effect sizes, CIs, and calibration curves; discuss limitations and external validity.

7) Limitations and caveats
- Direct empirical precedent for “output diversity sampling” in production, real-world A/B contexts remains limited in the sources provided; study design must clearly operationalize originality and calibration to avoid conflating evaluation dimensions.
- Measuring originality can be domain-sensitive; ensure robust, domain-appropriate metrics and triangulation with human judgments.
- Calibration metrics for multi-output generation require careful specification (Top-k, grouping lenses, or alternative calibration formulations) to reflect how users interpret multiple options.
