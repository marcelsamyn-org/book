Operationalizing credible causal evidence for everyday LLM use: a taxonomy, quality indicators, and a practical methodology spine

Executive summary
- A robust evidentiary spine for everyday LLM use should integrate diverse empirical designs and evidence types, anchored by transparent reporting standards and bias-relevant quality indicators. The aim is to support credible causal inferences about how people interact with LLMs in everyday contexts (education, work, design practice, health, design tasks) without overclaiming when design space or data are constrained (Zhou & Chen, 2025; Guo et al., 2023; Tam et al., 2024).
- Foundational scaffolds for the book’s Methodology chapter include: a PRISMA-based systematic approach to assembling evidence; a design taxonomy aligned to credible causal claims (RCTs, field experiments, quasi-experiments, observational designs, qualitative studies, meta-analyses, mixed methods); and a clear hierarchy of evidence with explicit quality indicators.
- Core quantitative and qualitative design types to anchor the methodology:
  - Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and field experiments for causal identification.
  - Quasi-experiments and natural experiments when randomization is infeasible.
  - Meta-analyses and systematic reviews to synthesize cross-study consistency.
  - Rigorous qualitative and mixed-methods studies to illuminate mechanisms, context, and human factors.
- Quality indicators and reporting standards should be tailored to LLM contexts and AI-specific evaluation challenges. These include:
  - Internal validity and bias controls (randomization integrity, pre-registration, robust analysis plans).
  - External validity and transportability across domains, languages, and contexts.
  - Construct validity for outcome measures (task performance, safety, reliability, usability, and fairness).
  - Reproducibility and transparency (data provenance, prompts, model versions, evaluation pipelines).
  - Ethics, governance, and safety considerations (bias, privacy, accountability, and risk mitigation).
- Actionable blueprint for the Methodology chapter:
  - Organize around a three-to-four-path evidentiary spine (RCTs/field experiments; transportability-based observational designs; meta-analyses; qualitative/mixed-methods) with explicit mappings to outcome types, design strengths, and human-in-the-loop requirements.
  - Introduce a domain-informed framework for inclusion criteria and evidence weighting, drawing on established AI-guidelines (PRISMA-AI, SPIRIT-AI, CONSORT-AI, TRIPOD-AI) and governance-sensitive extensions (PROBAST-AI, DECIDE-AI, CLAIM-AI) to ensure high-quality reporting and applicability across domains (education, healthcare, business).
  - Provide concrete templates, auditing checklists, and exemplar appendices (prompts, prompts-with-tools configurations, data provenance, and governance artifacts) to enable reproducible, auditable research pipelines.
- Domains and cross-domain relevance: While many sources originate in health AI, the taxonomy and methodology are designed for everyday LLM use across education, design, business, and health. The design principles emphasize ecological validity, human-in-the-loop oversight, and governance framing to ensure credible causal conclusions across contexts (Guo et al., 2023; Diehl et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2025).

1) Foundational concepts and evidentiary spine for everyday LLM use
- Credible causal inference in everyday LLM use requires explicit attention to causal structure, identifiability, and generalizability across contexts and cultures. While LLMs enable powerful capabilities, everyday use invites confounds, data drift, and context-dependence that demand rigorous evaluation designs and transparent reporting (Guo et al., 2023; Pearl & Bareinboim; Diehl et al., 2017).
- A systematic, design-centered evidentiary spine should map LLM capabilities to observed outcomes within a design-framework that distinguishes LLMs as tools versus materials, and that connects core LLM abilities (e.g., reasoning, tool use) to design choices and measurement approaches (Zhou & Chen, 2025; Guo et al., 2023).
- Core methodological anchors to consider:
  - Systematic reviews: PRISMA-based approaches, with AI-specific extensions (PRISMA-AI) to standardize reporting for AI-enabled evidence syntheses (Equator Network; PRISMA-AI) [PRISMA-AI, 2022].
  - Causal inference in AI/Evaluations: foundational causal frameworks (Pearl, Robins; Rubin), and domain-adaptation/transporter frameworks for cross-domain generalization (Sobel et al., 2017; Dahabreh et al., 2019/2020; Bareinboim & Pearl, 2021).
  - Domain-specific evaluation scaffolds: LLM-evaluation taxonomies (Knowledge/Capability, Alignment, Safety) that align with credible causal claims in everyday use (Guo et al., 2023).
- Practical realization
  - An evidentiary spine should present three-tier evidence: (a) causal-effect-oriented primary studies (RCTs/field experiments), (b) cross-study syntheses (meta-analyses, MBMAs), and (c) mechanism- and context-focused qualitative/mixed-methods studies with rigorous auditing guidelines. This aligns with the broader AI-evaluation literature, which emphasizes the value of triangulation and transparent reporting (Guo et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2025; Tam et al., 2024).

2) Taxonomy of study designs anchored to credible causal evidence for everyday LLM use
The following taxonomy integrates the key design families and their quality indicators, linking to the broader AI-evaluation and health-evidence standards described in the sources:

A. Randomized and quasi-randomized field designs (RCTs and field experiments)
- Design intent: Assess causal impact of LLM deployments, prompts, or tool augmentations in real user contexts.
- Evidence alignment: Direct causal inference in naturalistic contexts; supports external validity when contexts are representative of everyday use.
- Implementation notes: randomization at participant, task, domain, or cluster level; pre-registration of hypotheses and analysis plan; include diverse domains and contexts to enhance external validity (Guo et al., 2023; Tam et al., 2024; Zhou & Chen, 2025).
- Quality indicators:
  - Randomization integrity; allocation concealment; blinding where feasible.
  - Pre-registered hypotheses and analysis plans; transparent reporting of deviations.
  - Effect sizes with CIs; robust statistics; multiple outcome measures (including safety, usability, and alignment metrics).
  - External validity: multi-domain replication, domain diversification, and transportability considerations (Sobel et al., 2017; Bareinboim & Pearl, 2021).
  - Reproducibility: data, prompts, model versions, and evaluation scripts openly documented where permissible (PRISMA-AI alignment).
- Representative exemplars: CLadder-type causal tasks; real-world LLM prompts tested across multiple models with ground-truth causal framing; classic randomized trials in AI-enabled decision support.

B. Field experiments across domains (natural experiments)
- Design intent: Real-world deployments with staged or policy-driven variations; measure causal impact with quasi-randomized or natural variation.
- Implementation notes: use staggered rollouts, policy changes, or region-based treatment differences; record baseline and post-intervention measures with attention to confounders (DiD/ITS; natural experiments).
- Quality indicators:
  - Clear domain context; baseline equivalence checks; confounds considered; sensitivity analyses for unobserved confounding.
  - Documentation of implementation fidelity and context changes across sites.
- Representative exemplars: domain-adaptive evaluation in education, health, or enterprise contexts; field AB tests of prompts or tools integrated into workflows.

C. Meta-analyses and synthesis (cross-study aggregation)
- Design intent: Aggregate effect estimates across studies to improve precision and assess cross-domain consistency.
- Implementation notes: standard meta-analytic procedures; heterogeneity assessment; domain moderators; potential use of model-based meta-analyses (MBMA) or time-to-event meta-analyses (META-TTE) where appropriate.
- Quality indicators:
  - Pre-registered synthesis protocols (PRISMA-P); explicit inclusion criteria; rigorous risk-of-bias appraisal (PROBAST-AI; TRIPOD-AI) per domain.
  - Heterogeneity metrics (I^2, tau^2); sensitivity analyses; publication-bias checks; transparent reporting of study-level data and effect sizes.
  - AI-specific reporting considerations (PRISMA-AI) for AI-in-synthesis contexts; data provenance and reproducibility controls.
- Representative exemplars: syntheses across LLM-evaluations in education/health/business with standardization of outcomes and AI-tool disclosures.

D. Rigorous qualitative studies (and mixed-methods)
- Design intent: Contextualize causal claims with mechanisms, user experiences, decision processes, and governance considerations.
- Implementation notes: rigorous qualitative design (e.g., Braun & Clarke-style thematic analyses; grounded theory; framework/systems analysis); triangulation across data sources; reflexivity and audit trails.
- Quality indicators:
  - Sampling strategy; saturation; inter-coder reliability; triangulation; explicit coding schemes; audit trails; transferability descriptions.
  - Integration with quantitative outcomes (where applicable) to triangulate causal interpretation.
- Representative exemplars: studies examining everyday LLM use in design practice; health-care governance-integration ethnographies; mixed-methods studies linking outcomes and user experiences.

E. Rigorous rapid reviews and scoping studies
- Design intent: Provide timely mapping of the evidence landscape when LLM-evaluation research is evolving quickly.
- Quality indicators:
  - Transparent, concise methodology; explicit limitations; rapid-review compromises documented; scoping-review mapping with clear inclusion criteria.

F. Causal benchmarks and task-specific framing (emerging)
- Design intent: Establish ground-truth-like causal-inference tasks and ground-truth reasoning traces (e.g., causal graphs, CLadder-like causal benchmarks).
- Quality indicators:
  - Ground-truth architecture; transparent prompting and reasoning traces; open data and code; reproducible reasoning paths.

3) Quality indicators and evidence appraisal (anchor set)
- Data quality and task coverage
  - Coverage across knowledge, reasoning, safety, alignment; domain-specific, real-world tasks; domain-adapted datasets; inclusion of specialized domains.
- Measurement validity and reliability
  - Valid evaluation datasets; clear protocols; transparent metrics for truthfulness, toxicity, bias, robustness; reliability metrics for human coders and AI-assisted coding.
- Robustness and safety testing
  - Prompt robustness; task robustness; alignment robustness; agent behaviors; guardrails; risk assessments.
- External validity and domain coverage
  - Domain-specific evaluations in education, healthcare, business; cross-domain replication; transportability diagnostics (Deriving cross-language generalization and cross-domain transferability signals).
- Reproducibility and transparency
  - Public benchmarks; open data/code; clear data provenance; model versions; transcriptable prompts; evaluation procedures.
- Ethical considerations and governance
  - Governance alignment with international standards; ethical safeguards; bias mitigation and equity considerations; privacy and safety governance.
- AI-tool transparency and reporting
  - Explicit disclosure of AI tools used, their version, and their role in the research workflow; description of how AI outputs contributed to decisions, including prompts and tool configurations.

4) Operational blueprint for the book’s Methodology chapter
- Chapter structure (suggested)
  - 1) Framing credible causal evidence for everyday LLM use: why a multi-design spine matters; overview of evidence types; link to governance and ethics.
  - 2) Evidentiary spine: taxonomies by design family; mapping to outcomes and domain contexts; core quality indicators per design.
  - 3) The three-to-four path design architecture:RCTs/field experiments; transportability/observational approaches; meta-analyses; rigorous qualitative/mixed-methods; cross-domain synthesis.
  - 4) AI-specific reporting and auditing, anchored by PRISMA-AI and SPIRIT-AI-like constructs: protocol, data provenance, prompts, model versions, bias checks, governance artifacts.
