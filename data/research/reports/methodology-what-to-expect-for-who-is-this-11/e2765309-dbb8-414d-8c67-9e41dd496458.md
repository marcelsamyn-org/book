# Learning depth, transfer, and calibration in LLM-assisted learning: prioritization and experimental designs for isolating effects and estimating long-run retention

Executive summary
- Across the converging strands of the literature, the most durable learning gains in LLM-assisted contexts arise when design explicitly targets depth of processing (prompts that elicit justification, elaboration, and problem-solving), transfer (application to novel contexts), and calibration (accurate self-monitoring and confidence signaling) within a long-run frame (Delikoura, Fung, & Hui, 2025; Huang et al., 2025; Mutanga et al., 2025).
- Experimental designs that isolate active recall prompts, spacing schedules, and reflective/metacognitive prompts, while tracking immediate, delayed, and transfer outcomes, are the most promising route to quantify long-run retention in real classrooms and workplaces (Delikoura et al., 2025; Stadler, Bannert, & Sailer, 2024; Karpicke et al., 2011; Cepeda et al., 2006).
- The recommended experimental toolkit includes multi-wave assessments (immediate, short-term, and long-run retention), transfer tasks across contexts, priors and covariates for prior knowledge, and calibration metrics (e.g., calibration curves, ECE/Brier scores) to capture the alignment of confidence with accuracy (Steyvers et al., 2025; Wang, 2023; de Oliveira et al., 2025).
- Actionable implications for authors and practitioners: ground designs in depth, transfer, and calibration; employ factorial or MOST-inspired designs to identify active ingredients; pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans; report effect sizes and retention curves; and monitor calibration signals to ensure trustworthy, scalable AI-assisted learning.

1) Introduction and context
- The literature converges on three learning outcomes that should be prioritized for LLM-assisted learning: learning depth (deep encoding and robust mental models), transfer (robust performance across contexts), and calibration (accurate self-monitoring and prediction of knowledge). These priorities align with long-run skill development in classrooms and workplaces and help address risks such as superficial learning, over-reliance on AI, and miscalibration of confidence (Delikoura, Fung, & Hui, 2025; Huang et al., 2025; Stadler et al., 2024).
- Evidence base synthesis highlights three clusters of LLM use in education (operational effectiveness, personalized applications, interactive learning tools) and foregrounds risk cascades from technical properties to learning outcomes, underscoring the need to design for depth, transfer, and calibration while monitoring long-run retention (Delikoura et al., 2025).
- A core empirical gap is explicit evidence on long-run retention and cross-context transfer under LLM-assisted learning, and explicit experimental isolations of depth, transfer, and calibration using prompts, spacing, and reflective practices (Delikoura et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025).

2) Core learning outcomes: definitions and targets
- Learning depth (depth of processing)
  - Targeted encoding processes include elaboration, organization, generation, self-explanation, and integration with prior knowledge. Indicators include the quality and coherence of explanations, mental-model reconstruction, and the ability to reconstruct solutions without prompts (Delikoura et al., 2025; Mutanga et al., 2025).
- Transfer
  - Target robust applicability across contexts, domains, and problem types, not just surface feature recall. Indicators include performance on novel problems, cross-domain problem solving, and the ability to transfer core principles to unfamiliar contexts (Delikoura et al., 2025; Mutanga et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025).
- Calibration
  - Target accurate self-monitoring and prediction of performance. Indicators include confidence-accuracy calibration curves, prediction accuracy on difficult items, and metacognitive judgments aligned with outcomes (Wang, 2023; Delikoura et al., 2025; Stadler et al., 2024).

3) Experimental designs to isolate effects and estimate long-run retention
Rationale
- The literature emphasizes risks such as over-reliance, cognitive load misalignment, and superficial learning when learners interact with LLMs. To optimize depth, transfer, and calibration, experimental designs should disentangle prompts, spacing, and reflective practices while tracking retention and transfer over time (Delikoura et al., 2025; Stadler et al., 2024).
- A multi-factor, longitudinal approach is recommended, with dense manipulation of prompts, spacing, and reflective practices, complemented by transfer tasks and calibration measures to estimate long-run retention (Delikoura et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025).

A. Core design patterns (illustrative, domain-adaptable)
- Design pattern 1: Active recall prompts (prompt-type manipulation)
  - Conditions:
    - Retrieval-heavy prompts: learners answer from memory before model feedback.
    - Passive/generative prompts: learners receive model-generated explanations with minimal retrieval.
    - Generation prompts: learners generate outputs prior to feedback without retrieval prompts.
  - Outcomes: depth of processing (explanation quality), immediate gains, delayed retention (1 week, 1 month), transfer performance, calibration (confidence vs accuracy).
  - Design notes: random assignment to conditions; equal content; track retrieval effort (time-on-task, recall accuracy).
  - Rationale: tests whether retrieval-practice–driven prompts yield deeper encoding and stronger retention vs model-assisted but retrieval-light conditions.
  Key anchors: retrieval practice literature (Karpicke et al., 2011) and spaced repetition (Cepeda et al., 2006).

- Design pattern 2: Spacing schedules (temporal distribution)
  - Conditions:
    - Massed: compressed study sessions.
    - Spaced: longer inter-session intervals (e.g., 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days).
    - Hybrid: initial massed phase followed by spaced reinforcement.
  - Outcomes: retention curves across post-tests (1 week, 1 month, 3 months), transfer performance, depth indicators.
  - Rationale: CLT and retrieval literature indicate spacing enhances consolidation and long-run retention; LLM-assisted learning can modulate this via prompt design and AI feedback cadence.

- Design pattern 3: Reflective prompts (metacognition)
  - Conditions:
    - Reflective prompts after each LLM interaction (e.g., “What did I misunderstand? How does this relate to prior knowledge?”).
    - No reflective prompts (neutral prompts or no prompts).
  - Outcomes: metacognitive accuracy, calibration (confidence-accuracy), depth indicators, retention, and transfer.
  - Rationale: metacognition boosts calibration and strategic learning, potentially enhancing transfer when combined with retrieval and spacing.

- Design pattern 4: Calibration-focused tasks (prediction-based calibration)
  - Conditions:
    - Calibration tasks interleaved with learning: learners predict their performance on upcoming quizzes.
    - Calibration tasks after learning: learners predict which items they will answer correctly.
  - Outcomes: calibration metrics (calibration curves, Brier scores, ECE), transfer performance, retention.
  - Rationale: explicit calibration tasks help align confidence with mastery, essential for long-run retention and transfer (Wang, 2023; Delikoura et al., 2025).

- Design pattern 5: Cross-context transfer experiments (contextual variability)
  - Conditions:
    - Same-domain transfer: apply learned concepts to slightly different problems within the same domain.
    - Cross-domain transfer: apply concepts to a different domain.
  - Outcomes: transfer success, time-to-solve, depth indicators, calibration metrics.
  - Rationale: transfer across domains is the essence of long-run skill development; explicit cross-domain tasks reveal generalization capabilities of depth- and calibration-focused prompts (Mutanga et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025).

- Design pattern 6: Longitudinal, within-subject mixed designs
  - Structure: each participant experiences multiple conditions (e.g., retrieval vs non-retrieval; spaced vs massed; reflective vs non-reflective) in a counterbalanced order across time.
  - Outcomes: retention trajectories, transfer patterns, depth and calibration indicators, with preregistered analyses.

- Design pattern 7: Cross-domain, multi-domain, multi-prompt platform (MOST-inspired)
  - Optional approach: a MOST-friendly design that screens a small set of active components (e.g., retrieval prompts, spacing, reflective prompts, calibration prompts) using a fractional factorial design to identify active ingredients, followed by a full-factorial expansion on the strongest components (Watkins & Newbold, 2020).

B. Measures and analytics to accompany designs
- Primary outcomes
  - Depth of processing: qualitative and quantitative rubrics for explanations, coherence, and problem-solving steps.
  - Transfer: performance on novel problems or tasks across domains; time-to-solve; quality of reasoning in transfer tasks.
  - Retention: multi-wave posttests (1 week, 1 month, 3 months, and beyond if feasible) to model forgetting curves.
  - Calibration: calibration curves, Brier scores, ECE, reliability diagrams; predicted vs actual performance across time.
- Secondary outcomes
  - Engagement, autonomy, and perceived agency; cognitive load proxies (response times, effort ratings).
  - Confidence signals: explicit confidence judgments associated with each response.
- Analytics
  - Mixed-effects models with random intercepts/slopes for learners; Bayesian hierarchical models for retention trajectories; mediation analyses to test whether depth and metacognition mediate transfer and calibration; preregistered hypotheses.
  - Effect sizes (Cohen’s d, Hedges g) with confidence intervals; model comparison criteria (e.g., WAIC/LOO for Bayesian models).

4) Operational considerations and implementation guidance
- Domain alignment and measurement fidelity
  - Start with domains where LLMs have shown robust explanatory and problem-solving support (education, CS, psychology) while monitoring for superficial learning risks (Delikoura et al., 2025).
  - Use validated rubrics and validated transfer tasks where possible; pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans to enable replication.
- Prompt design and scaffolding
  - Design prompts to elicit deep processing (elaboration, justification, causal reasoning) and to balance retrieval with AI-provided guidance. Include reflective prompts to foster metacognition.
  - Calibrate the prompts with pre-tests to ensure content validity and alignment with the learning goals.
- Calibration and safety
  - Include calibration metrics (ECE, Brier Score, reliability diagrams) and consider post-hoc calibration techniques (e.g., isotonic regression, histogram binning) to ensure reliable feedback signals (Wang, 2023; de Oliveira et al., 2025).
  - Monitor for AI errors, misinformation, and hallucinations; include human-in-the-loop checks where feasible and emphasize source verification.
- Ethics, equity, and privacy
  - Ensure equal access to LLM-enabled learning, protect privacy, and attend to potential biases in AI feedback. Integrate teacher/mentor oversight to maintain equitable evaluations.
- Reporting and replication
  - Pre-register designs, share prompt templates, and publish methodology, measures, and analysis plans to support replication, cross-domain comparison, and meta-analytic syntheses (Huang et al., 2025; Delikoura et al., 2025).

5) Patterns and expected interpretations
- If active recall prompts reliably increase depth and retention, expect retention curves to show steeper initial gains and slower forgetting with spaced recall across domains; transfer should improve with depth-promoting prompts, and calibration signals should converge toward a stable alignment over time (Karpicke et al., 2011; Cepeda et al., 2006; Stadler et al., 2024).
- If reflective prompts enhance calibration and depth, expect improved calibration curves and more robust transfer across novel contexts; effects on long-run retention should be observable, particularly when combined with spacing.
- If calibration-focused designs (e.g., calibration prompts, post-hoc calibration) are effective, expect improvements in reliability of feedback signals and higher learner trust without sacrificing depth or retention (Wang, 2023; de Oliveira et al., 2025).
- Cross-domain transfer readiness often requires sustained, structured pedagogy and domain-agnostic reasoning scaffolds; models with a tutor/coach role and long-term practice typically yield more robust generalization (Huang et al., 2025; Mutanga et al., 2025).

6) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Prioritize learning-depth and calibration in the design of LLM-assisted curricula; use retrieval practice and reflective prompts to promote deep encoding and metacognitive calibration.
- Use spacing to maximize long-run retention; augment with transfer tasks to assess generalization beyond training contexts.
- Implement calibration metrics (ECE, MCE, CECE, AECE) and reliability diagrams to quantify trust signals; apply post-hoc calibration methods as needed to improve reliability of AI guidance.
- Apply MOST-inspired screening designs to identify the most active components before scaling to full curricula; use fractional factorial designs to identify main effects and promising interactions.
- Collect both quantitative outcomes (retention, transfer, depth, calibration) and qualitative/process data (explanations, prompts usage, learner perceptions) to triangulate mechanisms and inform iterative improvements.
- Promote transparency and reproducibility by sharing prompt designs, prompts-tuning parameters, and analysis plans; document LLM configurations and prompts to facilitate replication.

7) Limitations and cautions
- The evidence base comprises heterogeneous studies across domains, with varying outcome measures and timescales. Generalization requires domain-specific replication and longer-term assessments (Delikoura et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025).
- Calibration results may be model- and task-dependent; robust calibration requires careful selection of models and calibration strategies, plus continuous monitoring as contexts shift (Wang, 2023; de Oliveira et al., 2025).
- Long-run retention is challenging to measure in real-world settings due to practical constraints; thus, multi-wave designs with feasible follow-ups (1 week to several months) should be adopted, with transfer tasks included where possible (Karpicke et al., 2011; Cepeda et al., 2006).
