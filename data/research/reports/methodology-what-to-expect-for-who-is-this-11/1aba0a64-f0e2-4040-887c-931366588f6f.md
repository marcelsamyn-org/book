# Social dynamics implications of shared LLM tools in teams
# and field-experiment designs to isolate tool-mediated effects from confounding factors

Executive summary
- Shared LLM tools reshape social dynamics in teams by altering collaboration rituals, communication motifs, trust calibration, and relationship work. Evidence from real-field experiments shows tool mediation increases task-focused exchanges and productivity, while social-emotional interactions may decrease in mixed human–AI teams, with output quality depending on modality (AI-assisted text vs human-led images) and domain context (Ju & Aral, 2025). Perceived teamwork quality may show no uniform shifts, suggesting nuanced trust and relationship dynamics that depend on modality, task, and governance of tool use (Ju & Aral, 2025).
- Foundational theory and measurement work emphasize trust as a dynamic, multi-faceted construct that must be calibrated against AI reliability and explainability. The CHAI-T framework foregrounds inputs, mediators, and outputs in human–AI teaming, highlighting time-varying trust formation and the need for process-level measurement to understand tool-mediated social effects (McGrath, Duenser, Lacey, & Paris, 2025).
- Methodologies for isolating tool-mediated effects in field settings include randomized or quasi-randomized designs, multi-level modeling to account for clustering, and mediation analyses (including causal mediation frameworks) to disentangle direct tool effects from socially mediated pathways. Where appropriate, stepped-wedge cluster randomized trials (SW-CRTs) and cluster randomized designs provide ecologically valid, ethically sound structures for staged tool deployment and causal inference (Hemming et al., 2015; Barker et al., 2016; Li & Wang, 2022).
- Actionable insights for researchers and practitioners:
  - Design field experiments with rigorous randomization at multiple levels (participant pairing, prompts/configurations, task prompts) and with multimodal collaboration capabilities to isolate tool-mediated effects.
  - Include both objective process/outcome metrics (e.g., communication volume, edits, click-through/ad performance) and subjective social-dynamics measures (trust, psychological safety, perceived teamwork) to map the causal chain from tool exposure to relationship dynamics and productivity.
  - Use trust calibration constructs (TIAS and S-TIAS), CHAI-T, and mediation frameworks to decompose effects into direct and mediated components, and to test boundary conditions such as cross-domain generalizability and context sensitivity.
  - Embrace governance-centered design: ensure data provenance, transparency, accountability, and ethical safeguards in real-world deployments, as governance shapes trust, adoption, and long-term collaboration norms (Madanchian & Taherdoost, 2025).

1) Research question and scope
- Central question: What are the social dynamics implications of shared LLM tools on collaboration, communication quality, trust, and relationship dynamics in teams, and how can field experiments isolate tool-mediated effects from confounding factors?
- Rationale: In AI-augmented work, shared LLMs act as co-actors in real teams, not merely as inert tools. Understanding how their presence shifts social dynamics is essential for organizational design and for rigorous, policy-relevant experimental practice.
- Key social-dynamics domains implicated:
  - Collaboration quality and coordination
  - Communication quality and rhetoric (process/content vs. social-emotional)
  - Trust calibration and relationship dynamics (psychological safety, perceived accountability)
  - Task performance and satisfaction with AI-enabled workflows
- Core drivers and contextual moderators identified across the literature:
  - Trust as a dynamic mediator (CHAI-T; multi-level trust formation) and as a function of AI reliability, explainability, and transparency (McGrath et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2024)
  - Modality and domain effects: AI-assisted text vs human-led imagery, and domain-specific task characteristics influence outcomes (Ju & Aral, 2025; Lee et al., 2024)
  - Governance and ethics as social levers: transparency, data provenance, accountability, and risk governance shape trust and collaboration (Madanchian & Taherdoost, 2025)
  - Experimental designs to isolate tool effects: SW-CRTs, factorial designs, CMA, and careful measurement strategies (Hemming et al., 2015; Barker et al., 2016; Imai et al., 2010a; Pearl 2001)

2) Theoretical foundations and concepts
- Social-dynamics lens on AI-enabled teamwork
  - Trust is multi-faceted (anticipations about AI capability, reliability, transparency, and perceived benevolence). Trust calibrates willingness to rely on AI in joint tasks and evolves with experience and feedback (Mayer, Davis, & Schoorman, 1995; Lee & See, 2004; McGrath et al., 2025).
  - CHAI-T (Collaborative Human–AI Trust) provides a process-oriented model: inputs (human, technology, environment) shape mediators (trust states, perceived collaboration quality, interdependence), which yield outputs (performance, process metrics), with feedback loops to reconfigure inputs (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Trust calibration is central: miscalibration yields over-reliance on flawed AI or underutilization of reliable AI. Calibration cues (transparency, explanations, confidence signals) can help or hinder depending on context and presentation (Okamura & Yamada, 2020; Lee et al., 2024).
- Field-experiment design paradigms for isolating tool-mediated effects
  - Randomized and SW-CRT designs provide causal leverage in real-world settings, enabling dissection of tool effects from time trends, learning, and confounding factors (Hemming et al., 2015; Barker et al., 2016; Li & Wang, 2022).
  - CMA (causal mediation analysis) enables decomposing total effects into direct and mediated components. When multiple mediators or exposure–mediator interactions exist, advanced CMA methods (including multiple mediators, non-linear outcomes, and exposure–mediator interaction terms) are appropriate (Imai et al., 2010a; Tingley et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2016).
  - Sure outcomes and time-to-event mediation frameworks address mediator–outcome confounding and left truncation, providing robust alternative identifiability conditions and bounds when sequential ignorability fails (Li, Geng, & Zhou, 2021; Yu & Huang, 2025).
- Representative measurement-architecture anchors
  - TIAS and S-TIAS (Trust in Automation Scale; Short TIAS) provide validated multi-item and rapid-iteration measures of trust in AI, predictive of reliance intentions; these scales enable repeated-trust measurement without heavy respondent burden (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Four-stage trust constructs and measurement frameworks (CHAI-T) support longitudinal modeling of trust formation and calibration in team contexts (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Overconfident/unconfident AI studies highlight the importance of calibration cues and their mixed effects on collaboration, distressing reliance and trust dynamics (Lee et al., 2024).

3) Representative quantitative findings (selected from sources)
- Ju & Aral (2025): Field experiment on Pairit platform
  - AI collaboration boosted overall communication by 63% per participant; direct human text editing decreased by 71%; process/content communication grew by 18% in human–AI teams, versus social/emotional communication increasing by 29% in human–human teams.
  - Per-worker productivity rose by 73% in human–AI teams; output quality varied by modality (AI improved ad copy; humans improved images); field ads (~5M impressions) showed higher image quality (human–human) and higher text quality (human–AI) linked to better CTR, VTR, CPC.
  - Perceptions of teamwork quality did not differ systematically by collaboration mode.
  - The Pairit data enabled fine-grained reconstruction of collaboration workflows via keystrokes, messages, edits, and API calls.
  - Inline citations: (Ju & Aral, 2025)
- McGrath et al. (2025): CHAI-T and trust dynamics
  - Trust is a dynamic construct shaped by inputs (human, technology, environment) and mediators (processes, communication) with performance phase feedback; trust evolves with experience and environmental cues.
  - Trust convergence and miscalibration have critical consequences for collaboration quality and team performance; governance, transparency, and calibration cues influence trust trajectories.
  - TIAS/S-TIAS provide validated measures for trust in AI and support field-level tracking of trust dynamics (McGrath et al., 2025).
- Okamura & Yamada (2020): Trust calibration cues
  - Adaptive trust calibration cues (TCCs) can reduce over-trust in overconfident AI and mitigate under-trust in underconfident AI; calibration support can both improve calibration and potentially increase distrust if misapplied.
- Lee et al. (2024): Overconfident/unconfident AI hinder collaboration
  - AI confidence that mismatches actual correctness undermines collaboration; calibration support helps but can increase disuse in some scenarios.
- Li, Geng, Zhou (2021) CMA with sure outcomes
  - Introduces a robust mediation framework that handles unobserved mediator–outcome confounding via a sure-outcomes model; identifies identifiability conditions and bounds for indirect/directed effects with binary treatment/mediator/outcome.
- Yu & Huang (2021): Simulation-based CMA for multi-mediator cases
  - Proposes simulation-based CMA for multiple mediators, enabling estimation of interventional direct/indirect effects when mediator-confounding exists; supports nonparametric and parametric implementations.

4) Methodological options for isolating tool-mediated effects in field experiments
- Randomized designs
  - Cluster randomized trials (CRTs): randomize teams/departments to shared LLM tool exposure; particularly suitable when the tool is deployed at the team level and contamination risks exist.
  - Stepped-wedge CRTs: sequentially roll out shared tools to all teams; all units receive the tool by the end; allows within- and between-cluster comparisons and adjustment for time trends (Hemming et al., 2015; Barker et al., 2016; Li & Wang, 2022).
  - Factorial designs: cross-manage tool exposure and configuration (prompts, fine-tuning, transparency) to identify main effects and interaction effects on social dynamics.
  - CMA-driven mediation analysis: plan to measure mediators such as trust, communication quality, psychological safety; decompose tool effects into indirect (mediated) vs direct components.
- Mediation frameworks
  - Causal mediation analysis (Imai et al., 2010a; Tingley et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2016): estimate ACME, ADE, and total effect; address mediator–outcome confounding via sequential ignorability assumptions; use mediate() in R or equivalent libraries; incorporate multiple mediators when needed.
  - Sure-outcomes CMA (Li, Geng, Zhou, 2021): address unobserved mediator–outcome confounding with a robust identification strategy; provide bounds when identifiability fails.
  - Time-to-event mediation (Yu & Huang, 2025): when mediators and outcomes are time-to-event, apply nonparametric Nelson–Aalen-type estimators or Cox-based models to decompose effects under left truncation and possible confounding.

5) Proposed field-experiment blueprint (integrated plan)
- Design choice
  - Primary design: SW-CRT if staged rollout is necessary due to organizational constraints; otherwise a parallel CRT with randomization at team level.
  - Alternative: CMA-based mediation analysis within a randomized design to quantify indirect pathways via trust/communication mediators.
- Experimental units
  - Teams or workgroups across domain contexts (e.g., software development, marketing, customer support); stratify randomization on team size, prior AI experience, and domain complexity.
- Treatments
  - Shared LLM tool exposure with standardized prompts and governance.
  - Control: no shared tool or non-shared tool baseline.
- Mediators
  - Trust in AI/tool (TIAS or S-TIAS), trust in teammates, perceived reliability, communication quality, psychological safety, and perceived fairness.
- Outcomes
  - Collaboration quality, coordination efficiency, information-sharing richness, task performance, and relationship dynamics (psychological safety, perceived team cohesion, satisfaction with collaboration).
- Data collection
  - Repeated measures across waves/time periods (baseline, during rollout, post-rollout), with log data capturing tool usage, prompts, outputs, and collaboration artifacts.
  - Qualitative data (interviews) to contextualize trust dynamics and governance implications.
- Analysis plan
  - Primary: mixed-effects models to estimate tool effects on social-dynamics outcomes; test time-by-treatment interactions in SW-CRT; account for clustering and time trends.
  - Mediation: CMA (ACME, ADE) for direct/indirect decomposition; multiple mediators if necessary; sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding.
  - Mediation with multiple mediators: advanced CMA approaches or simulation-based CMA (Zhou & Wodtke, 2025) to handle multiple pathways.
  - Power planning: SW-CRT power computations (number of clusters, steps, ICC) using standard SW-CRT power tools; account for potential decay in correlation structures (Hemming et al., 2015; Li & Wang, 2022).
- Practicalities and governance
  - Data provenance and privacy: document data flows, data governance, and access controls; implement data minimization principles as per governance guidance (Madanchian & Taherdoost, 2025).
  - Ethics: obtain appropriate consents for data collection; ensure opt-out provisions and governance alignment with organizational policy.
  - Replicability: preregister hypotheses, data collection protocols, and analysis plan; plan cross-domain replication across multiple domains.

6) Limitations and caveats
- No single universal design fits all contexts; cross-domain generalizability requires replication with domain-specific task environments and governance conditions.
- Trust dynamics are multi-layered (individual, team, organizational); field experiments must capture and model multi-level processes to avoid misattribution.
- Mediator measurement challenges: trust, communication quality, and psychological safety can be measured with matured scales, but measurement invariance across domains can be challenging; robust measurement strategies and sensitivity analyses are essential.

7) Actionable recommendations for authors and practitioners
- Use CHAI-T as a guiding framework:
  - Model inputs (human, AI, environment), mediators (trust, communication quality), and outputs (collaboration outcomes) with longitudinal design to map trust trajectories.
- Emphasize measurement triangulation:
  - Combine TIAS/S-TIAS with CMA approaches to parse direct vs mediated effects, and to test boundary conditions such as interaction effects and cross-domain generalizability.
- Design governance-aware experiments:
  - Include transparency, explainability, and data provenance as experimental manipulations (e.g., tool outputs with varying levels of rationale, confidence estimates, and audit trails) to study how governance cues influence trust and collaboration.
- Cross-domain replication:
  - Replicate field experiments across multiple domains to test generalizability and to identify domain-specific social-dynamics patterns and governance needs.
- Ethical and governance alignment:
  - Integrate governance principles into every design aspect; ensure consent, data privacy, auditability, and accountability are central in any field deployment.
