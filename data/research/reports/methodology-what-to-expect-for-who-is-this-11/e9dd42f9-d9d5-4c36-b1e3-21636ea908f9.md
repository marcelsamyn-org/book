Patterns of Attention and Affective States During LLM Use: Measurement, Causal Links, Contextual Variance, and Countermeasures

Executive summary
- EMA-based RT analyses using a drift-diffusion model (DDM) show that momentary affect during real-world tasks can be decomposed into processing speed (drift rate) and response caution (boundary separation). In a large EMA study (N ≈ 954; up to 6 prompts/day for 1 week; ~29,000 occasions), drift rate demonstrated moderate stability, varied with momentary context (e.g., work vs. recreation), and related to trait factors (neuroticism, depression). Practice effects emerged: drift rate rose and boundary separation fell over time; faster affect ratings associated with affective clarity and regulation strategies, supporting the validity of RT-based proxies for processing during real-time experiences (Schneider et al., 2023/2024). This framework directly informs how attention and affect unfold during LLM use in daily life.
- For LLM contexts, measurement should be multi-method: EMA prompts to capture momentary attention and affect, usage-logs to quantify exposure and interaction patterns, and physiological proxies (e.g., pupil dilation, HRV, EEG) when feasible to triangulate arousal and cognitive load.
- Direct evidence from LLM-related logs (e.g., Bard interactions) indicates substantial heterogeneity in engagement (long sessions, many prompts, varied task types) and mixed satisfaction signals tied to task type. Logs show high engagement in content-creation and multi-turn workflows, with explicit feedback skewed by task type (positive for general knowledge/advice; negative for math, directions, image generation). However, logs alone do not measure momentary affect or attentional cost, underscoring the value of embedding EMA and physiology in a multi-method design (Trippas et al., 2024).
- Neuroadaptive and multimodal measurement approaches (e.g., EEG-based engagement, eye-tracking, pupillometry; fNIRS in cockpit designs) demonstrate the feasibility and value of real-time state estimation to drive adaptive LLM behavior. Early results show improved engagement and reduced cognitive load, though lasting learning outcomes are not consistently observed in short pilots; these findings underscore the potential and the measurement challenges when translating to everyday LLM use (e.g., NeuroChat, EyeGPT, OCAM, AdaptiveCoPilot; Baradari et al., 2025; Woh).
- For causal inference and cross-context variance, quasi-experimental designs (REGRESSION DISCONTINUITY, IV, matching/PSM, comparative interrupted time series) provide credible strategies where randomization is infeasible. These designs require careful attention to identification assumptions (continuity, overlap, SUTVA) and pre-registration of analysis plans; they are particularly relevant for evaluating countermeasures (e.g., adaptive prompts, break reminders) in real-world LLM deployments (Kim, 2016).
- Actionable takeaway: to map patterns of attention and affect during LLM use and to evaluate countermeasures, adopt a multi-method design that (a) uses EMA+DDM as the core real-time processing/affect indicator, (b) triangulates with usage logs and (c) supplements with physiological proxies in targeted sub-samples or experiments, while (d) planning quasi-experimental designs to infer causal links and context-specific variance.

1) Introduction: why patterns of attention and affect matter for LLM use
- LLM-enabled workflows generate real-time cognitive and affective costs (distraction, fatigue, craving to continue or stop, satisfaction/dissatisfaction). Capturing these costs in real-world contexts requires methods that provide high temporal resolution and naturalistic context, alongside strategies to infer causality and context-specific variance.
- EMA provides momentary sampling in natural environments, reducing recall bias and enabling study of microprocesses as they unfold (Shiffman, Stone, Hufford; EMA fundamentals). EMA has been described as the gold standard for self-report in real-world settings, with flexible sampling (time-based, event-based, or hybrid designs) and integration with sensor data when feasible (McLeod; Simply Psychology; 2024). Importantly, EMA is suitable for tracking attention/distraction, fatigue, craving, and satisfaction in LLM-use contexts when prompts are carefully designed to target LLM-use moments and contextual factors (McLeod, 2024).
- The measurement landscape for LLM use includes three primary modalities:
  - EMA: real-time subjective states (attention, fatigue, craving, satisfaction) and context (task type, location, social setting).
  - Usage logs: objective indices of LLM interactions (session length, prompt complexity, timing, features used) that reveal exposure and engagement patterns.
  - Physiological proxies: pupil dilation, HRV, GSR, EEG/fNIRS in specialized settings to index arousal, cognitive load, and affective processing.
- The synthesis below integrates evidence across EMA RTs, LLM-usage logging, and physiological proxies, with emphasis on causal inference and context-variance in attention/affect costs and the evaluation of countermeasures.

2) Patterns of attention and affect accompanying LLM use: evidence-based synthesis

A. EMA RTs and diffusion-model insights for real-world processing during LLM-like tasks
- Large EMA RT data show that five momentary negative affect items could be modeled with item-response-theory diffusion modeling (DDM). Drift rate (processing speed/quality) and boundary separation (response caution) explained the majority of RT variance, with moderate stability across occasions. Contextual factors such as work vs recreation and person-level traits (neuroticism, depression) linked to diffusion parameters. Over time, drift rate increased and boundary separation decreased, indicating practice effects in EMA responses (Schneider et al., 2023/2024).
- Implications for LLM use: during real-world LLM interactions, momentary processing of affective states may reflect both cognitive processing (how readily affect is accessed/encoded) and decisional caution in reporting or acting on affect. Drift rate increases may indicate improved fluency in affect processing or task familiarity; reduced boundary separation may indicate greater confidence or reduced need for deliberation when prompted (or task familiarity). These parameters can serve as proximal indicators of attentional load and affective processing during LLM interactions.
- Practical takeaway: Use EMA prompts timed around LLM interaction moments (e.g., during or immediately after key interactions) to capture RTs to affect items, then apply DDM to extract drift rate and boundary separation as indices of cognitive-affective processing during LLM use.

Key quantitative findings (EMA RT + DDM)
- Sample: N ≈ 954; prompts up to 6/day; ~29,067 measurement occasions; 5 momentary negative affect items (Schneider et al., 2023/2024).
- Findings:
  - Drift rate and boundary separation explained a majority of RT variance for negative affect items.
  - Moderate stability of diffusion parameters across occasions (within- and between-person variance partitioning).
  - Drift rate increased; boundary separation decreased over time (practice effects).
  - Diffusion parameters associated with within-person activity contexts (work vs recreation) and with between-person traits (neuroticism, depression).
  - Faster affect ratings linked to higher affective clarity and to more positive emotion-regulation strategies.
- Takeaway: RT-based diffusion measures are viable and informative proxies for real-time affective processing and response tendencies during real-life LLM use.

B. Usage logs from LLM contexts (e.g., Bard Interactions) reveal engagement patterns and satisfaction signals, with gaps in direct affect/attention measurement
- Bard interaction logs (BIDD; Trippas et al., 2024):
  - Dataset: 15,023 interactions from 95 respondents; post-processing: 14,595 prompts, 407 ratings, 21 preferences.
  - Sessions: 4,666 sessions; 2,090 single-interaction sessions (44.79%).
  - Engagement: average ~158 interactions per user; sessions ~4 minutes, multi-turn sessions ~7 minutes; longest session 194 minutes; 3.22 interactions per session on average.
  - Explicit feedback: 407 prompts (2.71%); 54.74% of respondents provided feedback at least once; roughly equal positive (50.24%) and negative (49.76%) feedback.
  - Task-type effects: negative feedback more common for math, directions, and image-generation tasks; positive feedback for general knowledge/advice.
  - Prompt characteristics: average length 43.45 words (LLM prompts) vs 6.36 words (MS MARCO); 36% of prompts ≤15 terms; some prompts extremely long (>4,000 terms).
  - Content and behavior: top activities include content creation, requests for more content, image generation; cues like “are you sure” indicate iterative checks and validation; navigational patterns (more/next page) reflect discourse management and data-source integration (e.g., with personal accounts).
- Implications for attention/affect in LLM use:
  - Engagement patterns are highly task-context dependent, with long, content-rich prompts and multi-turn dialogues indicating sustained attention and cognitive engagement.
  - Satisfaction signals are noisy and context-sensitive, with positive signals tied to general knowledge/advice and negative signals tied to more challenging tasks (math, directions, image generation).
  - Explicit affective measurement (self-report) is sparse; logs provide robust engagement signals but do not directly capture momentary distraction or fatigue.
- Takeaway: Logs are essential for mapping exposure, context, and engagement but must be supplemented with EMA and physiology to capture momentary affect/attention and to infer causal links between usage patterns and affective/attentional costs.

C. Neuroadaptive measurement and real-time state estimation in professional/educational LLM contexts
- EEG/Neuroadaptive engagement (Baradari et al., 2025; NeuroChat):
  - NeuroChat uses real-time EEG signals (wearable dry-electrode headband) to generate engagement scores appended to LLM prompts to drive adaptive content depth, pacing, and style.
  - Pilot (n=24) showed enhanced cognitive and subjective engagement relative to non-adaptive baselines, but no immediate learning gains were observed; suggests engagement and affect can be modulated via neurofeedback-adaptive LLMs, though learning outcomes may require longer time or different outcomes.
- Multi-modal neuroadaptive systems (OCAM, Pay Attention!, AttentivU, Joie):
  - Systems that monitor engagement, concentration, cognitive load, and emotional arousal foster more nuanced adaptation strategies (content difficulty, pacing, delivery mode) than single-signal approaches; these hints support multi-modal sensing stacks as robust measurement architectures.
- Eye-tracking and gaze dynamics (EyeGPT; Eye-tracking in GenAI contexts):
  - Eye-tracking-driven adaptive GenAI assistants show promise for reducing cognitive load by adjusting responses based on gaze behavior; avatar-based instructor presence can reduce extraneous load; other forms of presentation (e.g., uncertainty displays) may induce arousal without altering subjective workload.
- Adaptive cockpit and professional contexts (AdaptiveCoPilot; fNIRS):
  - In aviation settings, fNIRS-based cognitive-state tracking drives context-aware guidance (modality switching, information-load adjustments) with improvements in optimal-load state rates and reduction in task completion time, suggesting feasibility of real-time neuroadaptive LLM interfaces in high-stakes tasks.
- Takeaway: Real-time physiological and neurophysiological proxies can support adaptive LLM interfaces by tracking attention and cognitive load; such approaches appear to improve engagement and efficiency in controlled settings, though clear causal links to broader outcomes (satisfaction, learning) remain context- and time-dependent.

D. Measurement design implications: what best captures causal links and variance across contexts?
- EMA + RT-based diffusion modeling (DDM-EMA) is a strong core approach for capturing real-time processing of affect, with interpretable latent processing parameters (drift rate, boundary separation) that relate to context and traits (neuroticism, depression). It provides causal-inference-friendly temporal structure for within-person analyses and horizon-explicit hypotheses (Schneider et al., 2023/2024).
- Usage logs provide essential exposure and context data to map LLM interaction patterns onto EMA-derived states, enabling causal inferences about how specific prompts, session lengths, and modalities impact affective states and processing costs.
- Physiological proxies (eye-tracking, pupillometry, HRV, EEG/fNIRS) offer converging evidence about arousal and cognitive load and can support causal conclusions when used in controlled or semi-controlled designs; they are especially powerful for real-time adaptation and for triangulating affect/attention beyond self-report.
- Quasi-experimental designs (RD, IV, PS, CITS) enable causal claims when randomization is not possible, particularly for evaluating countermeasures (adaptive prompts, break interventions) in real-world deployments (Kim, 2016).

3) Measurement approaches: caveats and guidance

EMA
- Strengths: Real-time capture of attention and affect; ability to model within-person fluctuations and lagged relationships; flexible designs (time-based, event-based, or hybrid).
- Limitations: Participant burden; missing data; potential reactivity; design decisions (prompt timing, frequency) influence power and bias; content validity of items must be established for LLM-specific contexts (COSMIN guidance helps).
- Practical guidance: Use random-time prompts or time-stratified blocks to reduce reactivity; pair with event-based prompts at salient LLM moments; pilot to calibrate burden; pre-register analyses.

Usage logs
- Strengths: Objective, high-resolution exposure data; scalable; low participant burden.
- Limitations: Do not provide direct measures of affect or attention; require alignment with EMA/physiological data to infer causal links and context-specific variance.
- Practical guidance: Align logs with EMA prompts for temporal sequencing; annotate prompts by task type, session length, prompt complexity; use logs for exposure-driven covariate adjustment in causal models.

Physiological proxies (eye-tracking, pupillometry, HRV, EEG, fNIRS)
- Strengths: Real-time proxies of arousal, attention, cognitive load; can detect covert states not captured by self-report; supports real-time adaptation.
- Limitations: Operational burden (equipment, data quality), privacy concerns, interpretation complexity; in naturalistic settings, confounds (lighting for pupillometry; movement artifacts for EEG) require careful controls; generalizability across contexts requires cross-validation.
- Practical guidance: Use targeted physiological sensing in sub-samples or controlled or semi-controlled settings; when deploying broadly, prefer scalable, less burdensome modalities (e.g., webcam eye-tracking) while acknowledging reliability trade-offs; combine with EMA and logs for robust inference.

4) Causal links and context variance: measurement design recommendations
- For robust causal inference and cross-context variance in LLM use:
  - Core design: EMA RTs + DDM as the primary processing-affect index; usage logs as exposure/context data; physiological proxies as supplementary triangulation data in targeted studies.
  - Causality: When feasible, embed quasi-experimental designs to test countermeasures:
    - Regression Discontinuity Designs (RDD) if exposure to a feature can be assigned by a continuous variable with a cutoff (e.g., threshold of daily usage that triggers an adaptive prompt).
    - Instrumental Variables (IV) if there exists an external instrument that affects exposure but not outcomes directly.
    - Matching/Propensity Scores (PS) to compare users with similar covariates across exposure levels.
    - Comparative Interrupted Time Series (CITS) around feature rollouts to compare pre/post trajectories across groups.
  - Pre-registration of estimands (ATE, ATT, LATE where appropriate) and bandwidth/robustness checks (McCrary test for RD, balance checks for PS, placebo tests for IV).
  - Multi-method triangulation to strengthen causal claims: EMA + usage logs + physiology; cross-validate findings across contexts (work vs recreation; task types).
- Practical guidance on countermeasures:
  - Adaptive prompting: adjust prompt complexity, pace, and presentation style based on detected cognitive load; evaluate with EMA-DDM metrics and usage-log outcomes.
  - Break prompts and micro-breaks: trigger based on diffusion parameters and contextual cues to reduce cognitive load and fatigue; test using CITS or RD/IV where possible.
  - Presentation design: use progressive disclosure, summaries, and chunking for long prompts; experiment with avatar-based guidance or subtle cues (attention-supportive UI) while monitoring engagement and affect.
  - Personalization: consider trait-based adjustments (neuroticism, depression indicators) to tailor pacing and content depth, guided by diffusion parameters and EMA signals.
- Data-quality considerations:
  - Ensure adequate sample size for RD/LATE estimates near cutoffs; maintain overlap across treated/untreated units for PS designs; define clear pre/post windows for CITS.
  - Safeguard privacy and consent for physiological data; minimize burden and maximize data quality with user-friendly interfaces and opt-in controls.

5) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners (summary of concrete recommendations)
- Measurement architecture
  - Core: EMA prompts aligned with live LLM use and RT measurement to support diffusion modeling of affect processing (drift rate, boundary separation).
  - Supplementary: Usage logs to map exposure and interaction contexts; physiological proxies (eye-tracking, pupillometry, HRV, EEG/fNIRS) in targeted sub-studies or controlled experiments to triangulate arousal and cognitive load.
  - Optional: Wearable-based or smartwatch EMA for scalable multi-modal data streams (e.g., multimodal EMA via smartwatch; leveraging m-Path-like platforms for design flexibility; 2023–2025 developments).
- Study design and causality
  - When RCTs are infeasible, adopt quasi-experimental designs (RDD, IV, PS, CITS) to identify causal effects of LLM design changes on attention/affect and countermeasure efficacy; pre-register hypotheses and analyses; report robust estimates and sensitivity analyses.
  - Include cross-context analyses (work vs leisure; different tasks) to quantify variance and identify task contexts where attention costs are highest and where countermeasures are most effective.
- Countermeasure design
  - Use neuroadaptive or physiology-informed prompts to adjust content depth/pacing in real time; apply break prompts and adaptive difficulty to maintain attention and reduce fatigue and distraction.
  - Explore uncertainty communication designs carefully; ensure arousal from uncertainty displays does not undermine engagement or trust; consider adaptive timing to minimize adverse effects.
  - Align with user context and task demands (e.g., more scaffolding for challenging tasks; shorter prompts for high-load situations).
- Reporting and transparency
  - Provide detailed descriptions of EMA items, prompts, sampling strategies, and missing data handling; report completion rates; publish pre-registered analysis plans and robustness checks.
  - When publishing results, present context-specific variance explained by EMA+DDM parameters, usage-logs-based exposure effects, and physiology-based triangulation; clearly delineate causal claims and limitations.

6) Limitations and directions for future work
- The evidence base directly linking LLM-use contexts to specific patterns of distraction, fatigue, craving, or satisfaction via EMA+DDM is still developing; more LLM-focused, purpose-built EMA/DDM studies are needed.
- Direct causal evidence over longer time horizons (beyond pilot studies) for learning outcomes and longer-term behavior change under adaptive LLM interfaces remains limited; longer-term studies are needed to examine transfer and durability.
- Integrating EMA with usage logs and physiology in large-scale, real-world deployments poses logistical, privacy, and data-management challenges; scalable, privacy-preserving pipelines are essential.
- Cross-domain generalization: while neuroscience-based measurement modalities (EEG, fNIRS, pupillometry) show promise in controlled settings, more work is needed to translate to everyday LLM use with diverse user populations and tasks.

7) Conclusion
- The convergence of EMA RTs with diffusion modeling, usage-data analytics, and physiological proxies provides a robust, multi-modal framework to understand attention and affect during LLM use and to evaluate countermeasures. EMA+DDM offers a principled way to parse processing and affective states in real time, while logs give exposure context and physiology provides arousal/load corroboration. Quasi-experimental designs enable causal inferences about countermeasures and context-dependent variance when randomization is not feasible. The combination of these approaches yields actionable design principles for reducing distraction and fatigue and increasing positive satisfaction with LLM interactions across contexts.

Appendix (glossary)
- EMA: Ecological Momentary Assessment — repeated real-time sampling of current experiences in natural environments.
- EMI: Ecological Momentary Intervention — real-time, context-aware interventions delivered in daily life.
- RT: Response Time — time to respond to prompts or items in EMA.
- DDM: Drift-Diffusion Model — a cognitive-modeling approach to decompose RTs into drift rate (information accumulation speed/quality) and boundary separation (response threshold).
- LLM: Large Language Model — generative AI systems used for dialogue, writing, coding, etc.
- Usage logs: Time-stamped records of user interactions with an application or system (e.g., LLM prompts, session lengths, features used).
- Physiological proxies: Objective indicators such as pupil dilation, HRV, GSR, EEG, fNIRS, etc., used to index arousal, cognitive load, or affective processing.
- Quasi-experimental designs: RD, IV, PS matching, CITS — methods to infer causal effects when randomization is not feasible.
