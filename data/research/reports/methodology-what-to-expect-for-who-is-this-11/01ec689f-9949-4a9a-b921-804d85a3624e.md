# Report: Measuring Originality and Novelty in Human–LLM Collaboration

Executive summary
- Objective: Establish a principled, evidence-based framework to measure originality and novelty in human–LLM collaboration, including robust scoring protocols, baselines, inter-rater reliability (IRR), and longitudinal diffusion of novelty within teams.
- Foundation: The framework integrates three pillars from the literature:
  - Faceted, focus-level evaluation and automatic annotation of reviewer focus (Shin et al., 2025) with strong automatic–human agreement (Cohen’s kappa Target Target: Target 0.81; Aspect 0.79) and practical baselines for human vs. LLM reviews (Shin et al., 2025) [inline: Shin et al., 2025].
  - A three-stage, evidence-driven novelty workflow for evaluating scientific novelty in papers via human–LLM collaboration (DeepReview lineage; three-stage novelty verification, reliability verification, multi-dimensional review; LLM-as-a-judge baselines; DeepReview‑13K dataset; Zhu et al., 2025) [inline: Zhu et al., 2025].
  - QUEST framework for human evaluation of LLMs in healthcare, emphasizing Planning, Implementation/Adjudication, Scoring/Review, and five originality-relevant dimensions, plus adjudication and reliability as core design considerations (Tam et al., 2024) [inline: Tam et al., 2024].
- Longitudinal diffusion: Diffusion of novelty within teams over time can be analyzed with diffusion frameworks (NBDA, diffusion curves, network diffusion) adapted to team contexts (Franz & Nunn, 2009; Rogers, diffusion of innovations) and extended to post‑AI workflows (Dearing & Cox, 2018; various diffusion literatures) [inline: Franz & Nunn, 2009; Rogers; Dearing & Cox, 2018].
- Actionable deliverables: A concrete protocol for scoring originality/novelty, a defensible set of baselines, an IRR plan, and a design for longitudinal diffusion analysis that can be implemented in real-world post‑AI workflows.

1) Context, goals, and core reasoning
- Originality and novelty in human–LLM collaboration are multi-faceted: novelty signals may arise from new methods, novel problem framings, cross-domain synthesis, or unique integrations of human insight with AI suggestions. Reliable measurement requires separating true novelty signals from surface-level stylistic variation, noise, or bias.
- The commissioning literature suggests three actionable levers:
  - A structured, automated facet-based annotation and scoring approach that aligns with expert focus and enables longitudinal analyses (Shin et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025; Tam et al., 2024).
  - A longitudinal diffusion lens for novelty signals to understand how novel ideas propagate through teams and cycles of work (NBDA-style diffusion, diffusion curves, network diffusion) (Franz & Nunn, 2009; Rogers, diffusion of innovations; Dearing & Cox, 2018).
  - A rigorous reliability framework (IRR/IRA) to ensure that novelty judgments are stable, transparent, and reproducible across raters and over time (Krippendorff, 2019; Hallgren, 2012; Geijer et al., 2025; Diaz et al., 2022).
- Inline syntheses from key sources:
  - Automated, facet-based focus/distribution evaluation with automatic annotators yields substantial agreement with human annotators (Cohen’s kappa Target Targets: Target 0.81; Aspect 0.79) and exposes where LLMs diverge on novelty (Shin et al., 2025) [inline: Shin et al., 2025].
  - A three-stage novelty framework (z1 novelty verification, z2 multi-dimension review, z3 reliability verification) plus an explicit marginalization of reasoning paths (z1→z2→z3) provides interpretable, auditable novelty judgments and strong baselines (DeepReview; Zhu et al., 2025) [inline: Zhu et al., 2025].
  - The QUEST framework provides a planning–adjudication–scoring structure with explicit reliability considerations and a set of five originality-relevant dimensions (Tam et al., 2024) [inline: Tam et al., 2024].
  - NBDA and diffusion-focused methods offer a principled way to distinguish social diffusion from asocial adoption patterns in team contexts (Franz & Nunn, 2009; Rogers; Dearing & Cox, 2018) [inline: Franz & Nunn, 2009; Rogers; Dearing & Cox, 2018].
  - Ground-truth and reliability considerations in reliability-heavy settings emphasize reporting, calibration, and multiple reliability indicators (Krippendorff, 2019; Geijer et al., 2025) [inline: Krippendorff, 2019; Geijer et al., 2025].

2) Scoring protocols for originality and novelty (core protocol)
- Objective: produce multi-dimensional novelty scores that are robust to model bias, domain variation, and rater differences, and that can be tracked longitudinally.
- Core scoring dimensions (proposed, aligned with existing literatures):
  - Originality: degree of unique contribution beyond prior team outputs and domain norms.
  - Novelty: degree of departure from established conventions, including cross-domain recombinations.
  - Utility/Impact: relevance and potential practical value of novelty to the task/domain.
  - Integration quality: coherence of human and LLM inputs, the quality of synthesis, and fidelity to user goals.
  - Diffusion-readiness: how easily the novelty could diffuse within teams (e.g., presence of documentation, training materials, or modular prompts).
  - Optional: Safety/Trust/Quality signals (aligned with QUEST dimensions) to ensure novelty is not achieved at the expense of safety or reliability.
- Scoring scale and anchors:
  - A 5-point scale for each dimension (0–4) with explicit anchors and example exemplars to stabilize interpretation.
  - A composite originality/novelty score could be a weighted sum across dimensions (e.g., 0.40 originality, 0.30 novelty, 0.15 diffusion-readiness, 0.15 impact).
- Data and tasks:
  - Artefacts to score: prompts, prompts with iterations, AI-generated outputs, human edits, and final deliverables produced in collaboration.
  - Domain settings: multiple domains to support cross-domain generalization (science design, coding, writing, design prompts, etc.).
- Scoring procedures:
  - Stage 1 (independent ratings): 3–5 independent raters evaluate each artifact on the above dimensions.
  - Stage 2 (adjudication): resolve disagreements via a formal adjudication step with a senior rater or consensus panel; document rationale.
  - Stage 3 (temporal tracking): for longitudinal diffusion, re-score the same artifact as it diffuses (or re-creates related artifacts) across timepoints to capture diffusion trajectories.
- Baselines (to calibrate novelty measurement):
  - Human-only baseline: artifacts produced solely by humans under the same context (no LLMs).
  - AI-alone baseline: outputs from AI without targeted novelty prompting or human curation.
  - Hybrid baselines with varying levels of AI assistance (e.g., different prompting regimes or different grounding corpora) to determine the sensitivity of novelty scores to prompting and grounding.
  - Cross-domain baselines: scoring across different domains to assess generalizability of the rubric and to identify domain-specific biases.
- Reliability planning:
  - Use Krippendorff’s alpha as the primary IRR metric for multi-rater, multi-category, possibly missing data; supplement with ICC for continuous composite scores; also track Cohen’s kappa for pairwise comparisons.
  - Prepare confidence intervals via bootstrap or analytic methods; provide a reliability-reporting section in all deliverables (as per Krippendorff 2019; Marzi et al. 2024; Hallgren 2012).
  - Conduct calibration rounds; document anchor exemplars; provide adjudication logs; report per-dimension IRR and composite reliability.
- Data governance and reproducibility:
  - Publish rubrics, prompts, and anonymized datasets when possible; provide scripts to compute scores and IRR; adopt preregistration where possible.

3) Inter-rater reliability and diffusion over time
- Inter-rater reliability (IRR/IRA) standards:
  - Krippendorff’s alpha: data-type agnostic; handles missing data; multi-rater contexts; interpretive thresholds as per Krippendorff (2019) and guidelines in Hallgren (2012), LeBreton & Senter (2008), and related sources (McHugh, 2012; Gwet, 2014) [inline: Krippendorff, 2019; Hallgren, 2012; LeBreton & Senter, 2008; McHugh, 2012; Gwet, 2014].
  - Complementary metrics: Cohen’s kappa, Fleiss’ kappa, ICC variants; Svensson; Bland–Altman (for continuous outcomes) [inline: Hayes & Krippendorff, 2007; Cohen, 1960; Fleiss, 1971; Svensson; Bland–Altman].
- Diffusion and time-series:
  - NBDA (Franz & Nunn, 2009) provides a clear framework for distinguishing social learning from asocial learning and can be adapted to team diffusion of novel workflows.
  Diffusion theory from Rogers (diffusion of innovations) and Dearing & Cox (2018) provides a basis for diffusion metrics: adoption latency, diffusion curves, central actors, and the social system context.
- Practical guidance on diffusion measurement:
  - Build time-stamped adoption data, construct team social networks from collaboration data, and fit NBDA-like models to diffusion events.
  - Compare models with DCA as a baseline to infer social learning vs asocial adoption; report Akaike weights and likelihoods to justify inferences.

4) Practical recommendations and concrete action items
- Implement a three-stage novelty workflow:
  - z1 Novelty Verification: use a facet-based, automatic annotator to label strengths/weaknesses with target facet and aspect facet; compute normalized focus distributions; identify novel directions per artifact (Shin et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025).
  - z2 Multi-dimension Review: synthesize multiple perspectives (human and LLM); preserve supporting evidence; generate actionable feedback that improves novelty integration (Zhu et al., 2025).
  - z3 Reliability Verification: formal evidence analysis with confidence annotation; produce a Meta-Review that summarizes strengths/weaknesses and concrete improvements (Zhu et al., 2025).
- Baseline suite:
  - Human-only baseline; AI-alone baseline; AI-assisted with domain grounding ( Wu et al., 2025); cross-domain baselines for generalizability (Shahid et al., 2025; Saakyan et al., 2025).
- IRR plan:
  - Use Krippendorff’s alpha as the primary reliability measure, with ICC for continuous scores and Cohen’s/Fleiss’ kappa as supplementary metrics; provide confidence intervals and baselines (Krippendorff, 2019; Hallgren, 2012; Marzi et al., 2024; Geijer et al., 2025).
  - Calibrate rubrics with anchor exemplars; report per-dimension IRR; adjudicate when alpha falls in the tentative range (0.67–0.79) or below crank thresholds.
- Longitudinal diffusion plan:
  - Adopt NBDA-style diffusion analysis to distinguish social learning from asocial adoption of novel workflows; track diffusion signals over time; build time-stamped diffusion events and networks; estimate diffusion parameters (τ and network influence) and compare NBDA vs DCA (Franz & Nunn, 2009).
  - Construct diffusion curves per team/domain; measure adoption latency, growth rate, reach, and network centrality of diffusion hubs.
- Data governance and reproducibility:
  - Publish rubrics, prompts, and code for novelty scoring pipelines; share data and scripts to support cross-lab comparability; register analysis plans when possible (as per QUEST and DeepReview practices).
- Domain considerations:
  - Align scoring frameworks with domain-specific novelty indicators (e.g., method innovation in ICLR/TNS literature; cross-domain novelty as in Shahid et al. 2025; functional novelty while preserving validity).
- Limitations to watch for:
  - IRR thresholds are not universal; report multiple metrics and interpret them in the context of task type and dataset distribution (Geijer et al., 2025; Krippendorff, 2019).
  - Ground-truth signals can be biased by rater composition and prompting strategies; calibrate and adjudicate to mitigate biases (Tam et al., 2024; Shahid et al., 2025).
  - Diffusion analyses require careful temporal and network data; cross-domain diffusion may require domain-specific network definitions and data pipelines (NBDA, Rogers).

5) Gaps and future research directions
- Explicit diffusion metrics in post‑AI team contexts remain underdeveloped in most sources; future work should prioritize diffusion of novelty within teams, with well-specified NBDA-style models and diffusion curves.
- There is a need to harmonize cross-domain novelty measurement; multi-domain baselines and domain adaptation of scoring rubrics would enhance generalizability.
- The literature shows that LLMs alone struggle to assess novelty; fusion approaches and grounding in domain-specific references improve performance (Wu et al., 2025; Shahid et al., 2025; Saakyan et al., 2025).
- There is a need for standardized reporting guidelines for IRR in novelty contexts, including reporting of multiple metrics, calibration logs, and confidence intervals (Geijer et al., 2025; Tam et al., 2024).
