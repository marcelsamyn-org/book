# report.md

Title: Metrics and Ecologically Valid Study Designs for LLM-Augmented Knowledge Work

Purpose and scope
- The goal is to identify objective and subjective metrics that best capture productivity and output quality shifts when large language models (LLMs) augment knowledge work, and to describe ecologically valid study designs capable of yielding robust estimates across three common tasks: writing, coding, and research.
- The synthesis integrates findings from a broad literature of ecologically grounded evaluation frameworks, benchmark design, human–AI collaboration studies, and production-oriented observability. It emphasizes task-alignment, multi-metric measurement, and real-work data to produce benchmarks actionable for designers and practitioners.

1) Executive summary of core insights
- Do not rely solely on traditional surface metrics (e.g., BLEU/ROUGE) for evaluating LLM-augmented knowledge work. While surface metrics have utility, objective measures that capture semantic quality, context relevance, factuality, and task practicality are essential for ecologically valid benchmarks (e.g., BERTScore, MoverScore, retrieval-relevant metrics) [Xie et al.; Miller & Tang; Confident AI discussions; Du et al.]. Inline citations: (Miller & Tang, 2025); (Confident AI, 2025); (Du, Li, Gehringer, 2025).
- Semantic and task-aware evaluation frameworks grounded in real-world workflows outperform abstract benchmarks in predicting actual productivity gains and output quality shifts when LLMs augment knowledge work (e.g., ecologically valid pipelines that map prompts to task-relevant tags and end-to-end data flows) [Miller & Tang; Expertiza/peer-review data approaches; retrieval-augmented evaluation]. Inline citations: (Miller & Tang, 2025); (Xgehringer et al., 2007; 10; 71).
- A balanced measurement philosophy is essential: combine objective performance metrics (time-to-delivery, accuracy, fault/defect rates, task completion) with subjective assessments (perceived usefulness, trust, satisfaction, cognitive load, tone) to obtain a holistic view of productivity and output quality in real work. Consider LLM-as-a-judge approaches to scale subjective evaluation with calibrated rubrics and human alignment signals [Hashemi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Confident AI].
- Study designs that yield ecologically valid estimates should emphasize:
  - Grounding metrics in live-work data (e.g., peer-review datasets, real course tasks, production environments) to ensure ecological validity.
  - Multi-stage evaluation pipelines (independent/retriever evaluation, end-to-end evaluation) to map outputs to concrete workflows.
  - Longitudinal, cross-task, and cross-domain designs to assess generalizability and adaptation over time.
  - Transparent scoring rubrics and calibrations (human + model-based) to ensure reliability and reproducibility.
  - Observability and production-grade instrumentation (A/B testing, logging, dashboards) to detect regressions and guide design iterations in production contexts [Rahman et al.; Miller & Tang; Hashemi et al.; Du et al.].

2) Objective metrics: what to measure for productivity and output quality
- Traditional automated metrics (surface-level benchmarks) and their limitations
  - BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR: useful for baseline surface similarity, but limited in capturing reasonableness, coherence, and practical task relevance in educational feedback and complex knowledge-work tasks (e.g., writing feedback, code explanations) [X2002BLEU; Xlin2004rouge; Banerjee2005meteor; survey-oriented synthesis]. Relevance: use these cautiously and in conjunction with task-aware, semantic metrics [Xie et al.; Du et al.].
  - Semantic metrics: BERTScore, MoverScore, and related semantic similarity measures address meaning beyond surface text but require task-aware interpretation and contextual grounding; education and feedback contexts can degrade their predictive validity if task semantics are far from training data [Xzhang2019bertscore; Xzhao2019moverscore].
  - Limitations in ecologies: automated metrics alone often fail to capture reasonableness, practicality, and task-context relevance; human evaluation and preference-based approaches are indispensable for ecologically valid estimates [Xlyu2024evaluating; Xgehringer2007reusable].
- Retrieval- and end-to-end task metrics (objectives aligned with real workflows)
  - Relevance and fidelity for retrieval-augmented outputs: “Answer Relevance,” “Answer Fidelity,” and “Contextual Relevancy/Precision/Recall” capture how well outputs align with the user’s project context and sources; particularly critical when outputs integrate external documents or domain knowledge [Confident AI; RAG Metrics; Use Case Specific Metrics].
  - Output usefulness, localization, and actionability: tags such as “Includes Explanation,” “Suggests Actions,” “Localized,” “Helpful” enable structured subjective assessments tied to workflow goals (e.g., actionable feedback in writing; remediation steps in coding) [Table 1; 3; 10].
  - End-to-end evaluation: pipelines that map prompts through retrieval, generation, and end-to-end outputs; when labels exist, end-to-end metrics such as correctness and fidelity can be computed; when unlabeled, surrogate signals like tone and usefulness may be used [3].
- Framework design for objective measurement
  - Combine objective pipelines (retrieval relevance, factual accuracy, citation fidelity, code correctness) with model-/human-aided judgments (G-Eval, DAG, Prometheus; RLHF, DPO) to get robust, repeatable scores that map to real-world work tasks [Confident AI; Du et al.; Li et al.].
  - Emphasize a small core metric set (roughly five) that covers productivity and output quality across writing, coding, and research, with task-specific additions as needed. This supports stable, interpretable benchmarks in ecologies with multiple tasks and models [Confident AI; Miller & Tang].
- Model-based evaluators and human-aligned rubrics
  - LLM-as-a-judge: use LLM judges to score outputs via rubrics; calibrate with human judgments to improve reliability and interpretability. Model-based scorers (G-Eval, DAG, Prometheus) can complement traditional statistical scorers, enabling robust, scalable evaluation in ecologies with LLM augmentation [Hashemi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024].
- Data provenance and benchmarking infrastructure
  - Ground benchmarks in externally sourced data and ecologically valid task datasets (peer-review data; Expertiza; Claude.ai mapping to O*NET tasks) to anchor metrics in authentic workflows. Use end-to-end pipelines (retriever + end-to-end) to reflect real-world data flows and feedback loops [10; 71].

Inline citations for objective metrics: (Miller & Tang, 2025); (Confident AI, 2025); (Du, Li, Gehringer, 2025); (Hashemi et al., 2024); (Rahman et al., 2025); (10; 71).

3) Subjective metrics: capturing user experience and perceived quality
- Subjective quality dimensions
  - Usefulness/helpfulness: user judgments about how AI outputs advance their tasks; perceived reliability and usefulness in context.
  - Clarity and explainability: how clearly outputs are explained, justified, or traced to sources; transparency improves trust and adoption.
  - Tone and politeness: perceived courtesy and alignment with professional norms; can affect user satisfaction and collaboration quality.
  - Trust and autonomy: user trust in AI assistance and perceived control over AI directions; important for sustainable adoption in knowledge work.
  - Cognitive load and workload: perceived mental effort and cognitive burden during AI-assisted tasks; crucial for long-term productivity and well-being.
- LLM-as-a-judge as a subjective metric
  - LLM-based judges can calibrate human judgments and scale the evaluation of subjective qualities by applying explicit rubrics; calibration with a gold standard set improves reliability. Pairwise comparisons offer a robust way to rate relative quality across model/prompt variants [Hashemi et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023].
- Domain-specific subjective rubrics
  - Use rubrics aligned with domain practices (writing, coding, research) and task-specific goals; ensure inter-rater reliability and cross-task comparability by standardizing labels and anchors.
- Inline citations for subjective metrics: (Hashemi et al., 2024); (Zheng et al., 2023); (Kurt & Duris, 2024); (Godwin-Jones, 2024)

4) Ecologically valid study designs to yield real-world estimates across writing, coding, and research
- Core design principles
  - Ground metrics in authentic workflows: real work tasks, multi-turn interactions, and prompts that resemble day-to-day use cases.
  - Multi-stage evaluation pipelines: independent/retriever evaluation, generation/synthesis evaluation, and end-to-end evaluation to map model capabilities to concrete outputs and workflows.
  - Longitudinal, cross-domain studies: track productivity and quality shifts over time across writing, coding, and research tasks to assess generalizability and adaptation.
  - Cross-domain task kits and rubrics: apply a consistent evaluation rubric across writing, coding, and research tasks, enabling comparability and aggregate interpretation.
  - Observability and production-like pipelines: employ production observability tooling (tracing, monitoring, evaluation dashboards) to capture performance dynamics, regressions, and system health, feeding into governance and policy decisions.
- Data sources and tasks
  - Real-world peer-review data (Expertiza-based) to ground evaluation in authentic feedback workflows for writing and research support; Claude.ai usage datasets mapped to O*NET tasks to validate task-capability mappings (writing, coding, research modalities) [10; 71].
  - Multi-domain and multi-tenant data sources to ensure robust ecological validity across workplace domains (education, software engineering, research).
- Experimental modalities
  - Independent evaluation pipelines (retriever-based relevance; end-to-end scoring) to map context to outputs.
  - Pairwise comparisons to compare model variants and prompting strategies.
  - LLM-as-a-judge rubrics to calibrate human judgments and produce scalable subjective quality signals.
  - Hybrid human–AI evaluation, with human validators for critical outputs and AI judges for scalable triage.
- Production-observability integration
  - Use LLM Evaluation Benchmarks, LLM Observability, and A/B testing to monitor real-world deployment, with dashboards tracking latency, throughput, accuracy, and user-reported experience.

5) Concrete measurement plan (blueprint) across writing, coding, and research
A. Tasks and capabilities (mapping)
- Writing: Summarization, Generation, Information Retrieval, Feedback/Reviewing Work
- Coding: Technical Assistance, Information Retrieval, Generation, Data Structuring; code correctness and maintainability
- Research: Data Structuring, Information Retrieval, Summarization; synthesis quality and citation fidelity
- Use the six-capability frame as organizing principle for metric selection and task design; align objective and subjective metrics per task category.

B. Metrics to collect (per task)
- Objective metrics (quantitative)
  - Time-to-delivery, total completion time, iterations per deliverable
  - Output quality proxies: coherence, accuracy, relevance, completeness; code correctness (unit test pass rates), maintainability proxies, citation accuracy for research
  - Relevance and fidelity metrics: Answer Relevance, Contextual Relevancy/Precision/Recall, Faithfulness (for RAG contexts)
  - Tool correctness and usage: Tool-usage precision and parameter correctness in coding tasks; retrieval precision/recall for research tasks
  - Fine-tuning–related metrics: Hallucination rate, toxicity, bias signals (domain-sensitive)
  - Task-level quality rubrics mapping to objective outcomes; end-to-end scores when labels exist; otherwise tag-based proxies (e.g., Includes Explanation, Suggests Actions)
- Subjective metrics (qualitative)
  - Perceived usefulness, trust, satisfaction, cognitive load, perceived workflow fit
  - Perceived reliability and explainability; perceived alignment with rubrics and scoring
  - Inter-rater reliability indicators if human raters are used for rubrics
- Observability metrics
  - Latency, throughput, resource utilization; AI tooling usage, prompt-classification signals; auditability and provenance of outputs
- Domain-specific risk metrics
  - Safety, bias, toxicity, ethical concerns, data provenance and citation integrity

C. Data collection plan
- Use real-world data sources (peer-review corpora, real course tasks, live coding tasks, research synthesis tasks) and synthetic data for boundary testing
- Ensure data splits are temporally and conceptually separated to prevent leakage; maintain data provenance for reproducibility
- Use a mix of within-subject and between-subject designs to assess both within-person improvements and cross-person generalizability
- Employ LLM-evaluators (LLM-as-a-Judge) in conjunction with human validators to calibrate subjective quality metrics and to reduce scaling costs
- Pre-register the measurement plan and publish enough methodological detail to support replication

D. Analysis plan
- Use multi-level/mixed-effects models to account for task type, participant, prompt design, and model variant; model non-linear interactions between metrics (as is common in ecologically valid studies)
- Report effect sizes, confidence intervals, and heterogeneity across tasks and domains
- Triangulate quantitative metrics with qualitative feedback from participants and domain experts
- Include sensitivity analyses to check robustness to prompt design, model variants, and task complexity
- Visualize relationships between objective performance, output quality, and subjective experience to support interpretation and design decisions

E. Reporting and benchmarks
- Report per-task-type results (writing, coding, research) and per-capability results; provide granular insights into which capabilities drive productivity and output quality in each domain
- Provide actionable benchmarks and recommended measurement practices for designers and practitioners
- Document limitations and caveats; discuss generalizability across domains, models, and deployment contexts

F. Limitations and cautions
- The literature often provides conceptual guidance and design principles rather than universal numeric benchmarks; numeric effect sizes require context-specific measurement campaigns
- Bias, data leakage, and model drift require ongoing monitoring; maintain governance and calibration pipelines to sustain ecological validity
- Ethical and safety considerations are central to productivity benchmarks; ensure measurement designs align with domain-specific risk tolerances

6) Actionable implications for authors and designers
- Ground metrics in ecologically valid capabilities: use the six capabilities as the organizing schema to measure productivity and output quality across writing, coding, and research
- Combine objective metrics with subjective assessments: use a mix of task-focused objective metrics and user-centric subjective rubrics to capture both performance and experience
- Implement end-to-end evaluation pipelines: independent/retriever evaluation, generation/synthesis evaluation, and end-to-end evaluation to map model performance onto real workflows
- Use LLM-as-a-judge or calibration networks to scale subjective evaluation with reliability; calibrate against gold-standard human judgments

8) Concrete, domain-focused guidance and recommendations
- For authors designing ecologically valid benchmarks:
  - Use an organizing framework based on capabilities that map to real-world tasks (writing, coding, research), and couple objective metrics with structured subjective rubrics to cover practical usefulness, reliability, and task practicality.
  - Ground measurement in externally sourced, real-world data (peer-review datasets; usage datasets mapped to domain tasks) to ensure ecological validity.
  - Implement end-to-end evaluation pipelines with retriever and end-to-end stages, plus independent evaluations to map model outputs to authentic workflows.
  - Calibrate LLM-based evaluators with gold-standard human judgments; use pairwise comparisons (A/B tests) to compare model/prompt variants; incorporate uncertainty estimates and calibration diagnostics.
  - Track model/hardware observability and gate outputs with governance signals (trust, safety, bias) to ensure sustainable adoption and risk management.

- For practitioners implementing LLM augmentation:
  - Start with a compact, stable core metric set (e.g., Task Completion, Correctness, Contextual Relevancy, Faithfulness, Tool Correctness) and augment with 1–2 task-specific metrics (e.g., Summarization quality for writing; Code correctness for coding; Citation fidelity for research).
  - Use LLM-as-a-judge rubrics to scale subjective assessment; calibrate against human judgments with a small gold-standard set; use pairwise comparisons to evaluate model variants and prompting strategies.
  - Build ecologically valid study designs: field deployments across writing, coding, and research tasks; longitudinal data collection; cross-domain replication; and production observability for continuous improvement.
  - Ensure governance: incorporate safety bias checks, model upgrades, and output interpretability; design dashboards to monitor performance, reliability, and trust signals.
  - Publish prompts, rubrics, and data splits (where privacy permits) to enable replication and cross-domain benchmarking.
