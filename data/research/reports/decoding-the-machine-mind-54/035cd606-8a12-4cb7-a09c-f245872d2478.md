Measurable gap Between capability and reliability for modern LLMs across domains: evidence, correlations, and calibration-guided guidance

Executive summary
- Across a representative set of domains (code, math, factual editing, creative writing), there is accumulating evidence that high capability does not automatically imply dependable reliability. This gap is visible across domain benchmarks, various prompting styles, and different model scales.
- Domain-specific results illustrate both capability gains and reliability shortfalls:
  - Code and math domains show substantial capability improvements with scale, prompting, and grounding, but reliability (alignment of confidence with correctness, resistance to distribution shifts, and cross-task generalization) remains imperfect without calibration-focused interventions. Notable data points include Arabic code benchmarks (3LM) showing strong but uneven performance across HumanEval-Arabic and MBPP-Arabic, with cross-language transfer and prompt-sensitivity effects; and reliability signals in code summarization (calibration with Brier/ECe-like metrics) demonstrating overconfidence in long sequences despite plausible correctness elsewhere. Inline: (3LM, 2025); (Virk et al., 2025).
  - Creative-writing domains (LitBench) show that zero-shot judges trail human alignment by around 5 percentage points on human-preference agreement, while domain-tuned reward models and chain-of-thought variants can close some of that gap; however, the strongest zero-shot judges still lag domain-specific human alignment, underscoring a persistent capability–reliability gap in non-factual tasks. Inline: (LitBench, 2025).
  - In multi-domain benchmarking (ReliableMath; HOPE; domain benchmarking efforts like 3LM; Domain-Specific Benchmarks for LLMs), reliability metrics such as reliability diagrams, ECE, and calibration errors reveal meaningful gaps where poseable prompts and larger models alone do not guarantee dependable correctness across domains or languages. Inline: (Xue et al., 2025); (Brådland et al., 2025); (Virk et al., 2025); (3LM, 2025); (LitBench, 2025).
- Across domains, a few robust calibration levers emerge: domain-tuned prompting, reliable prompting signals (unsolvability signaling, explicit uncertainty), grounding and retrieval augmentation (RAG), and post-hoc calibration (Platt scaling, isotonic regression, and analogous reliability-adjustment procedures) can substantially reduce the gap, but domain-specific evaluation remains essential. Inline: (Xue et al., 2025); (Virk et al., 2025); (Ni et al., 2024); (Anjum et al., 2025).

1) Domain-specific findings: measurable capability versus reliability

- Code domain
  - Arabic code benchmarks (3LM): GPT-4o achieves 83.5% pass@1+ on HumanEval-Arabic and 63.6% on MBPP-Arabic; cross-language correlation between Arabic and English pass@1 scores is approximately 0.97, indicating strong cross-language capability but substantial language- and prompt-sensitivity effects on reliability across dialectal domains. Gemma-3-27B attains 43.2% on STEM-completion tasks, suggesting scale and model class influence reliability differently across tasks. These results illustrate a high capability signal in code-generation tasks with Arabic prompts but a domain-specific reliability profile that hinges on language, prompts, and model class. Inline: (3LM, 2025); (Arabic code results in 3LM; cross-language correlation 0.97).
  - Reliability signals: LI (long instruction) prompts improve reliability in several domains of 3LM (e.g., QIC, SD, DD) and SI (short instruction) often reduces reliability; overall, the gains from LI are domain-dependent and typically modest (average LI gain ~0.04 in F1; base ~0.62 vs LI ~0.66 across nine tasks). This shows that longer prompts can improve dependability but do not guarantee universal reliability; in some cases, SI degrades performance. Inline: (Liu et al., 2025).
  - Summary: domain-specific calibration for code in Arabic requires domain-aligned prompts, translation-quality control, and post-processing to align outputs with domain-Relevant correctness criteria (e.g., unit tests, code execution checks). Cross-language transfer can be large (0.97), but reliability is domain-and-language dependent. Inline: (3LM, 2025).

- Math domain
  - 3LM and GATmath/GATLc data indicate strong domain-relevant math capabilities in Arabic and cross-language contexts, with notable differences across native vs synthetic datasets (e.g., native STEM MCQs vs synthetic STEM MCQs). The math-domain results show that scale and translation quality influence performance, yet reliability signals (e.g., consistency of intermediate steps, proof validity) require domain-specific calibration. Inline: (3LM, 2025); (AlBallaa et al., 2025).
  - GATmath (66.9% accuracy) and GATLc (64.3% accuracy) on Arabic math-language tasks illustrate substantial capability gaps relative to English or multilingual baselines, and dialect/lingual variation adds a reliability layer: performance is domain- and dialect-sensitive. Inline: (AlBallaa et al., 2025).
  - Takeaway: math-domain reliability is highly task-dependent; achieving near-human reliability requires domain-specific calibration (domain prompts, rationale checks, and validation). Inline: (AlBallaa et al., 2025).

- Factual editing (fact-checking, truthfulness, and editing tasks)
  - TruthfulQA-type results show a substantial reliability gap: even strong models can produce truthful content at rates below human baselines; calibration-focused interventions (training data, RLHF, explicit truthfulness objectives) improve reliability but do not eliminate miscalibration. This indicates a robust capability–reliability gap in factual-editing-like domains without targeted calibration. Inline: (TruthfulQA; Roy et al., 2022; cited in LitBench and related calibration literature).
  - Related domain-calibration literature (LitBench, HOPE) suggests that domain-specific factual grounding and verification pipelines are essential to close the gap between capability and reliable accuracy in fact-checking/editing tasks. Inline: (HOPE; LitBench).

- Creative writing
  - Creative-writing benchmarks (LitBench) show that zero-shot judges align with human preferences at roughly 73% accuracy; domain-tuned reward models can approach 78% human agreement on LitBench test data; however, chain-of-thought augmentations can reduce accuracy in some creative settings (e.g., GenRM-CoT ~72%) indicating that reasoning traces can hurt in certain creative-writing evaluation tasks. This demonstrates a domain-specific capability–reliability gap in generation and evaluation tasks where creativity and human preference alignment matter. Inline: (LitBench, 2025).

- Cross-domain correlation patterns
  - Domain sensitivity to prompting: prompt length and style produce variable reliability signals; LI prompts often improve reliability (LI boosts F1 in 9-domain study; average LI improvement ~+0.04 in F1), while SI prompts frequently degrade reliability (average SI change ~-0.04). This indicates prompt design is a key lever in domain-specific calibration (Liu et al., 2025).
  - Model scale and reliability: larger models tend to show improved capability but not uniform reliability across all tasks and domains; cross-domain domain results (3LM, LitBench, 3LM) reveal scale helps but calibration remains domain-specific.
  - Grounding and retrieval augmentation: frameworks that ground outputs in external knowledge (RAG) or provide explicit uncertainty signaling (calibration signals) are repeatedly shown to improve reliability, particularly in tasks requiring up-to-date knowledge, multi-step reasoning, or domain-specific accuracy (HOPE; L2CEval; 3LM; KnowFM; Dichotomy across tasks).
- Actionable guidance drawn from the domains above is described below.

2) Domain-wise actionable insights for authors and designers

Code domain
- Domain calibration approach:
  - Use domain-specific Arabic code benchmarks (3LM) to measure both capability and reliability; pair with a calibration framework (confidence signals, post-processing) to ensure outputs meet domain-specific correctness thresholds.
- Prompt design:
  - Employ LI prompts for higher reliability on domain tasks while monitoring potential SI degradation; systematically test multiple LI templates for each coding task.
- Grounding strategies:
  - Use code execution and unit tests as post-generation validation to close the capability–reliability gap in code tasks (execution-grounded verification).
- Multilingual considerations:
  - Leverage cross-language transfer (Arabic–English) with caution; ensure that language-specific prompts and dialect variants do not degrade reliability.

Math domain
- Domain calibration:
  - Calibrate math problem solving with stepwise verification and proof checking; align chain-of-thought reasoning with domain-specific validation (e.g., formal proofs, symbolic manipulation).
- Prompt design:
  - Use LI reasoning prompts and problem decomposition prompts; test across native Arabized math tasks and synthetic tasks (native vs synthetic dataset differences).
- Tooling:
  - Integrate external math solvers or symbolic engines where possible to improve reliability for high-stakes math tasks and reduce reliance on internal chain-of-thought alone.

Factual editing
- Grounding and verification:
  - Implement retrieval-grounded verification or external fact-checkers; calibrate confidence signaling to reflect uncertainty when facts are contested or beyond model memory.
- Domain-specific calibration:
  - Use domain-specific truthfulness benchmarks (TruthfulQA) and domain-grounded data; calibrate prompts to explicitly request sources and verifications.

Creative writing
- Reliability in evaluation:
  - Use domain-specific reward models and human-in-the-loop evaluations to align outputs with human preferences and domain constraints; monitor for prompts that elicit overly confident yet weak or misaligned outputs.
- Prompt design:
  - Use LI prompts for narrative coherence and alignment with constraints; monitor chain-of-thought prompts for potential reliability degradation in evaluation tasks.

Cross-domain calibration framework (practical steps)
- Define domain-specific capability metrics (task success, code correctness, math solution validity, factual edits, narrative coherence).
- Define domain-specific reliability metrics (calibrated confidence, factual accuracy, consistency across prompts, resistance to leakage or hallucination).
- Compute per-domain gaps: Gap_domain = StandardizedCapability_domain − StandardizedReliability_domain.
- Examine correlations:
  - Model scale versus Gap_domain
  - Prompt style versus Gap_domain
  - User expertise proxies (prompt engineering experience, domain knowledge) versus Gap_domain
- Use calibration techniques:
  - Post-hoc calibration (isotonic regression, Platt scaling, temperature scaling) where domain data and ground truths exist
  - Domain-grounding with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and external tools
  - Confidence signaling and “unsolvability” signals when uncertain
- Reporting:
  - Use reliability diagrams and ECE/Brier scores to illustrate calibration performance
  - Include domain-specific qualitative analyses of error types and failure modes

3) Limitations and cautions
- The domain landscape is heterogeneous; direct, unified cross-domain gap metrics require carefully harmonized definitions of “capability” and “reliability” per domain, plus domain-grounded gold standards.
- Many sources provide domain-specific data and methodology without universal cross-domain baselines; explicit cross-domain outcomes require dedicated benchmarking programs.
- Some domains (creative writing, factual editing) have more nuanced reliability signals (stylistic fidelity, factual grounding, ethical considerations) that are difficult to quantify with a single metric, necessitating a multi-metric calibration approach.

4) Recommendations for authors and researchers
- Build a cross-domain calibration program:
  - Choose a domain-coverage plan that includes code, math, factual editing, and creative writing; apply a standardized calibration metric suite (Brier Score, ECE, reliability diagrams) and domain-specific correctness metrics.
- Invest in domain-grounded evaluation loops:
  - Incorporate RAG, external-knowledge grounding, and explicit uncertainty signaling to improve domain reliability.
- Use long-horizon and stepwise reasoning evaluations:
  - For math and code, emphasize verifiable intermediate steps; for creative writing, emphasize consistency with constraints and quality of narrative drive.
- Document model-scale effects:
  - Report results across model scales (small, medium, large, and specialized domain-adapted models) and analyze how reliability improves with scale in conjunction with calibration interventions.
- Encourage human-in-the-loop evaluation:
  - Where reliability is critical (high-stakes domains, factual edits), include human adjudication and a robust rubric to capture domain-specific reliability signals beyond automated metrics.

Conclusion
- The literature collectively supports the central claim: there is a measurable gap between capability and reliability for modern LLMs across domains, and this gap is modulated by model scale, prompt style, language, and domain-specific grounding. Calibration-focused frameworks—whether via reliability metrics (Brier Score, ECE, reliability diagrams), domain-grounded prompting, grounding with external tools (RAG), or post-hoc calibration—are essential to close the gap and to guide designers and users toward dependable deployments. For future work, construct and report multi-domain calibration benchmarks that explicitly quantify gap size per domain (code, math, factual editing, creative writing) and explicitly test correlations with user expertise, prompt style, and model scale.
