Comprehensive Evidence-Based Report: Embedding a Diversity Prompt in AI Outputs to Surface Multiple Perspectives and Its Effect on Perceived Conversational Diversity and Social Capital in User Networks (4–8 Week Field Experiment)

Executive summary
- A body of work suggests that deliberate surface of diverse perspectives in AI outputs can influence user experience, engagement, belonging, and social dynamics in online communities, but robust, multi-week field evidence remains sparse and context-dependent. Existing studies indicate qualitative benefits and potential risks, with outcomes often mediated by user type, context, and alignment with group identity (Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Dahlke et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024) [Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Dahlke et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024].
- The literature also highlights that social capital is multifaceted (bonding, bridging, linking; cognitive, relational, structural) and that online measurement requires multi-dimensional instruments (Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998) [Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998].
- Pragmatic takeaway: to robustly test a diversity-prompt intervention in a 4–8 week field trial, design a controlled study with clearly specified prompts (surfacing at least three distinct perspectives in a single reply), measured across validated social-capital and perceived-diversity instruments, and analyzed with longitudinal, network-aware statistics. Important considerations include ethics, governance, user heterogeneity, and potential diffusion/participation dynamics (Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Dahlke et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024) [Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Dahlke et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024].

1) Introduction and research question
- Research question: Does embedding a “diversity prompt” in AI outputs that requires surfacing at least three distinct perspectives within a single reply increase perceived conversational diversity and social capital in user networks, in a 4–8 week field experiment?
- Rationale: Prior work indicates that surfaced diversity can influence motivation, engagement, and belonging in conversational settings, but there is limited quantitative, longitudinal evidence linking perspective-diversity prompts to social-capital outcomes in real networks. A field trial can clarify causal directions, mediating mechanisms, and boundary conditions (Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025) [Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Quinn & Gutt, 2025].
- Relevance of theory: 
  - Social capital theory distinguishes bonding, bridging, and linking forms, plus cognitive, relational, and structural dimensions. Online contexts require adapted measurement and may yield differential effects for bridging (diversity exposure) versus bonding (in-group cohesion) ties (Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998; Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003) [Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998; Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003].
  - Multi-perspective prompts connect to role-based prompting literature and diversity-focused design, with governance and ethics as central considerations (Lai et al., 2024; Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270) [Lai et al., 2024; Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270].

2) Theoretical foundations and measurement framework
- Social capital in online settings:
  - Dimensions: cognitive (trust, norms), relational (quality of relationships, reciprocity), structural (networks, connections). Bonding (within-group) and bridging (across-group) are central subtypes; linking capital crosses power/resource differentials (Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998; Williams, 2006; Carrillo Álvarez & Riera Romaní, 2017) [Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998; Williams, 2006; Carrillo Álvarez & Riera Romaní, 2017].
  - Measurement challenges: no single universal metric; a core-plus-extension (core metrics plus context-specific items) approach is recommended for robustness and comparability (Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003) [Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003].
- Perceived conversational diversity and belonging:
  - Qualitative evidence suggests that surface-level diversity can be novel and interesting, but quantitative links to social capital require field measurement across time (Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024) [Kun et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024].
  - Diversity prompts may influence belonging and community resilience when designed with ethics and governance in mind (Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270) [Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270].
- Boundary conditions and diffusion:
  - Online diffusion of content is moderated by social capital and group identity compatibility; higher bridging capital can aid diffusion when content aligns with group norms but can hinder diffusion when misaligned (Pyo et al., 2023; Dahlke et al., 2024) [Pyo et al., 2023; Dahlke et al., 2024].
  - Multi-agent/parallel-prompt designs and context-sensitive framing can influence diffusion and perceived diversity; governance and safety considerations remain essential (Lai et al., 2024; Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270) [Lai et al., 2024; Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270].

3) Evidence synthesis from relevant literature (highlights)
- Kun et al., 2024 (arXiv:2402.08558): Diversity-aware chatbots influence expectations and perceptions; engagement described as novel, but quantitative social-capital outcomes are not reported; two small student communities; qualitative insights suggest diversity prompts can influence motivation and exploration, not yet network-level outcomes (Kun et al., 2024) [Kun et al., 2024].
- Chaudhry & Debi, 2024 (mHealth): AI-driven mental-health support can foster real-time support and belonging but raises concerns about personalization, dependency, interface quality, and long-term effects; emphasizes safety and ethics for field deployment (Chaudhry & Debi, 2024) [Chaudhry & Debi, 2024].
- Quinn & Gutt, 2025 (JMIS): GenAI adoption reduces overall participation in some online communities (casual users) but can increase content value for engaged users; effects are heterogeneous by user type; a difference-in-differences framework can reveal causal-like effects (Quinn & Gutt, 2025) [Quinn & Gutt, 2025].
- Dahlke et al., 2024 (Research Policy): Diffusion of AI adoption exhibits epidemic-like patterns mediated by exposure to knowledge hubs and relational ties; clustering can impede diffusion; social capital influences diffusion pathways (Dahlke et al., 2024) [Dahlke et al., 2024].
- Lai et al., 2024 (arXiv:2402.12590): Emergent AI collectives show that exposure diversity and network evolution depend on self-organization; measurements for exposure/diversity include distinct conversations and cross-network connections; prompts to surface diversity require governance considerations (Lai et al., 2024) [Lai et al., 2024].
- Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998: Foundational measurement frameworks for online/offline social capital, including bridging vs bonding and structural vs cognitive dimensions; argue for mixed-methods and context-adaptive instrumentation (Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998) [Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998].
- NIST SP 1270: Trustworthy and Responsible AI; emphasizes governance, transparency, and socio-technical factors beyond bias; recommends stakeholder engagement, TEVV (testing/evaluation/validation/verification), and multi-stakeholder oversight (NIST SP 1270) [NIST SP 1270].
- Field-methodology references for social capital measurement in online contexts:
  - ISCS (Internet Social Capital Scales; Williams, 2006) and SC-IQ (Grootaert et al., 2003) provide validated modalities to measure online/offline and bonding/bridging/linking. These instruments inform the primary outcome metrics for a 4–8 week trial (ISCS; SC-IQ) [Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003].
- Additional perspectives on ethics, governance, and multi-stakeholder design:
  - Dwivedi et al., 2023; Campo-Ruiz, 2025; Roche et al., 2022; Victor, 2024; etc., provide governance, equity, and ethics considerations that shape how a field experiment should be designed and reported (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Campo-Ruiz, 2025; Roche et al., 2022; Victor, 2024) [Dwivedi et al., 2023; Campo-Ruiz, 2025; Roche et al., 2022; Victor, 2024].

4) Proposed field-experiment design (4–8 weeks)
- Experimental design
  - Type: randomized controlled trial or cluster-randomized design with parallel arms.
  - Arms:
    - Treatment: AI outputs embed a diversity prompt requiring surface of at least three distinct perspectives within a single reply; perspectives labeled and sourced to ensure transparency.
    - Control: Standard AI outputs without enforced multi-perspective surface requirements.
  - Setting: A real-world online community or platform where AI-assisted conversations occur (e.g., forum threads, chat-based collaboration tool, learning environment).
  - Duration: 4–8 weeks, with data collection at baseline, mid-point (week 2–4), endline (week 6–8), and optional follow-up.
- Participants
  - Diverse user base across ages, genders, education levels, and cultural backgrounds to examine heterogeneity in effects (Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024).
  - Informed consent and opt-out options; privacy-preserving data collection; ethics approval.
- Intervention specifics
  - The diversity prompt should mandate surfacing three distinct perspectives in a single reply; each perspective should be coherent, non-inflammatory, and anchored to domain-relevant viewpoints (e.g., stakeholder roles such as user, subject-m matter expert, policy perspective).
  - Logging: capture per-reply perspective count, source attribution, response length, response time, and user engagement metrics (e.g., follow-up interactions, reciprocation).
- Outcome measures
  - Primary: Perceived conversational diversity (validated scales or multi-item index); sense of belonging in the user network (psychosocial scales).
  - Secondary: Social capital proxies (bridging vs bonding online and offline; network centrality; reciprocity; network density); engagement metrics (session frequency, duration, reply rate); perceived AI trust and credibility; perceived quality of AI outputs.
  - Exposure metrics: diversity of sources/perspectives surfaced per reply; cross-group interactions initiated or sustained.
- Measurement instruments (selected, with rationale)
  - Social capital: ISCS (Williams, 2006) adapted for online contexts to capture online bonding/bridging across online/offline spheres; or the two-factor SCO measurement approach (bonding vs bridging) as a practical proxy in online settings (Tiwari et al., 2019) [Williams, 2006; Tiwari et al., 2019].
  - Social capital framework: SC-IQ (Grootaert et al., 2003) for multi-dimensional measurement (groups/networks, trust, collective action, information/communication, inclusion, empowerment) with adaptation to online contexts (Grootaert et al., 2003).
  - Perceived diversity: multi-item scales or adapt validated “diversity perception” items; consider integrating items from AI-diversity literatures and domain-specific diversity measures (Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Purcell et al., 2025).
  - Ethical governance: document governance/mechanisms in line with NIST SP 1270 guidance and ethical frameworks (Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270; Victor, 2024).
- Analysis plan
  - Primary causal analysis: mixed-effects models with random intercepts for participants and clusters, fixed effects for treatment, time, and interaction (treatment × time).
  - Longitudinal network analysis: compute centrality, density, bridging metrics; assess diffusion-like outcomes (exposure to diverse perspectives) and cross-group interactions.
  - Mediation analyses: test whether perceived diversity mediates the effect of the diversity-prompt intervention on social-capital outcomes.
  - Moderation analyses: test whether effects vary by user type (casual vs engaged), demographic factors, or baseline network position.
  - Pre-registration: specify hypotheses, primary/secondary outcomes, and analysis plan to enhance transparency and reproducibility (per best practices suggested by Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Dwivedi et al., 2023).
- Practical and ethical safeguards
  - Transparency: clearly disclose the diversity-prompt mechanism and sources of perspectives to users; include opt-out and explainable AI components.
  - Safety: monitor for misinformation, polarization, or cognitive overload; include moderation or content safeguards as needed (NIST SP 1270; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024).
  - Privacy: minimize data collection, anonymize logs, and obtain required approvals; ensure compliance with platform policies and data protection regulations.
  - Governance: establish an ethics/governance board with multi-stakeholder input; maintain documentation and allow for interim adjustments if harms or disutility emerge (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Victor, 2024).

5) Operationalizing measurement and data collection
- Data sources
  - System logs: per-reply perspective counts, source diversity indicators, response lengths/timing.
  - User surveys: baseline, mid-point, endline, with validated scales for perceived diversity, belonging, and social capital.
  - Network data: ego-network maps and cross-network interactions to derive bridging/bonding measures.
  - Qualitative data: optional interviews or open-ended survey prompts to contextualize quantitative results.
- Instrumentation mapping
  - Perceived conversational diversity: scale items developed or adapted from diversity perception literature and AI-diversity studies (Chaudhry & Debi, 2024; Purcell et al., 2025).
  - Social capital: ISCS (online vs offline; bridging vs bonding) and/or SC-IQ-based items; consider latent-variable approaches (Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998; Peng et al., 2021).
  - Exposure diversity: operationalize as distinct-perspective counts per reply; ratio of replies meeting the “three perspectives” criterion; surrogates like cross-source diversity metrics.
- Timelines
  - Week 0–2: baseline measurements; training on the prompt design and logging.
  - Weeks 2–6: active intervention with ongoing data collection.
  - Weeks 6–8: endline measurements; optional follow-up at week 10–12 to assess persistence.

6) Limitations and caveats
- Generalizability: results may vary by platform, task domain, community norms, and demographic composition; careful reporting of context is essential (Quinn & Gutt, 2025; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024).
- Measurement validity: social capital in online contexts is multi-faceted; adapt instruments carefully and justify modifications; triangulate with qualitative data.
- Potential adverse effects: risk of cognitive overload, polarization, or mis/disinformation amplification; implement safety safeguards and governance oversight (NIST SP 1270; Dwivedi et al., 2023; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024).
- Causal inference: field experiments in online settings face attrition and noncompliance; pre-registration and robust sensitivity analyses are warranted (Quinn & Gutt, 2025).

7) Actionable insights for the author
- Ground the study in established multi-dimensional social-capital theory and measurement: plan for online/offline, bonding/bridging, and cognitive/relational/structural dimensions; incorporate core metrics plus context-specific supplements (Williams, 2006; Grootaert et al., 2003; Nahapiet & Ghoshal, 1998).
- Treat social capital as a primary outcome but balance with perceived diversity and boundary conditions (identity compatibility, platform type, and user demographics) to understand heterogeneous effects (Pyo et al., 2023; Dahlke et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024).
- Build in governance and ethics from design through execution: include stakeholder input, risk assessment, transparency, and accountability measures as recommended by multiple sources (Dwivedi et al., 2023; NIST SP 1270; Victor, 2024).
- Pilot the diversity-prompt design prior to full deployment to calibrate the number and framing of perspectives (three or more) and to refine prompts for coherence and safety (Lai et al., 2024; Chaudhry & Debi, 2024).
- Provide actionable reporting: publish both quantitative results (effect sizes, confidence intervals, mediation/moderation analyses) and qualitative insights (user experiences, perceived harms/benefits) to inform policy and practice.

- Diversity/prompts outcomes:
  - Perceived diversity of perspectives; Exposure diversity metrics; Cross-group engagement; Sense of belonging; Trust in network.
- Moderators and mediators:
  - Identity compatibility; Platform type; User demographics; Baseline engagement; Mediation via perceived diversity to social capital.

This report integrates evidence and design guidance from diverse sources to inform a rigorous 4–8 week field experiment testing the effect of a diversity prompt on perceived conversational diversity and social capital in user networks. The design emphasizes validated measurement strategies, longitudinal causal inference, ethical governance, and practical considerations for real-world deployment.
