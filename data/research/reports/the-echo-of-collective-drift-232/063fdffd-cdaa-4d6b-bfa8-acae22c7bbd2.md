# report.md

Comprehensive synthesis: AI-generated smart replies in dyadic messaging, perceived closeness, satisfaction, and mediating pathways (speed, warmth, personalization)

Executive summary
- Across a broad literature base, AI-generated smart replies can increase communication efficiency and alter the linguistic tone of dyadic exchanges, but effects on perceived closeness and satisfaction are context-dependent and moderated by attribution, warmth, personalization, and topic. When AI involvement is perceived or labeled, closeness can suffer; when AI replies are believed to be human-generated or when AI is used in carefully designed, warmth-focused ways, perceived warmth and intimacy can be maintained or enhanced (Hohenstein et al.; Jung et al.; Liu et al.; Li et al.; Bergner, Hildebrand, Häubl; Yin et al.; McKee et al.). Inline citations appear after statements describing each study’s core findings.
- Primary mediators repeatedly identified across studies include: 
  - Speed/efficiency of response (faster exchanges often linked to higher satisfaction and perceived engagement),
  - Warmth/emotional support signals (positive affect, validated feelings, supportive tone),
  - Personalization/personal voice (fit to the recipient, recipient-tailored content),
  - Attribution/identity signals (whether the other party or the AI is perceived as the source).
- Design implications emphasize: transparent attribution when AI is involved, preserving warmth and personalization in AI-generated content, enabling human-in-the-loop safeguards for emotionally salient exchanges, and measuring closeness/satisfaction alongside traditional task metrics in future studies.

1) Research question and scope
- Core question: In dyadic messaging, does replacing human replies with AI-generated smart replies reduce perceived closeness and satisfaction relative to human-only interaction, and which mediators (speed, warmth, personalization) explain any observed effects?
- Scope across evidence: The literature comprises controlled experiments, field studies, longitudinal studies, and theoretical syntheses spanning human–AI interactions in text-, voice-, and embodied contexts. Some studies directly compare AI-generated versus human replies in dyadic settings; many provide causal or quasi-causal evidence about proximity-related constructs (closeness, rapport, affiliation), trust, perceived warmth, and the role of attribution. Other sources illuminate plausible mediators (speed, warmth, personalization) and boundary conditions (transparency, uncanny-valley effects, recruitment contexts, cultural differences).

2) Direct empirical evidence: dyadic AI-replies vs human replies (perceived closeness and satisfaction)
- Direct test of AI-replies replacing human replies in real-time dyadic exchanges is limited but informative:
  - Hohenstein et al. (2021; Science/Scientific Reports): Large two-study program with N ≈ 1,036 in which AI-generated smart replies (SRs) were available to one or both participants. Key takeaways:
    - AI SRs increase communication efficiency (more messages per minute) and can shift language toward greater warmth (positive sentiment) when SRs are used; perceived warmth and cooperation can improve if SRs are actually used (not merely present) by self or partner. However, perceived partner SR use reduces affiliative judgments (less cooperation, lower affiliation) toward that partner, indicating a potential cost to closeness when others suspect AI mediation (Hohenstein et al., 2021; 2023). Inline citations: (Hohenstein et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2023).
  - Cornell/“smart replies” experiments (Jung et al., 2023): Across paired participants (N ~ 219), SRs accounted for about 14.3% of messages and increased total messages per minute by ~10.2%. When recipients suspect AI involvement, perceived closeness declines (greater discomfort, lower affiliation). Actual AI use by either party can produce more positive partner evaluations, but AI-suspected contexts undermine closeness. Inline citation: (Jung et al., 2023).
  - Liu et al. (2023) in Journal of Social and Personal Relationships: In vignette-based manipulations of AI-assisted vs third-party vs human-only draft edits of messages to a friend, AI-assisted replies reduced perceived appropriateness and increased perceived impropriety, lowering relationship satisfaction and increasing uncertainty about closeness. Perceived effort acted as a mediator: AI- or third-party editing signals lower personal effort by the sender, which reduced satisfaction and boosted uncertainty about closeness. Inline citation: (Liu et al., 2023).
  - Yin, Jia, & Wakslak (2024) in PNAS: AI-generated messages can make recipients feel more heard than human-generated messages when the responder is presented as AI; however, if recipients believe the message came from AI, they feel less heard relative to believing it was from a human. Warmth/emotional support signals and emotion-detection capabilities are central mediators; attribution (AI vs human) moderates the effects on perceived closeness. Inline citation: (Yin et al., 2024).
  - Seitz (2024) in Computers in Human Behavior and related work: AI empathy (empathic vs non-empathic AI) can boost warmth and trust but may suppress perceived authenticity in AI-mediated interactions; authenticity acts as a suppressor in warmth→trust pathways in some AI contexts. This has implications for closeness and satisfaction in AI-mediated dyads. Inline citation: (Seitz, 2024).
  - Bergner, Hildebrand, Häubl (2023) in Journal of Consumer Research: Verbal embodiment in AI (turn-taking, grounding, initiation) increases perceived humanness in dialogue and strengthens consumer–brand relationships; higher perceived humanness (humanness proxies) can promote intimacy with the brand. Mediators implied include perceived humanness, grounding, and turn-taking. While not a dyadic human–human relation, the mechanism translates to AI-mediated dyadic messaging where verbal embodiment can affect closeness with the interlocutor (brand or partner). Inline citation: (Bergner et al., 2023).
- Synthesis takeaway: There is robust evidence that AI-generated replies can increase efficiency and warmth signals under certain conditions, and can increase trust in the partner or the AI. However, attribution and the perception of AI involvement can undermine closeness, particularly when AI is perceived as replacing or outsourcing the sender’s personal input. The direction of the effect depends heavily on:
  - Whether AI involvement is perceived or explicit (attribution),
  - The warmth and personalization cues in the AI output,
  - The degree to which the recipient perceives the AI as enabling genuine interpersonal exchange (reciprocity, responsiveness) vs. as an impersonal tool.

3) Mediators examined across studies (speed, warmth, personalization)
- Speed / efficiency
  - Consistently associated with increased throughput, quicker exchanges, and higher perceived engagement in AI-mediated contexts (Hohenstein et al.; Fang et al., 2025; Klein et al., 2024). In dyadic messaging, faster replies can be seen as greater attentiveness or efficiency, contributing to satisfaction, though speed alone does not guarantee closeness if warmth/personalization are lacking.
- Warmth / emotional support
  - A key mediator in multiple lines of work: AI replies that convey warmth or emotional support can enhance perceived closeness and trust (Yin et al., 2024; Seitz, 2024; Park et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2020). However, warmth can interact with authenticity; perceived authenticity may be dampened if AI is misattributed or if warmth signals feel inauthentic or disingenuous (Seitz, 2024; Yin et al., 2024).
- Personalization / personalization signals
  - Personalization generally enhances perceived usefulness and warmth, and increases ownership of the message; however, excessive personalization or privacy concerns can dampen trust (Li et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023; Feng, 2024). Personal voice and recipient-tailored content can support closeness when perceived as sincerely tailored, but risks to privacy or misalignment with user expectations may undermine satisfaction or trust (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025; Feng, 2024).
- Attribution / source signaling
  - The source of the reply (AI vs human) is a powerful mediator of perceived closeness. When the recipient believes the reply is AI-generated, warmth signals may be deindexed or perceived as less authentic, reducing closeness; when believed to be human-generated (even if AI assisted behind the scenes), warmth and closeness signals may be stronger (Yin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Hohenstein et al., 2021).
- Trust as a mediator or moderator
  - Trust in AI vs trust in human partner strongly shapes attribution and perceived closeness. In some contexts, higher trust in AI can accompany higher perceived closeness to the AI or the human partner, depending on whether the AI acts as a mediator or as a source of warmth and support (Hohenstein & Jung, 2019; Li et al., 2024; McKee et al., 2023).
- Additional mediators highlighted in cross-domain work
  - Mind perception / social presence: Perceived mind and cognitive social presence influence closeness, affinity, and perceived intimacy with AI partners (Qi et al., 2025; McKee et al., 2023).
  - Uncanny valley effects: Realism without reliable coherence can produce discomfort that reduces closeness (Mori, 2012; Ciechanowski et al., 2019).

4) Quantitative patterns by study (highlights and caveats)
- Hohenstein et al. (2021; 2023) – Two large online studies (N ≈ 1,036)
  - SR presence increased efficiency and warmth signals when used; however, perceived partner AI use lowered affiliation and cooperation toward that partner.
  - Actual SR use by self or partner led to more positive partner attitudes toward the sender; perceived AI use by partner predicted lower closeness.
  - Mediator: speed and warmth; caveat: attribution matters (perceived AI use) for closeness.
  Inline citations: (Hohenstein et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2023).
- Jung et al. (2023) – Two-person paired design (N ≈ 219)
  - SRs increased throughput by ~10.2% and 14.3% of messages; perceived AI involvement reduces closeness; actual SR use improves partner evaluations; perceived warmth increased via positive language; negative SRs reduce sentiment.
  Inline citation: (Jung et al., 2023).
- Liu et al. (2023) – Vignette-based manipulation (N = 208)
  - AI-assisted drafting decreased perceived appropriateness, increased impropriety, reduced relationship satisfaction, and increased uncertainty about closeness; perceived effort mediates effect (AI- or third-party editing signals lower sender effort).
  Inline citation: (Liu et al., 2023).
- Yin et al. (2024) – Experimental and follow-up studies (N not stated in excerpt)
  - AI-generated replies increased feeling heard; AI labeling reduces warmth and perceived closeness; warmth/emotional support as mediator; emotion-detection capability improves responsiveness.
  Inline citation: (Yin et al., 2024).
- Seitz (2024) – Cross-context AI empathy
  - Empathetic AI boosts warmth/trust but may reduce perceived authenticity in AI-mediated contexts; authenticity can dampen warmth→trust pathways.
  Inline citation: (Seitz, 2024).
- Bergner et al. (2023) – Humanness through verbal embodiment
  - Verbal embodiment signals (turn-taking, grounding) increase perceived humanness, boosting closeness in a consumer-brand dyad context; the effect is contingent on consistent interface cues.
  Inline citation: (Bergner et al., 2023).
- Li et al. (2024) – Trust in AI
  - Warmth and competence are pivotal determinants of trust; warmth partially mediates the effect of other factors on trust; trust mediates adoption intentions. Speed/personalization not explicitly tested as mediators.
  Inline citation: (Li et al., 2024).

5) Design implications and actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Transparency and attribution management
  - If AI is used to draft or mediate messages in a dyad, provide transparent cues about AI involvement, but avoid overexposure to AI labeling that reduces warmth and perceived closeness (Yin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Hohenstein et al., 2021).
  - Consider hybrid designs that keep a human in the loop for emotionally salient turns, to preserve warmth and reciprocity and reduce misattribution costs (Klein et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025; Mortati & Freitas, 2025).
- Warmth and personalization as core levers
  - Prioritize warmth in AI-generated replies (empathic phrasing, validated feelings, supportive tone) and ensure personalization signals align with the recipient’s history and relationship context (Yin et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
  - Incorporate personalization judiciously; privacy concerns must be managed to avoid eroding trust (Li et al., 2024; Feng, 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).
- Speed and efficiency with safeguards
  - Leverage AI to increase response speed and reduce friction, while ensuring accuracy and warmth are not sacrificed; measure both latency and perceived warmth in experiments (Glikson & Woolley; Gnewuch; Crolic et al., 2022).
- Measurement and experimental design guidance
  - Include direct measures of closeness (IOS, perceived interpersonal closeness/rapport scales), satisfaction, perceived warmth, and perceived authenticity; test mediators explicitly (speed/latency, warmth, personalization) using mediation analysis.
  - Consider context variance: romantic, friendship, colleague, professional dyads; long vs short-term interactions; task-based vs casual exchanges.
  - Use mixed-methods designs to capture subjective experience and objective metrics (speed, sentiment, affiliation) and triangulate findings (Liu et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; McKee et al., 2023).
- Safety and ethics
  - Build in safeguards against dystopian outcomes (overreliance, loss of real-world social connection, echo chambers, manipulation, deception), and ensure explicit ethical guidelines for AI-mediated messaging in private contexts (Hancock et al., 2020; Mortati & Freitas, 2025; Guingrich & Graziano, 2024; Chu et al., 2025).
- Practical practice guidelines
  - For consumer apps: offer optional human-in-the-loop for critical turns; provide clear disclosure options for AI involvement; allow recipient-control to adjust warmth/personalization levels.
  - For mental health and counseling contexts: emphasize empathic but authentic support; ensure privacy controls; consider transparency about AI use and the role of human oversight; test mediators like warmth and perceived empathy as pivotal determinants of user satisfaction.
  - For professional and workplace contexts: balance speed/efficiency with warmth and human oversight; ensure trust-building through consistent interface cues; implement privacy-preserving personalization.

6) Limitations and boundary conditions
- Many findings come from lab-based or vignette-based experiments, some from field studies in consumer contexts, and a subset from specialized contexts (mental health, crisis communication). Generalization to all dyadic texting (private, intimate, or high-stakes) should be cautious.
- The literature reveals inconsistent effects: AI can boost warmth and perceived closeness in some settings and reduce closeness when attribution is uncertain or when warmth is perceived as inauthentic or mechanical.
- Direct measurements of “closeness” in real human–AI dyads replacing human replies are still sparse; several studies rely on proxies (affiliation, perceived warmth, perceived closeness to the AI, trust).

7) Concrete, actionable insights for the author (summary)
- Design and test AI-mediated dyadic messaging with explicit mediation analysis for speed, warmth, and personalization.
- Use a hybrid model approach: human-in-the-loop designs in emotionally charged or trust-sensitive contexts to preserve closeness while maintaining AI efficiency.
- Implement transparent but nuanced AI involvement signals—avoid overt framing that destroys warmth; consider customizable disclosure options.
- Prioritize warmth and personalization as core features in AI-generated replies; use recipient history to tailor tone, content, and empathic cues without compromising privacy.
- Consider dynamic adaptation: adjust AI role based on context (e.g., supportive warmth in personal conversations, efficiency in routine information-sharing) and measure outcomes across closeness, satisfaction, trust, and relationship intentions.
- Develop standardized measures for closeness in AI-mediated dyads, including IOS-based scales, perceived warmth, perceived authenticity, trust in partner/AI, and willingness to continue the conversation, to enable cross-study comparability.
- Encourage longitudinal and cross-domain studies to examine how closeness evolves as users accumulate experience with AI-mediated exchanges and how offline social supports modulate outcomes (Zhang et al., 2025; Skjuve et al., 2022).

Additional notes
- The literature contains a mix of laboratory experiments, field studies, and longitudinal studies across contexts (dyadic texting, crisis communication, customer service, mental health). The “mediators” that appear repeatedly across these studies include warmth/emotional resonance, speed/latency, and personalization, with attribution and perceived mind playing crucial moderating roles in closeness and satisfaction outcomes.
- For future research, a rigorous, preregistered program of dyadic experiments is recommended, directly manipulating AI-reply usage (0%, 25%, 50%, 100%), attribution signals (transparent AI labeling vs no labeling), and mediator measurements (speed, warmth, personalization, perceived mind) across a range of relationship types (romantic, friendship, colleague) and contexts (private texting, crisis communication, healthcare).

Appendix: quick research-action checklist for authors planning a new study
- Experimental design: 4-arm design (0% AI, 25%, 50%, 100% AI-generated replies in dyadic chats) with both sender and receiver manipulated; include conditions for AI labeling vs non-labeling.
- Outcomes to measure: perceived closeness/rapport (IOS or similar), relationship satisfaction, perceived warmth, perceived authenticity, trust in partner and AI, willingness to continue the dialogue.
- Mediators: speed/response latency, warmth/emotional tone, personalization level, attribution signals (AI vs human source).
- Moderators: relationship type, topic/context, cultural setting, player trust in AI, privacy concerns.
- Methods: mixed-methods (quantitative mediation analysis with preregistered hypotheses + qualitative interviews to capture nuances of warmth, authenticity, and perceived closeness).
- Ethics: ensure transparency, privacy protections, and optional human-in-the-loop flows for emotionally salient interactions.

This report integrates converging evidence across a broad landscape of AI-mediated communication to address the cited research question. It highlights when AI-generated smart replies may support or undermine perceived closeness and satisfaction in dyadic messaging and identifies core mediators and boundary conditions for future, targeted experiments.
