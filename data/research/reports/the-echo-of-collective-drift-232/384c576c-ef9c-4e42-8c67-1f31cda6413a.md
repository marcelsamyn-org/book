Framing the Question
- Research question: Does a friction budget—a limited daily number of AI interactions or prompts—shift user reliance from AI to human contacts and affect long-term belonging and well-being? A 12-week field experiment.
- Rationale: A friction budget could suppress over-reliance on AI, preserve human social practice, and promote long-run belonging and well-being. Evidence from short-duration AI-use studies suggests that higher AI usage correlates with worse psychosocial outcomes and that interaction quality (topic, modality) shapes these effects. However, causal evidence for a 12-week friction-budget intervention remains to be established in field settings (Fang et al., 2025; Yuan et al., 2025; Jacobs, 2024; Klingbeil et al., 2024).

Executive summary
- Key empirical foundation:
  - A four-week randomized trial (n = 981; >300k messages) found no robust group-level effects of modality (text vs voice) or conversation type on loneliness or real-world socialization, but longer daily AI usage was associated with worse psychosocial outcomes (loneliness, emotional dependence, problematic AI usage) and reduced real-world socialization. Daily duration mediated some modality- and task-related effects; personal conversations tended to reduce emotional dependence and problematic usage relative to open-ended prompts (Fang et al., 2025).
  - Additional evidence indicates that longer usage, higher attachment to AI, and higher trust in AI predict worse or more pronounced psychosocial responses in some designs; conversely, high-quality AI social support can reduce loneliness or anxiety in certain contexts, though effects are nuanced and not uniformly long-term (Merrill Jr. et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2022–2023; Skjuve et al., 2021).
- Core implication for a 12-week friction-budget trial:
  - A friction budget could plausibly reduce AI-driven substitution of human contact if designed to limit total AI exposure and encourage bounded, meaningful AI interactions paired with real-world social engagement. Evidence consistently highlights that quantity of AI use interacts with interaction quality (topic, modality) to shape belonging and well-being, suggesting that a well-calibrated budget with human-social prompts may stabilize or improve long-term social capital (Fang et al., 2025; Merrill Jr. et al., 2025).
- Practical takeaway for design:
  - Start with a graded friction-budget design that caps daily AI prompts and includes structured nudges toward human social activity, with additional arms that vary whether human support is encouraged vs. simply capped.
  - Emphasize bounded, personal, or goal-directed AI interactions over open-ended, non-personal prompts to reduce emotional dependence and encourage healthier usage patterns (Fang et al., 2025).
  - Monitor daily AI usage as a time-varying mediator and include real-world social-behavior outcomes (contacts, in-person/social activity) as primary outcomes alongside belonging and well-being (Fang et al., 2025; Klingbeil et al., 2024).

Background and theoretical framing
- Conceptual frame: Friction budgets are a design lever to balance AI assistance with preservation of human social practice and social capital, informed by socio-psychological theories of belonging, attachment, and trust in automation (Kirk et al., 2024a; Geertz, 1973; Nelson, 2023; Peter, Riemer, West, 2025; Jacobs, 2024).
- Relevant themes from prior work:
  - AI can provide emotional support and reduce loneliness in certain contexts, but long-term effects on social belonging are mixed and may depend on interaction quality, dependence risks, and the possibility that AI substitutes human contact (Merrill Jr. et al., 2025; Skjuve et al., 2021; De Freitas et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025).
  - Trust and attachment dynamics shape AI reliance: higher trust or stronger attachment can predict greater emotional dependence or loneliness, and explanations or transparency can calibrate reliance (Dzindolet et al., 2003; Klingbeil et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025).
  - Design considerations emphasize safeguarding human social capital, including anchor points for human contact and governance that mitigates harms (Peter et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023).
- Gaps the 12-week trial would address:
  - Direct experimental evidence on friction budgets (usage caps) and trajectories of belonging/well-being over a multi-week horizon.
  - Mediation by daily AI duration and moderation by baseline psychosocial variables (attachment, trust) in a field setting.

Research questions and hypotheses (12-week field study)
- Primary question: Does imposing a daily friction budget on AI prompts shift reliance away from AI and toward human contacts, and does this, in turn, influence long-term belonging and well-being?
- Hypotheses (provisional, testable):
  - H1 (usage and reliance): A friction-budget condition will reduce total daily AI prompts and time spent with AI compared with a control condition, and this reduction will mediate improvements in real-world social engagement and belonging.
  - H2 (belonging and well-being): Participants under a friction budget will show greater or stable long-term belonging and well-being trajectories over 12 weeks compared with controls, mediated by increased human social interaction and reduced AI dependence.
  - H3 (interaction quality): Friction budgets that emphasize personal, bounded AI interactions and include explicit prompts for human social activity will yield larger benefits for belonging and well-being than caps that merely reduce AI usage.
  - H4 (moderators): Baseline attachment to AI, trust in AI, and initial levels of loneliness will moderate effects; those with higher vulnerability (e.g., higher loneliness, higher AI attachment) may benefit most from structured friction and human prompts but may require adaptive adjustments.

Proposed 12-week field experiment design (high-level)
- Design: Randomized controlled trial with three arms over 12 weeks
  - Control: Unrestricted AI usage (standard practice)
  - FB (Fixed Friction Budget): Daily cap on AI prompts (e.g., 5 prompts/day) with cooldowns; no mandatory human prompts
  - FB+HP (Fixed Friction Budget + Human Prompt): Daily cap plus mandatory prompts to contact a human social contact or participate in a structured real-world activity after AI usage
- Population: Adults who regularly use AI companions for social or emotional support; strata by baseline loneliness, attachment tendencies, and trust in AI
- Randomization: Individual-level, block-randomized by strata to ensure balance on baseline loneliness and AI-use profiles
- Timepoints and data collection:
  - Baseline (week 0): Psychosocial measures, AI-use baseline, human-contact baseline
  - Weekly: AI-usage logs (prompts, duration, modality, topics), brief well-being check-ins
  - Midpoint (week 6): Reassessment of all primary/secondary outcomes
  - End (week 12): Full primary/secondary outcomes
  - Follow-up (week 16): Optional maintenance assessment
- Measures (core and extended):
  - Primary outcomes: Belonging (e.g., social belonging scales), loneliness (UCLA or shorter), and overall well-being
  - Secondary outcomes: Real-world socialization (frequency/quality), emotional dependence on AI, problematic AI usage, AI trust/attachment, perceived AI social presence
  - Mediators: Daily AI duration, proportion of AI-led vs human-led interactions
  - Moderators: Baseline loneliness, AI attachment (EHARS), trust in AI, demographic characteristics
- Analytic plan:
  - Mixed-effects models to assess trajectories and condition-by-time interactions
  - Mediation analyses to test whether AI duration reduction and increased human contact mediate effects on belonging/well-being
  - Moderation analyses to identify subgroups most responsive to friction budgeting
  - Pre-registered primary analyses with multiple-testing corrections; sensitivity analyses for adherence, missing data, and site differences
- Power and sample size (rough guidance):
  - For three arms with repeated measures, detecting small-to-moderate effects on belonging (d ≈ 0.25–0.35) with 80% power at α = 0.05 typically requires approximately 150–250 participants per arm, accounting for attrition. A cautious target of N ≈ 600–900 total (200–300 per arm) would provide reasonable power for primary outcomes and allow subgroup analyses. Exact power calculations should be finalized with a formal a priori analysis given the planned mediation and moderation tests.
- Safety, ethics, and governance:
  - Informed consent, privacy-preserving analytics, opt-out and crisis-protocols
  - Safeguards to ensure AI access remains therapeutic and does not replace essential human support
  - Data governance considerations aligned with broader ethical, regulatory, and governance literature on AI companions
- Contextual design levers:
  - Emphasize bounded AI interactions, meaningful, goal-directed prompts, and human-in-the-loop triggers
  - Provide transparency about AI limitations and enable user feedback on AI outputs
  - Surface prompts that encourage real-world social engagement (e.g., reminders to connect with friends/family, scheduling social activities)
  - Balance AI utility with human social capital preservation, leveraging insights about personal conversations reducing dependence and real-world socialization benefits of bounded AI use (Fang et al., 2025)

Operationalization and measurement details (selected instruments)
- Primary belonging/well-being measures:
  - Belonging scales (validated multi-item measures)
  - UCLA Loneliness Scale (short form or full form)
  - WHO-5 or similar short well-being index
- AI-use metrics:
  - Daily prompts, total minutes, modality (text, neutral voice, engaging voice), and topic type (open-ended, personal, non-personal)
- Psychosocial mediators and moderators:
  - AI attachment (EHARS or equivalent)
  - Trust in AI (standardized trust scales)
  - Baseline loneliness and social capital proxies
- Human-contact metrics:
  - Frequency and quality of real-world interactions (self-report; optional passive sensing with consent)
- Qualitative components:
  - Brief weekly diaries or monthly semi-structured interviews to capture experiences of friction, perceived belonging, and changes in social capital

Design considerations and justifications
- Why a friction-budget design? The MIT four-week study shows that greater AI usage correlates with worse psychosocial outcomes in aggregate; a budget could reduce AI exposure and potentially preserve human social practice and belonging, provided we pair it with human social engagement nudges (Fang et al., 2025).
- Why include a human-prompt arm? Qualitative and quantitative evidence suggests that human social engagement is central to belonging; friction budgets should not simply minimize AI use but reallocate time toward human interactions and meaningful social activity (Yuan et al., 2025; Merrill Jr. et al., 2025).
- Why measure usage duration as a mediator? Across multiple studies, daily duration mediates the relationships between AI modality/conversation type and psychosocial outcomes, indicating that duration is a key mechanism linking design choices to belonging and well-being (Fang et al., 2025).
- Moderators to consider: Baseline loneliness, AI attachment, trust in AI, prior exposure to AI companions, context (work, education, home), and demographic factors.

Risks, limitations, and mitigations
- Heterogeneity of effects: Effects may differ markedly across individuals; ensure sufficient sample size for subgroup analyses and pre-registered analyses to limit p-h hacking.
- Ethical concerns: Reduction of AI access could cause distress for some users who rely on AI for coping; provide safe-guarded overrides, crisis resources, and adaptive friction policies.
- Generalizability: Study context (type of AI system, population, and setting) will influence external validity; include diverse participants and settings if possible; document ecological constraints for real-world deployment.
- Measurement challenges: Self-reported measures and usage logs may be noisy; triangulate with qualitative data and, where feasible, passive sensing with consent to improve measurement fidelity.

Actionable insights for authors and practitioners (synthesis)
- Design principle: Use a graded friction-budget approach with a structured shift toward human social engagement, ensuring AI remains a supportive tool rather than a replacement for human relationships.
- Interaction quality matters: Prioritize bounded, personal, goal-directed AI interactions and avoid long open-ended AI dialogue during high-usage periods; combine with explicit prompts to engage in real-world social activity.
- Human-in-the-loop safeguards: Build adaptive friction that triggers human input or social activities when AI-use indicators cross thresholds associated with risk (loneliness, isolation, or AI over-dependence).
- Moderation and customization: Personalize friction budgets to user dispositions (baseline loneliness, AI attachment, trust) and contexts, with channels to adjust caps as needed.
- Governance and ethics: Implement transparent guidelines on data handling, disclosure of AI status, and safety nets for distress or crisis signals; consider governance features to monitor harms and enable remediation.
- Reporting and preregistration: Pre-register hypotheses, analysis plans (mediation/moderation tests), and provide robust effect sizes with confidence intervals; publish null findings to advance understanding of friction budgets.

Quantitative and qualitative integration (evidence-based posture)
- Integrate quantitative findings from Fang et al. (2025) into the design rationale:
  - Higher daily AI usage linked to worse loneliness, more emotional dependence, more problematic usage, and less real-world socialization; duration mediates modality/task effects; personal prompts mitigate some risks vs open-ended prompts (Fang et al., 2025).
  - Text vs voice contrasts and topic type effects should inform a friction-budget design that emphasizes bounded, meaningful AI interactions (personal topics) and robust human-social nudges (Fang et al., 2025).
- Leverage broader evidence on trust, attachment, and AI-sociocultural effects:
  - Trust in automation and how explanations or transparency influence reliance (Dzindolet et al., 2003; Klingbeil et al., 2024) provides a basis for including transparency and explainability components in the friction-budget intervention.
  - AI’s potential as social support offering emotional validation may be leveraged by designing budgets that preserve or optimize high-quality AI interactions while ensuring human social contact remains central (Merrill Jr. et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2025).
  - The role of anthropomorphic agents, recognition, and social-practice concerns underscores the need for governance and cultural sensitivity in the friction-budget design (Peter, Riemer, West, 2025; Jacobs, 2024).

Implementation timeline and milestones (illustrative)
- Phase 0–1 (0–3 months): Protocol development, preregistration, ethics approval, instrument selection, pilot testing of friction-budget interfaces.
- Phase 2 (4–6 months): Recruitment, baseline assessments, randomization, and initiation of 12-week intervention.
- Phase 3 (7–12 weeks): Ongoing friction-budget implementation, weekly monitoring, mid-point assessment at week 6–8, safety monitoring.
- Phase 4 (13–16 weeks): Post-intervention follow-up assessments (optional at week 16) and data cleaning.
- Phase 5 (months 16–20): Data analysis, reporting, publication, and dissemination of design guidelines for friction budgets in AI companions.

Ethics, governance, and safety
- Informed consent with explicit discussion of AI-use limitations, data-logging, privacy protections, and potential psychological risks
- Access to human support resources and crisis protocols
- Transparent disclosure about AI status and the boundaries of AI support
- Data handling: secure storage, de-identification, and strict access controls

Appendix: Key measurement intents and instrumentation options
- Belonging: Multi-item belonging scales; social connectedness scales; sense of community measures
- Loneliness: UCLA Loneliness Scale (short and long forms)
- Real-world socialization: Frequency and quality of in-person or video/social interactions; social activity logs
- AI-use metrics: Prompts per day, total minutes with AI, modality usage, topic type classification (open-ended, personal, non-personal)
- AI-attachment and trust: EHARS or equivalent attachment scales; trust-in-AI scales
- Moderators: Baseline loneliness, baseline AI attachment, baseline trust, digital literacy, demographic covariates
- Mediators: Daily AI duration, proportion of AI-led vs human-led interactions
- Qualitative: Participant diaries and periodic interviews to capture perceived belonging, social practice changes, and contextual factors affecting AI use
