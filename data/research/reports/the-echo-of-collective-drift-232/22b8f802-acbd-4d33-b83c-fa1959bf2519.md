# Dopamine Nutrition Label: Randomized Exposure Study on Transparency about Cognitive Effort, Novelty, and Potential Bias in AI Outputs

Abstract
- This report articulates a comprehensive plan for a randomized exposure study to evaluate a lightweight transparency cue—the Dopamine Nutrition Label (DNL)—which communicates cognitive effort, novelty, and potential bias in AI outputs. Building on a broad evidence base about trust in AI, transparency, and human–AI collaboration, the study aims to assess how such a label influences trust (across cognitive and affective dimensions), perceived authenticity of AI–human collaboration, and willingness to engage with human partners in subsequent tasks. The synthesis indicates that transparency framing can both enhance and undermine trust depending on design, context, and user characteristics; thus, the DNL should be designed as a calibrated, user-centered signal embedded in a broader governance and oversight framework (Park & Yoon, 2025; Zerilli et al., 2022; Schilke & Reimann, 2025). The proposed randomized design integrates multiple transparency modalities and outcome measures to quantify causal effects and inform ethical design.

 Introduction and research question
- Core question: What is the impact of a lightweight Dopamine Nutrition Label—transparency about cognitive effort, novelty, and potential bias in AI outputs—on trust, perceived authenticity, and willingness to engage with human partners? A randomized exposure study.
- Rationale: Transparency about AI cognitive processes and biases can reduce uncertainty and support calibrated trust, while preserving human agency and meaningful human–AI collaboration. Yet, evidence shows that transparency can also reduce trust when it signals illegitimate authority or undermines perceived accountability, particularly in high-involvement contexts (Park & Yoon, 2025; Schilke & Reimann, 2025). A carefully designed, lightweight label could harness transparency benefits while mitigating drawbacks by signaling accountability, data provenance, and decision criteria in a knowledge-centric pipeline model of trust (Park & Yoon, 2025).
- Theoretical anchors:
  - Trust is multidimensional (functional, emotional, and cognitive/trust signals). Transparency and explainability influence trust, but effects are contingent on audience, context, and task demands (Zerilli et al., 2022; Bozkurt & Sharma, 2024).
  - Trust calibration requires aligning user beliefs with AI capabilities, maintaining human agency, and avoiding both over-trust and miscalibrated skepticism (Mayer, Davis, & Schoorman, 1995; Hoff & Bashir, 2015; Lee & See, 2004).
  - Lightweight transparency cues can function as practical signals of governance and accountability, particularly when content is designed around cognitive effort, novelty, and bias without imposing high cognitive load (Ngo, 2025; Glassberg et al., 2025).

Governing evidence and core insights informing design
- Trust calibration and transparency signals:
  - Light-touch transparency can calibrate trust, with effects mediated by perceived reliability, cognitive load, and user attitudes toward AI (Ngo, 2025). Moderate transparency often yields the strongest adoption signals, while excessive transparency can trigger cognitive overload and reduced willingness to engage (Ngo, 2025).
  - A pipeline-model view of trust (transparency signaling knowledge rather than reputational signals) can mitigate negative attitudes toward AI and preserve human agency, especially when issue involvement is high (Park & Yoon, 2025).
- Mixed effects of transparency on trust:
  - Some studies show that disclosure about AI involvement reduces trust in the trustee due to legitimacy concerns or perceived manipulation, while others demonstrate trust gains when transparency is framed as accountability and governance (Schilke & Reimann, 2025).
  - The impact of transparency signals depends on task domain, user experience, and framing; explanations alone are not a panacea for trust, and misalignment with user needs can undermine trust (Zerilli et al., 2022; Bozkurt & Sharma, 2024).
- Practical considerations for a Dopamine Nutrition Label:
  - A DN Label should be lightweight, context-sensitive, and layered, offering an at-a-glance cognitive-effort/novelty/bias signal with optional deeper rationale, ensuring human oversight remains accessible and explicit (Liu, 2021; Leichtmann et al., 2023).
  - Transparency alone is insufficient; governance, data provenance, validation, and user-centered design are essential to sustain trust and meaningful human collaboration (Bozkurt & Sharma, 2024; NIST SP 1270, 2022).

Inline citations: Park & Yoon 2025; Zerilli et al. 2022; Schilke & Reimann 2025; Ngo 2025; Glassberg et al. 2025; Bozkurt & Sharma 2024; NIST SP 1270 2022; Leichtmann et al. 2023; Liu 2021; Leichtmann et al. 2023; Luo et al. 2024 (where cited).

Research questions, hypotheses, and design propositions
- Primary hypotheses (to be tested in a randomized exposure):
  - H1: A Dopamine Nutrition Label (DN Label) will improve trust calibration relative to a no-label control by signaling cognitive effort and bias and by providing accessible uncertainty signals.
  - H2: The DN Label will increase perceived authenticity of AI–human collaboration, compared with no-label or baseline transparency alone.
  - H3: The DN Label will increase willingness to engage with human partners for verification, oversight, or joint decision-making, particularly in high-involvement or high-stakes tasks.
  - H4: The label’s effects will be moderated by user involvement with AI, domain context, and prior attitudes toward AI, with strongest effects in high-involvement contexts.
- Design rationale:
  - The DN Label aligns with evidence that transparent cognition, uncertainty signaling, and explicit bias disclosures can calibrate trust and support human agency (Ngo 2025; Park & Yoon 2025; Zerilli 2022). A multi-arm randomized design allows parsing the additive value of cognitive-effort, novelty, and bias signals, while controlling for confounding variables such as domain, task type, and user expertise.

Proposed study design (three-arm or four-arm randomized exposure)
- Population: Adults with varied AI experience; domains of interest include high-stakes decision support (healthcare, finance) and information evaluation tasks (news verification, decision support).
- Experimental arms (example 3-arm design):
  - Arm 1: Control – AI output with no DN Label or with standard transparency (baseline).
  - Arm 2: DNL-Cognition – AI output with cognitive-effort signals only (e.g., “effort level: medium” and a brief justification).
  - Arm 3: DNL-Cognition+Bias – AI output with cognitive-effort signals plus bias/uncertainty flags and a concise data provenance note.
  - Optional Arm 4: DNL-Full – Cognitive effort + novelty + bias signals plus optional deeper explanations on demand.
- Task design: Randomized exposure to AI outputs across a set of tasks with varying complexity and stakes; after each task, participants rate trust, authenticity, and willingness to engage with human partners. A subset of tasks includes explicit opportunities to escalate to human oversight.
- Measurements:
  - Trust: multi-dimensional measures capturing cognitive trust (confidence in reasoning), emotional trust (perceived benevolence/ethics), and trust towards the collaborating human partner.
  - Perceived authenticity: judgments of genuineness and alignment with human collaboration.
  - Willingness to engage with human partners: intent to query, consult, or escalate to human oversight in future tasks.
  - Calibration indicators: correlation between participants’ trust and AI performance across tasks; proportion of decisions overridden or escalated to humans.
  - Cognitive load: NASA-TLX or similar measures to track burden from label reading and processing.
  - Engagement metrics: time-on-task, number of audit or human oversight actions, rate of subsequent engagement with human partners.
- Analytic plan:
  - Between-arm comparisons using ANOVA or mixed-effects models; regression analyses with interaction terms for domain context and user characteristics.
  - Mediation analyses to examine whether trust and authenticity mediate the effect of the DN Label on willingness to engage with human partners.
  - Moderator analyses for task type (high vs low stakes), domain familiarity, and prior AI experience.
  - Reporting: effect sizes with confidence intervals; Bayesian or frequentist indices as appropriate; preregistered hypotheses.

Outcomes and interpretation
- Expected patterns, informed by the literature:
  - Moderate transparency signals (cognition-focused with optional bias signals) may maximize trust calibration and willingness to engage with human partners, particularly in high-stakes contexts (Ngo, 2025; Park & Yoon, 2025).
  - Excessive transparency signaling cognitive effort and bias without support mechanisms can overload users, reducing trust and willingness to engage with humans (Ngo 2025; Zerilli 2022).
  - Labels that balance cognitive effort with actionable bias disclosures and governance cues can support authentic human collaboration by enabling users to see when human oversight is warranted (Bozkurt & Sharma 2024; Glassberg 2025).
- Cautions:
  - The DN Label could undermine trust if seen as signaling illegitimate AI influence or if it signals inconsistent or unreliable reasoning (Schilke & Reimann 2025).
  - The interface design should avoid cognitive overload and ensure labels map to meaningful, task-relevant signals; user testing with diverse populations is essential (Liu 2021; Leichtmann 2023).

Implementation guidance for authors and designers
- Label content design:
  - Cognitive-effort cue: concise one-line indicator and a brief justification (e.g., “reasoning depth: medium; 3-step justification”).
  - Novelty cue: a simple indicator such as “novel approach used” or “new argument path.”
  - Bias/uncertainty cue: a short note on potential biases and data limitations; link to a deeper explanation on demand.
  - Data provenance: one-line data source cue; optional deeper provenance accessible on demand.
  - Human-in-the-loop cue: an explicit signal that human review or oversight is available or engaged for critical decisions.
- Presentation and UX:
  - Ensure the label is lightweight and scannable; avoid information overload; allow tiered disclosure with a “read more” option.
  - Use color-coding and icons to convey signals quickly; maintain accessibility and readability.
  - Enable user control: allow users to adjust depth of disclosure and to toggle additional explanations on/off.
- Task and domain considerations:
  - Test DN Label across multiple domains and task types to assess generalizability and context sensitivity.
  - Include high-stakes contexts (healthcare/finance) to assess trust calibration and human engagement patterns under risk.
  - Incorporate a human-in-the-loop design where users have an explicit option to consult or defer to a human partner.
- Measurement plan:
  - Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans; report primary and secondary outcomes with effect sizes; use both subjective and objective metrics to capture trust calibration, perceived authenticity, and willingness to engage with humans.
- Ethical and governance considerations:
  - Ensure transparency labels align with policy and ethical guidelines; emphasize governance, accountability, and data provenance; provide channels for user feedback and ongoing updates.
  - Consider fairness and bias disclosures, ensuring labels do not exacerbate inequities or mislead underrepresented groups.

Limitations and caveats
- The literature indicates mixed effects of transparency cues on trust; a DN Label’s effects are likely to be context-dependent and moderated by user attitudes, domain, and task risk (Park & Yoon 2025; Zerilli 2022; Schilke & Reimann 2025).
- There is no universal consensus on the optimal level of transparency; iterative testing and domain-specific adaptation are essential (Ngo 2025; Leichtmann 2023).
- The DN Label’s effectiveness will depend on content fidelity (accuracy of signals), readability, and alignment with user mental models; avoid overclaiming AI capabilities or misrepresenting limitations (Bozkurt & Sharma 2024).

Practical actionable insights for authors
- Ground the DN Label in established transparency and explainability frameworks; ensure content corresponds to COP-12 dimensions (Content, Presentation, User-Focused) to maximize interpretability and usefulness (Kaminsky et al.; Ramachandram et al., 2025; COP-12).
- Use a modular design allowing adaptation to different domains and user cohorts; test with non-domain experts to ensure wide usability and avoid bias toward expert populations (Zerilli 2022; Leichtmann 2023).
- Integrate DN Label into governance and oversight practices; ensure labeling is complemented by data provenance disclosures, model evaluation updates, and explicit pathways for human review and accountability (NIST SP 1270; Bozkurt & Sharma 2024).
- Document and report the label’s content mapping to theoretical constructs (cognitive effort, novelty, bias) with transparent measurement plans and preregistration to enable replication and cross-study syntheses.

Discussion: implications for ethics and human agency
- Transparency is a necessary but not sufficient condition for trust; labels must be embedded in governance structures, human oversight, and ongoing dialog with stakeholders to maintain ethical design and authentic human–AI collaboration (Bozkurt & Sharma 2024; NIST SP 1270 2022; Park & Yoon 2025).
- The ultimate aim is to preserve human agency, ensure accountability, and promote responsible AI adoption, especially in high-stakes domains where miscalibration can have material consequences (Schilke & Reimann 2025; Nouis et al. 2025; FUTURE-AI 2025).

Limitations of the evidence base and future directions
- While multiple studies show that transparency and explanatory cues shape trust, the literature also reveals robust contextual dependencies and potential trade-offs (e.g., increased workload, complacency, or trust penalties). A dedicated randomized exposure study of a concrete DN Label is needed to quantify causal effects and identify contextual moderators (Ngo 2025; Park & Yoon 2025; Schilke & Reimann 2025).
- Future work should compare multiple transparency modalities (DN Label vs standard XAI explanations vs external certifications) across domains and populations to illuminate optimal design configurations for trust calibration and human engagement (Cetinkaya & Krämer 2025; Ramachandram et al. 2025).

Conclusion
- A lightweight Dopamine Nutrition Label—signaling cognitive effort, novelty, and potential bias in AI outputs—has the potential to improve trust calibration and preserve human agency in human–AI collaboration, provided it is designed with domain sensitivity, user-centered framing, and governance integration. The evidence base suggests that transparency must be carefully tailored to audience, context, and task demands; broad adoption requires iterative testing, clear measurement, and alignment with regulatory and ethical frameworks (Park & Yoon 2025; Zerilli 2022; Bozkurt & Sharma 2024; Ngo 2025; Glassberg 2025; NIST SP 1270 2022).
