# report.md

Impact of Embedding Active Recall and Reflective Prompts in AI‑Enabled Learning on Depth of Understanding, Transfer to Novel Domains, and Calibration Accuracy

Executive summary
- Across multiple literatures, embedding active recall (retrieval practice) and reflective prompts within AI-enabled learning consistently shows potential for deeper processing, improved metacognition, and enhanced learning outcomes, but effects on transfer and calibration are varied and context-dependent.
- Quantitative syntheses and experiments provide a mixed but generally positive picture for depth-related outcomes when prompts are well-designed and contextually guided:
  - Systematic reviews/meta-analyses: prompts with action-based or learner-group tailoring effects yield substantively large effect sizes on learning achievement, with moderated robustness by domain, region, and prompt features (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
  - Experimental AI-enabled studies in higher education show substantial gains in knowledge retention and depth when retrieval practice is implemented via AI prompts, particularly when combined with reflective tasks and instructor scaffolds (An et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025; Garg et al., 2025).
  - Domain-transfer and calibration outcomes are less consistently demonstrated across studies; several lines of evidence point to transfer and calibration as promising but not uniformly quantified outcomes in AI‑assisted learning (Lin et al., 2025; Wu & Looi, 2010; Liebenow et al., 2025; Sajja et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025).
- The strongest, replicated implications relate to design features: action-oriented prompts, prompts tailored to learner groups, hybrid generic+directed prompting, and log-data–driven personalization. Cautions include variability by domain/context and the need for explicit measurements of depth, transfer, and calibration in future studies (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Lin et al., 2025; An et al., 2025; Garg et al., 2025; Wu & Looi, 2010; Liebenow et al., 2025).
- Actionable insights for authors: prioritize action-based, tailored, and hybrid prompt strategies; implement reflective components; leverage log data for personalization; pilot in target contexts; quantify depth, transfer, and calibration with objective measures.

1) Introduction: constructs, definitions, and scope
- Active recall (retrieval practice) and reflective prompts are central to many AI-enabled learning designs. They are touted as vehicles to promote deeper encoding, better knowledge organization, and more robust judgment under AI-assisted instruction (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Lin et al., 2025; Wu & Looi, 2010).
- Depth of understanding refers to the level at which learners articulate, justify, and apply principles, beyond surface memorization; transfer refers to applying learned knowledge to novel domains; calibration accuracy (metacognitive calibration) concerns alignment between learners’ confidence or self-assessments and actual performance.
- The literature comprises meta-analytic syntheses of prompts, controlled AI-assisted experiments in higher education, domain-transfer studies, and calibration-focused work. While there is convergent support for retrieval-based and reflective practices, direct, uniform evidence for depth, transfer, and calibration across AI-enabled learning remains context-specific and sometimes indirect (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Lin et al., 2025; An et al., 2025; Liebenow et al., 2025; Sajja et al., 2025; Wu & Looi, 2010; Vogt et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025).
- Key design guidance appears consistently: prompts should be concise, tailored to expertise, trigger knowledge application, support knowledge transfer, and be complemented by log-data for personalization; alignment with Bloom’s taxonomy and metacognitive targets is repeatedly recommended (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Lin et al., 2025; Wu & Looi, 2010; Vogt et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025).

2) Evidence overview by outcome domain

2.1 Depth of understanding (cognitive depth, explanatory quality, and reasoning)
- Meta-analytic/systematic evidence:
  - Thoman & Deutscher (2025) find action-based prompts yield the largest effects on learning achievement (d = 0.465 unadjusted; larger effects for tailored learner groups, d = 0.513; combined generic+directed prompts, d = 0.571). These effects are indicators of deeper processing when prompts are well-mitted to learner needs and task demands; however, the synthesis does not isolate “depth of understanding” as a direct outcome across all studies. Contextual moderators (region, domain) further shape effects.
  - The broader inference is that prompts designed to elicit active engagement and higher-order processing (e.g., generic+directed, group-tailored) tend to drive deeper processing, but direct depth-of-understanding metrics are not uniformly reported (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Experimental AI-enabled learning contexts:
  - An et al. (2025) report that AI-generated retrieval practice improves knowledge retention in data science courses (89% vs 73% quiz accuracy) and that open-ended prompts can enable exploration at higher cognitive levels, though depth was inferred rather than directly measured with a standardized “depth” index (An et al., 2025).
  - Garg et al. (2025) show that prompt training (few-shot, chain prompts, and CLEAR framework) yields higher post-test scores across Bloom’s taxonomy levels (G3: 6.60 vs G2: 4.94 and Control: 4.28). This indicates deeper engagement and problem-solving capability when structured prompting is deployed in AI-assisted learning (Garg et al., 2025).
  - Vogt et al. (2021) found elaboration prompts in VR-enhanced learning improved knowledge-level outcomes and influenced the depth and structure of mental models, particularly for learners with lower initial accuracy (Vogt et al., 2021). Depth improvements were domain-sensitive (knowledge vs comprehension vs application).
- Qualitative/think-aloud style evidence:
  - Wu & Looi (2010) underscore that reflective prompts foster metacognition and self-explanation; the design aim is to deepen understanding through reflection, though quantitative depth metrics are not provided in their abstract.

2.2 Transfer to novel domains
- Domain-transfer signals from AI-enabled prompts:
  - Lin et al. (2025) demonstrate domain adaptation benefits, with Active Reading prompting improvements in SimpleWikiQA (66% accuracy from 16% baseline) and FinanceBench (26%), and a notable transfer signal to finance domain (+16% absolute, +160% relative). They also show pre-training benefits (WikiExpert-8B, 1T tokens) that support fact recall and cross-domain applicability. This provides evidence that strategy-driven prompts can facilitate domain transfer when extended to new domains and larger data scales (Lin et al., 2025).
  - Kranz et al. (2024) show that retrieval practice for higher-order skills (CV strategy) yields transfer-like results in assessments of science reasoning, but in a domain with young learners, retrieval practice did not outperform restudy for a high-level skill transfer. This points to domain- and task-specific transfer variability (Kranz et al., 2024).
  - An additional strand (Ma et al., 2025 in GenAI literacy) reports reflective-game modalities that surface biases and cross-domain prompting implications; transfer to novel domains is discussed conceptually, but direct empirical transfer metrics are limited in the reported excerpt (Ma et al., 2025).
- Domain heterogeneity and caution:
  - Domain differences in AI feedback quality (Kay et al., 2025) show STEM domains rewarding procedural accuracy and humanities domains delivering richer analytic depth, implying that domain characteristics strongly condition transfer potential when embedding prompts. This suggests that cross-domain transfer is feasible but requires domain-tailored prompt strategies and calibration (Kay et al., 2025).

2.3 Calibration accuracy (metacognition, confidence, and calibration)
- Direct calibration measurements are relatively sparse in the retrieved literature; several works discuss calibrating learning with AI but provide limited explicit calibration metrics:
  - Liebenow et al. (2025) find that LLM-generated feedback did not improve self-assessment accuracy (SAA) on average; however, improvements occurred for learners with low baseline SAA, indicating selective calibration gains and the potential for adaptive, baseline-informed prompting to improve calibration (Liebenow et al., 2025).
  - Sajja et al. (2025) describe AI-enabled learning analytics and a dual-layer approach to decision support but do not present calibration metrics for depth/transfer outcomes; they emphasize reliability, explainability, and guardrails as important for calibration-like trust in AI guidance (Sajja, Sermet, & Demir, 2025).
  - CalibRAG (Calibrated Retrieval-Augmented Generation) argues for calibration-focused retrieval in AI decision contexts; while not a learning depth study, it provides methods (ECCEs vs. cumulative calibration errors) for evaluating calibration in probabilistic AI outputs. The direct application to learning contexts is an open area (CalibRAG, arXiv:2411.08891).
  - Wu & Looi (2010) emphasize metacognition and calibration through reflective prompts, but direct calibration metrics are not reported in their study.
- Additional calibration-focused work in AI-assisted learning:
  - An emerging line of work (self-assessment calibration in AI feedback) indicates that adaptive, targeted feedback may help calibrate learners’ confidence, especially for learners with lower initial calibration, but universal improvements are not guaranteed (Liebenow et al., 2025).

3) Moderators and contextual factors
- Domain and subject matter
  - Domain differences influence both depth and transfer: STEM domains tend to favor accuracy and procedural mastery, while humanities domains tend to favor interpretive depth and transfer to broader reasoning; effective prompting should reflect domain-specific affordances (Kay et al., 2025).
- Learner characteristics
  - Baseline expertise and prior experience with AI strongly shape outcomes. Several studies show log-data–driven personalization and learner segmentation improve gains for targeted groups (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Lin et al., 2025; Liebenow et al., 2025).
- Regional and cultural context
  - Regional differences exist in meta-analytic findings; East Asia studies showed larger effects in some cases, suggesting cultural/educational context as a moderator of effect sizes (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Technology and implementation
  - The fidelity of AI prompts, prompt quality, and the presence of human-in-the-loop supervision are repeatedly highlighted as critical determinants of outcomes; poor-quality prompts or absence of guardrails can undermine benefits (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Garg et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2025).

4) Design implications and best-practice recommendations

4.1 Prompt design and prompting regimes
- Favor action-based prompts and mixed generic+directed prompts to maximize learning gains and depth (d ≈ 0.46–0.57 range in meta-analytic syntheses) (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Tailor prompts to learner groups; segment learners by prior knowledge, proficiency, or demographics and customize prompts accordingly (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Use a hybrid prompting strategy: combine generic prompts targeting metacognition with directed prompts that push domain-specific reasoning and problem-solving; aim for total prompt diversity to sustain robust knowledge use (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).

4.2 Design of AI-enabled learning environments
- Implement log-data–driven personalization to tailor prompts to expertise and reduce extraneous cognitive load; ensure prompts are concise and contextually grounded to minimize cognitive overhead (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Trigger prompts contingent on learner progress and events (progress-based triggering) to maintain desirable cognitive load and ensure prompts are relevant to the current knowledge state (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025).
- Integrate reflective prompts as a core mechanism to promote metacognition and calibration; pair reflective prompts with retrieval prompts to create a cohesive retrieval-reflection loop (Wu & Looi, 2010; Lin et al., 2025; Garg et al., 2025).

4.3 Measurement and evaluation
- Include objective metrics for depth (conceptual explanations, justification quality), transfer (novel-domain performance), and calibration (confidence–accuracy alignment) alongside process metrics (log-data).
- Use both traditional measures (pre/post tests, transfer tasks) and calibration-specific metrics (calibration curves, ECCE/MAD, ECCE/R) when feasible (Liebenow et al., 2025; Arrieta-Ibarra et al., 2022).
- Report effect sizes and heterogeneity across contexts to enable cross-study synthesis (Thomann & Deutscher, 2025; Garg et al., 2025).

4.4 Domain considerations and transfer
- For cross-domain transfer goals, design domain-adapted prompts and cross-domain practice tasks; test transfer with explicit novel-domain prompts and assessments (Lin et al., 2025; Kranz et al., 2024).
- When evaluating transfer, ensure tasks truly require applying prior knowledge in new contexts, not merely repeating surface features of trained tasks (Kranz et al., 2024).

4.5 Ethics, equity, and trust
- Continue to address privacy, bias, and data governance; ensure guardrails, human oversight, and explainable AI mechanisms are embedded in AI-enabled prompts (Shen et al., 2025; Masood, 2025; Ali et al., 2024).
- Build trust through transparent prompts and provenance for AI-generated guidance; consider calibration-focused reporting for outputs and confidence (CalibRAG, arXiv:2411.08891; Liebenow et al., 2025).

5) Limitations and gaps in the current evidence base
- Many studies report depth and engagement proxies rather than standardized, cross-domain depth metrics; direct cross-domain transfer metrics are sparse and often domain-specific.
- Calibration accuracy is less frequently measured with rigorous, standardized metrics in AI-enabled learning, though several lines of evidence point to potential calibration benefits for low-calibration learners and the need for baseline-adjusted analyses.
- The majority of evidence is context- and domain-specific, with heterogeneity in AI systems, prompts, and learner populations; generalizable conclusions require more standardized multi-site experiments and preregistered protocols.

6) Concrete, actionable recommendations for authors and practitioners
- Start with a principled prompting framework:
  - Use the RTRI structure (Role, Task, Requirements, Interaction) or equivalent, ensuring that prompts guide retrieval and reflective processing while making explicit the cognitive level targeted (e.g., Bloom’s levels).
- Implement a staged prompting plan:
  - Phase 1: Retrieval-practice prompts to elicit recall of core concepts.
  - Phase 2: Reflective prompts to justify reasoning, evaluate approaches, and identify gaps.
  - Phase 3: Transfer prompts that require applying concepts to near-domain and then novel-domain contexts.
- Use log-data to drive personalization:
  - Tailor prompts by proficiency, progress, and signaled knowledge gaps; adjust prompt difficulty and density to maintain desirable cognitive load.
- Incorporate calibration checks:
  - Collect confidence judgments after prompts, compare against actual outcomes, and provide calibration-focused feedback or prompts to improve metacognitive accuracy.
- Apply robust evaluation designs:
  - Use randomized or quasi-experimental designs across domains; include pre/post assessments, transfer tasks, and calibration metrics; report effect sizes with CIs and heterogeneity analyses.
- Ensure domain-specific adaptation:
  - Align prompting strategies to domain affordances (e.g., procedural mathematics vs. interpretive humanities) to maximize depth and transfer while preserving calibration.
- Plan for ethical and safety considerations:
  - Maintain privacy controls, minimize data leakage, and ensure transparent AI explanations and source citations where relevant.
