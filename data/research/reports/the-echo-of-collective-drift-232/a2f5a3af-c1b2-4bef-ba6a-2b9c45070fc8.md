# report.md

Platform Incentives, Engagement, and Flourishing: A Comprehensive Report on Long-Run Relationship Quality, Resilience, and Nudges for Flourishing Use

Executive summary
- Engaging platforms that optimize for short-run metrics can undermine long-run relationship quality and resilience if flourishing goals (trust, meaningful interaction, character formation) are neglected. A macro-level perspective treats engagement incentives as potentially misaligned with welfare and resilience unless governance and design steer outcomes toward flourishing [Brantley et al., 2023a; World Bank 2015; Chan et al., 2020]. 
- Evidence across domains indicates that nudges and design constraints can shift outcomes toward flourishing by (a) reframing incentives toward welfare-aligned goals, (b) increasing transparency and accountability, and (c) constraining short-horizon gamification or manipulation. Notable findings include: macro-control frameworks that balance long-run welfare with engagement; accuracy nudges improving content-signal discernment; and in-feed diagrams enhancing harm-detection capability with persisting effects after prompt removal (TRAC-2024; Pennycook & Rand; Celadin et al.) [Brantley et al., 2023a; Pennycook & Rand, 2022; Celadin et al., 2024].
- A maturity-based pathway for AI-assisted financial/advisory tools shows how tools can evolve from basic calculators to more capable planners, with welfare metrics (not engagement metrics) guiding governance and accountability (Feng et al., 2025). A fiduciary AI framework provides a principled guardrail: loyalty to users (best interests) and care (robustness) can constrain perverse incentives and align technology design with flourishing outcomes (Boine 2023; Benthall & Shekman 2023).
- Actionable design principles emphasize: formal fiduciary alignment; adaptive personalization anchored in welfare objectives; transparent risk/algorithmic explanations; explicit fairness constraints; independent audits; macro-level exposure objectives; planning-based controls for non-stationary dynamics; and governance mechanisms that respond to emerging risks (Feng et al., 2025; Brantley et al., 2023a; Arrieta et al., 2020; Challoumis et al., 2024) [Five-Level Maturity; Ranking with Long-Term Constraints; Arrieta et al.; Challoumis et al.].

1) Introduction: the problem, scope, and research question
- Core question: How do platform incentive structures that optimize engagement over flourishing shape long-run relationship quality and resilience, and can nudges or design constraints shift outcomes toward more flourishing use?
- Why it matters: macro-level incentives (e.g., engagement maximization) can distort information flows, risk-communication, and relationship quality in financial, social, and information ecosystems. Without design levers that tie incentives to welfare and resilience, platforms may scale reach but degrade legitimacy and long-run welfare [Akerlof 1978; Coase 1993; Akerlof & Stiglitz-influenced literature; Brantley et al., 2023a; World Bank 2015].
- What “flourishing” means in this context: flourishing encompasses long-run relationship quality, resilience, trust, pro-social development, and character formation, as captured in normative frameworks and longitudinal studies (e.g., multi-domain flourishing indices, societal well-being measures) [Global Flourishing Study; VanderWeele et al.; IPBES/ Meadows leverage points; Brantley et al., 2023a; VanderWeele et al., 2025].

2) Conceptual framework: macro incentives, micro actions, and the design levers that shift toward flourishing
- Macro-level incentives and long-run welfare
  - Engagement-centric incentives can produce information asymmetries and systemic fragility unless welfare-aligned targets are embedded in governance and design (e.g., fiduciary duty, adaptive governance) [Brantley et al., 2023a; Boine, 2023; Benthall & Shekman, 2023].
  - A macroscopic control framework maps long-horizon welfare targets to per-request actions, using model-predictive/forward-looking controls to balance macro goals (e.g., welfare exposure targets) with micro-level engagement utility (e.g., ranking quality) [Brantley et al., 2023a].
- Five design levers for flourishing
  - Exposure targets, diversification/novelty, and external regulations can re-anchor platform incentives toward flourishing outcomes (trust, resilience, diverse ecosystems) while preserving some short-run engagement [Brantley et al., 2023a].
  - Nudges that emphasize welfare (fiduciary alignment, transparent risk framing) and constraints that limit risky engagement (e.g., gamification that inflates herd-like behavior) can mitigate fragility and enhance long-run relationship quality [Tierney 2022; Gregersen & Ørmen 2024; Pennycook & Rand 2022; Donabauer et al. 2024].
- Normative frameworks that translate into design
  - Five foundational principles for responsible AI-driven financial planning: fiduciary duty, adaptive personalization, technical robustness, ethical/fairness constraints, and auditability; mapped to welfare-oriented outcomes rather than engagement or revenue metrics alone [Feng et al., 2025; Arrieta et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2024; Challoumis et al., 2024; Inavolu, 2024].
  - A maturity roadmap from calculator-style tools to anticipatory AI (Level 1–5), with Level-specific governance and welfare metrics; importance of linking maturity to welfare, not engagement alone [Feng et al., 2025].

3) Evidence: what we know about engagement incentives, flourishing, and nudges
3.1 Case studies and mechanisms of perverse incentives
- Robinhood and gamified interfaces/PFOF: design elements that incentivize engagement or trading volume can distort risk communication and create conflicts of interest, threatening client welfare and long-run credibility (Tierney 2022) [Tierney, 2022].
- eToro: opaque rankings and copy-trading platforms can foster herd behavior, obscuring risk signals and enabling systemic fragility (Gregersen & Ørmen 2024) [Gregersen & Ørmen, 2024].
- AI-based advisory contexts: AI-enabled platforms lower barriers but risk magnifying information asymmetry and incentive misalignment if fiduciary alignment, fairness, robustness, and auditability are not embedded (Feng et al. 2025; Arrieta et al. 2020; Zhu et al. 2024; Challoumis et al. 2024; Inavolu 2024).
- Takeaway: efficiency and scale help reach more users, but without governance to guide incentives toward welfare, resilience, and meaningful interaction, reputational risk and systemic fragility rise.

3.2 Nudges and design constraints toward flourishing
- Pennycook & Rand (2022): accuracy nudges (prompts to assess veracity) alter decision processes by reallocating attention to accuracy without changing underlying preferences; these nudges reduce the sharing of false content when prompts are present, with rapid deployability and no need to pre-know content accuracy. Limitations: durability and cross-cultural generalizability need more study (Pennycook & Rand, 2022; Pennycook et al., 2021; Epstein et al., 2021) [Pennycook & Rand, 2022; Pennycook et al., 2021; Epstein et al., 2021].
- Celadin et al. (2024): seven norms-based nudges in a social-media-like setting; outcomes include increased engagement with harmless content and improved quality of harmful-content discourse when reputation-focused cues are used; null effects on overall engagement with harmful content; N=4,081; 2-week lab-like RCT in a controlled setting. Implication: norm-based nudges can shift engagement toward flourishing content and civil discourse, though effects are nuanced and domain-specific (Celadin et al., 2024).
- Donabauer et al. (2024, TRAC-2024): unobtrusive, diagram-based warnings integrated into feeds improved users’ ability to detect misinformation/hate-speech; effects persisted even when diagrams were no longer visible, indicating potential for lasting improvements in discernment without heavy moderation [Donabauer et al., 2024].
- Zeng et al. (2022): social nudges increase content generation and diffusion; network effects matter; nudges that increase creator diffusion can raise supply without sacrificing content quality in the short run; long-run effects on flourishing remain unmeasured (Zeng et al., 2022).
- The World Bank Mind, Society, and Behavior (2015): a broad, cross-domain evidence base showing defaults, reminders, social cues, framing, and adaptive design yield meaningful welfare effects; emphasize iterative, diagnostic, adaptive design to sustain welfare gains over time (World Bank 2015; Box 2.5; 6.2; 6.3; 8.3; 11.1–11.2).

3.3 Maturity pathways and fiduciary design
- Feng et al. (2025): Five-level AI-financial planning maturity model; practical pathway to elevate design maturity and tie it to welfare metrics; Level alignment with welfare (not just engagement) is critical.
- Boine (2023); Benthall & Shekman (2023): fiduciary AI frameworks for loyalty and care; governance and auditability as core design features; data governance and consent frameworks (GDPR/DGA) are relevant anchors; design nudges/constraints should promote flourishing outcomes rather than purely engagement gains.

3.4 Flourishing measurement and long-run outcomes
- VanderWeele et al. (2025): Global Flourishing Study (GFS) – longitudinal, cross-country, 22 countries; six domains of flourishing (including close social relationships and meaning/purpose); open data; outcome-wide analysis; five waves across five years; first wave reveals cross-country differences and age/demographic patterns; indicates potential to link platform design with flourishing outcomes through longitudinal measurement.
- Chen et al. (2022): six-domain flourishing index among employees; baseline domains (meaning/purpose, social connectedness, financial security) show strong associations with follow-up composite flourishing; provides a framework for multi-domain welfare measurement that can be adapted to platform-design contexts.
- General implication: flourishing is multi-dimensional; platform success metrics must be expanded beyond engagement to include relationship quality, trust, meaning, and resilience.

4) Normative framework: design levers to shift toward flourishing
4.1 Fiduciary framing and loyalty/care
- A fiduciary AI design frame argues that platform operators have duties of loyalty (align with users’ best interests) and care (robustness, safety) toward users, with implicit constraints on engagement-centric optimization if it harms welfare [Boine 2023; Benthall & Shekman 2023].
- Sub-duties (prudent investor rule, impartiality) provide concrete governance tools for risk, fairness, and multi-principal contexts; data governance and consent regimes (GDPR/DGA) function as regulatory anchors for loyalty and care in AI platforms [Boine 2023; Benthall & Shekman 2023].

4.2 Five principles for responsible AI-driven financial planning (as a normative blueprint for platform design)
- Principle One: Fiduciary Duty — align advice with client interests; disclose conflicts of interest and minimize self-serving design choices [Irving; Chieffe & Rakes 1999; Boine 2023].
- Principle Two: Adaptive Personalization — keep advice relevant over time and welfare-centered, not solely engagement-driven [Belanche et al., 2019; Adam et al., 2019; Morana et al., 2020].
- Principle Three: Technical Robustness and Resilience — stability under stress; system must tolerate adverse conditions and avoid cascading failures [Arrieta et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2025; Oelschlager 2024].
- Principle Four: Ethical/Fairness Constraints — ensure equity in automated advice and avoid systemic biases [Zhu et al., 2024; Inavolu 2024; Challoumis et al., 2024].
- Principle Five: Auditability and Accountability — transparent oversight of algorithmic advice; independent audits recommended [Arrieta et al., 2020; Challoumis et al., 2024].

4.3 Nudges and design constraints as levers toward flourishing
- Nudges: prompts that direct attention to accuracy, welfare, and long-horizon outcomes; design should preserve autonomy, avoid paternalism creep, and be complemented by education and explainability (Pennycook & Rand 2022; Donabauer et al. 2024; Celadin et al. 2024).
- Design constraints: governance rules and platform restrictions to limit short-horizon optimization; enforce fairness, transparency, and robust risk management; integrate with external regulatory frameworks (GDPR/DGA) and public-interest norms (CPD-like oversight). 
- Self-nudging (user-empowered): users design their own decision environments to improve self-control and goal attainment; recommended as a way to scale welfare-aligned choices with autonomy (Reijula & Hertwig 2020).

4.4 Leveraging leverage points and multi-actor governance
- IPBES leverage points and the eight leverage points provide a lens for deep system change: unleashing values and action as a high-potential lever; governance levers (A–E: incentives, coordination, pre-emptive action, adaptive decision-making, environmental law) can reorient platform ecosystems toward flourishing outcomes [IPBES SPM; Horcea-Milcu 2022; Chan et al., 2020].
- Multiactor governance: platform ecosystems intersect with civil society, regulators, and users; alignment requires cross-sector coordination and shared norms to sustain long-run flourishing [Chan et al., 2024a; 2024b; 2025].

5) Quantitative anchors and evidence gaps
- The literature shows robust, broad evidence for nudges affecting behavior; however, effect sizes vary by context, and long-run flourishing outcomes remain under-explored in cross-domain platform contexts (DellaVigna & Linos 2022; Hummel & Maedche 2019; Pennycook & Rand 2022; Celadin et al. 2024; Donabauer et al. 2024).
- Where numerical effects exist (e.g., Donabauer et al. 2024; van Mierlo et al. 2024), they generally show modest short-run engagement shifts or perceptual changes with potential for longer-term welfare improvements, but causal links to flourishing metrics require longer, well-designed longitudinal studies.
- GFS and Chen et al. provide rigorous measures for flourishing across domains and ages; these can serve as welfare metrics for platform interventions, but direct causal mapping to platform design needs dedicated field experiments.

6) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Embed welfare-centric metrics into design/testing
  - Define explicit flourishing outcomes (trust, relational quality, meaning, resilience) alongside engagement metrics; use a multi-domain flourishing index when feasible.
- Adopt macro-level exposure constraints and planning controls
  - Use exposure targets (e.g., maintain diversity, limit harmful herd copying) and model-predictive planning to handle non-stationary dynamics; allow governance to adapt to distributional shifts in users and content ecosystems [Brantley et al., 2023a].
- Implement fiduciary-aligned governance
  - Integrate loyalty (best interests) and care (robustness) into design reviews, product roadmaps, and auditing; ensure transparency about optimization targets and any conflicts of interest; adopt a risk-managed data governance posture in line with GDPR/DGA.
- Enable diffusion-aware nudges and self-nudging
  - Combine nudges with diffusion-aware strategies (e.g., social norms, reputation signals, cross-community exposure) to promote flourishing content and interaction patterns (Zeng et al. 2022; Celadin et al. 2024).
- Governance and regulatory alignment
  - Leverage DMA, FCA Consumer Duty, GDPR/DGA, and data-intermediary fiduciary concepts to build enforceable standards for welfare-aligned platform design; push for independent audits, transparency, and accountability mechanisms.
- Experimental design and open science
  - Prioritize longitudinal, cross-domain randomized experiments; report effect sizes, confidence intervals, and long-run flourishing outcomes; preregister studies; publish open data where possible to enable replication and synthesis.

7) Practical design blueprint: integrated path to flourishing
- Step 1: Scope and principal identification
  - Define the platform context and identify the principal(s) (users, providers, content ecosystems) whose flourishing is the design objective.
- Step 2: Best-interests assessment
  - Determine welfare metrics (trust, resilience, meaning, relationship quality) and the best-interests objective function, possibly via data-driven representations and stakeholder input.
- Step 3: Aggregation and impartiality
  - Develop rules for balancing competing interests (e.g., different user cohorts) with fairness constraints and independence from platform profit incentives.
- Step 4: Loyalty and care in design
  - Implement design constraints that align with best interests (avoid misaligned optimization) and institute robust safety/robustness features; embed privacy-by-design and explainability.
- Step 5: Auditability and oversight
  - Establish ongoing internal/external audits; publish independent assessments; build transparency dashboards that show how platforms balance macro welfare targets with micro-engagement metrics.
- Step 6: Macro-to-micro translation
  - Use model-predictive controls and exposure planning to translate macro welfare targets into per-interaction decisions (ranking, recommendations) with soft constraints and fallback options.

8) Implications for policy and practice
- A portfolio approach to interventions:
  - Combine macro-level exposure targets with micro-level ranking controls; use planning-based controls to handle non-stationary content ecosystems.
  - Pair nudges with design constraints and governance to mitigate reward hacking and ensure accountability.
- Cross-domain experimentation:
  - Validate flourishing outcomes in domains such as social networks, finance/advisory platforms, and information ecosystems; incorporate cross-cultural variation and demographic subgroups to ensure equitable flourishing.
- Transparency and legitimacy:
  - Foster trust through transparency about optimization targets, data practices, and governance processes; engage independent researchers and civil-society actors in oversight.

9) Limitations and cautions
- The evidence base for long-run flourishing outcomes linked directly to platform nudges is still growing; reported effect sizes are context-specific and often short-term; more cross-domain longitudinal experiments are required.
- Caution regarding the risk of unintended consequences (e.g., over-prescribing friction, paternalism concerns) if nudges are not paired with adequate safeguards and user autonomy protections.

10) Conclusion
- There is a compelling theoretical and empirical case for reimagining platform incentive structures away from sole engagement optimization toward flourishing-oriented outcomes. Nudges and design constraints show promise, but robust, cross-domain, longitudinal evidence is needed to quantify causal effects on long-run relationship quality and resilience.
- The synthesis presented here provides a pragmatic, multi-layered blueprint: adopt fiduciary principles, implement macro-level planning controls, pursue transparency and accountability, deploy diffusion-aware nudges, and evaluate welfare outcomes with rigorous experimental designs. This integrated approach offers the most robust path to shift platform outcomes toward flourishing use.
