# report.md

Does ongoing use of AI companions homogenize language and taste across diverse users?
An evidence-based synthesis with actionable guidance

Executive summary
- A broad synthesis of evidence from sociolinguistics, HCI, AI-mediated communication, and related domains indicates plausible mechanisms by which AI companions could influence how people speak and what they discuss. Key mechanisms include acoustic-prosodic entrainment, lexical accommodation, and socioindexical signaling via engineered AI voices, all of which can steer language patterns over time (Székely et al., 2025; Parent & Eskenazi, 2010; Ostrand et al., 2023; Oviatt et al., 2004; Tsfasman et al., 2021; Horstmann et al., 2024) [Székely et al., 2025; Parent & Eskenazi, 2010; Ostrand et al., 2023; Oviatt et al., 2004; Tsfasman et al., 2021; Horstmann et al., 2024].
- Within-dyad convergence (user ↔ AI) is documented in several contexts, yet robust cross-user, cross-domain, longitudinal quantitative data linking AI companionship to reductions in novelty dispersion across topics, vocabulary, and tastes are scarce in the sources reviewed (Wilkenfeld et al., 2022; Chaves & Gerosa, 2022; Reviriego et al., 2024) [Wilkenfeld et al., 2022; Chaves & Gerosa, 2022; Reviriego et al., 2024].
- Some recent work challenges a blanket homogenization claim: model diversity, diversified AI personas, and advanced language models can sustain or even increase lexical and topical variety under certain designs (Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024) [Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024].
- The evidence base remains heterogeneous and context-dependent, with many studies reporting qualitative or task-specific effects rather than population-wide dispersion metrics. There is a clear need for longitudinal, cross-cultural, multi-metric studies to quantify novelty dispersion trajectories under AI companionship (Székely et al., 2025; Gries, 2022; Kalman & Wan, 2025) [Székely et al., 2025; Gries, 2022; Kalman & Wan, 2025].

Key mechanisms and empirical anchors
- Acoustic-prosodic entrainment and lexical accommodation
  - Users adjust lexical choices, speech rate, pitch, and prosody to align with AI interlocutors; alignment strength can depend on perceived politeness and humanness (Horstmann et al., 2024) [Horstmann et al., 2024].
  - In human–machine interactions, convergence on agent vocabulary and prosody has been observed across domains (Parent & Eskenazi, 2010; Ostrand et al., 2023; Oviatt et al., 2004; Tsfasman et al., 2021; Cohn et al., 2023) [Parent & Eskenazi, 2010; Ostrand et al., 2023; Oviatt et al., 2004; Tsfasman et al., 2021; Cohn et al., 2023].
- Socioindexical signals via AI voice design
  - Synthetic voices convey social identity cues (accent, intonation, style) that can influence user signaling and alignment in everyday speech; engineered socioindexical traits can steer language change trajectories (Székely et al., 2025) [Székely et al., 2025].
- Long-term accommodation and identity signaling
  - Accommodation can cascade into longer-term language change and diffusion of AI-influenced variants, especially when AI voice prestige is perceived as high (Hinskens & Auer, 2005; Nguyen & Delvaux, 2015; Lee, 2010; Lev-Ari & Peperkamp, 2014; Brandt & Hazel, 2025; Pollmann et al., 2023) [Hinskens & Auer 2005; Nguyen & Delvaux 2015; Lee 2010; Lev-Ari & Peperkamp 2014; Brandt & Hazel 2025; Pollmann et al. 2023].
- Media diffusion and engineered change
  - Media exposure diffuses linguistic features; AI voices could diffuse AI-influenced speech patterns through highly interactive media, with potential diffusion beyond immediate AI contexts (Tagliamonte 2014; Sayers 2014; Kristiansen 2014; Xi 2024; Yakura 2024; Yu 2024) [Tagliamonte 2014; Sayers 2014; Kristiansen 2014; Xi 2024; Yakura 2024; Yu 2024].
- Societal risks and ethics
  - Linguistic profiling and discrimination risks arise when AI voice design encodes stereotypes or biases; this calls for ethical safeguards and transparency in voice design and deployment (Purnell et al., 1999; Gluszek & Dovidio, 2010; Lippi-Green, 2012; Krahé & Papakonstantinou, 2020) [Purnell et al., 1999; Gluszek & Dovidio 2010; Lippi-Green 2012; Krahé & Papakonstantinou 2020].

Quantitative findings and gaps (what is known vs. what is missing)
- Quantitative anchors exist for within-dyad convergence and AI-driven language shaping in specific tasks, but cross-user dispersion metrics across diverse populations and long time horizons are not consistently reported across the reviewed literature:
  - Wilkenfeld et al. (2022): evidence of convergence within user–AI dyads; no standardized cross-user dispersion metrics reported in the study extract (qualitative synthesis) [Wilkenfeld et al., 2022].
  - Kalman & Wan (2025): diversified AI prompts via ten AI personas preserve and even expand collective diversity in a creative task (plot ideation) and yield richer descriptive/emotional language in AI-assisted outputs (N = 300 prompts; 100 participants’ stories) [Kalman & Wan, 2025].
  - Reviriego et al. (2024): model version matters for lexical diversity; GPT-3.5 tends to produce lower lexical diversity than humans, while GPT-4 achieves lexical diversity comparable to or higher than humans in some contexts; results are task- and dataset-dependent and not universal across domains [Reviriego et al., 2024].
  - Hohenstein et al. (2023): real-time AI mediation shifts language use toward more positive emotion and can accelerate exchange; perceived AI use also affects social evaluations; however, these are short-term, interaction-level effects rather than long-run dispersion metrics [Hohenstein et al., 2023].
  - Xu, Gries and related dispersion work (2000s–2020s): corpus-linguistic dispersion measures (MTLD, MATTR, VOCD, HD-D, etc.) provide robust tools for measuring lexical diversity and dispersion over time and across populations; these are essential for rigorous dispersion studies but have not been systematically applied in large-scale longitudinal AI-companion studies to date (Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024) [Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024].
- Cross-domain and cross-cultural evidence
  - Studies of cross-cultural AI language design (e.g., humor benchmarks, cultural alignment) show persistent challenges in achieving culturally sensitive and richly diverse outputs across languages; these findings imply caution against assuming universal homogenization or uniform dispersion effects (Chumor 2.0; He et al., 2025) [Chumor 2.0; He et al., 2025].
- Domain-specific counterpoints
  - In creative, multi-person input contexts with diverse AI personas, diverse prompts can sustain or increase dispersion in human outputs, challenging the notion that AI always reduces novelty (Kalman & Wan, 2025) [Kalman & Wan, 2025].

Direct implications for the research question
- The evidence does not support a blanket conclusion that ongoing AI companionship universally homogenizes language and taste. Instead, the direction and magnitude of any homogenization or diversification depend critically on design choices (voice socioindexical traits, diversity of AI personas, prompt diversity, content-domain constraints) and on user context and time horizon (Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024; Hohenstein et al., 2023) [Kalman & Wan, 2025; Revirigo et al., 2024; Hohenstein et al., 2023].
- Longitudinal, cross-domain studies with explicit dispersion metrics are needed to quantify novelty dispersion trajectories, establish causal relations, and guide diversification strategies for AI companions (Székely et al., 2025; Gries, 2022; Kalman & Wan, 2025) [Székely et al., 2025; Gries, 2022; Kalman & Wan, 2025].

Actionable insights and recommendations for authors and designers
- Diversify AI design to preserve or enhance novelty dispersion
  - Deploy multiple AI personas and voices with varied socioindexical profiles (accents, politeness, perceived humanness) to prevent uniform convergence in user outputs (Székely et al., 2025; Kalman & Wan, 2025) [Székely et al., 2025; Kalman & Wan, 2025].
  - Rotate and refresh AI prompts and prompts’ stylistic frames to maintain linguistic and topical diversity in user discourse; avoid persistent single-style prompts that risk convergence (Cox et al., 2021; Kalman & Wan, 2025) [Cox et al., 2021; Kalman & Wan, 2025].
- Use robust, longitudinal dispersion metrics
  - Measure lexical diversity (MTLD, MATTR, Maas, HD-D, VOCD, etc.), syntactic variety, topic dispersion (entropy, Jensen-Shannon distance across users), and semantic diversity (embedding-based dispersion) across time and across cohorts (Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024; Malvern et al., 2004; Covington & McFall, 2010) [Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024; Malvern et al., 2004; Covington & McFall, 2010].
  - Apply multi-metric approaches to mitigate metric-specific biases (e.g., text length concerns with TTR) and to capture a fuller picture of dispersion dynamics (Bestgen, 2023) [Bestgen, 2023].
- Experimental and sampling design
  - Conduct longitudinal, multi-domain experiments (work, education, social) with diverse populations to assess cross-context dispersion and diffusion patterns.
  - Randomize exposure to AI personas and styles; include control conditions and pre/post measurements to separate AI-driven effects from baseline variation.
  - Pre-register dispersion hypotheses and publish null results to enable cumulative meta-analytic assessments.
- Ethical safeguards
  - Monitor for homogenization risks, including linguistic profiling, bias in voice design, and cultural misrepresentations; implement governance mechanisms and provide user control over voice characteristics and stylistic settings (Purnell et al., 1999; Gluszek & Dovidio, 2010; Lippi-Green, 2012; Krahé & Papakonstantinou, 2020) [Purnell et al., 1999; Gluszek & Dovidio 2010; Lippi-Green 2012; Krahé & Papakonstantinou 2020].
- Domain-specific guidance
  - In domains with high creative or cultural sensitivity (education, cross-cultural communication, creative writing), leverage diversity-promoting prompts and mixed-voice generation to support novelty and relational richness (Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024; Tagliamonte 2014; Yakura 2024) [Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024; Tagliamonte 2014; Yakura 2024].
- Reporting and dissemination
  - Share dispersion metrics openly, along with description of AI design choices, to enable replication and cross-site validation (Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024).
  - Provide domain-specific guidance on when AI augmentation supports or undermines novelty dispersion, enabling practitioners to tailor AI deployments to desired dispersion profiles.

Limitations and cautions
- The landscape comprises a mix of qualitative syntheses, single-domain experiments, and model-version–dependent findings. There is currently no universal, longitudinal cross-domain dispersion benchmark for AI companions, so conclusions about homogenization remain contingent on design and context ( Székely et al., 2025; Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024; Wilkenfeld et al., 2022) [Székely et al., 2025; Kalman & Wan, 2025; Reviriego et al., 2024; Wilkenfeld et al., 2022].
- Translating dispersion metrics from overt language measures to broader taste/creative diversity requires careful operationalization and cross-domain alignment (Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024; Johnson et al., 2022) [Gries, 2022; Gries, 2024; Johnson et al., 2022].

Implications for policy and governance
- Develop guidelines and norms for AI voice diversity, representation, and explicit consent for socioindexical manipulation in AI companions.
- Encourage platforms to publish dispersion-related metrics and to allow user controls over voice identity parameters, to protect linguistic and cultural diversity.
- Support cross-domain research consortia to standardize dispersion metrics and enable comparability across studies, domains, and languages.

Limitations

Most cited work documents mechanisms, task-specific effects, or single-domain results rather than broad population-level dispersion trajectories. Future work should prioritize rigorous dispersion metrics and standardized benchmarking across domains.
