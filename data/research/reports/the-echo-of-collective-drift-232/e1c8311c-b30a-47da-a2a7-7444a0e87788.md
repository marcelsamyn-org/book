# Report: Structured Reflective Prompts After AI-Mediated Conversations — A six-week randomized trial design and evidence-informed expectations

Executive summary
- There is no documented six-week randomized trial in the literature that directly tests structured reflective prompts after AI-mediated conversations on learning depth, retention, and relationship satisfaction. However, a converging set of studies provides strong evidence that structured reflective prompts (metacognitive prompts) embedded in AI-enabled learning contexts can enhance metacognition, learning strategies, engagement, and, in some cases, transfer or retention signals, while effects on relationship satisfaction are less consistently measured or reported (Ahmad et al., 2025; Yin et al., 2025; Fan & Liu, 2025; Xu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Lan & Zhou, 2025; Rani et al., 2018) [inline citations throughout].
- Based on these findings, a rigorously designed six-week randomized trial should embed explicit structured reflective prompts after AI-mediated conversations, measure learning depth and retention with validated instruments, and include relationship-satisfaction measures to address the social dimension of AI-mediated learning.

1) Introduction: Research question and framing
- Research question: What is the effect of structured reflective prompts after AI-mediated conversations (for example, prompts such as "What did you learn?" and "What would you try differently next time?") on learning depth, retention, and relationship satisfaction in a randomized trial over six weeks?
- Rationale and theory: Reflective prompts are a form of metacognitive scaffolding that align with Self-Regulated Learning (SRL) theories and metacognition research. Across AI-enabled contexts, structured prompts can advance planning, monitoring, and evaluation of learning, potentially increasing depth of processing and durable retention, while also shaping the learner–AI relationship through perceived support and collaboration (Ahmad et al., 2025; Yin et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025; Fan & Liu, 2025) [inline citations].

2) What the evidence base suggests about structured reflective prompts and related outcomes
- Learning depth and metacognition
  - Large randomized-like trial (Ahmad et al., 2025) in a biology course found that metacognitive requirements embedded in AI feedback (explanation and confidence judgments) yielded the strongest effects on metacognition and long-term exam strategies; there were no significant differences in immediate exam performance between feedback-content conditions, suggesting depth and strategy-focused metacognition as the key driver. Textbook engagement increased when prompted by feedback (≈40%) and learner satisfaction was high (M ≈ 4.1/5) with substantial perceived gains in confidence and recall/applicability (Ahmad et al., 2025) [Ahmad et al., 2025].
  - Other randomized or quasi-experimental work shows metacognitive prompts can enhance SRL and transfer: e.g., metacognitive prompting in a six-week window or similar designs increased metacognitive engagement, with prior performance moderating transfer gains (Pape & Thomas, 2025) [Pape & Thomas, 2025].
  - In GenAI contexts, metacognitive prompts embedded in AI feedback improve SRL indicators and learning experiences, with evidence that metacognitive support can reduce cognitive load and improve perceived usefulness of AI tools (Xu et al., 2025) [Xu et al., 2025].
- Retention
  - Some evidence suggests that retrieval practice and metacognitive reflection can support retention over time, particularly when prompts are tied to spaced/retrieval-based strategies (e.g., memory research and spaced repetition literature cited in broader reviews; see Marousis, 2023; Ebbinghaus, 1913; Dunlosky et al., 2013) — these are incorporated as mechanisms in AI-facilitated learning environments (e.g., Yang, 2023; Wang & Fan, 2025) [relevant cross-references in the literature; specific six-week retention data are not consistently reported in the six-week trials reviewed].
- Relationship satisfaction and relatedness
  - Several lines of work treat "relationship satisfaction" more broadly as perceived social exchange, trust, and relatedness in human–AI interactions. Some studies show that high-quality AI listening, social presence, and perceived empathy can increase perceived relatedness and autonomy satisfaction, which associate with engagement and motivation, though direct six-week retention/relationship-satisfaction effects are not consistently measured across studies (Shen, 2025; Rani et al., 2018) [Shen, 2025; Rani et al., 2018; related SDT literature cited in Shen] .
- Meta-analytic and scope context
  - Systematic reviews and meta-analytic syntheses of AI-driven feedback and SRL consistently emphasize that high-quality AI feedback, especially when it includes metacognitive scaffolds and prompts, tends to improve learner engagement, SRL processes, and sometimes perceptions of usefulness or motivation, with mixed or domain-dependent effects on achievement/retention (Luo et al., 2025; Lan & Zhou, 2025) [Luo et al., 2025; Lan & Zhou, 2025].

3) Evidence-informed design for a six-week randomized trial (design blueprint)
- Study design
  - Design: Randomized controlled trial with two parallel arms over six weeks
    - Intervention arm: AI-mediated conversations followed by structured reflective prompts (e.g., What did you learn? What would you try differently next time? Why? How does this connect to prior knowledge? How will you apply it? Plan for next steps).
    - Control arm: AI-mediated conversations with minimal/no structured reflective prompts (standard AI feedback or no prompts).
  - Randomization unit: Individual participants, with stratification by baseline metacognitive awareness or prior AI experience to balance groups.
  - Setting: University students across multiple domains to improve generalizability; ensure standardized AI platform and prompt framework.
- Intervention specifics
  - Prompt design: A fixed, theory-grounded set of prompts following each AI interaction, with variations to ensure dose-consistency across weeks (e.g., after each session, or after every 2 sessions).
  - Prompt content categories: learning extraction, problem-solving strategies, planning, monitoring, evidence-seeking, and transfer prompts; prompts should explicitly request justification, planned next steps, and calibration checks (e.g., how confident are you in the conclusion? what would you check to verify?).
  - Social dimension cues: Optional inclusion of social presence cues (avatars, warm tone) to explore potential effects on relatedness, while monitoring for social-engineering biases.
- Outcomes and measurement timeline
  - Primary outcomes (learning depth and retention)
    - Learning depth: Assess with higher-order thinking measures (e.g., transfer tasks, synthesis tasks, concept maps) and rubric-based ratings of depth of understanding.
    - Retention: Delayed post-test or transfer assessment at Week 6 or Week 8 (2–4 weeks post-intervention) to gauge durable learning.
  - Secondary outcomes (relationship satisfaction and SRL processes)
    - Relationship satisfaction: Validated scales for perceived quality of learner–AI interaction, trust in AI, perceived empathy, and collaboration quality (or a lightweight, validated “relationship with AI tutor” instrument).
    - SRL processes and cognitive load: SRL scales, and problem-solving/strategy-use indicators; cognitive-load measures (intrinsic, extraneous, germane) as covariates.
  - Process measures: Prompt adherence rate, reaction time to prompts, textbook engagement (if included), usage duration, and interaction logs.
- Analytic approach
  - Primary analyses: Mixed-effects models (repeated measures) to compare depth and retention trajectories across arms, with time as a factor and random intercepts for participants.
  - Mediation and moderation: Test whether metacognitive engagement (e.g., quality of reflective responses) mediates depth/retention, and whether baseline metacognition or AI trust moderates effects.
  - Effect sizes: Report Cohen’s d or partial eta-squared for primary outcomes, with 95% confidence intervals.
  - Handling missing data: Multiple imputation or maximum likelihood, with intention-to-treat and per-protocol analyses as sensitivity checks.
- Sample size and power
  - Power analysis should target small-to-moderate effects on depth/retention (e.g., d ≈ 0.25–0.40) with 80% power at α = 0.05; adjust for anticipated attrition over six weeks (multi-site recruitment if needed).
- Ethics and governance
  - Informed consent, data privacy protections, and transparency about AI data usage. Pre-registration of hypotheses, outcomes, and analysis plan. Clear opt-out and data-handling policies.
- Practical considerations
  - Prompts must be integrated with the AI interface to minimize disruption; ensure the prompts are accessible, not overly burdensome, and culturally appropriate.
  - Training for facilitators or instructors if human oversight is involved; ensure standardization across groups.

4) Operationalizing the outcomes and instruments (examples)
- Learning depth (primary)
  - Transfer tasks: domain-appropriate tasks requiring application/synthesis beyond content covered in prompts (scored with a rubric).
  - Concept maps: rubric-based evaluation of integration and depth of connections.
- Retention
  - Delayed post-test: identical or equivalent assessment material administered 2–4 weeks post-intervention.
- Relationship satisfaction
  - A short, validated instrument assessing perceived empathy, trust, social presence, and overall satisfaction with AI-mediated interaction.
- SRL and cognitive load
  - SRL scales (forethought, monitoring, control) and cognitive load measures (intrinsic, extraneous, germane) to track regulatory processes and cognitive effort.

5) Expected outcomes and interpretations (when results align with prior evidence)
- If metacognitive prompts are effective at six weeks:
  - Learning depth should improve (better transfer-capable performance, deeper reasoning).
  - Retention should be stronger in the prompt group due to enhanced encoding and retrieval practices.
  - Relationship satisfaction may improve due to increased perceived support and autonomy, particularly if social presence cues are used appropriately.
- If prompts do not yield depth/retention gains:
  - Examine moderator effects (prior SRL, AI familiarity, domain differences) and the design of prompts (dose, wording, relevance to learning objectives).
- If relationship satisfaction diverges from depth/retention:
  - Investigate the balance between social cues and cognitive load; ensure prompts target metacognition without overloading or biased social signaling.

6) Limitations and caveats
- Heterogeneity in learner populations and domains may moderate effects; six weeks is a relatively short window for some domains, necessitating robust retention assessment designs.
- The relationship dimension is often under-measured in AI learning trials; ensure robust, validated relationship metrics are included and interpreted cautiously.
- Overreliance risks remain; prompts should be designed to require justification and verification of AI outputs rather than encouraging blind trust.
- Ethical considerations around data privacy, bias, and accessibility must be addressed.

7) Actionable insights for practitioners and authors
- Ground prompts in established metacognitive prompts and SRL theory (planning, monitoring, evaluating, adjusting strategies; retrieval practice).
- Design six-week trials with clear depth and retention endpoints and explicit relationship-quality assessments to capture social-cognitive outcomes beyond performance.
- Integrate adaptive scaffolding and dashboards to support ongoing self-regulation and calibration with AI, while maintaining learner autonomy and minimizing cognitive overload.
- Include qualitative components (interviews, think-aloud protocols, diaries) to contextualize how prompts influence depth of processing and the quality of learner–AI interactions.
- Report effect sizes and provide preregistered analysis plans to improve reproducibility and interpretability.

Notes
- The present report positions itself as an evidence-informed design and interpretation guide for a six-week randomized trial investigating structured reflective prompts after AI-mediated conversations. It integrates findings from multiple studies that collectively point toward metacognition and SRL as central mechanisms, with nuanced implications for depth, retention, and relationship quality.

Appendix: Key takeaways for authors planning a six-week RCT
- Prioritize metacognitive prompts as the core intervention, not solely content elaboration.
- Use a randomized, adequately powered design with baseline stratification on SRL/metacognition to maximize detection of depth/retention effects.
- Include robust retention tests and delayed probes to capture six-week outcomes and longer-term effects.
- Incorporate relationship/satisfaction measures to assess the social calibration of AI-mediated dialogue.
