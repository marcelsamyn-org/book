# Diversity in AI-Enabled Social Feeds: Exposure to Diverse Conversations and Belonging in a Multi-Month Field Experiment

Executive summary
- Objective and scope: This report synthesizes evidence on whether increasing recommendation diversity in AI-enabled social feeds can broaden exposure to diverse conversations and foster social belonging over time, and it outlines a concrete, multi-month field-experiment blueprint across community groups to test these effects. The synthesis draws on algorithmic design theory, short- and long-horizon field experiments, exposure-diversity metrics, and belonging/person-connected outcomes from a broad literature base (Narayanan, 2023; Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020; Kelm et al., 2023; Fabbri et al., 2021; Godinot & Tarissan, 2023; Stray et al., 2023).
- Core finding synthesis:
  - Algorithmic ranking strongly shapes exposure; simply increasing input diversity does not guarantee broader exposure to diverse conversations or enhanced belonging unless diversity is embedded in ranking signals and accompanied by measures of cross-community engagement and belonging (Narayanan, 2023; Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020) [Understanding Social Media Recommendation Algorithms; Exposure Diversity].
  - Short- to mid-term experimental evidence suggests that diversity nudges at the algorithmic level can increase exposure to diverse content and reduce some biases, but effects on belonging are not consistently demonstrated and long-run effects require extended study (Haroon et al., 2023; Mattis et al., 2024; Kelm et al., 2023) [Nudging the Recommendation Algorithm…; It Ain’t Easy; How algorithmically curated environments influence polarization].
  - Long-run exposure dynamics can be shaped by network structure, minority size, and homophily, which can produce “rich-get-richer” exposure patterns or under-exposure for certain groups unless diversity interventions counterbalance structural biases (Fabbri et al., 2021; Lazovich et al., 2022) [Long-Term Effects of Link Recommenders; Measuring disparity in exposure].
- Actionable design implications:
  - Explicitly operationalize diversity as part of the ranking objective (not only as a sampling or post-hoc adjustment) and pair with explicit cross-community exposure targets (Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020; Narayanan, 2023) [Exposure Diversity; Algorithmic Mechanisms].
  - Use multi-metric evaluation that combines exposure diversity with belonging-related outcomes (e.g., social connectedness) and habit formation indicators over time (Mitova et al., 2023; Kelm et al., 2023; Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020) [Policy/Measurement Recommendations; Longitudinal Evaluation].
  - Plan for conflict-sensitivity and safeguards to prevent the amplification of polarization or harmful content when widening exposure, drawing on Stray et al. (2023) and related literature (e.g., SSA/echo-chamber dynamics) [Conflict-aware Design; Synthetic Social Alienation].
  - Pre-register hypotheses, specify primary and secondary outcomes, and use cluster-randomized, multi-community designs to assess heterogeneity of effects (Kelm et al., 2023; Mitova et al., 2023) [Panel Experiments; Programmatic Review].
- Core quantitative cues to guide power planning and interpretation:
  - Exposure patterns on large-scale platforms can be highly skewed; e.g., top 1% of creators often receive a disproportionate share of views (Patterns, 2022) and only a portion of posts are surfaced in feeds (Instagram ~30%) (Mosseri, 2021) [Exposure inequality; feed visibility].
  - Short-term diversity nudges can shift exposure and reduce ideological bias in some settings, but long-run effects on democratic attitudes or belonging require longitudinal assessment (Haroon et al., 2023) [Nudging algorithmic bias; long-run attitudes].
  - In a YouTube-like context, algorithmic selection can affect exposure but may not magnify polarization beyond content-direction effects; user-level nudges were generally ineffective (Haroon et al., 2023) [Algorithmic nudges in feeds].
- Caveats:
  - There is substantial heterogeneity across platforms, domains (news, social feeds, book communities, music services), and populations; a multi-month field experiment must account for baseline network structure, minority representation, and topic sensitivity (Fabbri et al., 2021; Godinot & Tarissan, 2023; Whitley, 2025) [Long-term exposure dynamics; diversity in content exposure; publication-specific contexts].
  - Many studies report on mechanisms, simulations, or short-term field tests; direct causal evidence for sustained belonging/bi-directional cross-community dialogue in real multi-month field experiments remains an open research area (Mitova et al., 2023; Narayanan, 2023) [Strategy reviews; algorithmic design frameworks].

1) Research question and framing
Does increasing recommendation diversity in AI-enabled social feeds lead to greater exposure to diverse conversations and higher social belonging over time, compared with baseline, in a multi-month field experiment across community groups?

- Rationale: The literature converges on two core ideas:
  - Exposure diversity is primarily mediated by how ranking signals combine with content provenance, user history, and platform incentives; naive diversification without explicit ranking-architecture changes may yield limited cross-view exposure (Narayanan, 2023; Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020) [Algorithmic ranking; normative frameworks].
  - Belonging and cross-community interaction depend on longitudinal exposure to diverse conversations, cross-cutting topics, and supportive platform affordances; short-term diversification signals may be insufficient for durable belonging improvements (Mitova et al., 2023; Stray et al., 2023) [Long-horizon design; conflict-aware metrics].
- Expected contribution: A multi-month field experiment across community groups that explicitly manipulates diversity signals in the feed, tracks exposure diversity and cross-community conversations, and measures belonging outcomes over time—paired with a robust causal identification strategy.

2) Evidence synthesis: what we know about exposure diversity, belonging, and long-run effects

- Algorithmic exposure and diversity dynamics
  - Algorithmic feeds are major exposure determinants; they optimize for engagement, not diversity by default, and include feedback loops between human behavior and algorithmic ranking (Narayanan, 2023) [Understanding Social Media Recommendation Algorithms].
  - Diversity signals must be integrated into ranking to meaningfully alter exposure to diverse conversations; otherwise, engagement signals can dominate and homogenize exposure (Narayanan, 2023; Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020) [Algorithmic signals; normative diversity].
  - Short-term field experiments show algorithm nudges can increase exposure to diverse content and reduce ideological bias; notably on a field test of news recommendations, algorithmic nudges increased quality-consistent diversity and decreased bias with larger effects on some subgroups (Haroon et al., 2023) [Nudging recommendation algorithms; short-term effects].
- Long-run exposure, inequality, and network structure
  - Long-run exposure dynamics can yield unequal exposure due to minority size and homophily; a “rich-get-richer” effect emerges in simulations of multi-round link recommendations, with disparities influenced by group size and homophily levels (Fabbri et al., 2021; 2022) [Long-Term Effects; exposure inequality].
  - Distributional inequality metrics reveal substantial skew in exposure in real platforms (e.g., Twitter); top 1% of authors capture most views, and out-of-network impressions are more skewed, indicating that diversification strategies must target tail exposure to meaningfully broaden conversations (Lazovich et al., 2022) [Patterns].
- Multidimensional measurement and normative aims
  - Public-service and normative frameworks emphasize exposure diversity as a pathway to inclusive discourse and belonging, but operationalization requires careful design and measurement across supply and demand sides (Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020; Mitova et al., 2023) [Public service; programmatic review].
  - Commonality-based designs advocate for explicit exposure targets to under-represented categories and human-in-the-loop design to foster universality and belonging, suggesting a path for long-run experimentation (Protopapa 2024; Ferraro et al., conceptually) [Commonality metric; public-service]. 
- Practical design implications for multi-month field studies
  - To test belonging, diversity must be embedded in the ranking objective and paired with longitudinal measures of belonging and cross-community conversation. Short-term engagement metrics alone are insufficient (Narayanan, 2023; Stray et al., 2023).
  - Conflict-aware design considerations emphasize that diversification should be coupled with safeguards, peacebuilder affordances, and measurement of discourse health to avoid adverse effects on belonging (Stray et al., 2023) [Conflict-aware design; peacebuilders].
  - Longitudinal, cross-community field experiments should combine exposure-diversity metrics with validated belonging scales, using multi-level (group and individual) analyses and pre-registered analytic plans (Mitova et al., 2023; Kelm et al., 2023) [Panel experiments; study protocol].

3) Proposed multi-month field-experiment design (blueprint)

A. Experimental units and design
- Population: Multiple distinct community groups with baseline exposure to online conversations (e.g., geographically clustered or interest-based communities).
- Design: Cluster-randomized, multi-arm, multi-month field experiment with four arms:
  1) Baseline feed: current algorithmic ranking (control).
  2) Diversity-forward ranking: feed ranking explicitly optimized for exposure diversity (topic, viewpoint, and cross-community content) while maintaining engagement constraints.
  3) Hybrid ranking: diversity-forward with an accuracy constraint (to prevent dramatic declines in perceived relevance).
  4) Peacebuilder-enabled diversity: diversity-forward ranking plus on-platform tools that facilitate cross-group dialogue and constructive engagement (e.g., prompts for cross-cutting topics, moderated discussion spaces).
- Duration: 6–12 months with monthly measurement waves; include a stabilization/warm-up period.

B. Interventions and mechanisms
- Diversity-forward ranking: incorporate explicit diversity signals into ranking signals, balance exploration vs. exploitation, promote cross-cutting viewpoints, and ensure topic and source diversity (Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020; Narayanan, 2023).
- Peacebuilder affordances: design UI/UX features that encourage constructive dialogue, cross-group introductions, and accountable discourse (Stray et al., 2023).
- Safeguards: monitoring for incivility, misinformation, or harassment; implement moderation-to-supportive-design hybrid governance.

C. Outcomes and measurement (primary and secondary)
- Primary outcomes:
  - Exposure diversity: entropy-based measures of content/topics seen by users; cross-community exposure rate; tail-share metrics (Patterns, 2022).
  - Belonging: validated belonging and social connectedness scales measured monthly; cross-group trust and perceived inclusion.
- Secondary outcomes:
  - Conversation diversity: cross-topic engagement, cross-group interactions, and topic-coverage breadth in conversations.
  - Habit formation: longitudinal engagement with diverse content; retention across waves; changes in engagement diversity over time.
  - Engagement metrics: time spent, number of interactions, and perceived quality of recommendations.
- Covariates and moderators: platform type, baseline homophily, group size, demographic composition, topic domains, and baseline exposure patterns.

D. Data and analysis plan
- Data sources: platform logs (exposure, interactions, cross-group activity), surveys (belonging scales, perceived diversity, trust), qualitative interviews (optional).
- Statistical models: longitudinal mixed-effects models with random intercepts/slopes for community groups and individuals; difference-in-differences with time interactions; mediation analyses to assess whether exposure diversity mediates belonging outcomes; moderator analyses by community type and baseline network structure.
- Pre-registration: clearly document primary and secondary outcomes, analysis plan, power calculations, and stopping rules; publish protocol to ensure transparency.
- Robustness and validity: sensitivity analyses across diversity definitions (topic-level entropy, source diversity, cross-community reach), alternative belonging scales, and alternative exposure metrics.

E. Ethics and governance
- Informed consent, opt-out options, data privacy measures, and transparent participant communication about feed interventions.
- Ongoing ethics review; monitor for unintended polarization or harassment; implement safety protocols and content moderation safeguards.

F. Practical considerations
- Platform compatibility: ensure the intervention integrates with feed ranking without compromising user experience; consider latency constraints.
- User autonomy: provide settings to adjust or pause diversity prompts; ensure users understand the aims of the study.
- Replicability: document metrics, code, and data pipelines; consider open science practices.

4) Quantitative cues to guide expectations and power calculations

- Exposure skew baseline (Patterns): Top 1% of creators receive ~80% of views; out-of-network exposure is more skewed than in-network exposure; these baselines help calibrate effect-size expectations for a diversity-promoting intervention (Lazovich et al., 2022) [Patterns].
- Short-term diversity interventions (Haroon et al., 2023; Kelm et al., 2023):
  - Algorithmic nudges can increase exposure to diverse content and reduce ideological bias, with stronger effects at algorithmic level than user nudges in a month-long YouTube-like setting; polarization effects vary by topic and are not universally amplified by algorithmic selection (Haroon et al., 2023; Kelm et al., 2023) [Nudging algorithmic bias; polarization panel].
- Long-horizon exposure dynamics (Fabbri et al., 2021, 2022):
  - Minority exposure can be amplified under homophily; rich-get-richer effects emerge across rounds; exposure disparities depend on minority size and homophily level; simulations suggest algorithmic design can shape long-run exposure (Fabbri et al., 2021, 2022) [Long-Term Effects; Exposure Inequality].
- Public-service and normative measurements (Helberger & Wojcieszak, 2020; Mitova et al., 2023; Protopapa 2024):
  - Define exposure diversity with normative and public-service lenses; develop cross-domain metrics (Commonality) and longitudinal measurement frameworks. These contributions guide outcome selection and measurement strategy for belonging in field studies (Public Service; programmatic reviews) [Exposure Diversity; Commonality].
- Feasible measurement approaches for belonging in field studies (Whitley, 2025; Sonoda et al., 2024; Piccardi et al., 2024):
  - Longitudinal designs can incorporate belonging scales and cross-group dialogue metrics; use EMAs and browser-extension-based interventions to measure exposure, overlap with belonging, and cross-cutting conversations (NarraGive-like interventions; FeedMonitor). These sources provide methodological inspiration for measurement in real-world field tests (Whitley; Piccardi; NarraGive frameworks) [Belonging in book communities; Reranking Social Feeds].

5) Practical, actionable guidance for the author

A. Concrete study plan to test the central question
- Adopt a multi-month, cluster-randomized field experiment across diverse community groups with four arms:
  1) Baseline ranking (control)
  2) Diversity-forward ranking (explicit exposure-diversity signals)
  3) Hybrid ranking (diversity + accuracy)
  4) Peacebuilder-enabled diversity (diversity + dialogue-support features)
- Measurement plan:
  - Primary outcomes (monthly): exposure diversity metrics (topic entropy, source diversity, cross-community exposure), belonging scales (validated scales), cross-community conversation metrics (count of cross-group interactions, cross-topic threads).
  - Secondary outcomes: time-on-platform, engagement quality (comments depth, sentiment), retention across waves.
- Analytical plan:
  - Mixed-effects models with waves nested within individuals, and individuals nested within communities; test time-by-treatment interactions; conduct mediation analyses with exposure diversity as mediator for belonging.
- Ethical guardrails:
  - Clear consent; opt-out; privacy-preserving data handling; monitoring for adverse effects such as polarizing content surges or harassment.

B. Measurement and metrics toolkit
- Exposure diversity:
  - Topic entropy of seen content per user per month
  - Source diversity (distinct authors/entities seen)
  - Cross-community exposure rate (fraction of viewed content from outside user’s typical community)
  - Tail-share metrics (top 1%, top 5%)
- Conversation diversity:
  - Number of distinct interlocutors per month
  - Cross-group interactions (messages/posts involving multiple groups)
  - Topic heterogeneity within conversations
- Belonging and social connectedness:
  - Standard belonging scales (e.g., social connectedness scale) plus platform-specific belonging items
  - Perceived inclusion and alignment with community norms
- Habits and sustained engagement:
  - Repeated engagement with diverse content across waves
  - Stability/trajectories of exposure diversity

C. Design and implementation notes
- Start with a warm-up phase to stabilize the system and user expectations; provide opt-in consent and options to adjust diversity levels.
- Balance exposure breadth and user perceived relevance; avoid overwhelming users with irrelevant content; implement gradual ramp-ups and personalization tuning.
- Plan for cross-platform generalizability by selecting community groups that vary by topic domain and platform affordances.
- Prepare for potential regulatory and ethical considerations by pre-registering study design and sharing de-identified aggregated results where permissible.

D. Risk management
- Monitor for polarization amplification and incivility; implement safeguards (moderation, stress-testing, rate limiting of controversial content).
- Guard against gaming of diversity metrics by bots or coordinated behavior; implement anomaly detection and robust data validation.
- Ensure data governance and privacy safeguards are in place; establish a data access plan and a data retention policy.

6) Limitations and caveats to communicate
- The literature shows that increasing input diversity does not automatically yield durable improvements in belonging; effects are contingent on ranking architecture, content quality, and social-context factors (Narayanan, 2023; Stray et al., 2023).
- Real-world, multi-month field experiments across community groups are relatively scarce; expect heterogeneity across communities and platforms, and design adaptive analytics to account for context-specific dynamics (Mitova et al., 2023; Kelm et al., 2023).
- Measuring belonging is complex and requires validated instruments and longitudinal data; avoid over-reliance on engagement metrics as a proxy for belonging.

Appendix: Illustrative metrics definitions and sample formulas
- Exposure Diversity Entropy (per user per month)
  - H_topic = -∑ p_i log(p_i), where p_i is the fraction of seen content in topic i; higher H_topic indicates broader topic exposure.
- Source Diversity
  - S_source = number of distinct content sources seen in a month per user.
- Cross-Community Exposure Rate
  - CCR = (number of seen items from outside user’s baseline community) / (total seen items).
- Tail-Share Metric
  - Tail1_share = share of views attributed to the top 1% of content sources; lower Tail1_share indicates less concentration.
- Belonging Score
  - Belonging_t = validated Belonging Scale score at time t; analyze trajectory over months.
- Conversation Diversity
  - Topic-Entropy_in_conversations = entropy of topics discussed within a user’s conversations in a given month.
  - Cross-group_interactions = number of interactions that involve more than one community group.
