# report.md

Title: Slow-Mode Friction in AI Chatbot Interactions: Evidence Synthesis and Design Guidance for a Six-Week RCT on Loneliness and Perceived Conversational Depth

Executive summary
- There is no direct, published evidence that a fixed slow-mode latency of 2–5 seconds with a mandatory per-message user prompt reduces loneliness or increases perceived conversational depth over six weeks. The literature to date mainly tests latency as a general social cue, various interaction modalities, and short-term outcomes, with no six-week slow-mode RCT specifically measuring loneliness or depth (Fang et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Fang et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].
- Indirect evidence suggests:
  - Latency can shape perceived social presence, trust, and satisfaction, but effects are context-dependent and moderated by cues (e.g., typing indicators, justifications) and by user characteristics (e.g., age, prior experience) (Kim et al., 2025; Gnewuch et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2024; Choedak, 2020) [Kim et al., 2025; Gnewuch et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2024; Choedak, 2020].
  - Loneliness and emotional well-being can be influenced by AI-mediated social interaction, with “feeling heard” and perceived emotional support as robust mediators; longitudinal effects have been observed in AI-companion studies, albeit not specifically for a fixed 2–5 s slow mode (De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025].
- Gaps identified include: explicit six-week latency manipulation, explicit measurement of perceived conversational depth as a primary outcome, and systematic exploration of a mandatory-per-message prompt in a six-week timeframe.
- Actionable plan: If pursuing a six-week RCT, design a multi-arm trial that isolates the slow-mode latency (2–5 s) with and without mandatory prompts, compare to an instant-response control, and include loneliness, perceived conversational depth, belonging, social presence, and emotional-support mediators as primary/secondary outcomes. Pre-register hypotheses and use longitudinal mixed-effects models with mediation analyses to test hypothesized pathways (feeling heard, social presence) over time (Fang et al., 2025; Merrill et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Fang et al., 2025; Merrill et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].

1) Foundation: what the evidence says about latency, friction, and psychosocial outcomes
- Latency as a social cue and its effects on perceived social value
  - Dynamic/latency manipulations can increase perceived humanness and social presence and can boost overall satisfaction in some contexts (e.g., customer-service chatbots). However, effects depend on delay magnitude, context, and presence of social cues (e.g., typing indicators, justification text) and may not generalize to all tasks or user groups (Gnewuch et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Gnewuch et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].
  - In instant vs dynamic-delay designs, justifications for delay can improve transparency and trust for instant responses but may have little effect under dynamic delays (Zhang et al., 2024) [Zhang et al., 2024].
- Loneliness, AI companionship, and mechanisms
  - Longitudinal AI-companion work shows loneliness reductions can occur over time; feeling heard (empathic engagement) is a robust mediator of loneliness reductions, potentially more influential than raw performance metrics (De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025].
  - High-quality, person-centered messaging increases emotional validation, with social support quality and interpersonal warmth as mediators; social presence can moderate the emotional-support pathway (Merrill et al., 2025) [Merrill et al., 2025].
  - Non-loneliness outcomes (e.g., engagement metrics) and usage intensity interact with loneliness; higher daily usage can correlate with higher loneliness and problematic use in some AI-companion contexts (Fang et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024) [Fang et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024].
- Perceived conversational depth
  - Depth is often inferred from warmth, empathy, reciprocity, and self-disclosure, rather than latency alone; social presence plays a mediating role in perceived depth via evaluative cues (Merrill et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Merrill et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].
- Summary of key quantitative signals
  - Latency effects: Moderate delays can enhance perceived social presence and relational value in some contexts, but effects are context-dependent and may differ by user age (Wang & Lo, 2025) [Wang & Lo, 2025].
  - Loneliness outcomes: AI companionship can reduce loneliness over weeks, mediated by feeling heard and perceived support; six-week data are not consistently reported for slow-mode latency (De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025].
  - Depth/presence: Social presence mediates perceived depth and warmth; latency interacts with presence cues to shape outcomes (Kim et al., 2025; Merrill et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Kim et al., 2025; Merrill et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].

2) Core gaps for your six-week slow-mode RCT
- No directly published six-week slow-mode trial with a fixed 2–5s delay and mandatory per-message prompts measuring loneliness and perceived conversational depth.
- Most existing latency work tests: (a) instantaneous vs variable delays (not fixed 2–5s), (b) presence cues, and (c) sometimes prompts or justifications, but not a six-week longitudinal loneliness/depth endpoint.
- No consensus on the optimal latency window for six-week outcomes, and no standard metric for “perceived conversational depth” in latency-manipulation studies.
- Several studies show that the effect of latency is moderated by user characteristics (e.g., age, prior chatbot experience) and by accompanying cues (typing indicators, emotional support; etc.) which should be incorporated into a six-week design (Zhang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Choedak, 2020) [Zhang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Choedak, 2020].

3) Proposed six-week RCT design (multi-arm; latency-focused; with depth and loneliness outcomes)
- Objective
  - Test whether a slow-mode latency (2–5 seconds) with a mandatory brief user prompt per message reduces loneliness and increases perceived conversational depth over six weeks, compared with standard instant AI responses.
- Population and sampling
  - Adult participants who regularly interact with AI chatbots for social connection (diverse demographics; include older and younger adults to test age-moderation).
  - Baseline loneliness screening to enable stratified randomization and ensure sufficient dispersion in loneliness.
- Experimental arms (2–4 arms)
  - Arm A (Slow Mode only): AI replies delayed 2–5 seconds; mandatory brief user prompt per message; no additional cues.
  - Arm B (Slow Mode + Cues): Slow-mode latency with mandatory prompts plus social-presence/empathetic cues embedded (tone adaptation, backchannels, brief empathic statements).
  - Arm C (Instant Control): Instant AI responses; no mandatory prompts.
  - Optional Arm D (Instant + Justifications): Instant responses with brief justification text for each reply (to test whether justification cues transfer to depth/presence in an instant context; extrapolates Zhang et al. 2024).
- Primary outcomes
  - Loneliness: validated loneliness scale (e.g., UCLA Loneliness Scale) at baseline, weekly (weeks 1–6), and post-intervention.
  - Perceived conversational depth: validated depth/engagement scale or a multi-item index capturing depth, mutual understanding, topic progression, and meaningfulness.
- Secondary outcomes
  - Belonging/social connectedness; perceived social value of AI mediation; emotional validation; interpersonal warmth; social presence; satisfaction; usage metrics (frequency, duration, prompts compliance); cognitive/affective trust.
- Mediators and moderators
  - Mediators: perceived emotional support quality; feeling heard; social presence.
  - Moderators: age group, baseline loneliness, prior chatbot exposure, topic type (personal vs non-personal), and baseline social support.
- Measurement schedule
  - Baseline; weekly interim assessments (weeks 1–6); post-intervention week 6.
  - Interaction metrics logged continuously (latency, prompts, message counts).
- Sample size and power
  - Pre-register power analysis to detect small-to-moderate effects on loneliness and depth; account for attrition typical in six-week digital trials; oversample to ensure adequate subgroup analyses (e.g., by age, baseline loneliness).
- Randomization and blinding
  - Individual randomization; participants cannot be blinded to latency; outcome assessors blinded to arm.
- Analysis plan
  - Linear mixed-effects models to model trajectories of loneliness and depth over time; fixed effects for arm, time, and arm×time interactions; random intercepts for participants.
  - Mediation analyses (e.g., multilevel structural equation modeling) to test whether feeling heard and perceived emotional support mediate loneliness and depth; moderated mediation tests for social presence.
  - Sensitivity analyses for compliance with prompts; dose-response analyses for latency windows (2–3s vs 4–5s).
- Ethical considerations
  - IRB approval; participant safety monitoring for increased loneliness or distress; opt-out and resources if users experience negative effects.
- Implementation considerations
  - Technical: implement precise latency control (2–5s) per turn; enforce per-message prompts; ensure consistent bot capabilities across arms.
  - UX: design prompts to be brief and non-burdensome; consider optional prompts after a warm-up period to reduce fatigue.
  - Data governance: preregister protocol; publish data and code to enable reproducibility.

4) Measurements and instruments (examples)
- Loneliness: UCLA Loneliness Scale (short or long form)
- Depth/engagement: validated conversational depth scale or depth-of-dialogue items plus social presence proxy
- Social presence: standardized SP scales (e.g., Short Social Presence Scale)
- Emotional validation: scales capturing perceived emotional support/validation
- Interpersonal warmth: warmth measures (perception of agent warmth)
- Trust: cognitive and affective trust scales (for triangulation)
- Engagement metrics: messages per session, session length, prompts compliance rate, latency distribution

5) Practical and theoretical considerations
- Context sensitivity: prior research shows latency effects vary by context and user characteristics; design should test for age effects, prior chatbot experience, and topic type (Fang et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) [Fang et al., 2025; Wang & Lo, 2025; Kim et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024].
- Mechanisms: aim to test whether slow mode works via social presence and feeling heard. If slow mode reduces social presence or perceived depth, it may fail to reduce loneliness; if paired with empathetic prompts and cues, it could enhance depth and belonging.
- Ethical considerations: ensure participant well-being, avoid inadvertent harm from latency; maintain transparency about the purpose of latency and friction.

6) Actionable insights for the author (concise recommendations)
- Do not assume slow mode will automatically reduce loneliness or increase depth. Treat slow mode as a hypothesis-driven friction lever to be tested with explicit loneliness and depth outcomes over six weeks (Fang et al., 2025; Merrill et al., 2025).
- Use a multi-arm design to disentangle the effects of latency, prompts, and social presence cues; include an instant-control arm and at least one slow-mode arm with prompts; optionally add a slow-mode plus cues arm.
- Prioritize measurement of:
  - Loneliness (primary)
  - Perceived conversational depth
  - Belonging and social connectedness
  - Feelings of being heard and perceived emotional support
  - Social presence and trust
  - Engagement metrics and adherence to prompts
- Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plan, including mediation and moderated mediation hypotheses (feeling heard and social presence as key channels).
- Use week-by-week analyses to detect early vs. late effects and to understand trajectories of loneliness and depth under friction.
- Pilot-test the latency window (2–5 seconds) and per-message prompts with a small sample before committing to a full six-week RCT to vet usability, prompt burden, and early signals on the mediators.
- Consider adaptive or personalized latency policies (e.g., allow user preference settings or dynamic adjustments by context) if a fixed slow mode proves detrimental for some users (e.g., younger users who prefer speed; older users who may benefit from slower pacing) (Wang & Lo, 2025; Kim et al., 2025) [Wang & Lo, 2025; Kim et al., 2025].
- Ensure a robust ethical framework: monitor for adverse effects, provide opt-out, and document any unintended consequences (e.g., increased loneliness with friction) and mitigation strategies (Merrill et al., 2025; Choedak, 2020).

Appendix: Key terms and constructs
- Loneliness: perceived discrepancy between desired and actual social connection.
- Perceived conversational depth: subjective sense of depth, richness, and mutual understanding in a dialogue with an AI agent.
- Social presence: perceived sense of another agent’s social presence and emotional availability during interaction.
- Feeling heard: perceived empathic attentiveness and responsiveness of the AI.
- Emotional support: perceived adequacy of emotional reassurance and validation provided by the AI.
- Interpersonal warmth: perceived warmth of the AI as a social actor.
- Slow mode: deliberate delay in AI responses (2–5 seconds) with a mandatory brief user prompt per message, intended as a friction-based lever to influence user experience.
