Title: Do AI-mediated interactions increase parasocial attachments to platform personas and crowd out in-person social engagement? A synthesis and design-focused plan for a longitudinal randomized study on high vs low AI mediation

Executive summary
- A broad synthesis of experimental and quasi-experimental evidence across AI-mediated interactions indicates that higher AI mediation tends to elevate parasocial-type attachments to AI platform personas and can reshape perceived social connectedness, though direct causal links to crowding out offline social engagement and loneliness are not uniformly established across all studies or timeframes (various sources cited throughout). Key signals include substantial increases in AI-related attachment and perceived AI empathy under high AI mediation conditions in several randomized or quasi-experimental designs (Chandra et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024; Omeish et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).
- Direct measurement of offline social activity and loneliness as outcomes in longitudinal randomized designs is relatively sparse. Some studies report associations between higher AI usage and reduced real-world socialization, while others find no robust crowding-out effect over short windows or observe loneliness reductions under AI-mediated social support, suggesting heterogeneous effects by context, modality, and duration (Fang et al., 2025; Chandra et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024; Xie & Wang, 2024; Yang & Oshio, 2025; Kelley et al., 2025).
- Measurement needs: robust, multi-dimensional assessment of parasocial attachments to platform personas, offline social engagement, and loneliness, preferably with cross-cultural validity and pre-registered analyses. Validated instruments include MMPR (and cross-cultural adaptations; Poland), SCI (PED/PSD), UCLA Loneliness Scale, MSPSS, F-SozU K‑6, SPS‑10/5, and parasocial scales (PSI/PSR) distinguished from one another (Garcia et al., 2022; Witkowska et al., 2025; Kelley et al., 2025; Plackett et al., 2024; Rubin et al., 1985/1987; Horton & Wohl, 1956; Omeish et al., 2025; Chandra et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).
- Actionable takeaway: to answer the core research question with causal inference, implement a longitudinal randomized trial that manipulates AI mediation intensity (high vs low) and includes explicit, longitudinal measures of offline social activity and loneliness, with mediation by parasocial attachment to AI personas and moderated by individual differences and culture. Design should integrate safety safeguards (e.g., AI chaperone concepts) and countermeasures to promote real-world social engagement when needed.

1) Background and research question
- Core question: Do AI-mediated interactions increase parasocial attachments to platform personas and crowd out in-person social engagement? A longitudinal randomized study comparing high vs low AI mediation on offline social activity and loneliness.
- Rationale: Parasocial relationships (PSRs) are well-established in media contexts and can extend to AI-mediated platform personas. Evidence suggests AI mediation can heighten parasocial-like attachments and perceived social support, but whether such attachments substitute for or complement real-world social engagement, and whether loneliness changes accordingly, remains unsettled in longitudinal randomized designs (Horton & Wohl, 1956; Itzchakov et al., 2021–2023; De Freitas et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025; Chandra et al., 2025; Omeish et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).
- Measurement landscape (selected instruments and contexts):
  - Parasocial Attachment/PSR: PSI/PSR scales ( Rubin et al., 1985; Rubin & Perse, 1987 ); MMPR (Garcia et al., 2022) and cross-cultural adaptations (Witkowska et al., 2025).
  - Loneliness and social connectedness: UCLA Loneliness Scale; MSPSS; F-SozU K‑6; SPS-10/5; SCI (PED/PSD) for emotional vs social contributors to disconnection (Kelley et al., 2025; Plackett et al., 2024; Kelley et al., 2025).
  - Offline social engagement: validated loneliness and social-activity metrics, plus objective/diary-based social activity indicators (where feasible) (Plackett et al., 2024; Hawkley 2010; Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015).
- Notable patterns across relevant work:
  - High AI mediation can raise AI attachment and perceived AI empathy; some studies show reductions in loneliness when AI is designed to be empathetic or to feel “heard,” but long-term offline substitution remains unclear (Chandra et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024; Itzchakov et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).
  - Some evidence points to no robust crowding-out of offline social activity in short windows, while other work indicates higher AI usage correlates with reduced offline socializing (Fang et al., 2025; Chandra et al., 2025; Omeish et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).

2) Evidence synthesis by core constructs

2.1 Parasocial attachments to platform personas under AI mediation
- Evidence that AI mediation elevates parasocial-type attachments and perceived empathy:
  - AU vs BU in a five-week randomized/exploratory design shows large increases in AI attachment (+32.99 percentage points) and perceived AI empathy (+25.8 pp), plus increased entertainment-motivated AI usage (Chandra et al., 2025).
  - AI mediation intensity (active usage) increases comfort in seeking AI-based social support and personal help, indicating broader perceived capabilities of AI in emotional support contexts (Chandra et al., 2025).
  - Across other AI contexts, high-quality listening and empathetic AI can increase relatedness/autonomy needs in lab settings, with limited loneliness reductions as a downstream consequence (Weinstein et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023; De Freitas et al., 2024).
- Cross-cultural and instrument considerations:
  - MMPR and cross-cultural validations (Poland) demonstrate that parasocial engagement to platform personas can be measured along multiple dimensions (Affective, Behavioral, Cognitive, Decisional) and adapted across cultures (Witkowska et al., 2025; Garcia et al., 2022).
  - SCI (PED/PSD) offers a framework to decompose perceived social disconnectedness into emotional and psychosocial pathways that could mediate AI-mediated PSR formation and offline engagement (Kelley et al., 2025).
- Practical implication: Parasocial attachments to AI personas appear malleable to AI mediation intensity and AI’s empathic design features, but their translation into real-world behavioral changes remains contingent on downstream outcomes (loneliness, offline social activity) and context.

2.2 Crowd-out of in-person social engagement (offline activity and loneliness)
- Longitudinal, offline outcomes evidence:
  - Four-week randomized designs (Fang et al., 2025) show higher daily AI usage associated with greater loneliness, more AI dependence, more problematic AI use, and less offline socialization; benefits of voice modalities may be ephemeral at high usage.
  - Chandra et al. (2025) include measures of loneliness and indicate that increases in AI-perceived attachment and AI empathy do not automatically translate into reductions in loneliness within five weeks; explicit offline outcomes were not reported in their results excerpt.
  - Other lines of evidence provide mixed signals: Loneliness reductions observed in AI companionship literature (De Freitas et al., 2024) but without consistent offline-behavior measures; some lab experiments show increased relatedness needs without loneliness reductions (Weinstein et al., 2025).
  - Cross-cultural and real-world substitution frameworks emphasize the potential for AI-mediated interactions to substitute offline sociality, particularly when AI provides continuous, personalized, emotionally salient interactions; however, robust causal data linking AI mediation to sustained crowding-out in real-world social activity are limited (Arets et al., 2025; Rath et al., 2025).
- Caveats:
  - Short durations (4–5 weeks) limit conclusions about long-term crowd-out; longer-term trajectories remain underexplored; some studies report no group-level offline activity differences, while others show subgroup- or usage-level effects.
  - Measurement variability (self-reports vs objective offline-activity proxies) complicates cross-study comparisons; cross-cultural validity of offline social-activity measures in AI-mediated contexts is still developing.

3) Moderators, mediators, and heterogeneity across studies

- Moderators of AI-mediated PSR changes:
  - Participant characteristics such as gender identity, prior AI usage tendency, baseline loneliness, and trait attachment patterns moderate AI-perceived empathy and attachment trajectories (Chandra et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025; Omeish et al., 2025; Yang & Oshio, 2025).
  - Cultural context matters for parasocial dynamics (Omeish et al., 2025; Witkowska et al., 2025).
- Mediators connecting AI mediation to outcomes:
  - Perceived empathy, autonomy, and relatedness needs satisfaction function as mediators for loneliness or well-being in AI-mediated interactions (Weinstein et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023; Reis et al., 2017).
  - Feeling heard (as a core mediator) demonstrates robust loneliness-reduction effects in AI interactions in some lab studies, though not always translating to longer-term offline outcomes (De Freitas et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025).
  - Anthropomorphism and perceived mind (theory-of-mind perceptions) can drive parasocial attachments in GenAI; these attachments can be moderated by attachment anxiety and anthropomorphic tendencies (Zhong & Luo, 2025).
- Cross-cultural measurement considerations:
  - MMPR adaptation in Poland demonstrates cross-cultural validity for measuring PSR, with measurement invariance considerations for cross-context research (Witkowska et al., 2025).

4) Proposed longitudinal randomized study design (high vs low AI mediation)

Objective
- To causally test whether higher AI mediation increases parasocial attachments to platform personas and whether this substitution tendency reduces offline social activity and/or increases loneliness over time.

Design
- Type: Multisite, longitudinal randomized controlled trial with two arms:
  - High AI mediation: participants interact with AI platform personas that are highly responsive, personalized, and endowed with memory-based context awareness and empathetic feedback across multiple daily contexts.
  - Low AI mediation: participants engage with AI in a minimal or transactional manner (limited empathy, minimal personalization) or a control condition with a non-AI/low-AI-augmentation interaction.
- Timeframe: 6–12 months with assessments at baseline, 3, 6, and 12 months (plus brief monthly checks to capture trajectories).
- Population: Adults aged 18–65+, stratified sampling to ensure representation across gender, age, culture (including cross-cultural sites if feasible), baseline loneliness, and baseline offline social activity.

Interventions and manipulation
- AI mediation intensity: operationalized through modality, responsiveness, memory features, and presence of empathetic cues. Consider multiple arms to test dose-response (e.g., low, medium, high AI mediation) in addition to a pure control.
- Contextual controls: ensure similar levels of non-AI social opportunities across arms to isolate AI mediation effects.

Primary outcomes
- Offline social activity:
  - Frequency and duration of in-person interactions per week, validated via self-reports and, where possible, objective proxies (e.g., diary entries, contextual prompts, or passive sensing with consent).
- Loneliness:
  - UCLA Loneliness Scale or comparable validated measure administered at each wave.
- Parasocial attachment to platform personas:
  - Parasocial attachment measured with MMPR (adapted to AI personas) and, where feasible, PSI vs PSR distinctions.
- Secondary outcomes:
  - Perceived social support (MSPSS, F-SozU K‑6, SPS-10/5), SCI PED/PSD components, and perceived empathy/reciprocity from AI.

Mediators and moderators
- Mediators: changes in parasocial attachments to AI personas, perceived AI empathy, and perceived reciprocity; need-to-belong and relatedness/autonomy needs satisfaction.
- Moderators: baseline loneliness, digital literacy, cultural context, personality traits (extraversion/introversion), prior AI exposure, and baseline social network size.

Analytic plan
- Primary analyses: linear mixed-effects models to test group-by-time interactions on offline social activity and loneliness.
- Mediation analyses: longitudinal mediation (e.g., cross-lagged or latent-growth mediation) to test whether changes in parasocial attachment mediate AI mediation effects on offline engagement and loneliness.
- Moderation analyses: test interactions to identify subgroups for whom high AI mediation has stronger crowding-out or substitution effects.
- Power and sample size: conduct a priori power analysis considering expected small-to-moderate effects, anticipated attrition, multiple waves, and potential cross-cultural sites.

Ethics, safety, and governance
- Safety safeguards: implement an AI chaperone-like safety overlay or real-time monitoring to prevent problematic parasocial escalation and to promote balanced social engagement where appropriate (Rath et al., 2025).
- Privacy and consent: robust data protection, transparent AI disclosures, opt-out options, and participant well-being monitoring.
- Pre-registration: preregister hypotheses, measurement plans, and analysis strategies; publish a detailed protocol.

Measurement considerations and recommended instruments
- Parasocial attachments:
  - MMPR (Garcia et al., 2022; Witkowska et al., 2025) adapted to AI personas; distinctions among affective, cognitive, behavioral, and decisional dimensions.
  - PSI vs PSR differentiation in analysis, to parse one-sided engagement from deeper relational bonds.
- Loneliness and social connectedness:
  - UCLA Loneliness Scale; MSPSS; F-SozU K‑6; SPS‑10/5; SCI (PED/PSD) to decompose emotional vs social contributors.
- Offline social activity:
  - Self-reported in-person activity frequencies, social event participation; objective proxies if privacy allows.
- AI-mediated interaction metrics:
  - Usage intensity, modality (text/voice), memory features, degree of personalization, perceived empathy, and perceived reciprocity.

5) Limitations and caveats
- Feasibility: multi-site longitudinal RCT with AI mediation manipulation is complex and resource-intensive; ensure phased implementation if needed.
- Generalizability: ensure diverse participant demographics and contexts to test cross-cultural validity of measures (MMPR, MMPR Polish adaptation; SCI PED/PSD; UC). 
- Causal inference: the design aims for causal inference, but real-world complexities (life events, concurrent social changes) require robust modeling and sensitivity analyses.
- Measurement burden: multi-wave assessments may increase respondent burden; balance depth with feasibility.

6) Practical implications for researchers, practitioners, and platform designers
- If high AI mediation increases parasocial attachments but does not sustainably crowd out offline social engagement, AI-mediated interactions can be used to augment social connectedness in supportive, non-substitutive ways (some contexts).
- If crowding-out emerges robustly, implement safeguards:
  - Prompts to engage offline social activities after intense AI interactions.
  - Design constraints to prevent over-reliance on AI for social needs; promote a hybrid social ecosystem.
- Cross-cultural considerations: tailor AI mediation features to cultural norms around social interaction, belonging, and trust; ensure measurement invariance across sites.

7) Actionable insights for authors
- Build a longitudinal randomized protocol with clear AI mediation manipulation and standardized offline-outcome measurement, drawing on the measurement toolkit outlined (MMPR, SCI, UCLA, MSPSS, F-SozU, SPS, etc.).
- Include cross-cultural validity plans and sample diversity to test generalizability.
- Pre-register hypotheses, data-collection plans, and analysis strategies; publish protocol papers and data sharing plans.
- Incorporate safety nets (AI chaperone-style safeguards, opt-outs) and report safety/ethical considerations in detail.

8) Conclusions
- The current body of evidence suggests AI mediation can heighten parasocial attachments to platform personas and influence perceived social connectedness, with mixed findings on whether such attachments crowd out offline social engagement. A rigorously designed longitudinal randomized study with explicit offline-outcome measurement is required to ascertain causal effects on crowding-out and loneliness, and to identify mediating mechanisms (parasocial attachment, perceived empathy, autonomy/relatedness needs) and moderators (culture, baseline loneliness, personality). The measurement framework should leverage MMPR, SCI, and validated loneliness/social-support scales, with attention to cross-cultural validity and ethical safeguards. The proposed design offers a concrete blueprint for addressing the core question and for informing responsible AI design and policy guidance.
