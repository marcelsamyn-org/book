# Can deliberate friction interventions maintain agency, empathy, and creative outcomes in AI-mediated conversations? An evidence-based synthesis

Executive summary
- Deliberate friction interventions (e.g., enforced reflection prompts, time delays, structured pauses) are proposed design mechanisms to preserve or enhance user agency and relational depth in AI-mediated conversations, while aiming to sustain or improve creative outcomes. The evidence base largely comprises theoretical rationales, design guidelines, and scattered empirical findings across domains (art, education, design, healthcare, and enterprise) rather than a single, unified RCT. Key themes include the value of reflection, metacognition, and autonomy-supportive design for reducing homogenization and learned helplessness in AI-assisted tasks [Chen & Schmidt, 2024; Prom­siri, 2025; Maslych et al., 2025; McGuire et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2022].
- Across disciplines, friction is frequently portrayed as beneficial when it (a) interrupts autopilot, (b) scaffolds reasoning and justification, (c) fosters accumulation of evaluative thinking, and (d) preserves or enhances relational depth through slower, more deliberate interaction. However, explicit, replicated empirical evidence tying friction interventions to quantified agency, empathy, and creativity outcomes in AI-mediated conversations remains limited and highly context-dependent [DeryaSefer, 2024; Maslych et al., 2025; MIT Sloan, 2024; Harang & Aral, 2024].
- The practical takeaway is nuanced: friction can be valuable when designed to support autonomy, critical evaluation, and human-led sensemaking, but risks include reduced flow, perceived manipulation, cognitive overload, or miscalibration if not context-sensitive or ethically governed. The strongest actionable path forward is to design, test, and report rigorous, multi-method evaluations that explicitly measure agency, empathy/relational depth, and creativity across domains and task types [Chen et al., 2024; McGuire et al., 2024; Prom­siri, 2025].

Scope and research question
- Research question: Can deliberate friction interventions (e.g., enforced reflection prompts, time delays) maintain agency, empathy, and creative outcomes in AI-mediated conversations?
- Core interest: Identify whether frictionary design elements can offset homogenization, protect independence of thought, and preserve or enhance relational depth in AI-enabled interactions, while sustaining creative production and exploration.

Evidence base and synthesis

1) Evidence patterns from AI-mediated creativity and friction-design research
- Short-term performance with AI does not guarantee lasting creativity; independent work afterward tends to show dampened creativity among AI users unless friction-aware countermeasures preserve autonomy (University of Toronto–style findings summarized in broader articles) [DeryaSefer, 2024].
- Friction-inspired interventions (reflection prompts, time delays) are not consistently reported or evaluated in the base sources. Nonetheless, reasoning-enhancement strategies (self-explanation, justification prompts, deliberate pacing) are repeatedly highlighted as pathways to preserve or enhance agency and depth in AI-human collaboration [Chen & Schmidt, 2024; Maslych et al., 2025; McGuire et al., 2024; Prom­siri, 2025].
- In real-world settings, friction-like cues have been studied for improving attention, reducing automation bias, and supporting responsible decision-making, though often in non-AI-dialogue tasks (e.g., framing, counterfactual prompts, or contrastive explanations). Evidence suggests that moderate friction can improve accuracy and trust without sacrificing throughput when carefully calibrated [MIT Sloan, 2024; Harang & Aral, 2024].

2) Domain-specific insights that bear on friction design
- Art, design, and music co-creation contexts: Co-creation (human + AI) tends to outperform editing AI outputs alone, with creative self-efficacy as a key mechanism. Friction interventions that re-center human authorship and force justification of AI contributions can sustain or elevate creativity and agency over time [McGuire et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025/2026].
- Education and personal development contexts: Desirable difficulties, spaced reflection, retrieval practice, and delayed feedback can foster durable learning and metacognition—principles that align with friction strategies to preserve autonomy, curiosity, and critical thinking [Prom­siri, 2025; Ullrich & Lutgendorf, 2002; Hensley & Munn, 2020; Stephenson & Zygouris, 2007].
- Professional decision support and risk contexts: Structured arbitration, contrastive explanations, and adaptive friction can improve decision quality and reduce bias when AI outputs are integrated into human teams, albeit with caution about over-reliance and epistemic risk. This has direct implications for friction design in conversations where accountability and transparency are paramount [Cabitza et al., 2021; Harang & Aral, 2024; Hao et al., 2024].

Gaps and limitations
- There is no consolidated, widely generalizable body of experimental evidence on deliberate friction interventions (enforced reflection prompts, time delays) in AI-mediated conversations with standardized metrics for agency, empathy, and creativity.
- Many studies focus on one domain (e.g., poetry, design ideation, radiology decision-making, or education) or rely on qualitative design guidance rather than controlled experiments with preregistered outcomes.
- When friction interventions are studied, they are often embedded within broader co-creative or decision-support paradigms rather than isolated as standalone experimental manipulations, complicating cross-study comparability and meta-analytic synthesis.

Actionable insights and protocols for authors (practical steps to test friction interventions)

A. Experimental framework concepts
- Design a multi-arm randomized or factorial study that isolates friction modalities and measures: agency (perceived control and influence), empathy/relational depth (perceived warmth, understanding, and collaboration quality), and creativity (diversity, novelty, usefulness of ideas or outputs).
- Consider task domains with varying cognitive load and creative demands (e.g., brainstorming/design ideation, writing, data interpretation, conflict-resolution simulations).

B. Friction modalities to test
- Enforced reflection prompts
  - After a suggested idea or AI output, require the user to justify, critique, or reframe before accepting or moving forward.
  - Example prompts: “What assumptions underlie this idea? What is one alternative direction? How would you test its feasibility?”
- Time delays / deliberate pacing
  - Implement short, user-visible pauses (e.g., 2–5 seconds for routine replies; longer pauses for complex prompts) to allow reflection and reduce impulsive acceptance.
- Hybrid friction with optional human-in-the-loop (OoI)
  - Provide a switch to insert a human-in-the-loop review at critical moments, enabling accountability and richer feedback while preserving user agency.
- Contrastive and multi-explanation prompts
  - Present opposing AI viewpoints or explanations and require user evaluation before selecting a course of action, to counteract bias toward a single solution and promote critical thinking.
- Reflective journaling or post-interaction synthesis
  - After sessions, prompt users to articulate what they learned, how their perspective shifted, and how they would apply the insights in future tasks.

C. Metrics and measurement plan
- Agency
  - Scales capturing perceived control, autonomy, and influence on the conversation; measures of user-initiated pivots and topic steering.
- Empathy/Relational depth
  - Perceived empathy, warmth, and mutual understanding; depth of collaborative engagement; relationship satisfaction with the AI partner.
- Creativity
  - Output diversity, novelty, usefulness, and transferability; domain-specific rubrics for evaluation (e.g., originality in ideation, quality of outputs, coherence with brief).
- Task performance and satisfaction
  - Objective task outcomes and subjective satisfaction with the process; flow and engagement indicators.
- Process metrics
  - Response latency, number of friction prompts, time spent reflecting, turn-taking patterns, and engagement trajectories over sessions.

D. Design principles to guide friction implementation
- Intentionality and transparency
  - Clearly communicate why friction is used, what it aims to achieve, and how users can opt out or adjust friction levels if needed.
- Autonomy-respecting friction
  - Provide user controls to customize friction intensity and to toggle friction on/off per task or per session.
- Adaptive friction
  - Calibrate friction dynamically based on user state (engagement, fatigue signals, performance) and task complexity to maintain balance between flow and depth.
- Safety and ethics
  - Guardrails to prevent manipulation, ensure privacy, and maintain trust; include ethical review and alignment with user well-being as core criteria.
- Documentation and reproducibility
  - Publish friction protocols with precise prompts, timing, and evaluation measures to enable replication and cross-study comparisons.

E. Implementation considerations
- Domain-specific calibration
  - Tailor friction modes to the task, domain, and user cohort; e.g., high-stakes or ethically sensitive domains may benefit from stronger friction with robust justification requirements.
- Evaluation strategy
  - Pre-registered hypotheses, power analyses for sample sizes, and robust reporting of effect sizes and confidence intervals.
- Cross-domain generalizability
  - Include multiple domains in the experimental design to assess heterogeneity of friction effects and to identify robust patterns.

F. Practical recommendations for practitioners
- Start small and scale up
  - Begin with lightweight friction (brief reflection prompts, small delays) in low-stakes contexts (e.g., ideation, brainstorming) and evaluate user reception before adding more complex friction schemas.
- Focus on co-creative framing
  - Position AI as a co-creator with explicit human leadership roles; friction should support human sensemaking and justification rather than supplant it.
- Leverage governance and ethics
  - Embed transparency, accountability, and equity considerations into friction design; maintain clear provenance of AI contributions, attribution, and decision logs.
- Report rigorously
  - Publish both quantitative outcomes and qualitative reflections, including methodological details (prompt templates, friction parameters, and evaluation rubrics) to facilitate replication and meta-analytic work.

G. Caveats and cautions
- Friction can backfire if overapplied, leading to frustration or disengagement; careful calibration and user control are essential.
- The generalizability of friction effects across domains remains to be established; domain-specific adaptation and validation are critical.

H. Representative domains and use cases
- Arts and design (co-creation in art, music, or writing)
- Education and tutoring with AI tutors that emphasize metacognition and autonomy
- Healthcare decision-support dialogues requiring careful justification and accountability
- Enterprise collaboration tools that synthesize data while preserving employee agency and relational depth

I. Suggested reporting and dissemination plan
- Pre-registration of hypotheses and friction configurations
- Open reporting of effect sizes, confidence intervals, and robust standards for replicability
- Inclusion of both qualitative insights (interview or think-aloud protocols) and quantitative metrics

Caveats to consider
- The evidence base for friction interventions in AI-mediated conversations remains emergent; ongoing replication and cross-domain validation are necessary.
- Ethical considerations, user well-being, and equity must be integral to friction design and evaluation.

Conclusions
- Deliberate friction interventions show promise as design levers to preserve agency, empathy, and creativity in AI-mediated conversations when they are thoughtfully aligned with human-centered goals, domain context, and governance standards. The strongest direction forward is to design, implement, and publish rigorously controlled studies that explicitly test friction modalities (reflection prompts, time delays), with standardized metrics for agency, empathy/relational depth, and creativity across multiple domains.
