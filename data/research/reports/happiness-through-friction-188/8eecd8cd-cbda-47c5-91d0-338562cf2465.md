# Report: Invisible Scaffolds in AI Systems, Perceived Autonomy, and Intrinsic Motivation — Evolutionary Trajectories with Longer Exposure

Executive summary
- Across AI-enabled learning and work contexts, invisible scaffolds—design elements that guide performance or cognition without explicit user-facing prompts—can shape perceived autonomy and intrinsic motivation through needs-support (autonomy, competence, relatedness) and sensemaking processes. The SDT/METUX literatures provide converging evidence that autonomy-supportive, transparent, and human-in-the-loop scaffolds tend to bolster motivation over time, whereas opaque or coercive scaffolds can erode perceived autonomy and dampen intrinsic motivation, particularly under longer exposure or higher cognitive load (Promsiri, 2025; Calvo et al., 2020; Balaskas et al., 2025; Vaassen, 2022).
- Longitudinal field data are scarce but emerging: several well-cited works argue for tracking autonomy and motivation trajectories over weeks to months of sustained exposure to AI scaffolds (Promsiri, 2025; Ma & Chen, 2025; Long et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025). When such data exist, autonomy-supportive scaffolds often mediate or moderate intrinsic motivation via SDT pathways (autonomy competence, relatedness) and related constructs (self-regulation, metacognition).
- Across domains and designs, key mechanisms include: (a) autonomy-supportive personalization and transparent justification; (b) reduced cognitive load and enhanced sensemaking; (c) maintaining relatedness through human-in-the-loop or social scaffolds; (d) boundary conditions defined by task uncertainty, cultural context, user differences (gender, age, digital literacy), and scaffold visibility.
- Actionable implications for authors/designers: (i) operationalize invisible scaffolds as autonomy- and competence-supportive mechanisms with explicit fading plans; (ii) preserve user agency via transparent, interpretable feedback; (iii) embed human-in-the-loop and culturally attuned elements to sustain relatedness; (iv) design longitudinal studies with validated SDT/METUX measures and multi-sphere assessment to map autonomy trajectories and boundary conditions.

1) Introduction and research framing
- Research question: What is the impact of invisible scaffolds in AI systems on perceived autonomy and intrinsic motivation, and how do these effects evolve with longer exposure?
- Context: In AI-enabled learning and work settings, invisibility of scaffolds is proposed as a design principle to keep humans in the loop, while still providing developmental supports. Evidence drawn from SDT (Self-Determination Theory) and METUX (Motivation, Engagement and Thriving in UX) suggests autonomy-supportive design yields better intrinsic motivation and engagement, but opacity or misalignment can erode perceived autonomy over time (Calvo et al., 2020; Peters et al., 2018; Prom­siri, 2025).
- Conceptual anchors:
  - Self-Determination Theory (SDT): Autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs shape intrinsic motivation and well-being; satisfaction of these needs predicts engagement and persistence across domains (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Ryan & Deci, 2017).
  - METUX framework: Six spheres of technology experience (Adoption, Interface, Task, Behavior, Life, Society) for diagnosing autonomy-support and well-being in digital experiences; emphasizes multi-sphere, longitudinal assessment to detect potential boundary conditions (Calvo et al., 2020; Peters et al., 2018; Peters, 2022).
- Key empirical gaps: The literature provides strong theoretical and cross-sectional evidence, designs, and prototypes; however, robust longitudinal field data linking invisible scaffolds to autonomy trajectories and intrinsic motivation across varied domains remain limited (Promsiri, 2025; Balaskas et al., 2025).

2) Theoretical framing and mechanisms
- SDT foundations (autonomy, competence, relatedness)
  - Autonomy: feeling volitional and self-endorsed in actions; autonomy-supportive contexts preserve choice and meaningful engagement (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Ryan & Deci, 2017).
  - Competence: feeling effective; feedback that clarifies progress without undermining agency fosters intrinsic motivation (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Chiu et al., 2020).
  - Relatedness: social connectedness and supportive interpersonal contexts reinforce engagement, particularly in collaborative AI tasks (Deci & Ryan, 2012; Xia et al., 2022).
- METUX: six spheres for autonomy assessment
  - Adoption, Interface, Task, Behavior, Life, Society: assessing need satisfaction/frustration across these layers illuminates where invisible scaffolds support or thwart autonomy over time (Calvo et al., 2020; Peters et al., 2018; Peters, 2022).
- Invisible scaffolds as a design category
  - Promoted by user-centered design, invisibility aims to reduce cognitive load and prevent overt interruption, while still shaping cognitive strategies and task approaches. However, invisibility can threaten perceived justice and autonomy if opaque or misaligned with user goals (Vaassen, 2022; Brown et al., 2025).

3) Evidence synthesis: longitudinal trajectories and longer exposure
Note: Across the literature, longitudinal designs are still sparse; where available, they illuminate trajectories rather than single-point associations.

3.1 Longitudinal field evidence and longer exposure
- Ma & Chen (2025) – AI-enabled language learning with/without teacher scaffolding (16 weeks, N=150)
  - Key findings: AI with teacher scaffolding yielded greater and more sustained proficiency gains and motivation; SDT needs satisfaction mediated motivation (autonomy, competence, relatedness) and contextualized AI feedback via teacher scaffolding supported autonomy and relatedness; without human scaffolding, AI alone produced smaller gains and weaker motivation trajectories; cultural context influenced acceptance and engagement (Ma & Chen, 2025).
  - Mechanisms: human interpretation and contextualization of AI outputs anchored autonomy-supportive feedback; relatedness maintained via teacher involvement; autonomy preserved through agency in self-regulated practice.
  - Limitations: cross-domain generalization limits; 16 weeks is intermediate; longer exposure remains untested in this design.
- Longitudinal prompts, customization, and scaffolds (Long et al., 2024; DIS 2024)
  - 7-step AI workflow for science communication; two customization features (prompt display/editability; user-authored hooks); 3 phases over 12 users, 3 weeks
  - Findings: familiarization phase; after familiarization, usefulness rose (post-familiarization usefulness +12.1%, p<0.005); prompt-editing drove most of the usefulness gains (11.4%, p<0.005); ownership increased with continued involvement; novelty did not rapidly fade; internalization via appropriation supported autonomy
  - Implications: invisible scaffolds that users can personalize (prompts) promote autonomy and intrinsic motivation more robustly than passive scaffolds; longer exposure supports internalization when users exercise control and co-create (Long et al., 2024).
- Li et al. (2025) – 14 empirical studies on AI-integrated scaffolding in K-12 EFL writing
  - Key synthesis: AI scaffolds can reduce cognitive load, foster ideation, and support self-regulated learning without undermining autonomy; boundary conditions include equitable access, alignment with theory, and the need for longitudinal data to map autonomy/motivation trajectories; no consistent longitudinal data presented (Li & Wilson, 2025).
  - Mechanisms: three scaffold categories (cognitive, creative, language enhancement) can either support autonomy or risk undermining ownership if misaligned with learner decision making.
  - Implication: invisible scaffolds may be effective when embedded in a human-centered design that preserves learner choice and supports internalization, but longitudinal validation is needed (Li & Wilson, 2025).
- Jansen et al. (2025) – Situated Expectancy-Value Theory (SEVT) with AI socialization
  - Theoretical framing: AI functions as a socializer shaping expectancies and task values; identity, social comparison, and context modulate motivational trajectories; long-term trajectories require longitudinal testing
  - Practical implication: AI as socializing agent can influence autonomy and intrinsic motivation through evolving self-beliefs; boundary conditions depend on identity, social context, and AI transparency (Jansen et al., 2025).

3.2 Short-term/invisible scaffolds and autonomy perception
- Balaskas et al. (2025) – The Psychology of EdTech Nudging
  - Cross-sectional (N ≈ 740) with design features: Perceived Personalization (PP) directly boosts intrinsic motivation and autonomy; Nudge Exposure (NE) increases motivation via autonomy and reduced cognitive load; Perceived Persuasive Platform Design (PPS) boosts motivation directly but can increase cognitive load and reduce autonomy indirectly; autonomy (PAUTO) mediates effects; cognitive load (COG) mediates negative effects
  - Moderation: effects vary by gender, age, education, digital literacy, and exposure to persuasive features; cross-sectional design limits causal inferences about trajectories; still, results highlight how invisible scaffolds differentially affect autonomy and motivation across subgroups (Balaskas et al., 2025).
- Brown et al. (2025) – Real-time automated feedback in non-routine work
  - Randomized experiment: real-time automated feedback increases trust in algorithmic ratings and reduces rating surprise under high task uncertainty; sensemaking feedback supports autonomy/competence; transparency remains crucial to sustain perceived justice and autonomy
  - Limitation: short-term experimental context; longitudinal field data needed to map trajectories and fading/strengthening of autonomy over longer exposure (Brown et al., 2025).

3.3 Theoretical/ethical framing of opacity and autonomy
- Vaassen (2022) – AI opacity and personal autonomy
  - Opacity undermines autonomy when algorithms’ decisions are opaque in consequential domains (bail hearings, medical diagnoses, recruitment); argues for transparency to sustain autonomy; provides normative ground for making invisible scaffolds ethically accountable.
- Calvo, Peters, and colleagues (2020; 2022)
  - METUX six-sphere framework; autonomy-support is multi-scalar; ethical design imperative to preserve autonomy; emphasizes the need for longitudinal, six-sphere analysis of autonomy and well-being in AI systems.
- Hutson (2025) – Heutagogy and AI agents as co-designers
  - Emergent autonomy through agent collaboration; long-term autonomy and identity work through iterative agent design; underscores co-design with diverse user populations to sustain autonomy and motivation over longer horizons.

4) Gaps, boundary conditions, and boundary fading
- Longitudinal data gaps: Despite promising signals, robust longitudinal evidence mapping autonomy/motivation trajectories across domains and longer time frames remains scarce (Promsiri, 2025; Li et al., 2025; Ma & Chen, 2025; Long et al., 2024).
- Boundary conditions:
  - Scaffold visibility: When scaffolds are invisible or opaque, autonomy-support requires alternative sensemaking scaffolds (e.g., explainability, transparency, opportunity for user control) to prevent autonomy erosion (Vaassen, 2022; Brown et al., 2025; Calvo et al., 2020).
  - Cultural and domain differences: Autonomy and motivation trajectories differ by culture, discipline, and learner characteristics; the extent to which invisible scaffolds sustain autonomy over time depends on these factors (Ma & Chen, 2025; Li et al., 2025).
  - Task type and uncertainty: High-uncertainty tasks benefit more from real-time sensemaking scaffolds; lower-uncertainty tasks may rely more on autonomy-support and internalization processes (Brown et al., 2025; Prom­siri, 2025; Xie et al., 2023).
- Measurement diversity: A mix of SDT-based scales, METUX, and additional instruments (e.g., BANGS for need satisfaction/frustration; ACTA for adoption autonomy/competence) is recommended to capture multi-faceted constructs like autonomy, agency, creativity, and motivation in AI contexts (Peters et al., 2018; Ballou et al., 2024; Li & Wilson, 2025).

5) Practical implications and design guidelines
- Design for autonomy-supportive invisibility
  - Implement invisible scaffolds that preserve meaningful user choice, provide transparent rationale for AI actions, and enable user overrides. Combine with explicit (but optional) explainability where users request it (Vaassen, 2022; Calvo et al., 2020).
- Maintain relatedness in AI-mediated contexts
  - Incorporate human-in-the-loop elements or social scaffolds to preserve relatedness; even when AI scaffolds are invisible, ensure opportunities for collaboration, feedback, and emotional safety (Ma & Chen, 2025; Hutson, 2025; Xia et al., 2022).
- Optimize cognitive load and sensemaking
  - Use real-time feedback and sensemaking scaffolds to reduce cognitive load and improve interpretability of AI outputs; ensure feedback reduces rating surprise and supports progress (Brown et al., 2025; Gagné et al., 2022; Hermsen et al., 2016).
- Longitudinal measurement and boundary testing
  - Plan longitudinal field studies with multi-wave measures of autonomy, competence, relatedness, intrinsic motivation, and well-being; incorporate multiple contexts (education, work) and diverse populations to identify boundary conditions and fading-in/out dynamics (Calvo et al., 2020; Peters, 2022; Li et al., 2025).
- Domain-specific adaptive fading
  - Develop adaptive fading schedules that gradually shift autonomy-support from scaffolding toward learner self-regulation as competence and identity grow; tailor fading to learner subgroups (e.g., gender, achievement, digital literacy) and domain-specific norms (Long et al., 2024; Ma & Chen, 2025; Li et al., 2025).

6) Actionable insights for researchers and authors
- Ground studies in SDT and METUX as core design principles
  - Use METUX Scales and SDT-based measures to capture need satisfaction/frustration, autonomy support, and intrinsic motivation across multiple spheres and time points.
- Design for longitudinal rigor
  - Implement longitudinal field designs with repeated measures (baseline, mid-point, long-term follow-ups) to map autonomy trajectories and boundary conditions for invisible scaffolds.
- Include diverse contexts and populations
  - Study across domains (education, workplace, health tech) and populations with varied digital literacy and cultural backgrounds to identify boundary conditions and equity implications.
- Operationalize invisibility ethically
  - Clearly articulate what constitutes “invisible” scaffolds in each study context, and measure perceived invisibility alongside autonomy and motivation to distinguish effects of opacity from autonomy-support.
- Transparent reporting
  - Report effect sizes, trajectories, and moderation/mediation analyses; provide practical design guidelines and a replicable longitudinal protocol to support cumulative knowledge-building.

7) Conclusions
- Invisible scaffolds in AI systems can influence perceived autonomy and intrinsic motivation, particularly when they align with SDT principles and are complemented by sensemaking supports and human-in-the-loop contexts. Longer exposure tends to amplify the importance of autonomy-supportive design, but invisibility raises challenges around transparency and perceived justice that require explicit scaffolding (explainability, control) or alternative multi-sphere supports (METUX).
- The strongest evidence for sustained autonomy and intrinsic motivation with invisible scaffolds arises when scaffolds:
  - Support autonomy and competence without undermining self-endorsed goals,
  - Maintain relatedness through human or social scaffolds,
  - Reduce cognitive load and provide interpretable feedback,
  - Are integrated within a human-in-the-loop design and tested across time in longitudinal field studies.
- Gaps remain in longitudinal evidence, cross-domain generalizability, measurement harmonization (autonomy vs agency), and explicit fading/increasing visibility dynamics of scaffolds. Addressing these gaps will enable robust, ethical, and effective design of invisible scaffolds that sustain autonomy and intrinsic motivation in AI-enabled futures.
