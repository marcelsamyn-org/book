Title: Invisible versus Explicit AI Support in Creative and Knowledge Work: A Synthesis of Evidence on Flow, Autonomy, and Sustainable Performance

Executive summary
- Across domains, invisible (background, non-surfacing) AI support is proposed as a design principle to sustain flow and preserve perceived autonomy by reducing interruptions and cognitive load. However, the evidence base for purely invisible AI improving flow and sustainable performance is mixed and context-dependent. Several lines of work converge on the view that invisibility can preserve autonomy and flow when paired with robust sensemaking supports, governance, and user agency; yet opacity without sensemaking scaffolds can erode trust and perceived justice, particularly under uncertainty or risk. Visible, explicit AI guidance often enhances sensemaking, provenance, accountability, and trust in high-stakes or nuanced tasks, but can disrupt flow if overbearing or misaligned with user autonomy. The emerging consensus supports a hybrid, task- and phase-sensitive approach: background AI for low-friction, routine, or rapid iteration; surfaced outputs or explanations at critical decision points to sustain autonomy and learning. Actionable takeaways include designing adaptable visibility, embedding sensemaking interventions, and building governance that preserves human agency while leveraging AI’s strengths.
- Core quantitative signals come from diverse streams:
  - Real-time automated feedback in high-uncertainty tasks increased perceived trustworthiness of algorithmic ratings and reduced final-surprise (Brown et al., 2025) but involved explicit feedback (not purely invisible) and highlighted the importance of sensemaking interventions (Brown et al., 2025). DOI: 10.1016/j.chb.2025.108605.
  - Implicit guidance can sustain autonomy while enabling plan improvement in controlled experiments, albeit with domain- and task-specific contingencies (Nakahashi & Yamada, 2021); Bayesian Theory of Mind reasoning underpins how implicit guidance can help users maintain agency (Nakahashi & Yamada, 2021). No broad numeric effect sizes reported in the primary paper, but qualitative support for autonomy-preserving guidance.
  - In design-education contexts, invisible AI can support ideation and rapid iteration if it remains under human oversight and is paired with sensemaking prompts; explicit guidance tends to elevate accountability and evaluative clarity but can hinder flow if overused (Sîrb & Petrovici, 2025).
  - Across domains (healthcare, manufacturing, education, design), transparent or adaptive transparency generally improves trust calibration, whereas pure opacity risks misalignment; adaptive transparency balanced to user expertise and task context tends to perform best for trust and uptake (Yu & Li, 2022; van de Merwe et al., 2022; Seong et al., 2025; Felzmann et al., 2020).
- Bottom line: Invisible support can support flow and sustained performance when designed with explicit human-in-the-loop controls, task- and phase-sensitivity, and transparent governance. Explicit guidance remains valuable for high-stakes, high-uncertainty, or ethically salient work. A hybrid, context-adaptive approach is most consistently supported by the literature to optimize flow, autonomy, and sustainable performance.

1) Introduction: Research question, scope, and framing
Research question
- Is invisible AI support—non-intrusive, background assistance that does not surface suggested outputs—more conducive to flow, perceived autonomy, and sustainable performance than explicit, visible AI assistance in creative or knowledge-work tasks?

Scope and rationale
- The literature base across design education, organizational AI, healthcare, education, and domain-specific human–AI collaboration provides converging evidence about when, how, and for whom background AI supports flow and autonomy, and when explicit guidance is more beneficial. Several threads are especially relevant:
  - Sensemaking and autonomy: When AI support surfaces rationale and supports sensemaking, autonomy satisfaction and flow increase, particularly under uncertainty (Brown et al., 2025; Nakahashi & Yamada, 2021).
  - Transparency and trust: Transparency—whether surface-level or adaptive—facilitates trust; opacity without adequate sensemaking scaffolds reduces perceived organizational justice and receptivity to AI feedback (Yu & Li, 2022; Anichini et al., 2024; Felzmann et al., 2020).
  - Task- and phase-sensitivity: Early ideation versus execution, routine versus high-stakes decisions, and domain-specific constraints influence whether invisible or explicit AI guidance maximizes flow and sustainable performance (Sîrb & Petrovici, 2025; McKinsey, 2025; Boussioux et al., 2023).
  - Autonomy as a design objective: A recurring design aim is to preserve human autonomy and sense of agency while leveraging AI’s efficiency and analytic power (Nakahashi & Yamada, 2021; Sentisight AI, 2025; Li & Wilson, 2025).

2) Evidence synthesis by domain and design principle
Note: Inline citations refer to the cited sources in each claim.

2.1 Flow, autonomy, and sensemaking: invisible vs explicit AI
- Invisible AI and flow
  - Theoretical/experimental work suggests that implicit guidance can preserve autonomy by enabling humans to improve plans themselves after perceiving helpful cues, with AI acting as a facilitator rather than an orthogonal authority (Nakahashi & Yamada, 2021). This implicates invisible guidance as beneficial for flow when humans retain agency and can intervene. Quantitative data are sparse; the primary evidence is experimental and qualitative.
  - In design-education contexts, invisible AI can support ideation and baseline scaffolding without forcing outputs, provided there are sensemaking interventions and opportunities for human refinement (Naik et al., 2025; Sîrb & Petrovici, 2025).

- Explicit AI and trust/flow
  - Real-time automated feedback can increase trust in algorithmic performance ratings under high task uncertainty by reducing rating surprise and improving knowledge of results; however, this is a case where feedback surfaced outputs and rationale, thus not purely invisible (Brown et al., 2025) [DOI: 10.1016/j.chb.2025.108605].
  - In healthcare and aviation contexts, transparent explanations and rationale are critical for trust and safety; pure invisibility without traceable reasoning can erode trust, especially for complex tasks or high-stakes decisions (Anichini et al., 2024; van de Merwe et al., 2022; Seong et al., 2025).

- Design takeaway
  - Flow and autonomy are typically best supported by a hybrid approach: invisible AI handles routine, background processing to minimize interference; explicit guidance surfaces at critical junctures where autonomy and accountability require explicit human-in-the-loop validation.

2.2 Trust, transparency, and justification
- Transparency by design
  - A robust literature base shows that adaptive, well-scaffolded transparency improves trust when aligned with user state and task demands. Pure, static opacity often undermines trust; adaptive strategies that balance explainability with cognitive load tend to outperform both extreme opacity and excess surface detail (Yu & Li, 2022; Felzmann et al., 2020; van de Merwe et al., 2022; Seong et al., 2025).
- Just-in-time explainability
  - In safety-critical environments, explainability and audit trails are essential for trust, while maintaining flow; design patterns that make rationales visible on demand (or in a minimally intrusive way) often yield the best trust calibration (Zhang et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2022).

- Implications for invisible AI in knowledge work
  - Invisible AI can be acceptable if explainability and traceability remain accessible in a non-disruptive manner. The mere absence of surface outputs cannot substitute for understandability of AI reasoning, especially where professional judgment or ethics are at stake.

2.3 Sustainability and performance: measurable outcomes and trade-offs
- Productivity and performance signals
  - In organizational contexts, AI usage that focuses on task support and background automation is associated with productivity gains for both routine and some non-routine work, particularly for novices or less experienced workers (Zhang et al., 2025; Wei et al., 2025). However, gains depend on task type, domain, and how AI is integrated and governed.
  - In high-velocity or high-stakes workplaces, controlled experiments show that AI augmentation can improve efficiency but must be balanced with skill development, accountability, and ethics to preserve long-term performance.
- Sustainability concerns
  - A robust design approach requires measuring long-term sustainability, not only short-term performance, to avoid skill degradation or over-reliance on automation. The literature emphasizes longitudinal assessment and governance to ensure durable improvements.

2.4 Domain-specific patterns and governance
- Education (design, SRL)
  - AI scaffolding can support self-regulated learning while preserving autonomy, but requires explicit alignment with pedagogy, ethics, and equity. Invisible scaffolding is possible if it remains supportive and non-intrusive, and if learners retain control and reflective opportunities (Li & Wilson, 2025; Lan & Zhou, 2025).
- Radiology and medical imaging
  - The visibility of AI guidance interacts with tacit clinical knowledge; invisible AI work may necessitate arbitration, audit trails, and explicit human oversight to sustain trust and professional standards (Anichini et al., 2024; Star & Strauss, 1999; Pesapane et al., 2018).
- Creative design and education
  - In design education, invisible AI can support engagement and ideation, but human mentorship remains critical for originality and critical thinking; a balanced approach with explicit pedagogical scaffolds is recommended (Sîrb & Petrovici, 2025; Daniels et al., 2025).

3) Quantitative findings and gaps (synthesis)
- Where numeric results exist:
  - Brown et al. (2025) report increased perceived trust in algorithmic ratings with real-time feedback under uncertainty; exact effect sizes are not provided in the abstract excerpt.
  - Nakahashi & Yamada (2021) provide experimental evidence that implicit guidance is effective for maintaining autonomy, but without publicly reported effect sizes in the accessible material.
  - Zhang et al. (2025) show AI usage positively relates to innovation behavior, with self-efficacy mediating the AI–innovation path and openness/job complexity moderating the link; these are workplace metrics rather than flow/autonomy measurements, but they reflect motivational pathways relevant to autonomy and sustainable performance.
  - Yu & Li (2022) demonstrate that transparency increases trust through two mediating pathways (perceived effectiveness; perceived discomfort), signaling a nuanced, context-dependent effect of visibility on trust.
  - Felzmann et al. (2020) propose a “Transparency by Design” framework, arguing for ex-ante incorporation of transparency principles; the framework is conceptual and not experimental.
  - Li & Wilson (2025) show AI scaffolding can support autonomy and creativity in K-12 contexts; the evidence base spans 14 studies with qualitative emphasis and lacking standardized cross-study metrics for flow/autonomy.
- Gaps
  - There is no single, comprehensive, controlled, cross-domain experimental study that directly compares invisible (background) versus explicit (visible) AI guidance across creative and knowledge-work tasks with standardized flow/autonomy metrics.
  - Most studies provide evidence on trust, autonomy, or creativity in isolation or within specific contexts; generalization to broader knowledge-work and creative tasks requires targeted experiments.

4) Actionable guidelines for practitioners and researchers
- For practitioners designing AI in knowledge-work and creative tasks:
  - Use a hybrid visibility strategy: background AI for routine, data-driven, or fast-paced tasks; surface outputs or explanations at critical junctures to preserve autonomy and ensure accountability.
  - Embed sensemaking interventions when surfacing AI outputs: provide rationale, provenance, and context for AI suggestions to support user understanding and trust.
  - Implement adaptive transparency: tailor the level and modality of explanation to user expertise and task phase; provide user controls to adjust exposure, enabling a continuum from invisible to explicit guidance.
  - Build governance that preserves agency: incorporate human-in-the-loop checkpoints, audit trails, privacy protections, and ethical guidelines; avoid over-automation in high-stakes or ethically sensitive tasks.
  - Develop prompts literacy and critical-evaluation training: teach users how to interpret AI inputs, evaluate reliability, and contest or refine AI-produced outputs.
  - Design evaluation programs that measure flow and autonomy as primary outcomes, alongside performance metrics and long-term sustainability indicators (e.g., learning retention, skill maintenance, rework rates).
  - Domain-specific adaptation: tailor AI design to domain constraints (creative design, radiology, software development, education, etc.), ensuring alignment with professional norms, ethics, and regulatory considerations.

- For researchers designing studies on invisibility vs visibility:
  - Design longitudinal, multi-domain experiments comparing invisible background AI vs explicit surfaced AI across creative and knowledge-work tasks.
  - Use a core set of measures: flow (validated scales), perceived autonomy, cognitive load, task performance, rework rate, and trust, plus qualitative interviews to capture sensemaking and agency distribution.
  - Employ adaptive transparency manipulations (e.g., on-demand rationales, confidence indicators, data provenance) to map the optimal balance for different domains and task types.
  - Include governance and ethics as explicit variables, examining how transparency, accountability, and override mechanisms influence flow and sustainable performance over time.

5) Limitations and caveats
- The breadth of sources reviewed here spans education, healthcare, industry, and organizational behavior. While convergent themes support a hybrid invisible/visible approach, domain-specific effects may differ; direct cross-domain experimental data are needed.
- Some studies report qualitative or context-specific results without standardized effect sizes; care is required when generalizing to new domains or task types.
- The optimal balance between invisibility and explicit guidance is highly task- and context-dependent, requiring careful, user-centered evaluation.

7) Concluding remarks
- The preponderance of evidence supports a hybrid, task- and context-sensitive approach to AI visibility in creative and knowledge-work tasks. Invisible AI can support flow and autonomy when designed with explicit sensemaking scaffolds, transparent governance, and user agency. Explicit AI guidance remains valuable for high-stakes or novel work, to ensure accountability, explainability, and trust. The literature consistently points to design principles like adaptive transparency, human-in-the-loop, context-awareness, and explicit governance as critical to achieving sustainable performance and robust flow across domains.
