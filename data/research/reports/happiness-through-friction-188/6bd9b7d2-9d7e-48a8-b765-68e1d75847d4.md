Desirable Difficulty Prompts in AI-Assisted Learning:
Evidence, Design Principles, and an Experimental Roadmap for Long-Run Transfer and PERMA Engagement

Executive summary
- Research question: Do deliberate desirable-difficulty prompts embedded in AI-assisted learning (e.g., requiring learners to explain reasoning, anticipate missteps, or generate partial solutions before AI guidance) produce greater long-run transfer and PERMA-related engagement than standard AI guidance alone?
- What the current evidence suggests:
  - Core Desirable Difficulties (spacing, interleaving, generation/retrieval, misstep anticipation) have robust lab and field support for enhancing long-run transfer and deep encoding, often at the cost of slower or more effortful initial performance (Bjork, 1994; Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Kerr & Booth, 1978; Spacing/interleaving effects; retrieval/practice effects) [Bjork, 1994; Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Kerr & Booth, 1978].
  - In AI-assisted learning, several randomized and quasi-experimental studies show that AI tutoring can outperform traditional approaches on immediate learning metrics and can improve engagement when pedagogical design is strong (Kestin et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Létourneau et al., 2025) [Kestin et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Létourneau et al., 2025].
  - Direct evidence linking specifically engineered desirable-difficulty prompts to enhanced long-run transfer and explicit PERMA engagement in AI-tutoring contexts remains limited. Several sources provide design pillars and mechanisms, plus guidance on rigorous evaluation, but few report a clean causal estimate for long-run transfer or PERMA outcomes in education domains with AI prompts (see below) [Bjork et al.; Kestin et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Practical implication: Given the mechanisms, a rigorously designed randomized controlled trial (RCT) is warranted to test depth-promoting prompts within AI tutors, including novel transfer tasks and PERMA-like engagement indicators, across domains and learner populations. The experimental design should integrate spacing, interleaving, generation/retrieval prompts, misstep-anticipation prompts, and partial-solution generation, while measuring immediate performance, long-run transfer, and PERMA-related engagement.
- Actionable plan: The report closes with concrete design recommendations for authors and practitioners, including multi-arm RCT formats, transfer-task batteries, PERMA-engagement instruments, prompt taxonomies, adaptive calibration, and robust QA/governance.

1) Background and theoretical foundations

- Desirable difficulties: Core idea and mechanisms
  - Desirable difficulties are learning conditions that increase cognitive effort during encoding/retrieval, often slowing short-term acquisition but improving long-term retention, deep understanding, and transfer when learners have adequate background and cues (Bjork, 1994; Bjork & Bjork, 1992; Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006). Key mechanisms include retrieval effort, generation effects, spacing, and interleaving; tests-as-learning and varied contexts support durable memory traces and flexible knowledge representations (Bjork, 1994; Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Landauer & Bjork; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006).
  - The storage vs retrieval strength framework (Bjork & Bjork, 1992) captures that retrieval attempts enhance storage strength when retrievability is varied and context is varied, supporting durable transfer across contexts.
- Relevance to AI-assisted learning
  - Embedding prompts that require explanation, generation, misstep anticipation, and partial-solution construction can operationalize desirable-difficulty mechanisms within AI tutors, aligning with generation effects, retrieval practice, and testing-as-learning in AI-enhanced settings.
  - Properly calibrated prompts can induce transfer-appropriate processing by foregrounding reasoning and flexible representations; missteps anticipation and partial-solution prompts encourage productive struggle and diagnostic reasoning, which are proposed to improve long-run transfer and deeper meaning (Bjork; generation/retrieval literature) [Bjork, 1994; Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006].
- PERMA-engagement frame
  - Engagement and meaning are linked to deeper processing and perceived mastery. PERMA-related indicators (Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment) are commonly used in education research to capture well-being and engagement dimensions that accompany learning depth. While robust transfer evidence often centers on knowledge and problem-solving, engagement metrics are increasingly emphasized as outcomes co-variant with durable learning (PERMA literature and allied engagement research).

2) Evidence landscape in AI-assisted learning (education-specific)

- Randomized and quasi-experimental AI-tutoring studies (recent syntheses)
  - Kestin et al. (2025) Sci Rep: In a large undergraduate physics course (N ≈ 194; cross-over design), AI tutoring outperformed in-class active learning on learning gains and engagement/motivation; AI group had shorter time-on-task and higher perceived engagement. Exact effect sizes reported: significant post-test gains (p < 10^-8; z = -5.6 for post-test differences; plausible effect size in the vicinity of Cohen’s d around 0.6–1.0 range in reported analyses), with median post-test scores 4.5 vs 3.5 on a 5-point scale; time-on-task ~49 minutes vs ~60 minutes. Importantly, this study did not isolate deliberate desirable-difficulty prompts as the experimental variable; it used an AI tutor designed with pedagogical best practices (e.g., structured guidance, prompt engineering) but did not report a direct manipulation of “desirable difficulty prompts” per se (Kestin et al., 2025) [Kestin et al., 2025; Sci Rep].
  - Bastani et al. (2025) PNAS: Field trial in high school mathematics comparing AI-based feedback with guardrails (AI tutor with safeguards) vs remote expert instruction vs no feedback. AI-based feedback yielded superior practice and transfer-like performance during AI-guided practice; post-removal transfer-like performance also showed advantages for guarded AI; however, explicit long-run transfer tasks and PERMA-like engagement metrics were not reported in the abstract; details indicate that safeguard design improves durability relative to naïve AI/unaided conditions (Bastani et al., 2025) [Bastani et al., 2025; PNAS].
- Systematic reviews of AI in education (ITS focus)
  - Létourneau et al. (2025) npj Science of Learning: Across 28 studies (N ≈ 4,597), ITS generally yield positive learning outcomes, but effects are heterogeneous and often smaller than or comparable to non-ITS controls in some contexts; longer interventions and broader samples are needed to establish durable effects, transfer potential, and well-being outcomes. Explicit transfer and PERMA engagement outcomes are not consistently reported; there is emphasis on robust experimental design and theory-aligned instruction (Létourneau et al., 2025) [Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Generative ITS design and evaluation
  - Liu et al. (2024) Electronics: A modular Generative ITS framework with GPT-4-based tutor capabilities (SPL: Socratic Playground for Learning) supports descriptive prompts, hierarchical customization, and scenario-based instruction; the article emphasizes design, evaluation, and pedagogy-centered prompts (e.g., pre-mortems, stepwise prompts) as enabling depth, engagement, and transfer potential. It presents a case study architecture rather than outcome data; the framework provides practical scaffolds for implementing desirable-difficulty prompting in AI tutors (Liu et al., 2024) [Liu et al., 2024].
- Ethical and safety considerations
  - Several sources emphasize the risk of AI hallucinations and misleading guidance in high-stakes education; robust QA, human-in-the-loop oversight, and governance frameworks (e.g., DASEX/DASEX) are repeatedly recommended when deploying AI-based tutors (Reddig et al., 2025; Ali et al., 2024; Gkintoni et al., 2025) [Reddig et al., 2025; Ali et al., 2024; Gkintoni et al., 2025].

3) Prompts and design patterns for desirable difficulties in AI tutoring

- Structured vs conversational prompts
  - Structured prompting (role, goal, step-by-step, pedagogy, constraints) provides a repeatable, auditable approach to generate deeper reasoning and better alignment with learning goals; it is easier to evaluate and reproduce than purely conversational prompts (Mollick, 2023; arXiv/SSRN references). Prompts such as pre-mortems or stepwise reasoning prompts often increase depth of processing and can guide learners into generating explanations before receiving AI guidance (Mollick, 2023; SSRN:4475995; arXiv:2201.11903) [Mollick, 2023; SSRN 4475995; arXiv 2201.11903].
  - Generation/retrieval prompts (chain-of-thought prompts) can improve problem solving in some domains but may increase hallucinations or produce overconfident incorrect outputs in smaller models; large models tend to handle chain-of-thought prompts more robustly (Weighing stable performance vs. hallucinations across model sizes) [arXiv:2201.11903; related works cited in design guidance].

- Design implications for AI tutoring
  - The design space includes two broad prompt pathways (conversational and structured). For desirable difficulties, structured prompts with explicit reasoning requirements, misstep anticipation, and partial-solution generation are central to depth-promoting instructional goals (Mollick, 2023; arXiv 2201.11903).
  - Implementing pre-mortems, stepwise prompts, and role-based prompts can anchor pedagogy and provide auditable, transferable patterns across domains (Mollick, 2023; SPL design in Liu et al., 2024).
  - QA governance frameworks (DASEX) and dual-layer validation are advocated to mitigate hallucinations and bias when deploying GPT-based tutors for education (Gkintoni et al., 2025; Ali et al., 2024).

4) Proposed experimental designs to answer the research question

A. Core two-arm RCT (depth-promoting prompts vs standard AI guidance)
- Population: learners in a domain with transfer potential (e.g., physics, math, biology) across undergraduate and/or K-12 settings.
- Intervention:
  - Arm 1: Depth-promoting prompts embedded in AI guidance
    - Examples: explain reasoning steps before AI guidance; anticipate missteps; generate partial solutions; interleaved and spaced practice prompts.
    - Prompt architecture: structured prompts with Role/Goal, Step-by-step reasoning tasks, misstep anticipation prompts, partial-solution generation, and delayed AI feedback to encourage retrieval and elaboration.
  - Arm 2: Standard AI guidance (baseline)
    - Direct explanations/solutions, minimal generation/retrieval prompts.
- Outcomes:
  - Primary: long-run transfer tasks (novel contexts, across domains if possible) assessed after a delay (e.g., 4–8 weeks).
  - Secondary: PERMA engagement indicators (validated scales for flow, meaning, engagement, relationships, accomplishment); cognitive-load measures; metacognitive judgments; time-on-task.
- Design specifics:
  - Spacing/interleaving: ensure spacing and interleaving across sessions; track generation and retrieval prompts usage.
  - Pre-registration: hypotheses, outcome measures, and analysis plan; preregister sample size and power.
  - Analysis: mixed-effects models to account for clustering; mediation analyses to test whether retrieval/explanation depth mediates transfer/engagement.
- Rationale: Aligns with desirable-difficulties theory (encoding/retrieval), and uses AI-prompt designs consistent with the structured prompting literature (Mollick; Liu et al., 2024) and transfer-focused outcomes (Bjork; Kornell & Bjork).

B. Multi-arm design to parse active components
- Arm A: Depth-prompts (reasoning explanations, misstep anticipation, partial-solution generation)
- Arm B: Interleaved spacing prompts (vary problem types across sessions)
- Arm C: Retrieval-based prompts (low-stakes quizzes with retrieval practice)
- Arm D: Control (plain AI guidance)
- Outcome: transfer and PERMA engagement; mediator analyses (depth-of-reasoning quality, retrieval strength, and misstep-anticipation accuracy).

- Rationale: Disentangles the distinct desirable-difficulty mechanisms (generation, retrieval, spacing/interleaving, misstep anticipation) and their individual contributions to long-run transfer and PERMA engagement.

C. Domain-general transfer across domains
- Rationale: Some-desirable difficulties effects generalize across contexts via transfer-appropriate processing; test across multiple domains (e.g., physics, language, math) to assess robustness of depth prompts to domain differences.

5) Measurement and data collection plan

- Transfer tasks
  - Near transfer: problems closely related to training content but with novel numbers/framings.
  - Far transfer: problems requiring applying core principles in new contexts or to novel representations.
  - Delayed post-tests at 4–8 weeks to examine durability of transfer.
- PERMA engagement indicators
  - Use validated scales for PERMA components (Positive Emotion, Engagement/Flow, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment) or domain-appropriate proxies if full scales are unavailable.
  - Include flow-state measures during sessions, and assessments of perceived meaning and accomplishment after tasks.
- Cognitive and metacognitive metrics
  - Depth of explanations (coding quality of reasoning; rubric-based scoring).
  - Retrieval strength indicators (latency, accuracy on retrieval prompts, number of correct generated steps).
  - Quality of misstep anticipation (alignment with actual learner misconceptions; diagnostic precision).
- Engagement/process data
  - Time-on-task; prompt usage logs; frequency and type of prompts engaged; transitions from partial-solution prompts to full guidance.
- Safety and QA
  - Monitor AI outputs for hallucinations or misleading guidance; implement human-in-the-loop checks where feasible; document governance processes (DASEX/DASE frameworks as applicable).

6) Practical considerations, limitations, and governance

- Evidence limitations to date
  - Direct empirical evidence linking explicitly engineered desirable-difficulty prompts to long-run transfer and explicit PERMA engagement in education contexts is limited. The strongest AI-tutoring evidence relates to immediate learning gains and engagement, with heterogeneity across studies (Kestin et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025; Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Generalizability and context-sensitivity
  - Domain, learner age, prior knowledge, and AI platform capabilities influence effects; multi-site, cross-domain replication is advisable.
- Ethics and safety
  - AI-based tutoring requires governance around data privacy, bias, explainability, and transparency. Structured prompting increases complexity of outputs; robust QA and potential human oversight are essential.
- Practicalities
  - Sample sizes: power analyses required; pilot studies recommended.
  - Duration: longer interventions yield more robust transfer signals; aim for 6–12 weeks or multi-session modules with delayed follow-ups where feasible.
  - Implementation: ensure prompt templates are well-documented for replication and cross-domain applicability.

7) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners

- Design strategies for depth-promoting prompts
  - Implement explicit prompts requiring reasoning, misstep anticipation, and partial-solution generation before AI guidance, tied to domain concepts and learning objectives.
  - Use structured prompts with role/goal, step-by-step reasoning, and pedagogical rationale to anchor learning design.
- Adaptive calibration
  - Calibrate difficulty to learner background using adaptive prompts; gradually increase demands to maintain desirable challenge without tipping into “undesirable difficulty.”
- Spacing and interleaving
  - Build spacing across sessions and interleave different topics/problem types to foster inductive transfer and boundary recognition (Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Rohrer, 2012).
- Retrieval practice as core
  - Include retrieval prompts and quizzes between AI-guided tasks; use delayed feedback to reinforce retrieval strength (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006).
- Measurement plan
  - Pre-register hypotheses and analytic strategy; publish effect sizes with CIs; report both transfer and engagement outcomes to assess broader impact.
- Governance and QA
  - Use dual-layer validation for AI content where feasible; document error rates and reviewer checks; consider bias audits and transparency disclosures (Ali et al., 2024; Reddig et al., 2025; Gkintoni et al., 2025).
- Domain breadth and replication
  - Plan multi-domain trials to test generalizability; pair with different AI teaching modalities to triangulate results.

8) Limitations and caveats

- Direct evidence gap: There is not yet a published, education-focused RCT that isolates the exact manipulation of desirable-difficulty prompts (explain reasoning; misstep anticipation; partial-solution generation before guidance) as the principal experimental variable with explicit long-run transfer and PERMA outcomes.
- Reliability of AI guidance: AI prompts can produce hallucinations or general guidance that may mislead; robust QA and human oversight are required for scalable, safe deployment (Reddig et al., 2025; Ali et al., 2024).
- Transfer and PERMA measurement challenges: Long-run transfer is inherently difficult to measure; PERMA engagement is multi-faceted and requires validated measures; careful study design and measurement choices are essential.

9) Recommendations for authors and practitioners (summary of actionable insights)

- Build depth-promoting AI prompts
  - Create prompt taxonomies that include reasoning prompts, misstep-anticipation prompts, and partial-solution generation prompts aligned with learning objectives.
  - Use Step-by-step reasoning scaffolds, role-based prompts, and explicit constraints to shape AI responses and learner engagement.
- Integrate spacing/interleaving and retrieval
  - Schedule spaced practice and interleaved problem sets; embed retrieval prompts and low-stakes quizzes to strengthen encoding and retrieval.
- Use CONCRETE PERMA engagement targets
  - Measure flow, meaning, relationships, accomplishment, and positive emotion; include both in-session engagement and delayed follow-ups.
- Conduct rigorous evaluations
  - Prefer multi-arm RCT designs (depth-prompts vs standard AI guidance; optional third control group); include transfer tasks and long-term follow-ups; preregister hypotheses and analysis plans; report effect sizes with CIs.
- Ensure governance and safety
  - Adopt QA pipelines, bias checks, and, where possible, human-in-the-loop review to maintain high-quality, pedagogically aligned AI guidance.
- Domain diversity and scalability
  - Plan cross-domain replications to gauge generalizability; document prompting templates and design choices to facilitate replication and scaling.

Appendix: Prompt taxonomy (illustrative examples)
- Depth-promoting prompts (educational aims)
  - Reasoning prompt: “Explain your reasoning for each step in solving this problem before you see the AI’s guidance.”
  - Misstep anticipation: “List two plausible misconceptions and explain why they would lead to incorrect conclusions; then show how to detect and correct them.”
  - Partial-solution prompt: “Draft a partial solution or outline of steps; then ask for feedback and the full solution.”
- Spacing and interleaving prompts
  - Retrieval prompt: “After a delay, present 2–3 retrieval questions on the topic to strengthen memory and application across contexts.”
  - Interleaved prompt: “Mix problems from related topics and require cross-topic reasoning in a single session.”
- QA governance prompts
  - Role-based prompts: “Act as a pedagogical advisor; ensure prompts align with learning objectives and provide auditable reasoning traces for feedback.”

Notes on interpretation and scope
- This report integrates evidence from multiple sources on desirable difficulties, AI tutoring effectiveness, and prompting architectures to present a coherent roadmap for testing whether deliberate prompts yield superior long-run transfer and PERMA engagement relative to standard AI guidance.
- Direct educational evidence for these exact prompts (desirable difficulty prompts designed as the primary manipulation) and explicit PERMA transfer outcomes remains an empirical gap; the recommended path is a well-powered RCT with transfer tasks and PERMA measures, guided by rigorously defined prompt taxonomies and governance standards.
