# Do sustained engagements with AI that emphasize challenging tasks foster post-learning growth and resilience? Moderators and design levers that shape durable learning

Executive summary
- Across a broad evidence base, sustained AI-enabled learning environments that emphasize meaningful, challenging tasks tend to be associated with beneficial learning trajectories, especially when design features emphasize high-quality feedback, domain alignment, and thoughtful human integration. However, direct evidence of durable post-learning growth or resilience (as distinct from short-to mid-term learning gains) is mixed across contexts and often requires longitudinal designs to disentangle longer-term effects (Pane et al., 2025; Létourneau et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Bauer et al., 2025).
- Moderators that repeatedly emerge as critical include:
  - Feedback quality and delivery (immediacy, specificity, interpretability; often enhanced by human-in-the-loop or hybrid models) (He et al., 2025; Harv. GSE summaries; Lai, 2025; Laun & Wolff, 2025).
  - Domain and instructional context (domain specificity, school level, real-world relevance) shaping magnitudes and durability of gains (Pane et al., 2025; Létourneau et al., 2025; Leão et al., 2025).
  - Noise, reliability, and data quality in AI outputs (signal-to-noise issues; purification and robust learning approaches reduce risk of degraded learning signals) (RiCL framework; DiCerbo et al.; DOE guidance).
  - Integration design (human-in-the-loop, workflow alignment, scaffolding, and metacognitive supports) affecting sustained engagement and transfer (WGU Kyron; Khanmigo dynamics; ISAR framework).
- Actionable takeaway: when designing AI-assisted learning to foster durable growth and resilience, prioritize (a) advanced or multi-focal feedback that supports metacognition and transfer, (b) domain-aligned and duration-appropriate tasks with robust pedagogy and teacher involvement, (c) explicit longitudinal measurement of growth/resilience indicators, and (d) governance considerations around transparency, ethics, privacy, and equity.

1) Introduction: defining the problem and organizing the evidence
- Research question: Do sustained engagements with AI that emphasize challenging tasks foster post-learning growth and resilience, and which moderators (feedback quality, noise, domain) matter most?
- Conceptual frame: post-learning growth and durable resilience are envisioned as outcomes akin to post-traumatic growth or meaningful struggle within learning contexts. The literature reviewed spans AI-assisted feedback, adaptive learning, domain-specific applications (e.g., language learning, STEM, health-professional training), and theoretical lenses including feedback quality, cognitive load, self-determination, and resilience.
- Key constructs and mechanisms commonly studied:
  - AI-assisted feedback (AIFB): AI analyzes tasks and learner data to provide task-, process-, self-regulation- or self-focused feedback at varying complexity levels (Shen et al., 2025).
  - Moderators/design levers commonly identified: feedback focus breadth, feedback complexity, algorithm transparency, cross-domain applicability, domain representation, and representation of mechanisms in conceptual models (Shen et al., 2025).
  - Long-run outcomes: while many studies show positive short- to medium-term effects on perceptions, actions, and outcomes, few provide explicit evidence on durable post-learning growth or resilience. There is a clear need for longitudinal designs and explicit resilience metrics (Shen et al., 2025; Pane et al., 2025; Leite, 2025).
- Why this matters for post-learning growth and resilience: durable growth requires repeated exposure to cognitively demanding tasks, scaffolded learning, and reflective processing, often augmented by AI alongside human guidance; moderators determine whether AI support yields sustainable growth or merely short-term gains or fatigue.

2) Evidence landscape: what we know about sustained AI engagement, challenging tasks, and growth moderators
- Systematic reviews and cross-domain syntheses
  - Shen et al. (2025): Systematic review of 129 AI-assisted feedback (AIFB) studies (2014–2023). Direction of effects is generally positive across learner perceptions, actions, and outcomes, with higher feedback complexity and domain breadth associated with greater potential for growth. Limitations include limited transparency about algorithms and mechanisms; no aggregated long-run growth metrics reported; a conceptual model guides future practice and research.
  - Létourneau et al. (2025): NPJ Science of Learning synthesis across 28 studies (N = 4,597). ITS effects are generally positive but smaller than some non-ITS controls; longest, sustained deployments show larger gains in some domains (e.g., high-school algebra CTAI with ~0.20 SD gains after sustained use; middle school effects uncertain). Core design features—immediate feedback, guided practice, adaptivity—underpin effectiveness when aligned with pedagogy; novelty effects can inflate early gains, but durable growth requires theory-aligned design and longer horizons.
  - Bauer et al. (2025): ISAR framework (Inversion, Substitution, Augmentation, Redefinition) to categorize AI effects on learning. Moderators include AI literacy, educator skills, and contextual factors; emphasizes designing for meaningful cognitive challenge and resilience through governance and human-in-the-loop design.
- Domain-specific evidence and domain moderators
  - He et al. (2025): In EFL undergraduates, corrective and motivational AI feedback improves self-reflection; AI fosters creativity and reduces performance anxiety with familiarity; delivery style and feedback content moderate effects; domain-specific reporting is important for understanding cross-domain generalization.
  - Pane et al. (CR21): Longitudinal CTAI program: high-school algebra gains after year 2; domain and school level moderate effects (high school yields stronger durable gains than middle school).
  - WGU Kyron Learning pilot (WGU Labs, 2025): Engagement and perceived value improved among users, but no significant short-term differences in core academic outcomes due to optional deployment and limited integration; ongoing AI support and integration are key levers for durable gains.
  - Khanmigo and Khan Academy context (Hess & DiCerbo, 2025): Large-scale adoption; feedback quality and teacher scaffolding are central moderators; prompt stability and domain considerations (math, science, humanities) matter for durable engagement; emphasizes teacher involvement as critical to effective AI-assisted learning.
  - Li et al. (2025): Systematic review of 144 AI-language-learning studies (2023–2024). Emphasizes writing as a dominant focus; domain breadth, feedback content, and social/affective factors influence outcomes; calls for longitudinal work to connect to resilience and post-learning growth.
- Domain-specific cognitive and affective outcomes
  - He et al. (2025): Self-reflection, creativity, anxiety, and resilience as central outcomes in EFL; feedback type and familiarity shape affective and cognitive outcomes; domain specificity matters.
  - Abdellatif et al. (2024): AI-driven listening assessments in Saudi EFL learners improve listening, mindsets, self-competence, and resilience. Domain-specific improvements observed, with the caveat that long-run resilience outcomes require longer observation.
  - Yan et al. (2025): Flow, grit, and resilience mediate the relationship between SDT needs satisfaction and intention to use AI; suggests that sustained, challenging AI engagements can foster motivation and persistence through SDT-aligned design, but long-run resilience measured over time remains an empirical question.
  - J. Demszky et al. (2023-2023): AI feedback dashboards and tutor coaching can increase uptake of student ideas and affect teacher practice and student experience in real classrooms; highlights the role of human-in-the-loop and feedback quality.

3) Moderators and design levers that matter most for durable growth and resilience
- Feedback quality and alignment
  - High-quality, timely, specific AI feedback is repeatedly tied to stronger engagement, self-regulation, transfer, and resilience indicators; human-in-the-loop often enhances feedback quality and alignment with disciplinary expectations (He et al., 2025; Lai, 2025; Khanmigo literature; Pane et al., CR21; Demszky et al., 2023).
- Domain specificity and contextual design
  - Domain alignment matters: STEM domains tend to show larger effects from AI tutoring; domain-specific tasks and authentic problem contexts support transfer and durable growth (Pane et al., CR21; Li et al., 2025; Laun & Wolff, 2025).
- Learning duration and sustained exposure
  - Longer duration and multi-year deployments yield stronger durable gains in some contexts (Pane et al., CR21). Short-term pilots may understate benefits due to novelty and implementation co-evolution with pedagogy (Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Human-in-the-loop and teacher scaffolding
  - Human guidance remains a central moderator; teacher scaffolding, prompt design, and collaborative human–AI workflows appear critical to maintain meaningful struggle and to prevent fatigue or overreliance (Hess & DiCerbo, 2025; Khanmigo/HGSE summaries; WGU Kyron; DOE guidance on human-in-the-loop).
- Noise, reliability, and governance
  - Reliability and governance (transparency, privacy, bias mitigation) moderate trust, engagement, and willingness to sustain AI-enhanced learning; noise in AI outputs can reduce learning gains unless mitigated by design features such as purification, explainability, and override options (RiCL framework; DOE guidance; Khan Academy governance discussions; Leite 2025 TSR lens).
- Emergent themes and recommended levers
  - Diversified feedback types (metacognitive, affective, and task-focused) can yield distinct learning benefits and neural correlates, suggesting design opportunities to foster durable learning signals through multiple cognitive channels (Yin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Demzskey et al., 2023; Harv GSE discussions).
  - Domain-aware, multi-modal, and cross-domain AI learning strategies (text-based chat, image inputs, multilingual support, cross-domain problem sets) may broaden durable growth potential, albeit with domain-dependent moderators (Khanmigo literature; Lu et al.; Li et al., 2025).

4) Quantitative snapshots to anchor the synthesis
- Pane et al. (CR21): CTAI high-school algebra gains ~+0.20 SD after year 2 with sustained use; middle-school gains directionally positive but not significant.
- Létourneau et al. (2025): 28 ITS studies; overall positive learning outcomes; effects smaller than non-ITS controls; longer duration associated with stronger effects in some domains.
- He et al. (2025): 205 EFL undergraduates; AI feedback types improve self-reflection; AI fosters creativity and reduces anxiety with familiarity; emphasis on feedback delivery style and AI familiarity as moderators.
- WGU Kyron (2025): Engagement favorable but no significant short-term academic outcome differences under optional deployment; integration depth emerges as a key determinant.
- 194 undergraduates in physics (Harvard PS2 Pal study; preprint rs-4243877): AI tutoring produced learning gains > twice those of active learning; z = -5.6; effect sizes 0.73–1.3; engagement and motivation improved; domain-specific design (stepwise prompts) critical.
- 135 effect sizes (Laun & Wolff, 2025): initial large effects attenuated after publication bias correction; interaction mode (text vs speech), STEM domain, longer duration, and lower educational level show stronger effects; chatbots can improve performance but not uniformly across domains.
- 12-month Brazilian GenAI study (Leite, 2025): six themes; qualitative TSR lens; no direct resilience metrics; emphasizes ethical and trust implications and the adoption journey.

5) Actionable insights for authors, designers, and policymakers
- Design principles to maximize durable growth and resilience
  - Ensure robust, domain-aligned feedback with explicit metacognitive scaffolds; incorporate mixed feedback modes (metacognitive, affective, neutral) to engage diverse cognitive processes and neural correlates.
  - Maintain human-in-the-loop governance: teachers, tutors, or mentors should supervise AI feedback, co-create prompts, and provide reflective practice opportunities to sustain meaningful struggle.
  - Implement longitudinal evaluation: plan for follow-up measurements of resilience, transfer, and growth beyond immediate outcomes; embed resilience-oriented scales and post-learning growth indicators.
  - Domain-aware design: tailor AI tutoring to disciplinary norms and transfer requirements; test across domains to identify domain-specific moderators and generalizable design levers.
  - Noise mitigation: deploy purification, override options, and transparent explanations to reduce cognitive and affective noise; ensure data privacy and bias mitigation to sustain trust and engagement.
  - Equity and access: address AI literacy and digital divide; ensure representation of diverse learners and contexts to support durable growth across populations.
- Research agenda priorities
  - Longitudinal studies across domains to quantify durable growth and resilience linked to sustained challenging AI engagement.
  - Experimental manipulation of feedback quality, feedback modality, and noise levels to isolate their moderating effects on growth and resilience.
  - Cross-domain meta-analyses to identify robust moderators and design levers that generalize beyond single-domain contexts.
  - Theoretical integration: unify ISAR, SDT, resilience frameworks, and SRL models to ground empirical work in coherent theory, guiding measurement and interpretation of durable growth signals.
- Policy and governance considerations
  - Provide guidelines for human-in-the-loop AI integration, teacher professional development, data governance, and ethical use.
  - Support district- or institution-level guardrails to prevent fatigue and ensure equitable access to AI-enhanced learning.

6) Limitations and caveats
- The evidence base for durable post-learning growth (beyond short- to mid-term outcomes) remains relatively sparse and domain-dependent; many studies focus on learning gains or engagement proxies rather than resilience or post-traumatic growth. Longitudinal, cross-domain research is needed to establish causal links to durable growth and resilience (Pane et al., 2025; Leite, 2025; Laun & Wolff, 2025; DOE guidance 2023; ISAR 2025).
- Moderators such as feedback quality, noise, and domain-specific effects are variably reported across studies; direct, controlled testing of these moderators on durable growth is rare.
- Generalizability varies by domain (STEM vs humanities), education level (primary vs tertiary), and context (online vs in-person; high schools vs universities). Careful replication and context-sensitive designs are required.

7) Concluding synthesis
- Do sustained engagements with AI that emphasize challenging tasks foster post-learning growth and resilience? The evidence suggests that when AI tutoring is designed with high-quality, domain-aligned feedback and integrated human guidance, engagement and learning gains can be substantial and potentially conducive to durable growth and resilience. However, direct causal links to post-learning growth or resilience require explicit longitudinal measurement and cross-domain replication to corroborate across contexts. The moderators that matter most consistently across studies include feedback quality and delivery, domain-context alignment, duration of exposure, and governance considerations addressing noise, ethics, and equity (Pane et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Laun & Wolff, 2025; Leite, 2025; Bauer et al., 2025).
