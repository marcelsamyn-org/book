# report.md

AI-assisted collaboration in pair-work: relationship quality, trust, and shared meaning — comprehensive evidence synthesis

Executive summary
- Across a broad set of empirical and theoretical studies, AI-assisted collaboration in pair-work (e.g., pair programming, co-writing) can strengthen task-related outcomes (motivation, anxiety, performance) and influence relational dynamics (collaboration quality, social presence, trust) in nuanced ways.
- Key patterns:
  - AI-assisted pairing tends to boost intrinsic motivation and reduce anxiety relative to solo work, while often matching or surpassing human–non-AI comparisons on objective task performance. In many educational contexts, AI-assisted pair programming (AI-PP) yields higher programming performance than both solo and traditional human–human pair programming (HH-PP) but may not achieve the same depth of relational quality (collaboration, social presence) as HH-PP (Fan et al., 2025; Lyu et al., 2025).
  - Perceived usefulness and trust in the AI partner mediate positive outcomes, underscoring trust-building as a central lever for realizing AI’s benefits in pair-work (Fan et al., 2025).
  - Human–human pairing continues to deliver richer relational dynamics (social presence, collaborative depth), suggesting AI serves best as a complement rather than a replacement for human collaboration (Fan et al., 2025; Bhatti et al., 2021; McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Trust in AI is multifaceted: cognitive trust (competence, reliability) and affective trust (benevolence, alignment) jointly shape willingness to rely on AI, with calibration essential to sustain long-term collaboration (Shang et al., 2024; McGrath et al., 2025; Solberg et al., 2022).
- Practical takeaway for authors/educators: deploy AI to augment motivation and performance, but preserve or deliberately bolster relational depth through structured social-presence strategies (structured dialogue, reflection prompts, role-rotation, social-scaffolds). Train students to interpret AI outputs, assess usefulness, and negotiate meaning with partners, not merely accept AI-generated content (Fan et al., 2025; Kong et al., 2025; Dhillon et al., 2024).
- Important caveats: many studies measure proxies for relationship quality (e.g., collaboration perceptions, social presence) rather than direct trust or shared mental models; direct comparisons of relational outcomes across AI-assisted vs non-AI pair-work are still relatively sparse and domain-dependent (various sources cited here).

Key sources and inline citations
- AI-assisted pair programming (education context) — quantitative findings
  - Fan et al. (2025): AI-PP increases intrinsic motivation vs solo (p < .001, d = 0.35) and reduces programming anxiety vs solo (p < .001); AI-PP comparable to HH-PP on these outcomes; AI-PP outperforms HH-PP and IP on programming tasks (p < .001); HH-PP shows higher collaboration and social presence than AI-PP or IP (p < .001); perceived usefulness of AI mediates outcomes; no model-differences between GPT-3.5 Turbo vs Claude 3 Opus (Fan et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (Fan et al., 2025)
- AI-assisted pair programming in higher education — cross-study patterns
  - Lyu et al. (2025): PAI yields highest scores; SAI yields lowest; motivation improves with AI collaboration; no direct trust measure reported; preferences linked to perceived usefulness and collaboration quality (Lyu et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (Lyu et al., 2025)
- Trust measurement and trust dynamics in AI collaboration
  - Shang et al. (2024): Trust in AI is two-dimensional (cognitive and affective) via TIAS and S-TIAS scales; both scales show validity and predictive power for reliance; affective and cognitive trust interact to shape overall trust; AI manipulation via prompts can influence affective trust (Shang et al., 2024).
  - Inline: (Shang et al., 2024)
  - McGrath et al. (2025): CHAI-T framework for active management of trust in human–AI collaboration; antecedents (human, technology, environment); processes; outcomes; emphasizes trust calibration, bidirectional interaction, and shared meaning as dynamic outcomes (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (McGrath et al., 2025)
- Trust contagion and social dynamics in multi-human–AI teams
  - Bhatti et al. (2021): Trust contagion dynamics in human–AI collaboration; trust in an AI teammate can spread or recede based on human teammate cues; adaptive information sharing (APOI) and reliance depend on reliability and interaction context (Bhatti et al., 2021).
  - Inline: (Bhatti et al., 2021)
- Shared mental models and explainability in Human–AI teams
  - Andrews et al. (2022): Review of Shared Mental Models (SMMs) in human–AI teams; identifies gaps in terminology, measurement methods, and the role of explainable AI in forming SMMs; design implications for SMM experiments (Andrews et al., 2022).
  - Inline: (Andrews et al., 2022)
- Trust and design in AI-assisted writing and co-writing
  - Dhillon et al. (2024): Co-writing with language models; co-creator vs editor roles; impact on creativity and creative self-efficacy; suggests role design influences relational dynamics and perceived agency (Dhillon et al., 2024).
  - Inline: (Dhillon et al., 2024)
- Social presence and AI in education
  - Borup et al. (2025): Framework for integrating AI-generated text with human-created video to strengthen social presence; design implications for AI-assisted pair-work in online contexts (Borup et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (Borup et al., 2025)
- AI-assisted collaboration in a MR environment
  - Dang et al. (2025): Embodied GAI agents in MR; AISQ vs LII interaction patterns; socio-emotional engagement comparable to GPT-based interactions; implications for relationship quality and shared meaning in MR pair-work (Dang et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (Dang et al., 2025)
- AI-assisted writing and performance in education
  - McGuire et al. (2024): Two-state study on co-creating with AI in writing; co-creator role improves creative self-efficacy and outcomes; editor role can degrade agency (McGuire et al., 2024).
  - Inline: (McGuire et al., 2024)
- AI-supported discussion representations in online collaboration
  - Kong et al. (2025): Four representation modes (behavioral, cognitive, mixed, control) in AI-supported online discussions; cognitive presence shows strongest gains for higher-order presence with cognitive representations; motivation not significantly affected; moderation effects observed (Kong et al., 2025).
  - Inline: (Kong et al., 2025)
- AI and social presence in education

  - Inline: (Borup et al., 2012; Borup et al., 2014; Lowenthal, 2022; Li et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b)

Contextual synthesis: how the findings fit the research question
- Relationship quality
  - HH-PP tends to maximize social presence and collaborative depth; AI-PP often yields strong task-related benefits (motivation, anxiety, performance) but lower social presence proxies than HH-PP (Fan et al., 2025).
  - Social-presence-focused design (video + AI text) can mitigate relational gaps in AI-assisted pair-work (Borup et al., 2025; Dang et al., 2025).
- Trust
  - Trust is multifaceted: cognitive trust (ability, reliability) and affective trust (benevolence, alignment) jointly influence reliance and collaboration quality; trust calibrations via explicit cues and explanations improve outcomes (Shang et al., 2024; McGrath et al., 2025; Solberg et al., 2022).
  - Trust contagion and socially reinforced trust dynamics can amplify or dampen AI reliance within multi-user teams (Bhatti et al., 2021).
- Shared meaning
  - Shared mental models (SMMs) in human–AI contexts are developing; effective SMMs require explainability and explicit coordination to bridge cognitive and social dimensions (Andrews et al., 2022; Kaur et al., 2019).
  - Co-writing and co-creation research suggests that roles matter: co-creator roles precipitate stronger ownership and meaning-making than editor roles that can suppress agency (Dhillon et al., 2024; Hutson, 2025).
- Domain-general patterns
  - Across domains (education, design, MR, writing), AI-assisted collaboration benefits from: (a) transparency and explainability, (b) explicit role delineation, (c) strategies to maintain social presence and relational warmth, (d) governance around ethics and ownership, and (e) aligning AI tools with human goals and workflows to sustain trust and meaning (McGrath et al., 2025; Borup et al., 2025; Dhillon et al., 2024; Lyu et al., 2025).

Discussion: synthesis and practical implications
- When AI is integrated into pair-work, expect a trade-off between task efficiency gains and relational depth. AI can substantially improve performance and reduce affective barriers (anxiety) and can be perceived as a credible co-worker if its usefulness is demonstrated, but human–human pairing remains the gold standard for social presence, mutual meaning, and relational warmth (Fan et al., 2025; Lyu et al., 2025; McGrath et al., 2025; Borup et al., 2025).
- Design implications:
  - Use AI as a complementary partner, not a replacement for human dialogue. Explicitly define AI roles (e.g., AI as brainstorming partner, editor, or feedback generator) and ensure humans retain final decision authority and authorship responsibility (Dhillon et al., 2024; McGuire et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2025).
  - Build trust through transparency, explainability, and calibration prompts. Provide real-time reasoning traces or justifications for AI suggestions; incorporate trust measurement tools (TIAS/S-TIAS) to monitor changes in cognitive and affective trust over time (Shang et al., 2024; McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Strengthen social presence with multimodal communication: combine AI outputs with human social cues (video, voice, personal framing) to enhance perceived presence, collaboration quality, and shared meaning (Borup et al., 2012; Borup et al., 2025; Dang et al., 2025).
  - Manage risks: address AI bias, privacy, ownership, and the potential for over-reliance or misalignment by instituting governance, ethical guidelines, and human-in-the-loop review (Motoki et al., 2023; Hutson, 2025; McGrath et al., 2025).
- Recommendations for future research
  - Conduct domain-specific longitudinal studies comparing AI-assisted pair-work with traditional HH-PP and IP across coding, writing, and design tasks, with explicit measures of relationship quality, trust (cognitive and affective), and shared meaning.
  - Integrate SMM and CHAI-T constructs to quantify how trust dynamics relate to relationship quality over time, and how interpretability and explanation quality influence shared mental models.
  - Explore cross-cultural and cross-domain validity of trust measures (TIAS, S-TIAS) in AI-assisted pair-work, ensuring robust measurement across contexts.
  - Systematically test design interventions (social presence scaffolds, multimodal communication, role delineation, governance) to identify best practices for sustaining relational quality alongside performance gains.

Limitations and caveats
- The evidence base includes a mix of education-focused experiments, design studies, and theoretical reviews. Direct, replicated empirical measurements of relationship quality and shared meaning in pair-work are still relatively sparse and domain-dependent.
- When summarizing across studies, be mindful of heterogeneous AI technologies (LLMs, embodied agents, VR/MR avatars, text-only assistants) and task types (coding, writing, design), which influence relational outcomes and trust dynamics in distinct ways.
- Some studies rely on proxy measures for relationship quality (e.g., collaboration perceptions, social presence) rather than direct trust or shared-mental-model metrics; direct pair-work measurements are needed to strengthen conclusions about relational dynamics.

Actionable insights for authors (condensed)
- Design AI-assisted pair-work with explicit roles and governance, ensuring human authorship and final decision-making remain central.
- Prioritize transparency and explainability; calibrate AI outputs to match human goals and mental models.
- Strengthen social presence through multimodal communication (text + video/voice), personalizing interactions to enhance relation quality and meaning-making.
- Use trust measurement as a core outcome alongside performance metrics; apply multi-wave longitudinal designs to capture trust trajectories.
- Incorporate ethical safeguards (privacy, bias, accountability) and governance frameworks to support sustainable, trusted collaboration.

Bottom line
- AI-assisted collaboration can meaningfully influence relationship quality, trust, and shared meaning in pair-work, primarily through improvements in task performance and perceived AI usefulness, and through strategies that support social presence and mutual sense-making. To maximize relational outcomes, designers should prioritize hybrid collaboration models that preserve human agency, incorporate social-presence scaffolds, implement trust calibration mechanisms, and address ethical considerations. Direct, longitudinal, domain-specific measurements of relationship quality and shared meaning are essential next steps for robust evidence in AI-assisted pair-work contexts.
