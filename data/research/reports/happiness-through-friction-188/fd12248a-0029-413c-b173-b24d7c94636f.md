Causal effects of deliberate difficulty prompts from AI on learning depth and transfer: evidence across data-analysis, coding, and writing tasks

Executive summary
- Across a growing body of work, deliberate difficulty prompts delivered via AI (structured prompt design, few-shot and chain-of-thought prompting, and frameworks like CLEAR) show promise as depth-enablers that can deepen learning depth (conceptual understanding and procedural skill) and promote transfer-like outcomes, relative to weaker or no prompting, within single-domain tasks. Evidence spans data-analysis and programming tasks, with limited cross-domain transfer data and sparse direct comparisons to pure direct-answer AI across multiple domains (writing, coding, data-analysis) [Garg & Rajendran, 2024; Garg et al., 2025; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Padurean et al., 2025].
- Data-analysis/programming evidence:
  - Garg & Rajendran (2024) found a large within-subject gain (d ≈ 0.89; p < 0.05) on pre–post MCQ tests when learners used structured prompts (CLEAR) with GenAI to tackle data-analysis tasks; 45% of novices completed tasks with GenAI + prompts; no cross-domain transfer was measured and writing tasks were not included in this study [Garg & Rajendran, 2024].
  - Garg et al. (2025) demonstrated that, in a three-arm RCT (control, GenAI without prompt training, GenAI with prompt training), post-test performance across Bloom’s levels favored the prompt-trained condition (G3 > G2 > Control) with means of G3 = 6.60, G2 = 4.94, Control = 4.28; ANOVA reported significant group differences, indicating causal effects of structured prompt training on learning depth within data-analysis/programming tasks (no explicit transfer measures) [Garg et al., 2025].
- Domain-expansion and design insights:
  - Baltà-Salvador et al. (2025) reported that AI-assisted ideation in design education did not diminish fluency, flexibility, or originality and that early AI exposure could promote originality and diversity, with semantic convergence observed in AI-assisted outputs; this suggests potential domain-general mechanisms (depth, transfer-like effects) but in ideation rather than classic learning-transfer tasks across writing/coding/data-analysis [Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025].
  - Prompt Programming (Padurean et al., 2025) describes a scalable platform with >900 students, multi-function problems, multi-turn dialogs, and on-demand code execution—providing design primitives for deep cognitive engagement and iterative problem solving, though not providing causal estimates of learning depth or cross-domain transfer in its deployed form [Padurean et al., 2025].
- Limitations to note:
  - No single study provides robust, cross-domain, randomized evidence that deliberate difficulty prompts causally outperform direct-answer AI across writing, coding, and data-analysis with transfer demonstrated to novel tasks. Available work largely covers data-analysis/programming within a domain, with partial transfer signals (Baltà-Salvador et al.) or process-data (multi-turn prompting) rather than clean causal estimates across domains [Summary of Garg & Rajendran 2024; Garg et al. 2025; Baltà-Salvador 2025; Padurean 2025].

1) Introduction and research question
- Research aim: To determine the causal effect of deliberate difficulty prompts from AI on learning depth and transfer, compared with direct-answer AI, across writing, coding, and data-analysis tasks.
- Rationale: Deliberate difficulty prompts—embodying depth-enabler and constraint-based design goals (e.g., self-explanation, contrasting cases, structured prompts like CLEAR, chain-of-thought)—are hypothesized to push learners toward deeper cognitive processing and greater transfer to novel tasks, beyond the gains from mere direct answers. The evidence base includes diverse educational contexts (data-analysis, programming, ideation) with experimental and quasi-experimental designs, offering converging signals but also notable gaps in cross-domain transfer and PERMA-related outcomes [Garg & Rajendran 2024; Garg et al. 2025; Baltà-Salvador 2025; Ali et al. 2024; Lo 2023; OpenAI 2023].

2) Evidence synthesis by domain and design

2.1 Data-analysis and coding tasks (experimental and quasi-experimental evidence)
- Garg & Rajendran (2024) – data-analysis in engineering education
  - Design: Within-subject pre–post with structured prompt training (CLEAR) and GenAI assistance; target tasks involved data aggregation/merging using Python/pandas/NumPy.
  - Core findings: Significant pre–post gains on concept knowledge and data-analysis concepts; large effect size (d = 0.89; p < 0.05); 45% task completion with AI + structured prompts; no explicit transfer measurement or cross-domain tasks reported; writing tasks not covered in this study.
  - Implication: Structured prompts can produce meaningful learning-depth gains within data-analysis tasks; indicates potential for depth-enabler prompts in coding/data-analysis domains, but does not address cross-domain transfer or writing tasks directly [Garg & Rajendran, 2024].

- Garg et al. (2025) – data-analysis and programming in engineering education
  - Design: Three groups in an RCT-like structure:
    - G1 Control: Internet access only; no GenAI
    - G2 GenAI without prompt training
    - G3 GenAI with prompt training
  - Intervention: Structured prompt training using few-shot prompting, chain prompting (CoT), and the CLEAR framework; tasks in data-analysis and programming; outcome measures included post-test scores across Bloom’s taxonomy and task completion indicators.
  - Core quantitative findings: Post-test means showed G3 > G2 > Control across Bloom’s levels; ANOVA reported significant group differences, indicating causal effects of structured prompt training on learning depth within data-analysis/programming tasks.
  - Applicable insights: Demonstrates causal impact of deliberate prompts on learning depth for data-analysis/programming tasks; explicit transfer to novel tasks or cross-domain (e.g., writing) was not measured in this study [Garg et al., 2025].

2.2 Writing, coding, and cross-domain transfer signals
- Baltà-Salvador et al. (2025) – AI-assisted ideation in design education
  - Design: Randomized crossover study comparing AI-assisted vs unaided ideation; domain: design-engineering ideation (not writing or data-analysis/C++ coding). Measures included creativity metrics (fluency, flexibility, originality) and semantic divergence; results showed no deterioration in core creativity dimensions under AI assistance and suggested early AI exposure could shift output toward originality and diversity, with semantic convergence in AI-assisted outputs.
  - Implication for transfer: Provides causal evidence that AI prompting can shape downstream ideation characteristics and trans-domain transfer-like effects within a domain; however, transfer across writing/coding/data-analysis tasks was not tested. It hints at mechanisms (engagement, exploration of solution spaces) that could underlie depth and transfer in other domains if adapted, but direct cross-domain evidence remains absent [Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025].

- Padurean et al. (2025) – Prompt Programming platform
  - Design: Describes a platform with >900 students, multi-function problems, multi-turn dialogues, and on-demand code execution; reports progression counts (e.g., 781 progressions on a password-validation problem, 156 on a robot-navigation problem) and engagement patterns; primarily descriptive and deployment-focused rather than causal learning-depth measurements.
  - Implication: The platform provides scaffolding for depth-oriented practice in programming; its data illustrate scale and interaction patterns that could support causal experiments but do not themselves establish learning-depth or cross-domain transfer effects [Padurean et al., 2025].

2.3 Domain-agnostic and theoretical scaffolds
- Lo (2023) – CLEAR framework
  - Core idea: A structured prompt framework (Concise, Logical, Explicit, Adaptive, Reflective) to improve prompt quality and task framing, thereby enhancing the educational usefulness of AI outputs.
  - Relevance: In all data-analysis, coding, and writing contexts, CLEAR-type prompts can serve as a core mechanism for implementing deliberate difficulty prompts and for enabling more reliable transfer-oriented practice.

- OpenAI (2023) – prompting strategies
  - Relevance: Guidance on few-shot prompting, chain-of-thought (CoT), and prompt decomposition; supports the mechanistic design of deliberate-difficulty prompts used in the Garg et al. studies.

- Ali et al. (2024) – prompts and educational design considerations
  - Relevance: Emphasizes five dimensions of AI in education (user, operational, environmental, technological, ethical) and the importance of prompts, dataset diversity, and human oversight; frames the thoughtful integration of AI as complementary to instruction and highlights the need for empirical evaluation of prompts’ pedagogical value.

- Nathaniel et al. (2025) – GenAI-Ped within programming education
  - Within-subject, pre/post design; reported significant improvements in HOTS (problem-solving, critical thinking, creativity) and programming logic with structured GenAI prompts; no randomized control group and no cross-domain transfer measured; domain: programming/coding tasks
  - Provides quantitative signals (t-values, p-values, and effect sizes) illustrating potency of structured prompting for depth within coding tasks, again without direct cross-domain claims [Nathaniel et al., 2025].

- Yin et al. (2025) – AI chatbot feedback and learning-related neural correlates
  - Finding: Metacognitive feedback enhances transfer and metacognitive sensitivity with distinct neural signatures; affective feedback improves retention; neutral feedback yields different executive control activations.
  - Relevance: Suggests that feedback design within AI tutors (including metacognitive prompts) can influence depth of learning and transfer via cognitive/metacognitive processes; supports the theoretical plausibility of deliberate-difficulty prompts affecting transfer, though not in the form of domain-specific, multi-task RCTs [Yin et al., 2025].

3) Causal interpretation and current evidence base
- What the current evidence can support:
  - Within-domain data-analysis/programming: Multiple studies indicate that deliberate/difficulty-oriented prompting (e.g., few-shot prompts, CoT, CLEAR) can causally improve learning depth relative to weaker prompting or no prompting within the tested domain(s) [Garg & Rajendran 2024; Garg et al. 2025; Nathaniel et al. 2025].
  - Transfer signals within domains: Some studies (Baltà-Salvador 2025; Yin et al. 2025) hint at transfer-like benefits or metacognitive processes that could support transfer, but explicit cross-domain transfer evidence remains scarce in the current corpus.
- Gaps and limitations:
  - Cross-domain transfer (writing, coding, data-analysis) with direct-domain measures is largely untested in a single study; longitudinal or multi-domain RCTs are rare in the existing literature.
  - Direct comparison between deliberate-difficulty prompts and direct-answer AI across multiple domains with transfer measurements is not yet established in a single, cohesive program of work.
  - PERMA measures are rarely reported in these experimental programs; most work focuses on cognitive outcomes (learning depth) with limited attention to well-being and engagement as formal outcomes.

4) Mechanisms and practical design considerations
- Mechanisms likely underlying gains:
  - Deliberate prompts induce deeper cognitive processing (self-explanation, contrastive reasoning, stepwise reasoning) and scaffolded feedback, fostering more principled understanding and problem-solving skill.
  - Structured prompts help learners articulate reasoning, decompose tasks, and engage in reflective practice, which supports transfer to novel problems (conceptual and procedural) within a domain [Garg et al., 2025; Lo, 2023; OpenAI 2023; Yin et al., 2025].
- Design considerations for future work:
  - Domain coverage: Include writing, coding, and data-analysis tasks in a single, multi-domain experimental framework to quantify cross-domain transfer.
  - Control conditions: Include a true direct-answer AI condition (prompts designed to elicit direct solutions with minimal scaffolding) vs deliberate-difficulty prompt condition vs no-AI control to isolate causal effects.
  - Outcome suite: Measure learning depth (Bloom’s levels or equivalent), transfer (novel tasks across domains), and PERMA components (engagement, meaning, accomplishment, relationships, well-being); report effect sizes and confidence intervals.
  - Process data: Collect AI-interaction logs, prompt types, and reasoning traces to analyze how depth-enabling prompts shape cognitive strategies and transfer pathways.

5) Proposed rigorous study design to answer the research question
- Design: Randomized controlled trial (or rigorous longitudinal design) with a 2 (AI prompting style) × 3 (domain: writing, coding, data-analysis) factorial, plus a no-AI control condition if feasible.
- Groups:
  - Deliberate-difficulty prompts group (DDP): AI assistance delivered via structured prompts designed to induce reasoning, self-explanation, contrasts, and stepwise problem solving (e.g., CoT, CLEAR, few-shot prompts).
  - Direct-answer AI group (DAI): AI assistance focused on delivering direct solutions with minimal scaffolding and prompts.
  - Control group (optional): No AI assistance or traditional instruction only.
- Domains and tasks: A set of parallel tasks across writing, coding, and data-analysis, each with domain-appropriate performance tasks and novel-transfer prompts.
- Outcomes:
  - Learning depth: Bloom’s taxonomy-based assessments (Understanding, Applying, Analyzing); domain-specific rubrics for depth of reasoning.
  - Transfer: Performance on novel, unseen tasks in each domain and cross-domain transfer tests (e.g., applying coding reasoning to data-analysis tasks, or applying data-analysis reasoning to writing prompts).
  - PERMA components: Validated scales for engagement, meaning, accomplishment, plus potentially positive affect and relationships.
  - Usability and engagement: SUS and task engagement metrics; qualitative think-alouds or post-task interviews.
  - Process metrics: Prompt types used, number of turns, time-on-task, use of chain-of-thought prompts, and frequency of self-explanation outputs.
- Design features to strengthen causal inference:
  - Randomization at the participant or task level with stratification by prior domain experience.
  - Pre-registration of hypotheses, outcomes, and analysis plans.
  - Sufficient power calculation to detect domain-specific effects and interactions.
  - Mixed-effects modeling to account for within-subject correlations across domains and over time.
- Analytic plan:
  - Primary analyses: ANCOVA/ linear mixed models with post-test scores as outcomes, controlling for baseline (pre-test) scores.
  - Effect sizes: Report Cohen’s d, partial eta-squared, and 95% CIs.
  - Mediation: Test whether learning-depth gains mediate transfer performance.
  - Moderator analyses: Examine domain as a moderator; look for prior AI familiarity as a potential moderator.
  - Data visualization: Include progression graphs of task performance by group and domain, plus interaction plots.

6) Practical, domain-appropriate prompt-design guidance
- For writing tasks:
  - Use prompts requiring audience-aware reasoning, persuasive structure, and justification. Require the learner to explain rhetorical choices, audience adaptation, and error-correction strategies; incorporate self-explanation prompts and contrasting-case prompts to compare alternative writing approaches.
- For coding tasks:
  - Emphasize code design decisions, trade-offs (readability, efficiency, maintainability), and stepwise reasoning (CoT). Include prompts that decompose problems into subtasks, request explanations of chosen algorithms, and require justification of refactoring decisions.
- For data-analysis tasks:
  - Use structured prompts that require data wrangling steps, choices of analytical methods, justification of assumptions, and interpretation of results. Include prompts that elicit explicit code and narrative explanations of outputs.
- Across domains:
  - Leverage CLEAR to structure prompts: concise problem statement, logical step-by-step expectations, explicit output formats, adaptive prompts that tailor difficulty, and reflective prompts that require justification and critique of AI outputs.
  - Include on-demand code execution where appropriate to promote testing and verification while avoiding overreliance.

7) Actionable insights for authors and educators
- If you want to advance causal understanding of deliberate difficulty prompts vs direct-answer AI across multiple domains:
  - Implement an RCT with DD-prompts vs direct-answer AI vs control, across writing, coding, and data-analysis tasks, with explicit transfer measurements and PERMA outcomes.
  - Pre-register a clear analysis plan, including planned effect sizes, sample sizes, and domain-specific rubrics.
  - Use a multi-method assessment battery (performance metrics, transfer tasks, cognitive measures, and engagement metrics), combined with process data from AI interactions.
  - Ensure domain-specific expert standards to anchor prompts and reference tasks to real-world practice.
- For practitioners and researchers:
  - Start with a robust prompt-design curriculum (CLEAR + CoT) integrated into existing courses; pair with ongoing practice across domains to facilitate transfer.
  - Collect and report domain-specific learning-depth outcomes and transfer measures, plus well-being metrics, to build a cumulative evidence base.
  - Use cross-domain tasks to evaluate generalizability and identify domain-specific constraints or enablers.
  - Maintain ethical and safety considerations (privacy, bias, accuracy) in AI-assisted learning interventions.

8) Limitations and caveats
- While the causal evidence base for deliberate prompts is growing in data-analysis and coding domains, there is still a lack of comprehensive cross-domain (writing, coding, data-analysis) causal evidence comparing deliberate prompts to direct-answer AI with explicit transfer and PERMA outcomes.
- Many studies provide domain-specific results with limited generalizability; caution is warranted when extrapolating to writing or to long-term transfer without additional, multi-domain trials.
- Real-world classroom settings introduce variability in teacher guidance, student motivation, tool familiarity, and domain-specific prior knowledge, which can influence effect sizes and generalizability.

9) Concrete next steps (recommended plan to implement)
- Step 1: Design a multi-domain RCT (writing, coding, data-analysis) with three arms (DD-prompt AI, direct-answer AI, no-AI control) and a two-wave design (pretest and posttest with transfer tasks at a later time).
- Step 2: Develop domain-specific expert standards and lists of deliberate-difficulty prompts (CoT+CLEAR) for each task type; build a standardized prompt library with templates.
- Step 3: Pre-register the study, define outcome measures (learning depth via Bloom’s levels, transfer success, PERMA indicators), and specify statistical methods (mixed-effects models, mediation analyses).
- Step 4: Run a pilot to calibrate task difficulty, ensure comparability across domains, and refine measurement instruments (rubrics, transfer tasks, PERMA scales).
- Step 5: Publish and share materials (prompt templates, tasks, rubrics, code) to facilitate replication and meta-analytic synthesis.

Appendix: how to reproduce this synthesis
- The synthesis draws on several primary studies with varying designs (RCTs, within-subject pre/post, crossover) and across domains (data-analysis, programming, ideation). The causal claims are strongest for within-domain data-analysis/programming tasks with deliberate-prompt interventions; cross-domain transfer evidence remains limited. For a comprehensive cross-domain causal assessment, future work should implement a unified, multi-domain RCT or longitudinal design with explicit transfer measures and PERMA outcomes.

Notes
- This report integrates findings from multiple sources to answer the user’s research question. While several studies provide causal signals about the impact of deliberate prompts in AI-assisted learning (especially within data-analysis/programming domains), there is no single, published study that conclusively demonstrates causal superiority of deliberate difficulty prompts over direct-answer AI across writing, coding, and data-analysis tasks with explicit transfer to novel tasks and PERMA measures. The proposed study designs and actionable recommendations aim to address these gaps.
