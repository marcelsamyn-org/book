Durable long-run skill development and taste under depth-enabled AI designs: a synthesis and design-guidance

Executive summary
- Across diverse lines of inquiry, depth-enabled AI designs (reflective prompts, constraint-based workflows, and diverse output sampling) show promise for promoting deeper engagement, reflective practice, and richer idea generation during AI-assisted work. However, direct evidence that these designs yield durable long-run skills or lasting taste after AI support is removed remains scarce or indirect in the current literature (various sources summarized below).
- Several studies report durable-adoption signals or post-use performance gains within AI-assisted contexts, or post-AI engagement that suggests continued internalization of practices, but few provide rigorous longitudinal post-disengagement measurements. Notable signals include sustained use of AI-assisted workflows in production settings (Wang et al., 2025), growth in independent performance after familiarization phases in AI-assisted tasks (Long et al., 2024), and evidence linking higher GenAI literacy to stronger independent performance immediately after AI support ends (Yan et al., 2025). Yet none conclusively prove durable skill retention or taste after complete AI withdrawal (Yan et al., 2025; Long et al., 2024; Kang, 2024).
- Where evidence exists, design principles that emphasize human agency, explicit reflection, diverse outputs, and structured constraints align with theoretical accounts of durable competencies and discernment. Nevertheless, gaps remain for longitudinal, cross-domain, and post-disengagement outcomes (Zhai et al., 2024; Paglialunga & Melogno, 2025; ISRE 2024 editorial).
- Actionable pathway: advance longitudinal research that directly measures post-disengagement skill retention and taste, while continuing to improve depth-enabled designs (reflective prompts, constraint-based workflows, diverse sampling) and ensuring robust human-in-the-loop governance, ethics, and provenance practices.

Research question addressed
Do depth-enabled AI designs (reflective prompts, constraint-based workflows, and diverse output sampling) produce durable long-run skill development and taste, even after AI support is removed?

Key sources and inline evidence (selected)
- Depth-enabled design features and workflow (VisRep framework, depth exploration, structured prompts, multi-format outputs) and emphasis on reflective elaboration: Owen & Roberts (2025) [VisRep; DOI: 10.3390/make7030072]. Related discussion of depth-enabled cues in design workflows appears across CHI/ACM-related work (Xu & Konnova, 2025; Wang et al., 2025 CHI; Johnson & Paulus, 2024).
- Longitudinal and durability signals in AI-assisted contexts:
  - Longitudinal familiarization and early-prompts durability signals (Not Just Novelty): Long, Gero, & Chilton (2024) – not post-AI withdrawal, but shows durability of perceived usefulness after familiarization when users tailor prompts (DIS ’24; arXiv:2402.09894) [Long et al., 2024].
  - Field durability signals in AIideation in production contexts (AIdeation): Wang et al. (2025) – two studios continue to use AIideation after a field trial; however, no direct post-AI-removal skill-taste measurements are reported (CHI 2025). Inline: Wang et al., 2025.
  - GenAI literacy and durability of independent performance: Yan, Nakajima, & Sawada (2025) – higher GenAI literacy predicts better independent multimodal writing after AI removal; effect is moderated by chatbot interaction design (GLAT/VLAT measures; post-AI context) [Yan et al., 2025].
  - Durability gaps identified in broad AI-use literature: Zhai et al. (2024); Macnamara et al. (2024); Paglialunga & Melogno (2025) highlight the need for longitudinal assessment and caution about automation-related skill decay or reliance.
- Depth-enabled design patterns explored in educational and creative contexts:
  - Reflective prompts and reflective friction; strategy–tactics approaches; depth-enabled prompts in design studios and writing tasks (Johnson & Paulus, 2024; Kang, 2024; Karadağ & Ozar, 2025; Xu & Konnova, 2025).
  - Diverse output sampling to counter convergence and promote taste diversification (Kang, 2024; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Lee et al., 2025).

Synthesis by depth-enabled design element

1) Reflective prompts and talk-aloud, reflective elaboration
- What we know:
  - Reflective prompts and structured prompts are repeatedly suggested as mechanisms to sustain reflective elaboration, metacognition, and justification of AI-produced content (Djeffal, 2025; Johnson & Paulus, 2024; Xu & Konnova, 2025).
  - Qualitative/longitudinal signals suggest participants internalize workflows and increase perceived usefulness after familiarization, especially when prompts invite critical evaluation (Long et al., 2024).
  - In GenAI literacy work, metacognitive development and ethical reflection emerge as durable trajectories when learners engage in iterative practice with diverse tools (Yan et al., 2025).
- Durability implications:
  - These patterns plausibly support durable cognitive habits that persist post-AI if paired with ongoing independent practice and explicit post-AI evaluation tasks. However, no robust longitudinal post-disengagement measurements exist in the cited work to confirm durability beyond AI use.

2) Constraint-based workflows
- What we know:
  - Constraint-based workflows are highlighted in VisRep and design frameworks as a means to anchor reasoning, promote structured thinking, and slow the AI-assisted process to foster reflection (Owen & Roberts, 2025; Kang, 2024).
  - In higher-education contexts, cognitive-forcing functions and structured thinking aids are advocated to mitigate overreliance and maintain analytical rigor (Lee et al., 2025; Kang, 2024).
- Durability implications:
  - The mechanism is consistent with durable skill development if constraints embed core principles that can be recalled and applied without AI. Direct post-AI measurements are not provided; future work should test post-disengagement performance on constraint-anchored tasks.

3) Diverse output sampling
- What we know:
  - Diverse sampling is advocated to avoid convergence and to train evaluative taste; in practice, depth-enabled configurations yield more varied outputs and encourage critical appraisal (Kang, 2024; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Lee et al., 2025).
  - Post-AI durability signals are not established; convergence concerns persist when AI outputs become normative.
- Durability implications:
  - If learners internalize the habit of evaluating multiple outputs and selecting among alternatives, taste diversification could persist beyond AI use. This requires explicit post-AI practice tasks that exercise independent evaluation.

4) Post-AI durability evidence (or lack thereof)
- Notable sources on durability signals post-AI:
  - Not Just Novelty (Long et al., 2024): Moderate evidence that familiarity and customization yield sustained perceived usefulness during the AI-enabled phase, with ownership growing; but no post-disengagement skill measurements.
  - GenAI literacy (Yan et al., 2025): Post-AI performance in writing shows predictive power of literacy levels; outcome is immediate post-AI context; durability beyond AI is not established.
  - AIideation field durability (Wang et al., 2025 CHI): Continued production use after field deployment; not a direct test of post-AI withdrawal skill retention.
- Gaps:
  - No robust longitudinal data that explicitly quantify durable skill retention or taste development after AI support is removed across domains.

5) Cross-domain/generalizable durability frameworks
- ISRE 2024 editorial: Pathways framework emphasizes abstraction spectra, artifact typology, and desiring traits like rich domain adaptation to support cross-domain durability of competencies; however, it remains a design framework with limited empirical durability data (ISRE 2024).
- The broader literature emphasizes the need for longitudinal, cross-domain studies with rigorous measurement, preregistration, and open data to establish durability.

Quantitative findings (selected highlights)
- GenAI-literacy durability signal: Yan et al. (2025) report that higher GenAI literacy predicts stronger independent performance after AI removal; effect is moderated by chatbot interaction strategy (passive vs. proactive), suggesting that literacy plus interaction design can influence post-AI durability in the short term (Yan et al., 2025).
- Familiarization durability within AI-enabled tasks: Long et al. (2024) show post-familiarization increases in perceived usefulness, driven largely by prompt customization; durability beyond AI usage is not demonstrated (Long et al., 2024).
- AI-assisted production durability signals: Wang et al. (2025) show sustained use in production contexts, implying durability of workflow adoption within AI-assisted practices, but not durability after AI withdrawal (Wang et al., 2025).
- Meta-analytic and causal durability signals are scarce; Paglialunga & Melogno (2025) report strong short-term gains in LD populations with AI interventions but caution about potential bias and a lack of longitudinal durability data.

Actionable insights for designers and researchers (practical guidance)
- Plan longitudinal post-AI withdrawal measurements:
  - Design studies that measure skill retention, transfer, and taste after a clearly defined disengagement period.
  - Use objective performance tasks and blind expert assessments to gauge durable competencies and aesthetic judgments beyond AI involvement.
- Operationalize durability metrics:
  - Define concrete metrics for durable skill (e.g., independent problem-solving quality, consistency of reasoning, retrieval of core principles) and for taste (diversity, alignment with expert standards, nuanced judgments) in AI-free contexts.
- Preserve and evaluate provenance and self-regulated practice:
  - Document prompts, reasoning traces, and justification logs to support retrieval of internalized strategies after AI withdrawal.
- Encourage gradual disengagement with scaffolding:
  - Use a staged reduction of AI support, paired with reflective prompts and retrieval practice, to cultivate independence rather than abrupt withdrawal.
- Embrace cross-domain durability studies:
  - Extend experiments across multiple domains (education, design, writing, etc.) to test the generalizability of durability signals from depth-enabled designs.
- Emphasize governance, ethics, and human oversight:
  - Embed robust human-in-the-loop governance and reflection to ensure that durable competencies include critical appraisal, ethical use, and domain-aligned taste.

Limitations and caveats
- The majority of cited work provides insight into AI-assisted performance, or short-term durability signals within AI-enabled contexts. Longitudinal, post-AI-removal durability data remain sparse.
- Generalizability across domains is uncertain; many studies focus on specific tasks (design ideation, multimodal writing, science communication, LD education).
- Variability in AI systems, task types, and user expertise complicates cross-study synthesis.

Limitations of this synthesis
- The evidence base for direct longitudinal durability of skills and taste after AI withdrawal is still emergent and domain-dependent. Most cited sources provide cross-sectional or in-context durability signals, not robust post-AI-removal measurements.
- The design space (reflective prompts, constraint-based workflows, and diverse output sampling) is supported conceptually and in short-run demonstrations, but rigorous longitudinal validation remains an open research agenda.

Implications for the author
- If your objective is to advance durable long-run skill development and taste beyond AI support, prioritize:
  - Longitudinal study designs with predefined post-AI-removal assessment windows.
  - Mixed-methods measurement that captures durable skills (problem-solving quality, metacognitive accuracy) and taste (diversity, alignment with expert standards) in AI-free contexts.
  - Explicit integration of reflective prompts, constraints, and diverse outputs with scaffolding for independent practice, plus governance and ethics training.
  - Cross-domain replication to establish generalizability of durability effects.
  - Transparent preregistration, open data/code, and preregistered replication plans to support cumulative evidence.
