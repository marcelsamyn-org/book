# Report: Boundary Conditions for AI Support and Originality Across Domains with Varying Novelty

Artifact: report.md

Executive summary
- Across domains with varying novelty, AI support can simultaneously elevate individual originality and reduce population-level diversity (a homogenization risk) when AI outputs become a dominant source of ideas. This pattern has been observed in creative writing, design ideation, and related domains and depends on AI role, timing, modality, and user interaction patterns [Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Moon et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Ismayilzada et al., 2024].
- Strong boundary-condition signals center on: AI role (assistive vs. co-creative), stage of the creative process (divergent ideation vs. convergent evaluation), task/domain novelty, and the interaction quality between humans and AI (e.g., prompting strategies, joint generation vs. editing AI output) [McGuire et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025/2026].
- Domain mappings suggest concrete, domain-specific guidelines:
  - Creative writing: AI is most beneficial when used to stimulate divergent ideas early, with prompts that encourage unusual angles and perspectives; avoid reliance on AI for sentence-level elaboration that can increase semantic convergence [Doshi & Hauser, 2024; McGuire et al., 2024].
  - Coding: AI can outline novel approaches but requires human review to preserve originality of algorithms and data structures; co-creative modes may help at high novelty if prompts are divergent and human steering is explicit [Ismayilzada et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025].
  - Design: Early exposure to AI can expand the concept space if prompts support radical exploration; however, ensure diverse prompts and human critique to mitigate convergence toward familiar design motifs [Wang et al., 2025; Baltà-Salvadore et al., 2025/2026; Sano & Yamada, 2022].
- Actionable guidance for researchers and practitioners includes: (i) randomized three-arm AI-role designs (none, assistive, co-creative) across domains and novelty levels; (ii) domain-appropriate originality metrics complemented by process metrics; (iii) explicit attention to prompt design, model heterogeneity, and human-AI collaboration rituals to sustain originality while controlling homogenization.

Introduction: problem framing and boundary conditions
- Core question: What boundary conditions determine when AI support hinders versus helps originality across domains with varying novelty, using a study that randomizes AI assistance level (none, assistive, or co-creative) and measures domain-appropriate originality?
- Converging evidence points to four interrelated boundary-condition levers:
  1) AI role and interaction quality (assistive vs. co-creative; prompts and collaboration patterns) [Singh et al., 2025; McGuire et al., 2024; Doshi & Hauser, 2024].
  2) Timing and phase of AI involvement (divergent ideation vs. convergent refinement/evaluation) [Wang et al., 2025; Moon et al., 2024; Ismayilzada et al., 2024].
  3) Task/domain novelty and domain-specific originality metrics (domain-appropriate novelty vs. generic measures) [Ismayilzada et al., 2024; Kern et al., 2024; Lin, Peng, & Fang, 2024].
  4) Interaction patterns and embodiment (textual vs multimodal outputs; degree of human agency; transparency) [Singh et al., 2025; McGuire et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025].
- Representative evidence
  - Creative writing: AI can boost individual originality but reduce group-level diversity; early AI exposure yields cross-task carryover benefits; but output convergence arises when many writers draw from similar AI prompts or AI-generated ideas [Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Moon et al., 2024; Luther et al., 2024].
  - Design/Dimensional representations: AI can increase divergent ideation in text, image, and 3D spaces; however, convergent evaluation remains challenging; dimensional representation modulates the degree and type of originality augmentation [Wang et al., 2025].
  - Co-creativity frameworks and design implications: High user control and adaptive AI can deepen collaboration and ownership, but early-phase support is often under-addressed; a three-arm design across domains is recommended to map boundary conditions precisely [Singh et al., 2025; Baklaga, 2025].
  - Domain-specific metrics: Domain-appropriate originality metrics are essential; for scholarly contexts, retrieval-augmented novelty benchmarks (e.g., SchNovel, RAG-Novelty) outperform baseline judgments and emphasize domain-sensitive assessment rather than domain-agnostic semantics [Lin, Peng, & Fang, 2024; Wang et al., 2025].

Conceptual framework: boundary-condition levers and measurement axes
- Boundary-condition levers
  - AI role: none vs assistive vs co-creative; interaction quality and prompts determine whether AI augments or constrains originality [Singh et al., 2025; McGuire et al., 2024].
  - Task novelty: low, medium, high; higher novelty typically benefits from more exploratory, co-creative interaction, provided there is strong human guidance to counterbalance AI bias and homogenization [Ismayilzada et al., 2024; Krakowski, 2025].
  - Domain modality: text-only vs multimodal (text, image, 3D) with modality-specific originality manifestations (linguistic novelty, visual/conceptual novelty, functional/architectural novelty) [Wang et al., 2025; Meincke et al., 2024; Ghasemi et al., 2024].
  - Interaction patterns: exploratory, joint generation vs directive or editing AI outputs; prompts designed to maximize divergent thinking versus consolidation of existing patterns [Moon et al., 2024; McGuire et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2025].
  - Domain-specific metrics: novelty/originality, usefulness/feasibility, and domain-relevant proxies (lexical/semantic diversity in writing; novel algorithms/structures in coding; concept/visual novelty in design) [Kern et al., 2024; Lin, Peng, & Fang, 2024; Ismayilzada et al., 2024].
- Measurement axes
  - Individual originality (domain-specific novelty)
  - Collective diversity (across participants or artifacts)
  - Process measures (divergent vs convergent activity, AI usage prompts, iteration counts)
  - Perceived ownership, trust, and perceived control
  - Task performance/feasibility and usefulness

Study design: a comprehensive randomized, cross-domain protocol
- Experimental factors
  - Domain: creative writing, coding, design
  - AI role: none (control), assistive, co-creative
  - Task novelty: low, medium, high
- Design type
  - A factorial, randomized design with within-domain manipulation of AI role and novelty, plus cross-domain comparisons. A mixed design (domain as between-subjects; AI role and novelty as within-subjects) can balance power with practicality. A crossover design could enable carryover analyses of AI exposure effects (as in Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025/2026).
- Outcome measures (domain-appropriate originality metrics)
  - Creative writing
    - Novelty/originality: semantic/lexical novelty; metaphor usage; plot-angle divergence
    - Usefulness: narrative coherence, relevance to audience
    - Textual diversity: variety of voice, stylistic choices
    - Judges: independent expert raters with high inter-rater reliability
    - Auxiliary: Embedding-based similarity (DSI-like metrics) to quantify semantic convergence/divergence [Kern et al., 2024; Moon et al., 2024].
  - Coding
    - Algorithmic novelty: new approaches, data structures, or architectures
    - Structural originality: modularity and design patterns
    - Correctness/robustness: automated tests and manual review
    - Readability/maintenance: code clarity and maintainability
  - Design
    - Concept novelty: divergence from existing concepts; novelty of functional relationships
    - Visual/functional novelty: new visual idioms, interactions, materials
    - Feasibility/usability: expert evaluation of practicality and user value
    - Diversity metrics: clustering of concepts, concept-space entropy
- Evaluation approach
  - Domain-specific originality metrics plus multidimensional creativity scales (Novelty, Value/Usefulness, Surprise)
  - Process traces: AI prompts, iteration logs, and collaboration patterns
  - Mixed-methods: quantitative originality metrics plus qualitative expert judgments and participant feedback
- Analysis plan
  - Multilevel mixed-effects models with random effects for participants and tasks; fixed effects for domain, AI role, novelty, and their interactions
  - Effect sizes (Cohen’s d, partial eta-squared) with 95% CIs
  - Mediation analyses to examine whether prompts strategy, perceived control, or trust mediate the AI-role–originality relationship
  - Pre-registered hypotheses with planned contrasts across AI roles and novelty levels
- Pre-registration and ethics
  - Pre-register hypotheses, metrics, and analysis plan
  - Ensure transparency about AI prompts, model versions, and data provenance
  - Include guardrails for bias, authorship attribution, and fairness across domains

Domain-specific boundary-condition mappings (quick syntheses)
- Creative writing
  - Boundary condition: High potential for co-creative AI to boost divergent thinking; however, risk of homogenization across writers unless prompts promote diversity; early AI exposure can yield cross-task carryover benefits
  - Practical guidance: Use co-creative AI with explicit prompts for divergent phrasing and unusual narrative angles; track both individual novelty and group diversity; pair with human editorial steps to sustain voice and authenticity
  - Supporting evidence: Doshi & Hauser (2024); Moon et al. (2024); Kern et al. (2024)
- Coding
  - Boundary condition: Co-creative AI can surface novel algorithmic ideas, but needs strong human oversight to ensure originality and correctness; assistive AI may improve efficiency but could bias toward familiar templates
  - Practical guidance: Use co-creative prompts that encourage unconventional algorithmic strategies and cross-domain analogies; implement code-review rubrics focusing on originality of approach; monitor for convergence in templates
  - Supporting evidence: Ismayilzada et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2025)
- Design
  - Boundary condition: Early AI exposure can broaden concept-space in design, particularly with multimodal representations; however, to preserve originality, prompts must encourage radical concept exploration and critique
  - Practical guidance: Employ dimensional representations (text, image, 3D) to stimulate divergent ideation; maintain human-led evaluation with diversity-promoting prompts; apply explicit novelty/diversity/desirability constraints in generation
  - Supporting evidence: Wang et al. (2025); Sano & Yamada (2022); Baltà-Salvador et al. (2025/2026)

Design considerations for researchers and educators
- Prompts and co-creative workflows
  - Favor prompts that explicitly demand divergent thinking and multiple incompatible constraints; avoid prompts that merely optimize for probability-high outputs
  - Use multi-model ensembles to counter model monoculture and broaden the ideation space
  - Structure human–AI collaboration as iterative negotiation with explicit decision logs to preserve authorship
- Evaluation and metrics
  - Adopt domain-appropriate originality metrics with robust validity; include cross-domain metrics where possible (e.g., UnIC/RND-style domain-agnostic measures) for cross-domain comparability
  - Use expert panels and external raters to increase reliability of originality judgments
  - Report both individual originality gains and group-level diversity/trends, highlighting potential homogenization risks
- Ethics, attribution, and governance
  - Document AI contributions and prompts to support accountability and fair attribution
  - Consider fairness and bias in AI outputs; monitor training-data-derived biases and their impact on domain-specific originality assessments
  - Preemptively address concerns about homogenization and authorship by integrating transparency and controllability into AI tools

Limitations and cautions
- Generalization across domains requires careful domain-specific validation; many boundary-condition findings derive from writing and design experiments and may not transfer unconditionally to coding
- Many studies cited rely on short-term or experimental tasks; long-term effects in real-world settings warrant longitudinal designs
- AI model drift and evolving capabilities may shift boundary conditions over time; ongoing replication and protocol updates are recommended

Notes and cautions
- This report integrates diverse empirical strands to extract boundary-condition signals and proposes a comprehensive experimental framework. Given the heterogeneity of domains and modalities, researchers should tailor metrics, prompts, and evaluation paradigms to each domain and validate cross-domain applicability with careful replication.
- Where the literature provides quantitative details (e.g., percentages, AUROC scores), those should be treated as indicative bounds in planning studies; exact values should be consulted in the original articles during protocol design.

Actionable insights for authors (concise)
- Design a three-arm randomized study (none, assistive, co-creative) across creative writing, coding, and design tasks, each with low/medium/high novelty levels.
- Use domain-appropriate originality metrics complemented by cross-domain diversity measures; incorporate process data (prompt strategies, iteration counts) and interaction logs.
- Seek domain-specific expert ratings and, where feasible, automated proxies (embedding-based semantic distance for text; structural/algorithmic novelty for code; concept-space diversity for design).
- Emphasize human-in-the-loop and prompt-design practices to promote genuine divergent thinking and counter AI-induced homogenization.
- Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans; report both domain-specific originality outcomes and cross-domain diversity outcomes, with transparent documentation of AI prompts, model versions, and collaboration patterns.
