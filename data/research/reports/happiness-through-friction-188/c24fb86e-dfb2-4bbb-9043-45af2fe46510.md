Executive summary
- A broad body of AI-enabled learning research treats flow-like engagement as a mechanism that can be fostered by dynamically balancing challenge and skill (flow balance) in AI-assisted tasks. Across domains, adaptive difficulty, timely feedback, autonomy-supportive design, and domain-aware calibration emerge as the principal levers for sustaining engagement and promoting longer-term skill development (Melnikoff et al., 2022; Szűts & Molnár, 2022; Quan et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2014; Deterding & Cutting, 2023; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025).
- Domain differences are consistently highlighted: flow-driven engagement dynamics appear more robust in STEM/quantitative domains (e.g., math, physics, programming) than in some humanities contexts, though cross-domain applicability is a central goal of many adaptive systems. Empirical patterns show stronger and more consistent engagement and learning gains where adaptive difficulty is paired with domain-appropriate feedback and mastery pathways (Ma et al., 2014; Hamilton & Kim, 2021; Fryer & Howard-Noveck, 2020; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025).
- Quantitative anchors across key studies:
  - MI-centered flow experiments in AI-assisted tasks show higher reported flow and downstream attention/enjoyment as I(M;E) increases (Melnikoff et al., 2022).
  - In AI-controlled tasks with precise difficulty manipulation (e.g., tile games), balance toward 50% success did not guarantee higher enjoyment, highlighting domain-specific limits to classical HD/SD flow intuition (Cutting et al., 2023).
  - Large-scale AI tutoring in STEM yields modest-to-moderate gains over non-adaptive baselines, with larger effects in higher-stakes or longer-duration interventions and domain-specific contexts; cross-domain generalization remains a key research question (Ma et al., 2014; Steenbergen-Hu & Cooper, 2013; Lin et al., 2024; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025).
  - Domain differences in engagement durability are common when comparing STEM vs humanities or reading contexts; within STEM, subdomains show variability in mastery and engagement relations to adaptive cues (Villegas-Ch et al., 2025; Peng & Li, 2025).
- Actionable takeaway for authors/designers: embed MI/flow-inspired targets in adaptive AI design (dynamic difficulty, transparency, autonomy support), measure domain-specific engagement proxies alongside learning outcomes, and conduct longitudinal cross-domain studies to map where flow-driven durability and transfer hold best.

Theoretical framing: flow balance, autonomy, and domain generality
- Flow and the challenge-skill balance: Flow emerges when task difficulty aligns with the learner’s skill, with additional prime conditions including clear goals and immediate feedback. In AI-assisted contexts, this balance can be maintained via dynamic difficulty adjustment (DDA) and targeted feedback, even as domain-specific mappings between ends (goals) and means (actions) vary (Csikszentmihalyi; Melnikoff et al., 2022; Szűts & Molnár, 2022) [flow theory references; inline: Csikszentmihalyi, 1990; Melnikoff et al., 2022].
- Mutual information as a cross-domain design principle (MI framework): A central claim in Melnikoff et al. (2022) is that flow scales with the mutual information between means and ends, I(M;E). Higher MI indicates a stronger coupling between actions (instructional means) and ends (learning outcomes), predicting stronger immersion and downstream benefits. This provides a mechanism that generalizes beyond a single task (tile game) to AI tutoring, reading, dancing, or other goal-directed activities, provided ends and means are operationalized per domain (Melnikoff et al., 2022) [mutual information, domain-general claim; inline: Melnikoff et al., 2022].
- Instrumental value and intrinsic motivation: In Melnikoff et al. (2022), instrumental rewards/punishments have limited impact on flow, suggesting that flow can persist or grow when instrumental value shifts, provided MI coupling remains strong. This informs AI tutors that extrinsic incentives should be de-emphasized relative to increasing MI through instructional means (Melnikoff et al., 2022).
- SDT-based design in AI tutoring: Self-Determination Theory (autonomy, competence, relatedness) frames why adaptive feedback and dynamic difficulty calibration matter for engagement durability and long-term growth. Across numerous studies, autonomy-supportive interfaces and competence-affirming guidance mediate sustained engagement and skill growth (Quan et al., 2025; Derakhshan, 2025; Ma et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2025).
- Cross-domain generality with domain-specific mappings: Domain-general MI/flow mechanisms exist, but the ends-means mapping (M and E) is domain-specific. For AI tutors, this implies a single MI-centric framework must be instantiated differently for math, reading, coding, language learning, etc., with domain-relevant ends and means definitions (Melnikoff et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2014; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025).

Cross-domain evidence synthesis: what domains reveal about engagement durability and long-term skill development
- Tile-game MI experiments (Melnikoff et al., 2022): Increasing I(M;E) yielded higher reported flow and downstream attention/enjoyment; precise MI control was feasible in computer-based tasks, illustrating a robust mechanism to sustain flow across domains that can be implemented in AI tutors by mapping ends/means to domain-relevant trajectories (tile game design; inline: Melnikoff et al., 2022).
- Real-time classroom AI (Szűts & Molnár, 2022): In AI-supported classroom contexts, adaptive AI that calibrates difficulty and provides timely scaffolding supports flow-like engagement, with SDT-consistent autonomy/competence/relatedness metrics guiding adaptation; cross-domain extrapolation is suggested but not empirically tested beyond the classroom domain (Szűts & Molnár, 2022) [flow-like engagement; domain implications].
- SDT-mediated AI feedback across higher education (Quan et al., 2025): Perceived AI feedback quality directly increased engagement and persistence; intrinsic motivation and learner autonomy mediated these effects, underscoring SDT-driven channels by which adaptive feedback can sustain engagement over weeks. This supports MI-informed design as a practical axis in AI tutoring across domains (Quan et al., 2025) [SDT mediation; inline: Quan et al., 2025].
- ITS meta-analytic evidence (Ma et al., 2014; Steenbergen-Hu & Cooper, 2013): Across dozens of studies, ITS generally improve learning outcomes across domains, with effects varying by domain, baseline performance, and comparator. While engagement durability is not always directly measured, adaptive, feedback-rich, and model-based ITS designs align with flow principles and likely support longer-term skill development when implemented with fidelity and domain alignment (Ma et al., 2014; Steenbergen-Hu & Cooper, 2013).
- Domain-specificity and cross-domain variability (Peng & Li, 2025; Han et al., 2025; Leon et al., 2025; Szűts et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2014): Across reviews, domain differences are a recurring theme. STEM domains tend to exhibit stronger and more consistent benefits from adaptive tutoring and AI-assisted feedback, but humanities and social science domains show more variability; robust cross-domain longitudinal data remain a central gap.

Quantitative anchors by domain (selected highlights from the cited corpus)
- MI/flow experiments: MI increases produce higher flow measures in AI-assisted tasks in controlled tile-game contexts (Melnikoff et al., 2022).
- Short-/mid-term ITS gains: ITS typically yield modest-to-moderate gains (around 0.3–0.6 SD) versus non-ITS, with larger effects as duration and domain fit improve; human tutoring can outperform AI in some contexts, especially with longer interventions or highly scaffolded environments (Ma et al., 2014; Steenbergen-Hu & Cooper, 2013; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025) [domain synthesis; inline citations].
- Domain differences in AI tutoring: STEM domains (math, physics, CS) show robust gains from adaptive tutoring; humanities and reading show more heterogeneity, highlighting the necessity of domain-aware adaptation (Ma et al., 2014; Hamilton & Kim, 2021; Fryer & Howard-Noveck, 2020; Kortecamp & Peters, 2023; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025; Peng & Li, 2025) [domain-specific anchors; inline citations].
- SDT-mediated engagement durability: Direct SDT mediation of engagement/persistence by AI feedback quality (Quan et al., 2025) provides a practical design blueprint for flow-oriented adaptivity in education across domains (autonomy, competence, relatedness as mediators).

Actionable insights and design recommendations (synthesized from the evidence)
- Define domain-specific ends and means, then maximize MI
  - For each domain (e.g., math, reading, programming, language, etc.), formalize ends (domain goals/outcomes) and means (instructional actions, hints, feedback, difficulty adjustments) to maximize mutual information I(M;E). Use MI-informed targets to guide adaptive difficulty and instructional strategy across sessions and domains (Melnikoff et al., 2022).
- SDT-aligned adaptivity as a core design principle
  - Implement autonomy-supportive interfaces (task choice, pacing controls, flexible feedback modalities) and competence-affirming cues (clear mastery pathways, corrective strategies) to satisfy needs for autonomy and competence; integrate relatedness cues (social presence, instructor scaffolding) where appropriate. Use these as mediators to enhance engagement durability and long-term growth (Quan et al., 2025).
- Domain-aware cross-domain evaluation
  - Plan longitudinal, cross-domain studies (e.g., STEM vs humanities) with standardized flow proxies (autonomy, competence, relatedness) and domain-specific learning outcomes, to map where flow-based adaptive difficulty predicts engagement durability and transfer. Design studies with at least 6–12 weeks for initial durability signals and longer horizons for transfer assessment (Ma et al., 2014; Peng & Li, 2025; Leon et al., 2025; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025).
- Measurement strategies: multi-method flow proxies
  - Combine self-report flow measures with MI proxies, behavioral engagement metrics (time-on-task, persistence, help-seeking), and domain-appropriate learning outcomes. Where feasible, include physiological or neurophysiological proxies to triangulate flow signals (e.g., MI indices, SDT-mediated mediators) while ensuring ethical considerations and privacy protections (Melnikoff et al., 2022; Hang et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2025; Li et al., 2023).
- Longitudinal evaluation and transfer
  - Emphasize long-term follow-ups to capture durability of engagement and transfer to novel domains or tasks. Compare AI-assisted adaptive conditions to human tutoring and non-adaptive baselines to isolate the contribution of flow-balanced adaptation to durable engagement and growth (Ma et al., 2014; D’Mello & Graesser, 2023; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025; Leon et al., 2025).
- Ethics, equity, and governance
  - Prioritize transparency, privacy-by-design, bias mitigation, and inclusive access in all AI tutoring deployments. Integrate governance frameworks (UDL, trustworthy AI) to ensure equitable engagement and long-term growth across diverse populations and domains (Leon et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2014; Han et al., 2025).

Limitations and caveats
- The evidence base spans many domains and task types with heterogeneous methodologies; direct, consistent measurements of flow balance, engagement durability, and long-term skill transfer across multiple domains are still relatively sparse. Cross-domain generalization requires rigorous longitudinal, multi-domain studies with standardized measures and preregistration (Ma et al., 2014; Vu et al., 2025; Peng & Li, 2025; Leon et al., 2025; Szűts & Molnár, 2022; Cutting et al., 2023; Melnikoff et al., 2022).
- Several cited works focus on design principles, MI, or SDT mediation rather than reporting explicit effect sizes for flow-related durability outcomes; future work should report standardized effect sizes and confidence intervals for engagement durability and long-term skill development across domains (Ma et al., 2014; Quan et al., 2025; Hamilton & Kim, 2021; Villegas-Ch et al., 2025; Leon et al., 2025).
