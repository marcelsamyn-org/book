# Does presenting diverse AI outputs reduce output homogenization and improve user taste and decision quality, and under what conditions?

Executive summary
- A broad set of studies across creative writing, decision-support, and generative decoding strategies suggest that exposing users to multiple AI outputs (diverse priors) can reduce homogenization and support richer user judgments, but the effects are highly context-dependent. When diversity is explicitly modeled and aligned with quality criteria, multi-output strategies tend to broaden option space and can improve decision quality or taste preservation; when diversity is poorly constrained or misaligned with user goals, homogenization or misjudgments can still occur (Bradley et al., 2024; Holtzman et al., 2020; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Ashkinaze et al., 2025; Wang & Pellegrino, 2025) [inline citations].
- Key mediators and moderators identified in the literature include: domain/task type (open-ended vs constrained), user expertise, evaluation design (AI-based vs human evaluation), how diversity is defined and operationalized (axes of variation), and interface/workflow design that supports comparison and critical appraisal.
- Actionable guidance: (1) surface a curated set of diverse high-quality outputs rather than a single top choice; (2) implement AI feedback or domain-appropriate diversity metrics to guide generation; (3) pair multi-output exposure with human-in-the-loop validation and domain-specific prompts or mutation strategies; (4) calibrate the amount and type of diversity with user expertise and task demands; (5) validate across domains and with domain-specific benefits or risks in mind.

1) Research question and scope
- Core question: Does presenting diverse AI outputs (multiple priors) versus a single best output reduce output homogenization and improve user taste and decision quality, and under what task/user conditions?
- Why it matters: If diversity can be maintained without sacrificing quality, users can explore a broader design or decision space, potentially improving satisfaction, taste preservation, and decision outcomes in subjective or open-ended tasks. Conversely, naïve multi-output exposure may induce cognitive overload, anchoring, or homogenization at the population level if not carefully designed (Bradley et al., 2024; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Ashkinaze et al., 2025) [inline citations].

2) Evidence landscape by domain and method
- Creative-writing QD with AI feedback (QDAIF; Bradley et al., 2024)
  - What was done: MAP-Elites style quality-diversity search guided by AI feedback to generate and evaluate a population of candidate texts across three creative domains (opinion writing, short stories, poetry). Mutation via LMX; domain-specific prompts (poetry) added.
  - Key findings: QDAIF covers more of the search space with high-quality outputs than a quality-only baseline; human judgments align reasonably with AI assessments of quality and diversity; results described as improved coverage and diversity across multiple writing domains (Bradley et al., 2024) [inline citation].
  - Quantitative signals: The abstract notes “covers more of the specified search space with high-quality samples” and “reasonable alignment” between AI and human judgments; exact effect sizes are not provided in the abstract, but figures (e.g., Figure 1) illustrate broader coverage and richer archetypes than baselines (Bradley et al., 2024) [inline citation].
  - Conditions: Domain is subjective text; diversity is defined via a grid/archive across diversity axes; evaluation uses AI-driven judgments calibrated against human ratings; no model fine-tuning required (Bradley et al., 2024) [inline citation].
- Open-ended decoding and diversity in generation (Holtzman et al., 2020)
  - Core result: Maximization-based decoding (beam search) yields bland, repetitive outputs; pure sampling can be incoherent; nucleus/top-p sampling offers a principled balance, achieving higher quality and diversity in open-ended generation according to human judgments (HUSE framework).
  - Practical takeaway: For open-ended tasks where diversity matters, nucleus/top-p sampling is a preferred default over beam search or pure sampling alone, with the diversity-quality trade-off tunable via the nucleus parameter p (Holtzman et al., 2020) [inline citation].
- Selective sampling under risk constraints (Troshin et al., 2025)
  - Core idea: A sampling risk classifier dynamically decides when to apply high-temperature sampling; in mathematical reasoning tasks, selective sampling improves the quality-diversity trade-off under high-temperature regimes.
  - Practical takeaway: In domains requiring both diversity and correctness, a risk-aware selective sampling approach can reduce mode collapse while preserving accuracy (Troshin et al., 2025) [inline citation].
- REAL sampling with uncertainty-aware decoding (Chang et al., 2025)
  - Core idea: REAL sampling adapts the top-p threshold as a function of a hallucination-risk predictor (THF); in FactualityPrompts benchmarks, REAL improves factuality and diversity for 7B models and outperforms many baselines when combined with contrastive decoding (Chang et al., 2025) [inline citation].
  - Practical takeaway: Adaptive, risk-informed multi-output sampling can boost both factuality and diversity, especially in open-ended contexts where factual accuracy is essential.
- Diversity in dialogue generation (Stasaski, 2023)
  - Core ideas: Data diversity (DIDC), multi-response generation, NLI-based metrics for diversity, and pragmatics-informed diversity (PA diversity); diversity is domain- and task-dependent; axis design matters.
  - Quantitative signals: DIDC yielded statistically significant gains in diversity metrics (e.g., p < 0.001 in certain comparisons) and improved downstream performance in emotion classification with diverse corpora (Stasaski, 2023) [inline citation].
- Multi-domain, multi-output evidence synthesis (Ziems et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Doshi & Hauser, 2024)
  - Ziems et al. (CSS tasks): LLMs’ zero-shot classification vs fine-tuned baselines; generation tasks show AI can provide rich, diverse explanatory outputs; domain differences noted.
  - Doshi & Hauser (Science Advances, 2024): AI priors improve individual creativity but may reduce collective diversity; effects are stronger for less creative writers; suggests careful design when aiming for collective taste diversity.
  - Guo et al. (2024): Writers integrate AI with values-driven workflows; multi-iteration AI use preserves authorial voice and enables diverse outputs across stages; highlights interaction design as critical.
- Decision-support and governance perspectives (Bojic et al., 2024; Bloomfield & Rushby, 2025; Dwivedi et al., 2023)
  - Bojic et al. (Futures, 2024): diversity in recommender outputs and tripartite decision-making (individuals, society, tech firms) can mitigate homogenization; emphasizes governance, regulation, transparency to realize benefits of diverse priors.
  - Bloomfield & Rushby (2025): diversity as a guardrail within dependable AI; multi-output exposure should be embedded in defense-in-depth and accompanying explanations, with micro-ODDs to tailor context.
  - Dwivedi et al. (2023): calls for future research on multi-output prompts and derivatives in education/research; emphasizes governance, transparency, and credible evaluation.
- Human–AI collaboration frameworks (Ashkinaze et al., 2025; Wang & Pellegrino, 2025; Etcheverry, 2023)
  - Ashkinaze et al. (2025): high AI exposure increases diversity in idea space but does not increase creativity per se; domain is chemosensory creativity; suggests surface-level diversity and novelty effects that may differ by domain.
  - Wang & Pellegrino (2025): UK sample shows that stochastic AI configurations can align more with human judgments of creativity (novelty as driver) but are domain-specific (culinary).
  - Etcheverry (2023): diversity-driven exploration (IMGEP, meta-diversity) is effective in open-ended systems; emphasizes human-in-the-loop and scalable diversity representations for broad domains.

3) Quantitative signals and effect patterns (where available)
- QDAIF (Bradley et al., 2024): no explicit numeric effect sizes in abstract; qualitative claim that QD Coverage improves; alignment between AI and human judgments reported.
- Open-ended nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020): qualitative claim that nucleus sampling is best overall when balancing diversity and quality; concrete numerical values not provided in high-level summaries.
- REAL sampling (Chang et al., 2025): robust improvements in factuality and diversity for 7B models on FactualityPrompts benchmarks; specific numeric improvements depend on model size and baseline; outperforming several baselines (quantitative details in the paper’s benchmarks).
- DIDC (Stasaski, 2023): statistically significant improvements in diversity metrics (e.g., p < .001 for some measures) and downstream task performance when training with diverse data; domain-specific results reported.
- Ashkinaze et al. (2025): high AI exposure increases collective diversity; positive effects stronger for less creative writers; domain-specific differences in adoption of AI ideas; no direct productivity-only metrics provided.
- Doshi & Hauser (2024): individual gains in creativity but potential decline in collective diversity; domain is short-story writing; no direct multi-output experimental measurement across individuals.
- Bojic et al. (2024): conceptual framework; no direct experimental effect sizes for multi-output diversity; governance framing.
- Epitome/AI gatekeeping/CI literature (2023–2025): platform/tooling for multi-output experiments; not numeric results, but practical guidance for experimental design and measurement.

4) Conditions under which diverse outputs help (consistent patterns)
- Task domain and openness
  - Most robust evidence for benefits of diverse outputs appears in open-ended, subjective or ideation-rich domains (creative writing, brainstorming, exploration, design, policy reasoning) where multiple plausible outputs are judged as valuable (Bradley et al., 2024; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Stasaski, 2023; Ashkinaze et al., 2025) [inline citations].
- Evaluation and alignment
  - AI-based evaluation aligned with human judgments supports scalable, domain-agnostic deployment of multi-output strategies, but alignment strength and reliability depend on prompt design, model quality, task type, and domain familiarity (Bradley et al., 2024; Stasaski, 2023) [inline citations].
- Diversity axes and interface design
  - Explicit diversity axes (grid/archive) or multi-response presentations, paired with side-by-side comparisons and explicit reasoning for each option, help users weigh alternatives without overwhelming cognitive load (Bradley et al., 2024; Holtzman et al., 2020; Bojic et al., 2024) [inline citations].
- Human-in-the-loop and governance
  - Governance, transparency, explanations, and human oversight improve outcomes and guard against homogenization or misuses when presenting multiple outputs (Bojic et al., 2024; Bloomfield & Rushby, 2025; Dwivedi et al., 2023) [inline citations].

5) Practical implications and actionable guidelines
- Design principles
  - Surface diverse high-quality options: When appropriate, present a curated set of outputs that cover diverse stylistic, tonal, or strategic axes, rather than defaulting to a single best output.
  - Use AI/Domain feedback to steer generation: Implement AI-based quality and diversity evaluation prompts, or domain-specific mutation strategies, to maintain alignment with user taste and task objectives.
  - Calibrate diversity to the domain and user: For creative tasks, prioritize semantic diversity and stylistic variety; for constrained tasks (fact-checking, translation with strict constraints), prioritize quality and constraint satisfaction with limited but meaningful diversity.
  - Facilitate human comparison: Provide side-by-side views, explanations, and provenance of each output; enable users to rank, filter, or fuse outputs to support decision quality.
  - Monitor cognitive load and adaptively scale diversity: Offer user controls to adjust exposure or diversity intensity; use human-in-the-loop checks or progressive disclosure to mitigate overload.
- Domain-specific guidance
  - Creative domains: Expect beneficial effects from presenting multiple high-quality outputs with explicit diversity axes; validate alignment with human taste via human evaluation.y
  - Information/decision-support domains: Where accuracy matters, use risk-aware or constrained diversification (e.g., REAL sampling, selective sampling) to maintain factuality while offering alternatives.
  - Hiring/SDM or governance contexts: Use diversity within a decision-support workflow guarded by explanations and governance checks; support reflective decision-making with multiple priors and structured deliberation (Wilkens et al., 2025; Bojic et al., 2024).
- Evaluation strategy
  - Pre-register hypotheses about homogenization and decision-quality outcomes; use multiple outcome measures: diversity metrics (semantic, lexical, or task-relevant), decision-quality metrics (accuracy, calibration, confidence, satisfaction), and process metrics (time to decision, cognitive load).
  - Include human validation and cross-domain replication to ensure generalizability and to calibrate AI-based evaluation signals.

6) Limitations, gaps, and caveats
- Many cited works are domain-specific (creative writing, culinary creativity, dialogue) or provide high-level frameworks without universal numeric effect sizes; caution is needed when generalizing across domains (Bradley et al., 2024; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Stasaski, 2023; Ashkinaze et al., 2025) [inline citations].
- Direct experimental comparisons of multi-output versus single-output strategies across broad task types remain relatively sparse; several papers demonstrate potential, while others emphasize conditions under which diversification may not help (e.g., anchoring, cognitive load, or misalignment with user goals) (Alavi & Nozari, 2023/2025; Wang & Pellegrino, 2025; Han & Ren, 2025) [inline citations].
- Several studies rely on AI-based evaluation signals; while alignment with human judgments is promising, the strength and domain dependence of this alignment require careful validation in each domain and task (Bradley et al., 2024; Stasaski, 2023) [inline citations].

7) Research and design recommendations
- For researchers
  - Design cross-domain experiments directly comparing single-output vs multi-output (diverse priors) conditions, using standardized diversity metrics and domain-appropriate decision-quality measures.
  - Examine moderators: task type (open-ended vs constrained), domain, user expertise, interface design, and the presence of human-in-the-loop evaluation.
  - Use adaptive diversity strategies (e.g., risk-aware selective sampling, REAL sampling, QDHF-style online diversity learning) to balance diversity with reliability, particularly in high-stakes or factual contexts.
  - Explore governance and transparency tools to promote trust and acceptance when presenting multiple outputs.
- For practitioners
  - When deploying multi-output strategies, implement intuitive comparison interfaces, provenance, and explanations for each output; calibrate exposure to user needs and domain constraints.
  - Build diversity-promoting features into decision-support workflows with human-in-the-loop checks; avoid anchoring and cognitive overload by providing controlling levers for users.
  - Pilot and validate diversity approaches with domain-specific tasks and user cohorts, tracking both objective outcomes (decision quality, accuracy) and subjective outcomes (taste diversity, satisfaction, perceived usefulness).

9) Concrete actionable takeaways for authors and designers
- If the aim is to reduce output homogenization while preserving or enhancing decision quality:
  - Implement multi-output sampling with explicit diversity axes or dimensionless diversity architectures (MAP-Elites-inspired) and couple them with AI-based or human-in-the-loop diversity evaluation.
  - Use adaptive diversity controls (risk-aware decoding, dynamic p, or adaptive diversity prompts) to balance safety and novelty.
  - Provide side-by-side comparisons, explanations, and provenance for outputs to enable informed selection and minimize overreliance on a single output.
  - calibrate exposure to user expertise and domain context; avoid universal defaults that may induce homogenization across diverse user groups.
- For experimental validation:
  - Design cross-domain experiments with both objective task outcomes and subjective taste/decision-quality metrics.
  - Include moderator analyses for task type, user expertise, and interface design; preregister hypotheses and publish metrics for diversity and quality.
  - Use human-in-the-loop calibration to validate AI-derived diversity signals with domain experts.
- For policy and governance:
  - Incorporate diversity as a policy objective within AI-assisted decision workflows; pair with explanations and provenance to support democratic engagement and reduce homogenization risks.
  - Consider adaptive governance mechanisms to balance diversity with reliability, particularly in high-stakes domains.
