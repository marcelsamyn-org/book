# report.md

Do AI-facilitated reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback improve metacognitive calibration and reduce overconfidence during complex problem solving?

Executive summary
- A growing body of work investigates how AI-enabled reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback influence metacognition, confidence judgments, and problem-solving performance. Across studies, AI-facilitated prompts often boost metacognitive engagement (reflection, self-explanation, self-regulation) and can shift confidence judgments, but direct evidence that these interventions strongly improve metacognitive calibration (confidence–accuracy alignment) or reduce overconfidence during complex problem solving is mixed and context-dependent [Ma et al., 2024; Ahmad et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2020; Pape & Thomas, 2025].
- Key pattern: metacognitive design elements (reflective prompts, confidence declarations, explanations, and error-spotting feedback) tend to influence how learners monitor and regulate thinking, transfer strategies, and study behaviors more reliably than raw performance gains alone. However, calibration metrics (e.g., confidence–accuracy alignment, overconfidence) are not consistently reported; when reported, effects are nuanced—some conditions improve calibration, others reduce under- or overconfidence, and some increase cognitive load or do not affect performance directly [Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020; Ahmad et al., 2025; Pape & Thomas, 2025].
- Practical takeaway: if your goal is durable metacognitive calibration and reduced overconfidence in complex problem solving, design AI interventions that explicitly couple reflective prompts with explicit confidence judgments, error analysis, and calibrated feedback, while carefully managing cognitive load and domain specificity. Rigorous reporting of calibration metrics is essential to establish causal effects.

1) Research question and scope
- Core question: Do AI-facilitated reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback improve metacognitive calibration (alignment between confidence and accuracy) and reduce overconfidence during complex problem solving?
- Rationale: AI can nudge reflective thinking, self-correction, and self-assessment, potentially improving calibration over time if learners engage metacognitively and if feedback supports accurate monitoring and regulation of cognition.

2) Evidence landscape: what we know from relevant studies
Note: below, findings are summarized with explicit study design details when available. Where numerical results are not reported in the source excerpt, I provide qualitative outcomes and reported effect directions.

2.1 AI-facilitated reflective prompts, confidence judgments, and metacognitive calibration
- Ma et al. (2024) CHI: Are You Really Sure? Understanding the Effects of Human Self-Confidence Calibration in AI-Assisted Decision Making
  - Design: Three calibration mechanisms tested within AI-assisted and non-AI contexts:
    - Think the Opposite (Think)
    - Thinking in Bets (Bet)
    - Calibration Status Feedback (Feedback)
  - Conditions: AI-assisted and non-AI contexts; memory of human and AI confidence judgments; task performance in decision-making (Judge–Advisor System style)
  - Key findings:
    - Think and Feedback improved alignment between self-confidence and accuracy (metacognitive calibration) relative to control.
    - Think increased perceived cognitive load and decreased user satisfaction relative to Feedback.
    - Calibration improved associated with more rational reliance on AI suggestions; however, calibration did not universally reduce overconfidence, and there were boundary conditions related to domain and task type [Ma et al., 2024].
  - Calibration focus: Directly tested confidence–accuracy alignment and reliance patterns; explicit calibration metrics were reported conceptually, with emphasis on alignment rather than full-spectrum calibration statistics in the abstract.
  - Implication for practice: Reflective prompts and calibration feedback can improve calibration and rational use of AI, but design trade-offs (cognitive load, satisfaction) matter; overconfidence reduction is not guaranteed by calibration alone.

- Zhang et al. (2020) FAT* paper: Are You Really Sure? Understanding the Effects of Confidence and Explanation on Accuracy and Trust Calibration in AI-Assisted Decision Making
  - Design: Two experiments examining how confidence signals and local explanations influence trust calibration and joint human–AI decision making.
  - Findings:
    - Confidence signals can help calibrate trust, but trust calibration alone does not guarantee improved joint performance; human knowledge gaps still limit effectiveness.
    - Local explanations did not robustly improve calibration; design limitations indicate the need for more effective explanation strategies to support calibration.
  - Implications: Confidence signaling can aid calibration, but calibration alone does not ensure better outcomes; explanations require careful design to support metacognitive reflection and accurate self-assessment (calibration).

2.2 AI-facilitated reflective prompts, error-spotting feedback, and metacognitive SRL
- Ahmad et al. (2025) L@S: How Adding Metacognitive Requirements in Support of AI Feedback in Practice Exams Transforms Student Learning Behaviors
  - Design: Randomized controlled trial in a large undergraduate biology course (N ≈ 1002) with three conditions:
    - Guide-to-textbook feedback
    - AI-generated feedback
    - Control (correct/incorrect only)
  - Metacognitive prompts: All conditions required confidence ratings and explanations.
  - Key quantitative findings:
    - No statistically significant differences in exam scores across feedback conditions.
    - The metacognitive design elements (confidence ratings and explanations) produced the strongest impact on metacognitive processes and study behaviors, suggesting enhanced self-regulated learning and transfer strategies despite no performance gain within the midterms.
    - Textbook engagement (~40%) increased when prompted by AI feedback; learner satisfaction high (4.1/5); 82.1% reported increased confidence on practiced topics; 73.4% could recall/apply practiced concepts (Ahmad et al., 2025).
  - Interpretation: Metacognitive scaffolds (confidence declarations + explanations) can scale formative learning and metacognitive behavior even when direct performance gains are not observed; this aligns with SRL literature emphasizing process over immediate outcomes.
  - Calibration link: Direct calibration metrics (confidence–accuracy curves) were not reported; calibration in this study is inferred from metacognitive engagement and strategy transfer rather than explicit calibration statistics.

- Pape & Thomas (2025) IJCCI: Adaptive metacognitive prompting in young learners and the role of prior performance
  - Design: Pre-post experiment in 72 lower secondary students; prompt group (n=38) vs control (n=34)
  - Intervention: Adaptive metacognitive prompts (set-goal, prioritization, reflect, goals planner)
  - Measures: Self-reported metacognitive SRL activities (MSLQ-based), transfer performance
  - Findings:
    - Metacognitive prompting increased self-reported metacognitive SRL activities.
    - No significant improvement in overall learning performance.
    - Prior performance moderated the prompt effects on transfer; higher prior achievement altered the transfer benefit.
  - Calibration relevance: No direct calibration metrics reported; reflective prompting influenced metacognitive regulation, with moderation by prior performance indicating differential calibration potential across learner histories.

- Yin et al. (2025) npj Science of Learning
  - Design: AI-driven chatbot feedback with three types:
    - Metacognitive feedback
    - Affective feedback
    - Neutral feedback
  - Measures: Transfer scores, retention, metacognitive sensitivity (post-answer confidence), neural correlates (fNIRS), and machine-learning predictors of transfer
  - Key findings:
    - Metacognitive feedback enhanced transfer and metacognitive sensitivity; neural activations in frontopolar and middle temporal gyrus correlated with metacognitive sensitivity.
    - Affective feedback improved retention and engaged different neural circuits (supramarginal gyrus); neutral feedback engaged dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.
  - Calibration relevance: While metacognitive sensitivity (a proxy for calibration) improved with metacognitive feedback, explicit confidence–accuracy calibration metrics were not reported; however, results indicate a meaningful link between feedback type, metacognition, and transfer, with neural correlates supporting the metacognitive mechanism.

- Besigomwe (2025) Cognizance: Algorithmic self-deception in AI-generated feedback
  - Design: Mixed-methods study comparing AI-generated feedback vs human vs no feedback; undergraduate writing tasks
  - Findings:
    - AI feedback improved essay scores but increased overconfidence; qualitative data suggested algorithmic self-deception where AI feedback inflates self-perceptions and reduces critical reflection.
  - Calibration relevance: Highlights risk that AI feedback, if not designed to promote metacognitive reflection and error identification, can foster overconfidence and reduce reflective depth.

2.3 Domain-specific and developmental evidence
- Moșoi et al. (2025) BMC Psychology
  - Population: 473 pre-service teachers; moderation by cognitive traits (Need for Cognition, Cognitive Reflection)
  - Findings: Higher CRT and NFC predicted better performance and less AI reliance when satisfied with solving tasks; AI introduction sometimes reduced performance; trust in AI higher when reflective thinking and task satisfaction were lower.
  - Calibration relevance: Suggests individual differences in cognitive traits influence how AI-facilitated reflective prompts and AI usage relate to calibration dynamics; hints that prompts must be tailored to learner profiles.

- Zhang, Tian, Lu et al. (2025) BMC Psychology
  - Focus: The relationship between higher-order thinking and problem solving with generative AI; moderated mediation analyses
  - Findings: Metacognition acts as a moderator between creative and critical thinking; AI-facilitated reflective prompts can influence metacognition, though calibration metrics were not the central outcome.
  - Calibration relevance: Supports the idea that metacognition plays a central role in how AI-enabled problem solving unfolds, offering pathways to calibrate confidence through metacognitive strategies.

2.4 Cross-cutting insights on calibration and overconfidence
- Electricity of calibration signals: Several studies suggest that providing explicit confidence judgments, uncertainty information, and reflective prompts can influence calibration, but the effects are nuanced and context-dependent (domain, task difficulty, learner characteristics) [Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020; Ahmad et al., 2025; Pape & Thomas, 2025; Yin et al., 2025].
- The risk of overconfidence with AI assistance: Multiple lines of evidence point to AI-induced overconfidence in some contexts (Besigomwe, 2025; Cash et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2020). To mitigate this, calibration must be anchored in explicit feedback loops, not just confidence displays or explanations.
- The value of SRL and metacognition as levers: When designed well, reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback can foster self-regulated learning, better transfer, and potentially improved calibration over time, even if short-term performance does not improve (Ahmad et al., 2025; Pape & Thomas, 2025; Kautzmann & Jaques, 2019).

3) Methodological notes and limitations across the evidence
- Calibration metrics are inconsistently reported. When present, they include:
  - Confidence judgments and accuracy (direct calibration metrics like calibration curves, Brier scores, slope/intercept) (Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020; Cash et al., 2025).
  - Metacognitive sensitivity indices (e.g., meta-d′) or related SRL/monitoring measures (Yin et al., 2025; Moșoi et al., 2025).
  - Reliance quality and transfer metrics (Ma et al., 2024; Ahmad et al., 2025; Yin et al., 2025).
- Domains and populations vary widely (undergraduate biology, teacher education, younger learners, AI literacy contexts, professional decision tasks). This heterogeneity complicates universal claims about calibration improvements.
- Several studies report strong metacognitive engagement or SRL improvements without corresponding immediate performance gains, suggesting calibration effects may manifest more strongly in long-term learning, transfer, and self-regulation rather than in short-term test scores.
- Ethical and practical risks (overreliance, algorithmic self-deception, biases) require explicit safeguards when designing AI prompts and feedback for calibration-focused interventions.

4) Synthesis: what this means for your research question
- Yes, AI-facilitated reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback can improve metacognitive engagement and influence confidence judgments, but there is not a consistent, universal demonstration that such interventions robustly and durably improve metacognitive calibration (confidence–accuracy alignment) or reliably reduce overconfidence across diverse complex-problem contexts.
- Key determinants of success appear to be:
  - Explicit confidence reporting and justification prompts (to surface and adjust confidence in light of evidence).
  - Targeted error-spotting and corrective feedback that directly highlights miscalibrations and guides re-estimation.
  - Manageable cognitive load and domain-appropriate design to avoid prompt fatigue and performance penalties.
  - Personalization to learner characteristics (prior performance, cognitive traits, language/cultural context).
  - Explicit calibration metrics in evaluation (calibration curves, Brier scores, confidence–accuracy correlations) to quantify effects beyond SRL/process measures.

5) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners
- Design and implement AI interventions with explicit calibration goals
  - Include explicit confidence judgments per item, followed by AI feedback that connects confidence to correctness, potential biases, and evidence-based justification.
  - Add error-spotting prompts that require learners to identify where they might be wrong and to reason about alternative solutions before receiving final feedback.
  - Use uncertainty signaling (e.g., AI-confidences, calibrated uncertainty estimates) rather than single-point predictions to guide reflection and metacognitive adjustment.
- Minimize cognitive load and optimize UX
  - Balance the frequency and depth of prompts to avoid cognitive overload; consider adaptive triggering based on task difficulty, prior performance, and current calibration gaps.
  - Offer optional deep-dive reflective prompts for learners who want more extensive metacognitive engagement, preserving autonomy for others.
- Measure calibration directly and comprehensively
  - Predefine calibration metrics: calibration curves (observed vs. predicted confidence), Brier score, calibration-in-the-large/slopes, confidence-accuracy correlations, and overconfidence indices (difference between confidence and accuracy on incorrect responses).
  - Collect item-level data to analyze calibration across difficulty and domain, not only aggregate scores.
- Use mediation analyses to illuminate pathways
  - Examine whether improvements in metacognitive calibration mediate gains (or transfers) in problem-solving performance, and whether learning behaviors (reflection depth, self-correction, error analysis) mediate this pathway.
- Personalize prompts, mindful of individual differences
  - Tailor reflective prompts to learner profiles (prior performance, cognitive traits, language background, domain expertise) to maximize calibration benefits and minimize unintended increases in cognitive load.
- Report comprehensively and transparently
  - Publish calibration metrics alongside performance outcomes; provide effect sizes, confidence intervals, and model specifications to enable cross-study comparisons and meta-analyses.
- Safety and ethics
  - Be wary of overreliance and algorithmic self-deception risks; incorporate safeguards (transparency about AI limits, human-in-the-loop checks, and explicit prompts that require independent verification where appropriate).

6) Proposed robust study design for direct calibration assessment
- Objective: Directly test whether AI-facilitated reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback improve metacognitive calibration and reduce overconfidence in complex problem solving.
- Design: Randomized controlled trial with multiple arms
  - Arm A: Reflective prompts only (confidence prompts + self-explanation prompts)
  - Arm B: Reflective prompts + explicit error-spotting feedback
  - Arm C: Error-spotting feedback only
  - Arm D: Control (standard AI feedback without calibrated prompts)
- Tasks and domains: Complex problems across several domains (e.g., biology, economics, coding) with escalating difficulty
- Participants: Adequate sample size per arm (n ≈ 150–300 per arm) to detect small-to-moderate calibration effects
- Measures:
  - Calibration metrics:
    - Confidence ratings per item
    - Item-level accuracy
    - Calibration curve (reliability diagram), slope/intercept
    - Brier score
    - Confidence–accuracy correlation (Spearman or Pearson)
    - Overconfidence index (mean confidence of incorrect items)
  - Secondary outcomes: Transfer performance, time-on-task, cognitive load (validated scales), SRL indicators (reflective depth, error analysis frequency)
  - Process data: Prompt usage logs, error-spotting interactions, justification lengths
- Analytic plan:
  - Mixed-effects models with random effects for participants and tasks
  - Compare calibration metrics across arms; test mediation by reflective depth and error analysis
  - Robustness checks across domains and task difficulties
  - Pre-registration and replication-friendly design
- Reporting:
  - Effect sizes and 95% CIs for calibration metrics
  - Calibration curves and statistical tests for slope/intercept differences
  - Clear documentation of prompt content, frequency, and adaptivity rules

7) Practical takeaways for practitioners designing AI-assisted problem solving systems
- If your aim is to improve calibration and reduce overconfidence:
  - Combine reflective prompts with explicit error-spotting feedback and per-item confidence judgments.
  - Provide calibrated uncertainty signals rather than single-point predictions; show how confidence compares to actual outcomes.
  - Monitor cognitive load and adapt prompt frequency/content to maintain engagement without burdening learners.
  - Personalize prompts by learner profile and prior performance to maximize calibration benefits.
  - Report calibration metrics in addition to performance to demonstrate true metacognitive gains.
- If performance gains are the sole objective, be cautious: calibration-focused design may not translate into higher short-term scores, but it can foster deeper self-regulated learning and transfer over time.

8) Key takeaways from the broader evidence base
- Reflective prompts and metacognitive prompts can meaningfully influence metacognitive engagement and strategy use, sometimes without immediate performance gains, but with potential for longer-term calibration and transfer benefits [Ma et al., 2024; Ahmad et al., 2025; Pape & Thomas, 2025].
- Calibration metrics should be central in reporting to determine whether changes in confidence reflect actual performance improvements and whether overconfidence is reduced.
- Overreliance and overconfidence remain persistent risks when AI outputs are trusted without explicit calibration mechanisms; robust uncertainty signaling and external verification are prudent design choices [Zhang et al., 2020; Besigomwe, 2025; Cash et al., 2025].

Closing note
- The cumulative evidence suggests that AI-facilitated reflective prompts and error-spotting feedback hold promise for enhancing metacognitive engagement and the quality of self-assessment, but direct, robust demonstrations of improved metacognitive calibration (confidence–accuracy alignment) and reductions in overconfidence across complex problem solving require carefully designed experiments with explicit calibration metrics, multi-domain tasks, and longitudinal follow-up. The proposed research design in section 6 offers a concrete pathway to obtain these causal, generalizable estimates.
