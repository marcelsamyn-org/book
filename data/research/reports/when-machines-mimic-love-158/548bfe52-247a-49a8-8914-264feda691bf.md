Do connection-creator prompts improve real-world bonding? Moderation by prior closeness and cultural background — a synthesis of converging evidence and design implications

Executive summary
- Connection-creator features that promote real-time, interactive dialogue and mutual contributor roles tend to foster greater bonding signals in short lab chats, with effects often strongest when the feature encourages genuine reciprocal exchange rather than solo or purely asynchronous writing (examples: Fini et al., 2025; Leckfor, 2020; Aron et al., 1997). Topic type (abstract vs concrete) matters less than interaction quality, especially in interactive conditions (Fini et al., 2025; Leckfor, 2020; Welker et al., 2024).
- Across domains, proximity, pairing, and conversational structure influence bonding outcomes and depth of conversation. Proximity-driven pairing can raise observed friendship formation with caveats about boundary-crossing between dissimilar dyads (Rohrer et al., 2021). Hyperscanning work shows strangers converge neurally and linguistically with prompts that foster exploratory dialogue, while friends tend to diverge, yet alignment to exploratory dynamics can boost enjoyment in strangers (Speer et al., 2024).
- Moderation by prior relationship closeness and cultural background is variably tested across studies. Some evidence suggests baseline closeness moderates long-term bonding trajectories (e.g., months of closeness predicting future closeness; Ledbetter et al., 2007), while other studies emphasize culture-driven variation in conversation norms and effectiveness of prompts (Kim, 2006; Murata, 1991). In many key experiments, explicit moderation by culture or pre-existing closeness was not the primary focus or was not reported.
- Practical implication: when designing connection-creator features, emphasize real-time interactive exchanges, explicit prompts for mutual self-disclosure, and adaptive matching or targeting that accounts for relationship context and culture. Measure bonding with robust, multi-method outcomes (closeness scales, IOS, perceived contribution, depth of conversation) and test explicit moderators in well-powered designs.

1) Research question and scope
- Research question: Do connection-creator features that (a) facilitate real conversations in real time and (b) identify which friends to talk to improve real-world bonding and depth of conversations, and are such effects moderated by prior relationship closeness or cultural background?
- Scope: synthesis across experimental and quasi-experimental evidence on conversation quality, closeness, bonding, and moderator considerations. The literature spans lab-based interaction studies, field experiments, dyadic bonding paradigms, cross-cultural analyses, and large-scale observational datasets. While some work directly tests prompts or prompt-like features, other sources test adjacent mechanisms (e.g., interaction quality, proximity, self-disclosure, and culture-specific conversation norms), all of which bear on how prompts might affect real-world bonding.

2) Core evidence and how it bears on the question

2.1 Do interactive prompts that enable real-time conversation improve bonding and depth?
- Strong evidence that real-time, interactive dialogue boosts bonding, beyond topic content:
  - Fini et al. (2025) found that brief interactive chats with another person yielded significantly greater self-reported closeness than non-interactive writing, with closeness linked to dialogue pleasantness, intimacy, and perceived contribution by the partner [Fini et al., 2025].
  - Leckfor (2020) showed intimate prompts (self-disclosure–oriented) yielded higher reported closeness and affiliative outcomes than small-talk prompts in a within-subject design with strangers, with effects persisting to a 1-week follow-up; mood improvements did not mediate this effect [Leckfor, 2020].
  - Welker et al. (2024) extended the mechanism by showing self-views converge (inter-self alignment) during enjoyable conversations, and greater alignment predicted higher enjoyment and willingness to meet again; the design did not test moderators (culture or baseline closeness) directly but provides a concrete mechanism linking dialogue to bonding [Welker et al., 2024].
- Implication: prompts and features that structure interactive, reciprocal exchanges (with emphasis on mutual contribution and self-disclosure) appear likely to enhance bonding and perceived depth of conversation, at least in the short term and in controlled settings. Topic content (abstract vs concrete) appears less critical when interaction quality is high.

2.2 Do such effects generalize to real-world bonding beyond lab settings?
- Mixed but informative evidence:
  - Rohrer et al. (2021) demonstrated in a large naturalistic classroom trial that proximity (deskmate pairing) increases the likelihood of mutual friendship formation, with boundary conditions by dyadic similarity (e.g., gender) influencing manifest bonding; latent propensity to friendship rose across all dyads, suggesting proximity-related bonding can generalize in real-world group settings.
  - The CANDOR dataset (Reece et al., 2023) provides a rich resource for studying bonding, depth, and multimodal cues in naturalistic conversations and suggests that analysis-ready proxies (prosodic, facial, semantic cues) could underpin future tests of prompts that identify conversation partners and guide real-world bonding. The dataset itself does not test prompts but offers a platform for causal testing in the future.
- Implication: proximity-based and posture/structure-informed prompts can have real-world relevance; however, translating lab-based prompt efficacy to real-world bonding requires designs that induce repeated, meaningful interactions across contexts and cultures.

2.3 Moderation by prior relationship closeness or culture
- Moderation by closeness:
  - There is evidence that pre-existing closeness matters for bonding trajectories. Ledbetter et al. (2007) show months of closeness early in a relationship robustly predicts closeness years later, even when controlling for earlier closeness; this highlights that baseline closeness can moderate long-term bonding outcomes and may interact with intervention prompts that promote early depth (Months of closeness → 2002 closeness; manifest similarity’s role varies by model) [Ledbetter et al., 2007].
  - Leckfor (2020) uses strangers; the moderation by pre-existing closeness is not applicable there, but the broader implication is that baseline relationship context will shape how prompts influence bonding depth.
- Moderation by culture:
  - Kim (2006) finds cross-cultural differences in the perceived importance of conversational constraints (clarity, non-imposition, avoiding negative evaluation), implying that prompts should be culturally adapted to maximize efficacy. Murata (1991) emphasizes cross-cultural pragmatics in conversation culture and cautions that prompts must respect sociocultural norms around self-disclosure and turn-taking.
  - The World Happiness Report (2025) and related cross-cultural studies underline that culture shapes social connection, loneliness, and well-being—implying that a universal prompt design is unlikely to be optimal. Some evidence points to strong cross-cultural heterogeneity in bonding processes and the effectiveness of social interventions, suggesting culture–prompts interactions should be empirically tested.
- Moderation by other individual differences:
  - Fang et al. (2025) show trait loneliness moderates responses to AI listening quality, indicating individual differences in dispositions can govern the effect of conversational interventions on bonding-relevant outcomes.
- Takeaway: moderation by prior closeness and culture is plausible and likely; however, explicit empirical moderation results are heterogeneous and often missing or inconclusive. Future studies should directly test these moderators with adequately powered designs across diverse populations.

3) Mechanisms linking prompts to bonding and depth

3.1 Core mechanisms
- Self-disclosure and mutual contribution drive closeness: Across intimate conversation paradigms (fast-friends family of work), higher-quality self-disclosure and reciprocal contribution predict bonding (Aron et al.; Leckfor; Fini et al.; Welker et al.). IOS/self-other overlap measures track closeness changes after conversations and mediate bonding outcomes.
- Alignment and shared reality: Inter-self alignment (self-views converging during conversation) predicts higher enjoyment and willingness to engage again; this mechanism provides a plausible pathway for prompts that encourage perspective-taking and mutual reflection to foster real bonding (Welker et al., 2024).
- Conversation structure and cues: The presence or absence of nonverbal cues, and the structure of turn-taking, pauses, and topic flow, modulate perceived closeness and bonding; laboratory and hyperscanning studies show that different dyad types (friends vs strangers) require different conversational dynamics for bonding to occur (Templeton et al., 2021; Speer et al., 2024; Planalp & Benson, 1992; Tsang et al., 2024).

3.2 Moderation-relevant mechanisms to test in design
- Baseline closeness and trust networks: closeness acts as a moderator in several bonding pathways; early depth may be crucial for long-term bonding for less-close dyads, whereas established close dyads may benefit more from novel topic exploration or mutual reflection.
- Cultural norms and conversational constraints: cross-cultural norms shape which prompts are most effective (clarity vs. non-imposition, direct vs. indirect, preference for depth vs. breadth). Tests should incorporate culture-informed prompts and measure culture as a moderator.
- Individual differences (attachment, loneliness, trust in AI): as shown in Fang et al. (2025) and Welker et al. (2024), trait-level factors can influence how prompts affect bonding outcomes; personalization via user profiling could improve effectiveness.

4) Moderation by culture and closeness: synthesis and implications for design

4.1 Cultural background
- Evidence suggests that cultural background moderates how people perceive and engage in conversation: cross-cultural differences in conversational constraints (Kim, 2006; Murata, 1991) imply that prompts must be culturally tuned to maximize bonding and depth. The World Happiness Report emphasizes cross-cultural heterogeneity in social connection and well-being dynamics, reinforcing the need for culture-aware prompt design (Kim, 2006; Murata, 1991; World Happiness Report 2025).
- Design implication: build culturally adaptable prompt sets, offer locale-specific prompts, and test culture-by-prompt interactions in diverse samples.

4.2 Prior relationship closeness
- Evidence from long-run bonding trajectories highlights the importance of baseline closeness (Ledbetter et al., 2007) and proximity-based bonding (Rohrer et al., 2021). Hyperscanning work shows that friendship shape the dynamics of convergence/divergence in real-time conversation, suggesting that prior closeness interacts with the optimal conversational mode (friend vs. stranger) to optimize bonding (Speer et al., 2024). Fang et al. (2025) show that trait loneliness modulates the bonding benefits of AI listening, reinforcing the idea that individual relationship context moderates outcomes.
- Design implication: test interaction effects between intervention prompts and baseline closeness; tailor prompt intensity and partner matching by known closeness levels; consider matching vs random pairing depending on the contextual goals (bonding depth, breadth, or introduction to new ties).

5) Practical design recommendations for researchers and practitioners

5.1 Core design principles
- Favor real-time interactive dialogue: prompts that structure live conversation and foreground reciprocal contribution consistently outperform solo or non-interactive prompts, even when topic content is varied (Fini et al., 2025; Leckfor, 2020).
- Emphasize self-disclosure and mutual reflection: prompts that elicit personal sharing and mutual responsiveness align with bonding mechanisms and produce stronger closeness signals (Aron et al., 1997; Welker et al., 2024).
- Facilitate alignment dynamics: design prompts that encourage perspective-taking and mutual self-views alignment, enabling post-interaction closeness signals (IOS) and willingness to reconnect (Welker et al., 2024).
- Use adaptive matching and cultural tailoring: prompts should be configurable by relationship type (stranger, acquaintance, friend) and culture; local norms govern conversation style and depth, with cross-cultural training or adaptation improving outcomes (Kim, 2006; Murata, 1991).

5.2 Moderation-aware evaluation plans
- Include explicit Moderator variables:
  - Baseline relationship closeness (e.g., initial IOS or closeness scales)
  - Cultural background/orientation (self-identification, language/country)
  - Personality dispositions (attachment style, social anxiety)
- Design strategies:
  - Factorial experiments crossing prompt type with relationship type and culture
  - Longitudinal follow-ups (weeks to months) to test durability of bonding, not just initial closeness
  - Multimodal outcomes: self-report closeness, IOS, perceived contribution, depth of conversations, and objective proxies (turn-taking quality, reciprocity, topic diversity)
- Analytical plan:
  - Mixed-effects models with dyad- and person-level random effects
  - Moderated mediation to test whether alignment, disclosure, or perceived contribution mediate bonding differently across closeness and culture
  - Pre-registration and public sharing of data/code to support replication (cited methodology in multiple sources)
- Safety and ethics:
  - Ensure opt-in, privacy controls, and content safeguards, particularly for prompts that elicit self-disclosure or cross-cultural discussions. Build in safety rails and crisis resources for sensitive disclosures.

5.3 Research agenda and concrete experiments to run next
- Experiment A: Prompted conversation with friend vs stranger
  - Design: RCT with three arms: (a) real-time prompts with a friend, (b) real-time prompts with a stranger, (c) control prompts (neutral or no prompts)
  - Measures: bonding depth (IOS, self-disclosure depth, perceived support), offline follow-up (frequency and depth of meetings)
  - Moderators: baseline closeness, culture
- Experiment B: Culture-tailored prompts
  - Design: cross-cultural (e.g., US, East Asia, Europe) with prompts tailored to cultural norms (direct vs indirect communication, extent of self-disclosure)
  - Measures: bonding depth, conversation quality, cultural adaptation efficacy
- Experiment C: Longitudinal real-world bonding with near-real prompts
  - Design: 4–8 week trial with periodic prompts encouraging real-world meetings or calls; measure changes in close relationships and perceived depth over time
  - Moderators: prior closeness, culture, personality
- Experiment D: Multimodal prompts and matching
  - Design: combine prompts with near-real matching (based on similarity of values/disclosures) and with different modalities (text, voice, video) to test modality effects on depth and bonding across cultures

6) Limitations and caveats
- Causal inferences require carefully designed randomized experiments across diverse populations; many existing studies test related but non-identical constructs (e.g., closeness induction, self-disclosure prompts, or proximity-based bonding).
- Moderation by culture and prior closeness is plausible but not consistently demonstrated; robust cross-cultural replication is needed.
- Real-world bonding depth is difficult to capture; rely on multi-method measures (self-report, behavioral data, naturalistic follow-ups) to triangulate effects.

7) Summary conclusions for practitioners
- Connection-creator prompts that promote interactive, reciprocal, and reflective conversations hold promise for enhancing real-world bonding and conversation depth, especially when designed with awareness of relationship context and culture.
- Explicitly measure moderation by closeness and culture to identify for whom prompts work best and under which cultural conditions.
- Pair prompts with adaptive matching and multimodal conversation modalities to maximize bonding, while maintaining a strong emphasis on genuine contributor reciprocity.
- Invest in rigorous experimental designs with pre-registered plans and open data to build a robust evidence base for these features in real-world settings.
