 Hammer vs. Lover AI Companions in a Long-Term Randomized Trial: Evidence-Informed Design and Plan

Executive summary
- Research aim: Directly compare two AI companion persona designs in a randomized trial with long-term follow-up:
  - Hammer-style: neutral, boundary-setting design intended to protect autonomy and encourage durable human ties.
  - Lover-style: highly responsive, flattering design intended to maximize perceived warmth and social bonding.
- What existing evidence implies:
  - AI companions can reduce loneliness in short- to medium-term windows, with effects sometimes comparable to human interaction and mediated by perceived being heard (the importance of felt listening) and conversational quality (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025].
  - A robust set of studies shows that long-term durability of human ties from AI companionship remains unclear; effects on loneliness do not automatically translate into improved relationship quality or willingness to seek human contact unless mechanisms that promote real-world engagement are activated (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Zhang et al., 2025) [Zhang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025].
  - Evidence on explicit two-arm persona design comparisons (Hammer vs. Lover) with long-term follow-up is not yet published in the primary sources provided; existing work largely tests broader design features (empathy, boundary-setting, memory, social presence) or tests a 2×2 style of message content rather than a pure Hammer-Lover manipulation (Merrill et al., 2025; Noever & Rosario, 2024; Guingrich & Graziano, 2025) [Merrill et al., 2025; Noever & Rosario, 2024; Guingrich & Graziano, 2025].
- Actionable inference for the author: Build a rigorously pre-registered, long-term RCT with explicit Hammer vs Lover arms, standardize exposure, and measure loneliness, relationship quality, and willingness to seek human connection across multiple long-run follow-ups. Include mediation by perceived listening/being heard and moderators such as prior AI exposure and baseline loneliness.

1) Research question and rationale
- Core question: In a randomized trial with long-term follow-up, how do AI companions with highly responsive, flattering personas (Lover) compare to neutral, boundary-setting designs (Hammer) in affecting:
  - Primary outcome: loneliness
  - Secondary outcomes: relationship quality with others, and willingness to seek human connection
- Rationale: The Hammer vs Lover principle targets durable human ties versus parasocial comfort. The literature shows that:
  - Perceived listening or “feeling heard” is a key mediator of loneliness reductions in AI interactions (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Merrill et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025].
  - AI loneliness relief is not automatically translated into improved real-world relationships or human social engagement without mechanisms activating real-world ties (Zhang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024) [Zhang et al., 2025].
  - There is no published direct randomized head-to-head Hammer-vs-Lover persona comparison with long-term outcomes in the sources provided; thus, an explicit trial is needed to answer the core question.

2) What the evidence base suggests about mechanisms and design levers
- Key mediators
  - Feeling heard (perceived listening) is a stronger mediator of loneliness reductions than raw chatbot performance alone; the combination of high listening quality and responsiveness explains substantial portions of loneliness reductions (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Merrill et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Merrill et al., 2025].
- Design features linked to outcomes
  - Empathy and perceived social presence tend to reduce loneliness more when the AI sincerely attunes to user emotions (Merrill et al., 2025; Noever & Rosario, 2024) [Merrill et al., 2025; Noever & Rosario, 2024].
  - Boundary-setting principles (clear limits on disclosure, explicit prompts to engage real-world social activity) may help mitigate parasocial risks and support durable human ties, though direct long-term evidence specific to Hammer is lacking in the supplied sources (Noever & Rosario, 2024; Wu et al., various reviews) [Noever & Rosario, 2024].
- Evidence gaps
  - No published head-to-head RCT with long-term follow-up comparing Hammer vs Lover persona designs in the datasets provided.
  - Relationship quality and willingness to seek human connection are not consistently measured as primary endpoints in the AI-companion RCT literature to date; many studies focus on loneliness and mediators (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Zhang et al., 2025) [De Freitas et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025].
- Practical implication for trial design
  - An RCT should include longitudinal follow-ups (6–12–24 months), direct measures of relationship quality, and explicit willingness-to-seek-human-connection metrics, with a clear Hammer vs Lover manipulation and robust boundary-setting safeguards.

3) Proposed randomized trial design (Hammer vs Lover, long-term follow-up)
- Design type: A multi-arm randomized controlled trial with two primary arms testing persona design and an optional third arm as a neutral control (3-arm design).
- Arms
  - Arm A — Lover: Highly responsive, flattering persona designed to maximize warmth, affiliative language, and perceived empathy; includes dynamic memory features (to reinforce bonding) but with explicit boundary-setting cues only when necessary to prevent harmful disclosure.
  - Arm B — Hammer: Neutral/boundary-setting persona with conservative warmth, explicit boundaries for self-disclosure, and structured prompts to encourage real-world engagement; memory limited or governed by strict privacy/safety rules to avoid parasocial overreach.
  - Arm C — Control/Neutral AI: A baseline AI with standard interaction quality, minimal personalization, no explicit flattering or boundary-setting manipulations.
- Population and recruitment
  - Adults aged 18+ with elevated loneliness or social isolation risk; diverse demographic representation; recruitment across multiple sites (universities, communities) to ensure external validity.
- Intervention exposure
  - A fixed exposure protocol across arms (e.g., 12–16 weeks of daily interactions of 10–15 minutes, with standardized weekly prompts) to isolate persona effects.
- Primary outcomes
  - Loneliness: validated scales (preferably UCLA Loneliness Scale long form or Revised UCLA LS; consider 11-item short form for efficiency) administered baseline, immediate post-intervention, 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months.
  - Relationship quality: validated multi-domain scales (e.g., Quality of Relationships Inventory, Dyadic Adjustment Scale, or similar) administered on the same schedule.
  - Willingness to seek human connection: validated scales for social connectedness, social support seeking, and concrete behavioral indicators (e.g., self-reported attempts to re-engage friends/family; actual social activities when feasible).
- Mediators and moderators
  - Mediators: perceived listening/heardness, perceived empathy, perceived social presence, and memory usage/privacy satisfaction.
  - Moderators: baseline loneliness, attachment style (e.g., secure vs insecure), prior AI exposure, gender, culture, and offline social support.
- Measures
  - Use a mix of validated self-report scales and objective/logged interaction metrics (frequency, duration, topics) to triangulate outcomes.
  - Include qualitative components (semi-structured interviews or diaries) to contextualize quantitative results and capture boundary experiences and social-context dynamics.
- Sample size and power
  - Conduct an a priori power analysis to detect small-to-moderate effects on loneliness and relationship quality across time, accounting for attrition across long-term follow-ups. Plan for stratified randomization by key moderators (e.g., baseline loneliness, offline social support).
- Randomization and blinding
  - Randomization at the participant level with stratification on baseline loneliness and offline social support. Blinding of participants is not feasible given persona exposure; outcome assessors should be blind to arm allocation when possible.
- Statistical analysis plan
  - Primary analysis: linear mixed-effects models (or generalized linear mixed models for non-normal outcomes) with time, arm, and time×arm interaction terms to evaluate trajectory differences.
  - Mediation: longitudinal mediation analyses to test whether perceived listening and boundary-setting manipulations mediate loneliness and relationship-quality trajectories.
  - Moderation: test whether the arm effects differ by baseline loneliness, attachment style, gender, culture, or offline social support.
  - Robustness: sensitivity analyses for missing data (multiple imputation, full-information maximum likelihood), alternative loneliness measures, and per-protocol analyses.
- Ethical considerations and governance
  - Pre-registered protocol; independent ethics review; ongoing data governance; explicit consent forms detailing AI capabilities, boundaries, and the AI’s non-human status.
  - Safety protocols: monitoring for distress, potential dependence, or boundary issues; provisions to pause participation and escalate to human support if needed.
  - Privacy: data minimization, secure storage, and transparent data-use policies; strict controls around memory retention and user disclosures.

4) Rationale and mapping to the literature
- Mechanistic rationale
  - The key mechanism across AI-companion loneliness literature is perceived listening/being heard; a Lover-like, highly responsive persona may maximize immediate perceived listening, while Hammer-style boundary-setting aims to sustain real-world social engagement and prevent parasocial overdependence (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Merrill et al., 2025).
- Durable human ties and parasocial risk
  - The literature on parasocial AI and “digital loneliness” emphasizes that AI can relieve loneliness in the short term but may either (a) substitute for or undermine durable human ties, or (b) support social engagement if designed with boundary cues and human-connection prompts (The Dark Side of AI Companionship; Zhang et al., 2025; Jacobs, 2024; Shank et al., 2025) [Zhang et al., 2025; Jacobs, 2024; Shank et al., 2025].
- Design and governance considerations
  - The ethical governance literature emphasizes responsible AI design to avoid manipulation, protect privacy, ensure safety, and maintain user autonomy; this supports embedding boundary-setting features and transparent consent in the Hammer design while ensuring the Lover design remains transparent about AI capabilities (Fjeld et al., 2020; DARPA ITM; DoD AI Principles; Samuel et al., 2021) [Fjeld et al., 2020; DARPA; DoD; Samuel et al., 2021].
- Measurement anchors
  - UCLA Loneliness Scale and its variants (Version 3, Revised) are recommended for longitudinal loneliness measurement due to robust psychometrics and cross-cultural validity, with considerations for subdimensions such as isolation and connectedness (Russell 1996; Russell et al. 1980; de Jong Gierveld scales as alternatives) [Russell 1996; Russell et al., 1980; de Jong Gierveld & Kamphuis 1985; de Jong Gierveld & Van Tilburg 2006]).
- Mediator and moderator targets
  - Perceived listening/heardness (empathy/relational alignment) as a mediator; social presence as a moderator in some pathways (Merrill et al., 2025) [Merrill et al., 2025].
  - Attachment style, baseline loneliness, and offline social support as moderators (Lai et al., 2025; Guingrich & Graziano, 2025) [Lai et al., 2025; Guingrich & Graziano, 2025].

5) Practical insights for the author (actionable steps)
- Start from an explicit Hammer vs Lover manipulation
  - Operationalize Hammer as a boundary-setting persona with explicit prompts to encourage real-world social engagement and privacy-preserving features; operationalize Lover as a highly responsive, flattering persona with strong warmth cues and memory-based personalization, while including safety rails and disclosure of AI limitations.
- Include long-term follow-up
  - Plan multiple follow-up waves at 6, 12, 18, and 24 months to assess long-term durability of loneliness reductions, relationship quality, and willingness to seek human connection. This aligns with the observed short-term durability gaps in AI-companion literature (De Freitas et al., 2024; 2025; Zhang et al., 2025).
- Outcome measures
  - Primary: Loneliness (UCLA LS or Revised LS-R), future willingness to seek human connection, and relationship quality with significant others.
  - Secondary: Perceived listening/heardness, perceived empathy, boundary clarity, memory/privacy satisfaction, AI dependency, and social presence indicators.
- Mediators and moderators
  - Test perceived listening as a primary mediator; test social presence as a moderator; test boundary-signal strength as another moderator; test attachment style and baseline loneliness as potential moderators.
- Safety and ethics
  - Implement robust privacy protections, consent processes, debriefing strategies, and safety monitoring for adverse psychosocial effects; ensure alignment with DoD/DARPA-style governance principles and wider AI ethics guidelines.
- Data governance and plan for dissemination
  - Pre-register the trial; publish protocol and statistical analysis plan; share data and code where possible; ensure transparent reporting of results to enable meta-analytic synthesis.
- Practical design considerations
  - Use a standardized, platform-agnostic interaction framework to ensure consistent exposure across arms; consider both text-based and voice-enabled modalities; incorporate memory management with privacy safeguards to test memory-based personalization effects without compromising privacy.
- Resource and feasibility planning
  - Ensure sufficient sample size to detect small-to-moderate effects on loneliness and relationship quality across multiple follow-ups; plan for retention strategies given long-term follow-up.

6) Potential limitations and mitigation strategies
- Potential biases
  - Self-selection bias, attrition bias in long-term follow-ups; mitigate with stratified randomization, engagement strategies, and retention incentives.
- Generalizability
  - Ensure diverse samples across cultures, ages, and contexts; test cross-cultural validity of the Hammer vs Lover manipulation.
- Measurement challenges
  - Loneliness is multifaceted; triangulate with subscales (e.g., emotional loneliness vs social loneliness) and complementary measures of social connectivity.
- Ethical concerns
  - Guard against parasocial dependence; ensure transparent disclosures of AI capabilities; incorporate boundary policies and crisis response protocols.
- Platform dynamics
  - Variability in underlying AI platforms (multimodal capabilities, memory, and personalization) could confound arms; standardize exposure as much as possible and document platform-specific differences.

Closing remarks
- The Hammer vs Lover design principle is compelling for theoretical and ethical reasons, but the empirical ground for long-term, head-to-head randomized trials remains underdeveloped in the datasets provided. The most credible path forward is a rigorously pre-registered, long-term randomized trial with a direct Hammer vs Lover manipulation, standardized exposure, and multi-wave outcomes that include loneliness, relationship quality, and willingness to seek human connection, along with mediation by perceived listening and boundary-setting, moderated by individual differences and offline social support. This design would address the central question with robust causal evidence and inform ethically sound AI companion development that genuinely supports durable human ties.
