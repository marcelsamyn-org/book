Do Social Step-Back Nudges to Engage Real People Increase Actual Social Interactions and Perceived Relationship Quality Over 1–3 Months? A Comprehensive Evidence Synthesis

Executive summary
- The direct evidence that Social Step-Back nudges reliably increase real-world social interactions and perceived relationship quality over 1–3 months is limited. Most available data come from distinct literatures with different targets (clinical aphasia groups, college peer introductions, online social interactions, and lab-based nudging interventions), and only a subset explicitly tests nudges aimed at increasing real-life conversations.
- Plausible mechanisms exist: nudges can alter the decision environment to promote initiating conversations, and initiating positive social behaviors (e.g., gratitude, laughter) appears linked to affiliative interest and subsequent relationship development in naturalistic settings. These mechanisms are supported by multiple sources, though not all are causal nudging experiments targeting real-life interactions over 1–3 months (Jolink & Algoe, 2024; Han, 2023; Canevello & Crocker, 2010; Merolla et al., 2023/2025).
- Across diverse contexts, short-term improvements in engagement or self-reported capabilities are more consistently observed than robust, sustained increases in actual social interactions or long-run relationship quality within a 1–3 month window (Hoover et al., 2025; Genç & Coşkun, 2024; Jolink & Algoe, 2024; McLeod, 2024 EMA framework).
- Actionable guidance: to rigorously test Social Step-Back nudges for real-human engagement, design studies that (a) measure actual social initiations and conversation counts, (b) capture perceived relationship quality, (c) use EMA/diaries for real-time data, (d) pre-register analyses, (e) account for relational history/moderators, and (f) incorporate mediators such as affiliative behaviors and affiliating interest.

Integrated evidence base (key findings and contexts)

1) Social Step-Back nudges in a clinical/therapy-group setting (aphasia)
- Source: Hoover et al., 2025 (supplemental material for AJSLP multicenter trial)
- Population: adults with chronic aphasia in conversation-group treatments (Dyad vs Large Group vs Control)
- Primary outcomes: language/communication measures and some social-relationship–relevant metrics (perceived social support, CCRSA, etc.)
- Quantitative findings relevant to social interactions/relationship quality:
  - MOS Social Support Scale (perceived social support): Intercept 73.48 (SE 4.04), no significant effects of condition (Dyad p=.49; Large Group p=.46) or time (Posttreatment p=.59; Maintenance p=.24); interactions with time no significant (p-values > .05 for Dyad × Posttreatment, Group × Posttreatment, Dyad × Maintenance, Group × Maintenance). Interpretation: no reliable group- or time-related increases in perceived social support within 1–3 months in this population (Hoover et al., 2025).
  - CCRSA (Communication Confidence Rating Scale for Aphasia): Maintenance associated with a statistically significant increase (p=.04). Large Group vs Control at baseline showed a significant negative effect (p=.02). Interpretation: some maintenance-phase improvement in communication confidence, with a notable between-group difference for Large Group, but overall effects not uniformly robust across outcomes (Hoover et al., 2025).
  - Aphasia Communication Outcome Measure: Group × Posttreatment nears significance (p=.06) suggesting a potential posttreatment benefit for Large Group vs Control that did not reach conventional alpha; Time-Maintenance nears significance (p=.07). Interpretation: hints of transient posttreatment benefit in some groups, not consistent across measures (Hoover et al., 2025).
  - Other measures (Communication Activities of Daily Living-3; Living with Aphasia-Wall): Largely null group effects; site differences (Temple, Adler) influence some measures, indicating contextual site variation rather than pure nudge effects (Hoover et al., 2025).
- Implication for the research question: In this chronic aphasia sample, Social Step-Back–style nudges delivered through structured group formats did not produce clear, robust increases in perceived social support or other relationship-quality metrics within 1–3 months. Some hints of improved self-perceived communication confidence at maintenance exist, but the social-relational gains are not consistently demonstrated across measures (Hoover et al., 2025).

2) EMA-based evaluation concepts for testing social nudges (conceptual framework)
- Source: McLeod, 2024 (Ecological Momentary Assessment, EMA)
- Core ideas: EMA allows real-time, repeated assessments in natural environments to track actual social interactions and perceived relationship quality. Designs include time-based, event-based, and hybrid sampling; data are nested (observations within persons) and analyzed with multilevel models.
- Relevance to nudges: EMA provides the measurement framework to quantify actual social interactions (frequency, duration, initiator), perceived relationship quality, and potential mediators (mood, motivation) in response to nudges delivered in real-life settings.
- Actionable design guidance: Use EMA to capture (a) real interactions initiated after nudges, (b) perceived closeness/satisfaction post-interaction, (c) contextual moderators (location, company), (d) sensor data where feasible to corroborate self-reports.
- Limitations: EMA studies require careful planning to manage reactivity, burden, missing data, and ethical considerations; they are primarily a measurement framework rather than a causal intervention by themselves (McLeod, 2024).

3) Behavior-to-relationship development pathways in initial interactions (precursors)
- Jolink & Algoe (2023/2024) – What Happens in Initial Interactions Forecasts Relationship Development
- Design: Observational longitudinal data from first-year undergraduates tracking initial interactions and subsequent affiliative interest, with 143 baseline and 129 follow-up participants; about 591 interactions tracked over one semester (1–4 months)
- Exemplar behaviors and findings:
  - Affectionate touch, shared laughter, and partner’s gratitude expression were each associated with immediate affiliating interest beyond warmth, competence, and attractiveness (p-values not all provided here but described as significant) (Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
  - Relationship development over a semester was predicted by post-interaction affiliative interest, suggesting a mediated pathway: initial behaviors boost affiliative interest, which in turn forecasts relationship growth (Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
- Implication for nudges: Nudges that prompt initiating conversations or eliciting real feedback, and that encourage affiliative behaviors (gratitude, humor, warmth), may plausibly accelerate relationship formation over 1–3 months, though this literature does not demonstrate causal nudges and is observational (Jolink & Algoe, 2024).

4) Media of social interactions and perceived support in online contexts (private vs. public)
- Han (2023) – WeChat study on social network interaction size, frequency, and perceived social support
- Key findings:
  - Increased interaction size relates to greater perceived social support via a chain that includes personal topic involvement; interaction frequency contributes to perceived social support through its effect on personal topic involvement; social network evaluation directly mediates the size–support relation and also acts via personal topic involvement (Han, 2023).
- Implication for nudges: Nudges that increase the scale of interactions but also encourage private, personally meaningful topics may yield stronger perceived social support. Purely increasing contact frequency without fostering private-topic engagement may have weaker effects on perceived support (Han, 2023).
- Caveat: Cross-sectional observational design; not a causal nudging study; generalizes to online social networks (WeChat) rather than in-person interactions (Han, 2023).

5) Interpersonal goals and responsive dynamics over weeks to months
- Canevello & Crocker (2010) – Creating Good Relationships: Responsiveness, Relationship Quality, and Interpersonal Goals
- Key patterns:
  - Compassionate goals (desire to support others) predict increased responsiveness; responsiveness enhances perceived mutuality and trust and can raise relationship quality over time.
  - Self-image goals (desire to maintain a particular self-image) predict lower responsiveness and can lower relationship quality.
  - In two dyadic studies (semester-long and 3-week), these goal orientations shaped responsiveness cycles that, over time, related to changes in relationship quality and goals.
- Implications for nudges: Nudges that cultivate compassionate goals or reduce self-protective self-image concerns may enhance responsive behaviors and, over time, relationship quality. This suggests motivational components to nudges could be critical mediators (Canevello & Crocker, 2010).

6) Related brief evidence on social interactions and relationship quality dynamics
- Merolla, Otmar, & Salehuddin (2025; Experience Sampling; two samples)
- Findings:
  - Negative relational histories predict lower perceived partner responsiveness, higher stress, and lower well-being during daily interactions (across two samples, N=120 and N=220).
  - Stress and perceived partner liking mediate the link between negative relations and perceived responsiveness; positive moments in interactions can yield larger well-being gains and reductions in stress for those with negative histories.
- Implication for nudges: Nudges may need to be tailored to users’ relational histories; for individuals with negative relational experiences, nudges should consider emotional context and stress pathways and might require supportive framing to avoid unintended worsening of stress or suspicion. This evidence informs moderation rather than direct causation (Merolla et al., 2025).

Synthesis: what this all means for the research question

- Signal and plausibility
  - There is a plausible causal path from nudges that promote initiating conversations or seeking real feedback to increased real interactions and improved perceived relationship quality, but direct causal evidence, particularly over 1–3 months, is sparse in the sources at hand.
  - Mechanistic threads across studies suggest that:
    - Initiating conversation and affiliative behaviors (gratitude, laughter) promote affiliative interest and can forecast relationship growth (Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
    - Nudges that facilitate real-life engagement, with attention to privacy and autonomy, could lead to more robust social ties if they succeed in increasing meaningful, private exchanges rather than superficial contact (Genç & Coşkun, 2024).
    - The quality of social ties and perceived support may hinge on factors beyond contact frequency alone, including the private relevance of topics discussed and perceived trust/quality of network ties (Han, 2023; Canevello & Crocker, 2010).

- Evidence gaps
  - Direct, causal, long-horizon evidence on Social Step-Back nudges that explicitly aim to cause real-world conversations and feedback, and that measure subsequent 1–3 month outcomes in diverse populations, is limited.
  - The strongest direct data for long-horizon social outcomes with nudges come from lab-style or context-specific interventions (e.g., Boost in lab sessions) with limited external validity and lacking long-term follow-up (Genç & Coşkun, 2024).

- Practical takeaway for researchers and authors
  - While direct evidence for 1–3 month real-world social interaction increases via Social Step-Back nudges is not robustly established, there is converging evidence that (a) initiating, private, affiliative social behaviors are linked to relationship development, and (b) real-time measurement with EMA is essential to capture dynamic social processes.
  - A carefully designed study that combines a causal nudge manipulation with EMA-based outcome assessment across 8–12 weeks is well-positioned to close the evidence gap.

Actionable insights and design recommendations for studying Social Step-Back nudges (1–3 months)

- Study design
  - Use a randomized controlled trial (RCT) or a factorial design to test specific Social Step-Back nudges that explicitly encourage real conversations or real feedback, versus control.
  - Embed an EMA/diary component to capture real-world interactions, using time-based prompts (e.g., 3–5 prompts/day) and event-based prompts around social events/interactions, over 8–12 weeks (McLeod, 2024).
  - Include both objective and subjective outcomes:
    - Objective: counts of initiated conversations, real-world social contacts, duration of conversations where feasible; possibly app-logged interactions if privacy-preserving.
    - Subjective: perceived relationship quality (closeness, satisfaction), perceived social support, relationship intentions.
  - Moderator and mediator considerations:
    - Moderators: baseline relational history, personality/affect regulation, privacy preferences, type of relationship (friends, family, colleagues).
    - Mediators: affiliative behaviors (gratitude expressions, shared laughter, affectionate gestures), affiliating interest, mood, stress.
- Measures and instrumentation
  - Use validated scales for perceived social support (e.g., MOS Social Support), communication confidence, and relationship quality, complemented by EMA-adapted items for momentary relationship quality.
  - Include behavioral proxies for affiliative behaviors (self-report, or coder-rated interactions from session diaries if feasible).
  - Consider sensor data (where consent and privacy allow) to augment self-reports (e.g., proximity to others, conversation bouts) without overburdening participants.
- Analysis plan
  - Multilevel modeling to separate within-person (time-varying) effects from between-person differences; model the temporal ordering (nudges -> subsequent interactions -> subsequent relationship-quality outcomes).
  - Mediation analyses to test whether increases in initiations or affiliative behaviors mediate the effect of nudges on relationship outcomes.
  - Pre-registration of hypotheses, outcomes, timepoints, and analysis plan; specify handling of missing EMA data (e.g., maximum likelihood with missing-at-random assumptions, multiple imputation as appropriate).
- Practical considerations
  - Minimize participant burden: optimize prompt timing, use user-friendly interfaces, allow opt-out controls, and provide clear consent and privacy protections.
  - Address ethical concerns: ensure data privacy, transparent data use, and robust security; provide participants with control over nudges and the ability to pause or adjust intensity.
  - Pilot testing: run a small-scale pilot to calibrate prompt frequency, nudge content, and diary items; refine based on participant feedback prior to full deployment.
- Reporting
  - Report standardized effect sizes for primary outcomes, confidence intervals, and preregistered vs. exploratory analyses.
  - Include analyses of potential moderators (e.g., relational history) to understand differential effectiveness.
  - Provide a transparent discussion of limitations, including ecological validity, generalizability, and potential reactivity.

Concrete, study-level synthesis-ready bullet points

- If aiming to increase actual social interactions and perceived relationship quality over 1–3 months via Social Step-Back nudges, prioritize:
  - Nudges that drive initiation of real conversations and that prompt seeking genuine feedback from real others.
  - Interventions that explicitly promote affiliative behaviors (gratitude expression, shared humor, warmth) as part of real interactions.
  - EMA-enabled measurement of both the quantity/quality of real interactions and perceived relationship quality across 8–12 weeks.
- Expect heterogeneous effects across populations:
  - Individuals with a history of negative relational experiences may require tailored support to prevent stress or perception of lower responsiveness; moderation analyses are essential (Merolla et al., 2025; Canevello & Crocker, 2010).
- Documentation and replication:
  - Pre-register designs and analyses; replicate in diverse populations and settings to establish generalizability beyond college students or clinical populations.

Quantitative findings, inline citations, and summaries (selected highlights)

- Hoover et al. (2025): No significant effects of group condition or time on MOS Social Support within 1–3 months; maintenance showed a CCRSA improvement (p=.04) with a notable between-group effect for Large Group at baseline (p=.02). These results suggest limited evidence for immediate social-support gains from the nudging variants studied in chronic aphasia groups (Hoover et al., 2025).
- Jolink & Algoe (2024): Among first-year undergraduates, affectionate touch, shared laughter, and partner gratitude were each linked to immediate affiliating interest; affiliating interest after interactions predicted longer-term relationship development over a semester (1–4 months). Relationship-growing effects were mediated by affiliative interest, not merely by initial warmth or attraction (Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
- Genç & Coşkun (2024): Lab-like study of a conversation-support nudging tool (Boost) showed increased conversational engagement and reduced silences during sessions, but no long-term (1–3 months) outcome data on real-world interactions or sustained relationship quality; participants raised privacy/autonomy concerns and highlighted the need for context-aware, user-controlled nudges (Genç & Coşkun, 2024).
- McLeod (2024): EMA as a measurement framework; dense sampling over weeks to months is feasible; recommended designs include time-based and event-based prompts to capture actual interactions and perceived relationship quality in real-world settings; multilevel analyses are required for nested data (McLeod, 2024).
- Han (2023): WeChat-based study showing that larger interaction networks boost perceived social support via personal topic engagement; frequency and network evaluation contribute via mediated paths; emphasizes the importance of meaningful topics and high-quality ties for social-support outcomes (Han, 2023).
- Canevello & Crocker (2010): Longitudinal evidence that compassionate goals promote responsiveness and relationship quality over weeks to months; self-image goals show adverse effects on responsiveness; suggests motivational levers to embed in nudges (Canevello & Crocker, 2010).
- Merolla, Otmar, & Salehuddin (2025): Experience-sampling data show that negative relational histories depress perceived responsiveness, increase stress, and lower well-being during daily interactions; stress and partner liking mediate perceived responsiveness; positive interaction moments can offset some adverse effects. Implications for tailoring nudges to relational history and affective context (Merolla et al., 2025).

Implications for practice and theory

- Theory-informed design: Nudges should not only prompt social initiation but also foster compassionate, affiliative goals and supportive conversational dynamics (Canevello & Crocker, 2010). They should emphasize private, meaningful exchanges over superficial interactions (Han, 2023; Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
- Measurement rigor: To answer the 1–3 month question, combine EMA-based real-time interaction data with longitudinal follow-ups on perceived relationship quality, using robust causal-designs (RCTs) and mediation analyses (McLeod, 2024; Jolink & Algoe, 2024).
- Context sensitivity: Site/contextual factors and individual relational histories matter. Nudges may need customization or stratified analysis to identify who benefits most (Merolla et al., 2025; Hoover et al., 2025; Genç & Coşkun, 2024).

Limitations of this synthesis
- Heterogeneous populations and contexts across sources; few studies explicitly test Social Step-Back nudges with long-term (1–3 month) outcomes in real-world settings.
- Where nudges have been tested, effects on actual social interactions and long-term relationship quality are often indirect, small, or confined to specific populations (e.g., aphasia groups, students in lab settings).
- The strongest causal evidence for nudges affecting social outcomes in naturalistic settings remains sparse; most studies provide mechanistic or observational support rather than definitive causal effects.

Conclusions
- There is credible support for the plausibility that nudges promoting real-person engagement could influence short- to medium-term social interactions and relationship development, particularly when nudges target initiating conversations and key affiliative behaviors. However, robust, causal evidence showing sustained increases in actual social interactions and perceived relationship quality over 1–3 months, across diverse populations, is not yet established in the accessible literature.
- For researchers and authors aiming to test Social Step-Back nudges with a 1–3 month horizon, a rigorously designed EMA-enabled experimental program is recommended. Such a program should directly quantify real interactions (frequency, duration, initiator), perceived relationship quality, and potential mediators/moderators over 8–12 weeks, with appropriate controls and preregistered analyses.

Actionable next steps for the author (concrete)

- Design a longitudinal RCT (8–12 weeks) of a Social Step-Back nudging intervention that:
  - Delivers explicit prompts to initiate conversations or seek real feedback with identified real people.
  - Commits to real-life interaction targets (e.g., weekly initiated conversations with a named contact; at least one feedback-seeking interaction).
  - Measures outcomes with EMA: actual interaction counts, initiator vs. recipient roles, duration estimates, and context; plus real-time relationship-quality metrics (closeness, satisfaction, perceived support).
  - Includes mediators such as affiliative behaviors (gratitude, laughter) and affiliating interest, and moderators such as prior relational history.
- Use a hybrid sampling design (time-based EMA prompts plus event-based prompts after nudged interactions) to maximize data on whether nudges lead to real interactions and to capture the ensuing relationship dynamics.
- Pre-register Hypotheses and Analysis Plan:
  - Primary outcomes: change in real interaction frequency/duration; change in perceived relationship quality from baseline to 1–3 months.
  - Mediation: test whether increases in initiations and affiliative behaviors mediate effects on relationship quality.
  - Moderation: test whether prior relational history moderates the nudging effects.
- Ensure ethical safeguards:
  - Clear consent for data collection, privacy-preserving data handling, and opt-out options for participants.
  - Transparent disclosure of what nudges do and do not do in the study, with participants having control over the level of engagement.
- Pilot before scaling:
  - Run a small-scale pilot to tune nudge content, timing, and EMA prompt frequency to optimize compliance and minimize reactivity.
  - Use feedback loops to refine prompts based on participant experiences (privacy, autonomy, perceived usefulness).
