# Report: Do embodied AI companions produce different loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes than text-only AI? Do effects vary by gender or age group?

Executive summary
- Across multiple lines of evidence, embodiment (robotic or avatar-based presence) can influence loneliness, attachment-related responses, and social-cognition in human–AI interactions, but the direction and magnitude of effects depend on modality, population, and context. Direct head-to-head comparisons between embodied and text-only AI within the same trial are scarce, and results are heterogeneous.
- Key quantitative findings:
  - Meta-analytic evidence suggests that disembodied AI (text- or non-embodied AI) shows a positive association with loneliness in aggregate AI use (r ≈ 0.163), whereas physically embodied AI tends to show a non-significant trend toward reducing loneliness (r ≈ -0.266; p = 0.088) and disembodied AI shows a significant positive association with loneliness (r ≈ 0.352; p < 0.001) in older adults (Xu et al., 2025). Younger samples (≤35) show no significant association (r ≈ 0.039; p = 0.659) (Xu et al., 2025).
  - Embodied AI interventions such as robotic pets have been associated with loneliness reduction in some RCTs and quasi-experimental work, whereas conversational AI (chatbots) have shown mixed or smaller effects; effects vary by age and setting (Hansen et al., 2025; broad review of digital interventions for loneliness).
  - Within embodied interfaces, gender differences emerge in at least one randomized study of digital humans: women show greater increases in closeness, perceived social support, and caring when the avatar expresses emotion; men show similar benefits under neutral presentation conditions; neutral facial presence was more beneficial for women in reducing loneliness and stress, whereas men benefited more from a no-face, voice-only condition (Loveys et al., 2021).
  - Text-based AI interactions can increase perceived attachment and AI empathy over several weeks in longitudinal usage, with gender identity and prior AI usage moderating these effects; however, loneliness outcomes in these designs were not consistently assessed or were not reduced over time (Chandra et al., 2025).
  - Real-world deployment of embodied AI for older adults (e.g., ElliQ) demonstrates high adoption and user-reported companionship in beta programs, but rigorous randomized trials comparing embodied vs text-only AI and assessing loneliness/attachment outcomes are lacking in the available reports (Broadbent et al., 2024).
- Overall synthesis: Embodiment appears to be a relevant design feature that can modulate social outcomes, but robust, modality-specific conclusions require direct randomized comparisons, well-powered moderator analyses (gender and age), and consistent measurement of loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes over time (Malfacini et al.; Hansen et al.; Xu et al.; Loveys et al.; Chandra et al.). Evidence to date suggests:
  - Embodied AI may offer advantages for loneliness reduction in some older-adult contexts, particularly when social presence and interactive, affectiveexpressiveness are leveraged.
  - Text-only AI can foster perceived attachment and social-cognition benefits under certain conditions, with gender and prior AI experience shaping outcomes.
  - Gender and age can moderate effects in some embodied contexts; evidence for age- or gender-specific effects is inconsistent or limited across studies.
- Actionable takeaway: To advance the chapter, prioritize direct, well-powered randomized trials that contrast embodied AI companions with text-only AI control conditions, with explicit tests of loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes, and with pre-registered analyses for gender and age group moderators.

1) Introduction: what is being asked and why it matters
- Research question: Do embodied AI companions produce different loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes than text-only AI? Do these effects vary by gender or age group?
- Rationale: Embodiment is theorized to enhance social presence, relatedness, and engagement, which could alter loneliness and attachment-related processes. However, the current evidence base comprises a patchwork of study designs (RCTs, longitudinal studies, cross-sectional surveys, and qualitative work) with varying modalities (robotic/pet-like embodiments, avatarized digital humans, and text-based chatbots) and heterogeneous outcomes (loneliness, attachment proxies, social-cognition tasks). Understanding modality-specific effects and demographic moderators is essential for theory and for informing design decisions in elder care, mental health support, and education.

2) Evidence base and key quantitative findings (by modality and outcomes)
- Loneliness
  - Xu et al. (2025) meta-analysis across 47 studies (21 publications) finds an overall positive association between AI use and loneliness (r = 0.163, p < 0.05). Subgroup analyses show:
    - Physically embodied AI: r = -0.266, p = 0.088 (marginal; potential loneliness reduction but not statistically decisive).
    - Physically disembodied AI (text-based or non-embodied): r = 0.352, p < 0.001 (significant; higher loneliness with non-embodied AI).
    - Older adults (60+): r = 0.352, p < 0.001 (significant loneliness increase with AI use in older adults).
    - Younger individuals (≤ 35): r = 0.039, p = 0.659 (not significant).
  - Gender-based moderation: The abstract does not report gender-specific results; no evidence on gender differences is provided in Xu et al. (2025) abstract.
  - Attitudes toward AI: When attitudes toward AI are included, AI use–loneliness associations remain positive, suggesting robust association despite attitudes.
  - Implication: Embodiment and age are important moderators; embodiment may mitigate loneliness or even reduce it (though evidence is marginal) whereas text-only AI tends to be associated with higher loneliness, particularly in older adults. There is a lack of robust gender-specific effects in this meta-analysis.
- Attachment security and social-cognition
  - The Xu et al. (2025) meta-analysis reports loneliness as the primary social outcome; attachment security and social-cognition outcomes are not reported in the abstract, indicating a gap for those constructs in this synthesis.
  - Merrill et al. (2025) review of AI chatbots as social support emphasizes perceived social support and emotional validation as primary mechanisms; it also notes that humans, social robots, and AI were perceived similarly on social-support impressions in some studies, but it does not provide direct quantitative comparison for attachment security or social-cognition outcomes, nor modality-specific effects.
  - Loveys et al. (2021) randomized trial with a female digital human (embodied avatar) shows gender-specific moderation of loneliness and relatedness:
    - Women: emotional voice increases closeness, perceived social support, and caring perceptions; neutral face reduces loneliness and subjective stress relative to having no face.
    - Men: neutral voice increases closeness, social support, and caring perceptions; no-face (voice-only) reduces loneliness and stress relative to face-present conditions.
  - Chandra et al. (2025) longitudinal study of text-based AI usage shows large increases in perceived attachment to AI and AI empathy with active usage, moderated by gender identity and prior AI usage; loneliness outcomes were not reported as measures in their data snippet.
  - Broadbent et al. (2024) report real-world deployment of ElliQ (an embodied AI companion for older adults) with qualitative impressions of companionship but no RCT data or standardized loneliness/attachment outcomes in the presented materials.
  - Skjuve et al. (2021) qualitative study of human–chatbot relationships (text-based) documents stages of relationship formation, trust, disclosure, and perceived wellbeing benefits; no quantitative loneliness or attachment measures or gender/age moderation in that study; embodiment is discussed as a theoretical differentiator.
  - Overall: There is evidence that embodiment can shape attachment-like processes (e.g., closeness, perceived support) under gender-specific conditions, but robust cross-modal and cross-outcome causal comparisons remain sparse.
- Social cognition
  - Across these sources, social-cognition outcomes are variably measured. Some studies focus on perceived social support, trust, and attachment proxies; others assess cognitive aspects indirectly through engagement or emotion recognition in experiments with avatars or robotic agents. Rigorous, standardized social-cognition tasks across embodied vs text-only modalities are not consistently reported.
  - Notable patterns: Embodiment can increase perceived social presence and engagement, which may support social-cognitive processing in some contexts, but evidence for durable, modality-specific improvements in theory of mind, emotion recognition, or perspective-taking remains limited.

3) Moderators: gender and age
- Gender
  - Loveys et al. (2021) provide direct evidence of gender-specific moderation within an embodied AI context (digital human). Women benefited from emotional voice in terms of closeness and perceived support; neutral voice benefited women in reduced loneliness and stress; men benefited from a neutral/voice-only condition in terms of closeness and support. This indicates gender-specific sensitivities to modality and emotional expressiveness.
  - Other large-scale syntheses (Xu et al., 2025; Hansen et al., 2025) do not consistently report gender-specific effects, highlighting a gap in gender-focused moderation across many AI modalities.
- Age
  - Xu et al. (2025) show older adults (60+) experience a stronger positive association between AI use and loneliness (r = 0.352, p < 0.001), suggesting older adults may be more vulnerable to loneliness with AI exposure unless moderated by embodiment.
  - Younger groups (≤ 35) show non-significant associations.
  - The embedded/embodiment literature often centers on older adults (e.g., robotic pets for elder care); younger populations are underrepresented in modality-comparative trials, limiting conclusions about age-based moderation in embodied vs text AI.
- Implications: Gender and age are important moderators to test in direct embodied-vs-text AI trials. Evidence exists for gender-specific effects in embodied contexts (Loveys et al., 2021), and age-related differences in loneliness associations with AI use (Xu et al., 2025); however, many studies do not report these subgroup analyses, underscoring a critical gap for future research.

4) Gaps and limitations in the current evidence base
- Direct modality comparisons
  - Few randomized trials directly compare embodied AI companions to text-only AI controls within the same sample, and those that exist often do not measure loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes comprehensively.
  - Where embodiment effects are studied (e.g., Loveys et al., 2021), outcomes focus on closeness, perceived support, and loneliness in a very specific design (digital human with variable face/voice expressions); generalizability to other embodied forms or to all text-based AI types is limited.
- Outcomes
  - Loneliness is the most consistently measured social outcome; attachment security and social-cognition outcomes are less frequently measured with standardized instruments across modalities, and when they are, results are not consistently reported or may be proxies (e.g., closeness or perceived support).
- Moderation analyses
  - Gender and age moderation is often underreported. Where it exists (e.g., Loveys et al., 2021), findings are informative but not readily generalizable across contexts or AI modalities.
- Study quality and heterogeneity
  - Reviews note substantial heterogeneity in interventions, populations, and outcomes; risk of bias is moderate-to-high in many trials; long-term durability (beyond weeks or months) is underexplored (Hansen et al., 2025; Broadbent et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025).
- Real-world vs experimental evidence
  - Real-world deployments (e.g., ElliQ) show acceptability and perceived companionship but lack rigorous controlled trials with standardized loneliness/attachment measures (Broadbent et al., 2024).

5) Practical implications for authors and practitioners
- If aiming to claim modality-based advantages (embodied vs text-only):
  - Conduct head-to-head randomized controlled trials that directly compare embodied AI (robotic pets or digital-human avatars with physical or proximate embodiment) to text-only AI controls.
  - Use a factorial or multi-arm design to disentangle embodiment, expressiveness (voice/face presence), and interactivity level.
  - Include pre-registered subgroup analyses by gender and age, with power calculations to detect interaction effects.
- Outcome selection and measurement strategy
  - Loneliness: use validated scales (e.g., UCLA Loneliness Scale variants) at baseline and multiple follow-ups (short-term and longer-term).
  - Attachment security to AI: include explicit attachment measures (e.g., adapted ECR scales for human–AI attachments) or validated AI-attachment proxies.
  - Social cognition: include theory-of-m mind tasks, emotion recognition, social perception measures, and real-world social functioning indicators.
  - Mediators: include social presence, perceived emotional support, trust, autonomy, and relatedness as potential mediators; test mediation and moderated mediation by gender/age.
- Population and settings
  - Enroll diverse age groups (adolescents to older adults) to map age-related heterogeneity; balance gender and examine non-binary participants where feasible.
  - Consider clinical vs non-clinical populations (e.g., older adults with vs without cognitive impairment) to test differential benefits and acceptance of embodied AI.
- Design and reporting
  - Preregister hypotheses and analysis plans; publish null results to reduce publication bias in modality comparisons.
  - Report modality-specific effect sizes, confidence intervals, and heterogeneity statistics; provide transparent risk-of-bias assessments.
  - Include qualitative components to contextualize quantitative findings (e.g., user experiences with embodiment, trust, and relational scripts).
- Design implications for practitioners
  - If embeddable embodiment demonstrates benefits for loneliness or relatedness in older adults, implement embodied AI as part of a multi-component program that integrates with human touchpoints (caregivers, family), ensuring that AI supports and augments human relationships rather than substitutes them.
  - Provide user personalization options (face/no-face, emotional vs neutral voice) to accommodate gender- and age-related preferences and mitigate loneliness for subgroups.
- Ethical and practical considerations
  - Monitor potential dependency, privacy concerns, emotional manipulation risks, and misalignment with user expectations; ensure safety and consent protocols are in place.
  - Assess cross-cultural applicability; examine whether embodiment preferences and perceived social presence vary across cultural contexts.

6) Synthesis: guiding conclusions for the chapter
- Embodiment is a relevant design variable for AI companions and can interact with user gender and age to shape loneliness, attachment proxies, and social-cognition processes. However, the literature lacks a definitive, across-the-board conclusion that embodied AI universally reduces loneliness more than text-only AI, or that it consistently improves attachment security or social-cognition outcomes across all age groups.
- The strongest converging evidence for embodiment benefits comes from targeted studies in older adults and from qualitative/experimental work showing gender-specific responses to facial expression and vocal cues in embodied avatars (Loveys et al., 2021). Meta-analytic findings (Xu et al., 2025; Hansen et al., 2025) converge on the idea that embodiment and modality matter, but direct cross-modality comparisons and comprehensive gender/age moderator analyses are sparse.
- Given the current state of evidence, researchers and authors should emphasize direct, well-powered, preregistered embodied-vs-text AI trials with standardized, multi-faceted outcomes (loneliness, attachment security, and social cognition) and explicit gender and age analyses to advance theory and practice.

7) Actionable insights for the author (summary of concrete steps)
- Design recommendations for future research
  - Implement direct head-to-head randomized trials comparing embodied AI companions (robotic pets or digital humans with physical-like embodiment) versus text-only AI chatbots, with follow-ups at multiple time points (e.g., 1 month, 3 months, 6–12 months).
  - Stratify analyses by gender and age (e.g., 18–29, 30–44, 45–64, 65+), with preregistered interaction tests for modality × gender and modality × age.
  - Measure loneliness (validated scales), attachment security toward AI (adapted attachment measures for AI relationships or AI-specific attachment constructs), and social-cognition outcomes (theory of mind, emotion recognition, social perception tasks).
  - Include mediators such as perceived social presence, trust, perceived emotional support, and autonomy/relatedness needs (SDT framework).
  - Ensure diverse samples and settings (community-dwelling adults, older adults in care settings, cross-cultural groups) to map generalizability.
  - Pre-register protocols and share data where possible to facilitate replication and meta-analysis.
- If embedding within real-world programs (e.g., elder care)
  - Combine embodied AI with human social supports (caregivers, family involvement) to explore additive or synergistic effects on loneliness and social functioning.
  - Collect qualitative feedback on embodiment features (facial cues, voice expressiveness, physical proximity cues) to guide iterative design.
- Reporting and dissemination
  - Present modality-specific effect sizes with CIs; report heterogeneity sources (population, setting, usage patterns, interface design).
  - Publish gender- and age-specific results even if null to contribute to the evidence base and reduce publication bias.
  - Include practical design guidelines for developers (e.g., when to deploy face presence vs voice-only interactions, how to calibrate emotional expressiveness by user demographics).

Notes and caveats
- The evidence base is diverse in design and modality; direct, controlled, cross-modality comparisons (embodied vs text-only) with standardized loneliness, attachment security, and social-cognition outcomes are still relatively sparse.
- Gender and age moderation are inconsistently reported across studies; where available, gender differences exist (e.g., Loveys et al., 2021) but require replication and extension across a broader set of modalities and populations.
- Attachment security and social-cognition outcomes are not consistently measured across embodiment studies, necessitating explicit inclusion of these constructs in future trials.

- Outcomes and timing: Loneliness (baseline, 1 month, 3 months, 6–12 months); attachment toward AI (baseline, 1 month, 3 months, 6–12 months); social-cognition tasks (baseline, 3 months, 6–12 months); social presence/relatedness/autonomy mediators at multiple time points.
