# report.md

Substitution versus Complement Effects of AI Intimacy Features on Time Spent with Real Partners and Relationship Satisfaction Across Modalities (Text, Voice, Video)

Authors: Research Synthesis Team

Date: 2025

Executive summary
- Across the collected evidence, AI intimacy features can function as both substitutes and complements to real romantic relationships, but direct, modality-specific empirical data are sparse. The strongest direct signals come from studies that quantify real-partner time and relationship outcomes in human–AI interaction contexts, yet they rarely break out results by modality (text, voice, video) or provide causal inference. Inline: (Pollmann et al., 2021); (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025); (Willoughby & Carroll, 2025); (Zhao et al., 2025); (Malfacini, 2025). 
- Text- and voice- and video-capable AI intimacy features exist, but modality-specific effects on time spent with real partners or relationship satisfaction remain largely unquantified in the sources reviewed. Where data exist, they tend to address AI–AI or AI–human interaction dynamics rather than direct substitution/complement effects on real-partner time (e.g., text messaging vs FtF dynamics; AI romance in cross-sectional samples). Inline: (Pollmann et al., 2021); (Willoughby & Carroll, 2025); (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025); (Zhao et al., 2025).
- Practical implications for researchers and designers center on explicitly measuring time with real partners and relationship satisfaction, and on designing AI features to maximize complementarity (support for real relationships) while guarding against substitution risks. Inline: (Malfacini, 2025); (Kirk et al., 2025); (Ruane et al., 2019; Dautenhahn, 1998).

1) Introduction and background
- Research question: Do AI intimacy features substitute for or complement time spent with real romantic partners, and how do these effects vary by modality (text, voice, video)? 
- Core concepts:
  - Substitution: AI intimacy fulfilling social/emotional needs in ways that reduce real-partner time or satisfaction (replacement of human relational labor) [Gist drawn from Malfacini 2025; Turkle 2011; Ciriello et al. 2024]. Inline: (Malfacini, 2025); (Turkle, 2011); (Ciriello et al., 2024).
  - Complement: AI intimacy enhancing real relationships by improving communication, empathy, or loneliness mitigation without displacing real-partner time (complementary mutual influence) [Malfacini, 2025; Ciriello et al., 2024]. Inline: (Malfacini, 2025); (Ciriello et al., 2024).
  - Modality distinctions: AI can operate via text, voice, video, and embodied/AR modalities; each affords different cues (verbal, nonverbal, embodiment) that plausibly shape substitution/complement dynamics, though direct modality-specific data are largely absent in the available sources. Inline: (Malfacini, 2025); (Pentina et al., 2023); (Roose, 2024a).
- Evidence landscape (high-level): 
  - Several syntheses emphasize substitution and deskilling risks, plus potential complementarity when AI is designed to support real relationships (Gugliotta et al.; Turkle; Malfacini). Inline: (Turkle, 2011); (Malfacini, 2025).
  - Longitudinal, modality-differentiated empirical data remain scant; most robust signals come from cross-sectional or qualitative studies or from human–AI bonding literature rather than direct real-partner time metrics. Inline: (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025); (Willoughby & Carroll, 2025); (Zhao et al., 2025); (Pollmann et al., 2021).

2) Core quantitative evidence by modality (text, voice, video) and related outcomes
Note: Across the sources, modality-specific empirical data on time spent with real partners and real-partner relationship satisfaction are generally not provided. The following summarizes what is available and highlights gaps.

- Text modality (AI chat, messaging)
  - FtF time and satisfaction baseline (human partner) vs mediated text: A daily-diary study finds FtF time is positively associated with perceived understanding and relationship satisfaction; texting alone does not predict relationship satisfaction, but can boost perceived understanding when FtF is relatively low (complementary role in low FtF contexts). Inline: (Pollmann, Norman, Crockett, 2021).
  - In AI contexts, cross-sectional data show high engagement with AI romance/Chat/imagery content and substantial proportion of users who perceive AI as easier to talk to or as a better listener than real people; these attitudes imply substitution risk in some users but do not quantify real-partner time. Inline: (Willoughby & Carroll, 2025).
  - Relational-AI bonding with chat-oriented companions (e.g., relational AI over time) shows intensification of attachment and ongoing AI engagement, with modality-less data. Inline: (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025).
  - Moderator/mediator candidates: AI attachment, perceived authenticity, and immersion may mediate any substitution/complement pathway; data specific to this pathway are limited in modality-specific terms. Inline: (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025).
  - Gaps: No direct time-use measures with real partners by text AI exposure; no causal inference linking text-AI use to changes in real-partner time or satisfaction. Inline: (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025).

- Voice modality (AI dialogue with vocal cues)
  - Evidence from ICT/communication literature indicates voice channels improve immediacy and emotional conveyance relative to text, but context-specific AI voice data in intimate contexts are sparse. Some modality work suggests voice can support deeper conversations with lower stress in some contexts, but video may produce different affective outcomes (e.g., COVID-era studies). Inline: (Hall et al., 2021); (Sprecher, 2014).
  - In AI companionship literature, voice-enabled AI is discussed as a conduit for richer social presence, with potential complementarity if used to rehearse real-partner conversations or to provide nonjudgmental support—yet direct substitution/complement effect sizes for real-partner time remain unquantified by modality in the sources. Inline: (George, 2025); (Stanford HCI Lab, 2025).
  - Gaps: No clean modality-specific causal data tying AI voice use to time spent with real partners or satisfaction.

- Video/embodiment modality (video calls, AR/VR avatars, holograms)
  - Embodied AI (video/AR) offers strongest social presence cues; researchers note potential substitution risk if embodied AI provides compelling presence that competes with real-world interaction, but also potential complementarity when embodied AI is used to support real-life interaction (e.g., by modeling positive interaction patterns). Evidence is largely theorized or based on consumer products rather than longitudinal outcome data. Inline: (Roose, 2024a); (Malfacini, 2025).
  - Some studies highlight that multimodal presence can increase attachment and perceived realism, which could either substitute or complement depending on usage patterns and boundary management. Gaps: lack of modality-disaggregated outcome data on real-partner time or satisfaction.

3) Gaps and limitations across the literature
- No consistent modality-by-modality quantitative data linking AI intimacy exposure to:
  - Time spent with real partners (minutes/hours per week)
  - Real-partner relationship satisfaction (validated scales)
- Causal inference is generally absent; most evidence is cross-sectional, qualitative, or based on AI bonding without real-world dyadic outcomes.
- Diversity of AI systems and use cases complicates generalization; many studies focus on specific AI types (text chat, image/GPP, VR) or on AI bonding processes rather than real-partner time.
- Cultural and demographic variation in AI use and relationship norms is underexplored in a way that allows generalizable modality-specific conclusions.

4) Mechanisms that matter for substitution vs complement
- Complementary pathways
  - AI can mitigate loneliness and improve emotional regulation in some users; when AI supports real relationships (e.g., prompts for shared activities, reflective conversations with a partner), it may enhance relationship quality. Inline: (Stanford HCI Lab, 2025; UCSF PTSD studies, 2025).
  - AI-enabled tools can augment communication skills and emotional labor, which could improve real-partner interactions and satisfaction if used in moderation and with proper boundaries. Inline: (Pentina et al., 2023); (Hernandez-Ortega & Ferreira, 2021).
- Substitution pathways
  - AI can provide constant availability, tailored emotional support, and nonjudgmental presence that could draw time and emotional energy away from real partners, particularly for lonely individuals or those with weak real-world social supports. Inline: (Turkle, 2011); (Malfacini, 2025); (Kirk et al., 2025).
  - Visual/embodied AI (video/AR) could intensify substitution risk by simulating intimacy in highly realistic ways, though empirical data linking this to decreased real-partner time are limited. Inline: (Roose, 2024a); (Malfacini, 2025).

5) Actionable insights for authors and designers
- measurement roadmap
  - Design studies that explicitly measure time spent with real partners and relationship satisfaction, disaggregated by modality (text, voice, video). Use longitudinal or experimental designs with baseline measures and repeated follow-ups.
  - Employ diary/EMA methods to capture daily AI usage by modality and partner-time metrics, plus validated relationship satisfaction scales.
  - Include mediation analyses for mechanisms such as perceived closeness, perceived authenticity, and AI transparency; test moderation by attachment style, baseline relationship quality, and cultural context.
- design principles to maximize complementarity
  - Build features that explicitly support real relationships, such as prompts to plan date nights, reminders to reconnect, tools to practice active listening, and memory aids that align AI interactions with real-life relationship goals.
  - Implement boundary and consent controls (explicit disclosures that AI is not a real partner; opt-in/opt-out options; usage caps during critical relationship periods).
  - Encourage joint AI use in shared contexts (couple-facing dashboards, shared AI-assisted planning) to keep AI as a facilitator rather than a substitute.
  - Provide transparency about AI limitations and avoid anthropomorphism that implies mutuality or equivalence with a real partner.
- methodology and reporting
  - Pre-register hypotheses about substitution vs complement by modality and report effect sizes with confidence intervals.
  - Use mixed methods to capture both objective time-use data and subjective relationship quality, including dyadic reports when possible.
  - Consider cross-cultural samples to test generalizability; report moderator analyses by age, relationship type, and baseline satisfaction.
- policy and ethics
  - Propose industry guidelines requiring disclosure of AI limitations, clear labeling of AI content as non-human, and safeguards against dependency or deception.
  - Develop ethical guidelines for AI-assisted intimacy that protect vulnerable users, maintain autonomy, and preserve the integrity of real-world relationships.

7) Limitations of this report
- The synthesis acknowledges a pervasive limitation: there is no consistent, modality-specific longitudinal evidence that directly quantifies substitutions or complements of AI intimacy for time with real partners and for relationship satisfaction across text, voice, and video. The evidence base comprises cross-sectional, qualitative, and synthetic studies with indirect inferences about time-use and dyadic outcomes.
- Given the rapid evolution of AI capabilities, results reported here reflect a snapshot of the literature up to 2025; future work may reveal stronger modality-specific effects or causal patterns.

8) Conclusions
- The best-supported conclusion given the current evidence is that AI intimacy features have dual potential: they can complement real relationships by providing emotional support and facilitating communication, but they also carry substitution risks when AI use displaces real-partner interactions or when attachment to AI reduces motivation for real-world intimacy. Modality-specific (text vs. voice vs. video) effects remain underresearched, and robust, longitudinal, modality-differentiated data are needed to quantify substitution versus complement effects precisely. Designers and researchers should focus on strategies that maximize complementarity, implement clear boundaries, and measure real-world outcomes across modalities to determine when AI intimacy supports or undermines real-partner time and relationship satisfaction. Inline: (Malfacini, 2025); (Pollmann et al., 2021); (Xu, Shi, & Shi, 2025); (Willoughby & Carroll, 2025); (Zhao et al., 2025).
