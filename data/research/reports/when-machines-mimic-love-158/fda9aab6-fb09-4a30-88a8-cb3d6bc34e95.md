# report.md

Deliberate-Difficulty Prompts and Constraint-Based AI Workflows for Social Skills Training: Evidence, Gaps, and a Proposed Randomized Controlled Trial Design

Executive summary
- The convergence of evidence suggests that authentic human empathy and a strong working alliance robustly predict durable social-functioning gains. AI can support, but not replace, genuine empathic engagement; AI empathy tends to be cognitive/simulation-based and may not yield equivalent real-world transfer without human-in-the-loop elements (Rubin et al., 2024; Sanjeewa et al., 2024) [PMCID: PMC11200042; JMIR Ment Health, 2024].
- Empirical work across contexts shows promising gains from AI-facilitated empathy practice (e.g., Noora in ASD; AI-enabled MI) but with important caveats: outcomes often focus on in-session empathy or proximal skills, not consistently on real-world social engagement, conversational depth, or durable transfer, and often rely on small samples or non-active control conditions (Koegel et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2022; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2022; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025].
- Several RCTs indicate that deliberate-difficulty prompts and constraint-based workflows are feasible and potentially beneficial for targeted social-skill practice, but direct evidence linking these design features to reduced AI over-reliance and to robust transfer to real-world interactions remains limited. Notable signals: constraint-based, practice-oriented prompts can improve empathic expressions and generalize to human interactions in ASD populations (Koegel et al., 2025); MI-informed constraint-based prompts can improve readiness to change and may mitigate harms in AI-mediated behavior change (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025); in broader chatbot trials, longer engagement sometimes correlates with worse real-world social outcomes unless transfer scaffolds are embedded (Fang et al., 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025; Fang et al., 2025].
- Actionable plan: design a rigorously controlled RCT that directly compares standard AI use to a constraint-based protocol featuring deliberate-difficulty prompts, with primary outcomes on (i) multi-dimensional empathy, (ii) conversational depth, and (iii) real-world social engagement. Include mediator (digital/therapeutic alliance) and moderator analyses, objective transfer metrics, fidelity reporting, and safety governance.

1) Background and rationale

1.1 Conceptual foundations: empathy, alliance, and transfer
- Empathy is multidimensional (cognitive, emotional, motivational). AI can simulate cognitive empathy but lacks lived emotional experience; authentic emotional/motivational empathy remains a human trait, a crucial consideration for durable social-skill transfer (Rubin et al., 2024) [PMCID: PMC11200042].
- The therapeutic alliance (goals, tasks, warmth/genuineness) robustly predicts outcomes; in digital interventions, human support enhances adherence and reduces dropout, while the relationship with the AI interface can predict engagement and, in some contexts, symptom reduction (Rubin et al., 2024).
- For social-skill transfer, authentic warmth and alliance appear central; the AI in isolation may support practice but is unlikely to yield durable real-world skill transfer without ongoing human-in-the-loop (Rubin et al., 2024).

1.2 Evidence on AI-facilitated empathy and transfer (highlights)
- Noora ASD trial (Koegel et al., 2025): constrained prompts (330 leading statements) with immediate feedback yielded substantial gains in empathic responses (71% improved) and transfer to a live, human-interaction proxy (Zoom) with a ~38% post-intervention empathy increase versus baseline; four weeks, 200 trials per participant, N ≈ 30. Strengths: rigorous, targeted, prespecified prompts and feedback; constraint-based workflow in practice. Limitations: small sample; comparison to waitlist rather than standard AI use; limited breadth of transfer assessment beyond Zoom (Koegel et al., 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025; Stanford HAI release].
- AI-enabled MI and behavior-change prompts (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025): MI-informed prompts in GPT-4-based chatbot showed readiness-to-change improvements and differential effects by user type; fidelity to MI and safety considerations discussed; no direct test of deliberate-difficulty prompts versus standard AI prompting; implications for constraint-based protocols and human-in-the-loop design (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025).
- AI-assisted empathy training and transfer signals (Sharma et al., 2022): HAILEY system (just-in-time AI feedback to edit empathic responses) improved expressed empathy and showed transfer to online human-human empathy in TalkLife peers; engagement with AI feedback varied; evidence suggests a role for constrained, feedback-driven prompts in skill acquisition with real-world transfer proxies (Sharma et al., 2022).
- AI listening quality and psychosocial needs (Weinstein et al., 2025): high-quality AI listening increased perceived autonomy and relatedness needs, but did not reliably reduce loneliness; signals potential for alliance/empathy pathways but suggests that listening quality alone may be insufficient for durable social outcomes without transfer scaffolds (Weinstein et al., 2025).
- Empathy measurement and standardization (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023): lack of standardized empathy measures for dialogue systems; proposed Empathy Scale for Human–Computer Communication (ESHCC) to enable cross-study comparability; this has implications for designing and evaluating empathy outcomes in RCTs (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023).

1.3 Relevance to deliberate-difficulty prompts and constraint-based AI workflows
- Deliberate-difficulty prompts are designed to elicit deeper cognitive engagement (fluency, mental-state reasoning, perspective-taking) and to scaffold reflective practice. Constraint-based workflows bound the prompt/response space, aiming to reduce hollow ease and increase durable practice.
- Evidence from ASD, MI, and behavior-change contexts suggests constraint-based, scaffolded prompt designs can improve empathic responses and readiness to change, and can support transfer to human interactions when combined with appropriate transfer tasks and alliance-building components. However, direct RCT evidence specifically testing deliberate-difficulty prompts versus unconstrained AI prompts, with empathy/conversational-depth/real-world social-engagement outcomes, remains limited (Koegel et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2022; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025; Fang et al., 2025).

2) What we know and what remains uncertain

2.1 Quantitative findings relevant to the research question
- Empathy gains with AI-assisted training:
  - Noora ASD trial: 71% of participants improved in empathetic responses; 38% average post-intervention increase in empathy during a live Zoom interaction after training; small N, no active AI-control comparator (Koegel et al., 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025].
  - HAILEY (Sharma et al., 2022): AI feedback plus human training increased empathic responses versus control; significant but context-specific; reliance on AI feedback varied across participants; transfer proxy via online interactions observed; sample sizes around 300; multi-arm design (Hum+AI vs Human-only) [Sharma et al., 2022].
- Transfer vs. engagement dynamics:
  - Four-week longitudinal RCT (Fang et al., 2025): four-week design crossing modality and conversation-type; no main effects on loneliness or real-world socialization; personal conversations reduced emotional dependence and problematic AI use modestly; longer daily AI use associated with worse outcomes; duration mediated some modality effects. Conclusion: simple modality/conversation-type variation does not robustly yield transfer; design implications point to duration constraints and richer transfer scaffolds (Fang et al., 2025).
- MI-informed constraint-based protocols:
  - MI-based protocols with structured prompts demonstrate promise for readiness-to-change and safety, but direct transfer to real-world social behaviors remains to be established; fidelity to MI and AI safety are crucial (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025).
- Measurement challenges:
  - Empathy measurement in dialogue systems is heterogeneous; calls for standardized measures, e.g., ESHCC, multi-method assessments (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023; Sanjeewa et al., 2024).

2.2 Gaps specific to the current research question
- Direct comparison of standard AI use versus a constraint-based protocol with deliberate-difficulty prompts is sparse. The strongest evidence for a constraint-based design comes from ASD/empathy-training contexts and MI-style interventions; these are encouraging but not definitive for generalizable social-skill transfer.
- Real-world social engagement outcomes with robust, objective metrics are seldom reported, particularly in larger samples and longer follow-ups.
- Fidelity to constraint-based designs and deliberate prompt progression, including detailed reporting of prompts, contest of over-reliance signals, and HRL (human-in-the-loop) processes, are inconsistently documented across studies.
- Standardization of empathy measurement in AI-mediated interactions remains incomplete; psychometric rigor and cross-study comparability need attention (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023; Sanjeewa et al., 2024).

3) Proposed randomized controlled trial (RCT) design to address the question

3.1 Objective
Test whether a constraint-based AI workflow employing deliberate-difficulty prompts reduces AI over-reliance and enhances transfer of social skills to real interactions, compared with standard AI use, using an RCT with outcome measures on empathy (multi-dimensional), conversational depth, and real-world social engagement. Include mediator (digital/therapeutic alliance) and moderator analyses, and adolescent/adult-inclusive populations to maximize generalizability.

3.2 Population and sampling
- Population: Adults and young adults (age range 16–35; optional expansion to include older adults) engaged in social-skill training or therapy contexts, including ASD and non-ASD populations to test generalizability.
- Sample size: A-priori power analysis to detect small-to-moderate effects on empathy and real-world social engagement; plan for 300–600 participants per arm depending on feasibility, with multi-site recruitment to improve generalizability. This scale aligns with large RCTs in AI-mediated social interventions (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025; Fang et al., 2025).
- Inclusion/exclusion: Age-appropriate consent; familiarity with AI chat interfaces; no contraindicated psychiatric conditions; ability to participate in real-world social-transfer tasks.

3.3 Intervention arms
- Arm A: Standard AI use (control). Participants use a baseline AI social-skill practice protocol with typical prompts and feedback, without the explicit constraint-based design or deliberate-difficulty progression.
- Arm B: Constraint-based workflow with deliberate-difficulty prompts (deliberate prompts). Features:
  - A curated bank of prompts designed to increase cognitive engagement (perspective-taking, justification of social reasoning, counterfactuals) with a clearly defined progression (easy → moderate → hard).
  - Structured response templates and required reflective elements to be included before proceeding.
  - Explicit constraints on content (topic boundaries, length, pacing); mandated summaries of real-world goals after sessions.
  - Real-time, supervisor-validated feedback to ensure alignment with social-skill objectives.
- Arm C: Constraint-based workflow with deliberate-difficulty prompts plus enhanced transfer scaffolding (combined approach). Adds:
  - Structured real-world social-task assignments between sessions (e.g., arrange a social interaction, practice a specific conversational move in real life, report back with a brief outcome).
  - Additional human coaching sessions to reinforce alliance-building and transfer.

3.4 Outcome measures (primary, secondary, and mediators)
- Primary outcomes:
  - Empathy (multi-dimensional): Cognitive empathy, emotional empathy, and motivational empathy measured by validated scales (e.g., adapted Interpersonal Reactivity Index, Empathy Quotient) and augmented by expert-rated empathy in transcripts (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023; Sanjeewa et al., 2024; Koegel et al., 2025).
  - Conversational depth: Objective transcript-based metrics (turn density, depth of topic exploration, depth of reasoning, perspective-taking indicators) and validated in-session coding of conversational quality.
  - Real-world social engagement: Ecological momentary assessment (EMA) of social interactions, peer/partner reports, and activity logs; objective proxies (e.g., social activity counts, proximity-mensor data with consent) where feasible.
- Secondary outcomes:
  - Therapeutic/digital alliance: Digital Therapeutic Alliance (DTA) indicators and items from WAI-SR-like scales adapted to AI-human collaboration contexts (Cunningham et al., 2023/2024; Weisz et al., 2013; 2014).
  - Adherence and engagement: Session attendance, duration, dropout, and AI-dependence metrics (e.g., AI-initiated prompts used with or without user-initiated inputs).
  - Safety and ethics: Adverse events, perceived safety, and data privacy concerns.
- Mediators and moderators:
  - Alliance strength (goal alignment, task agreement, warmth) as a mediator of any empathic and transfer effects.
  - Baseline loneliness, social anxiety, trust in AI, prior social skill training as moderators.
  - AI usage patterns (frequency, duration) as potential mediators/moderators of outcomes (Fang et al., 2025; Weisz et al., 2013, 2014).

3.5 Procedure and timeline
- Baseline assessment of empathy, conversational style, real-world social engagement, alliance propensity, and demographic covariates.
- Randomization to Arm A, B, or C.
- Intervention period: 6–12 weeks to enable skill acquisition and transfer; sessions scheduled with fidelity checks.
- Assessments: Immediately post-intervention, plus follow-ups at 3 and 6 months to gauge durability.
- Fidelity checks: Regular auditing of prompts, prompt drift, and alignment with the constraint-based protocol; detailed logging of interaction traces for post-hoc analyses.

3.6 Data analysis plan
- Primary analyses: Mixed-effects models comparing changes in empathy, conversational depth, and real-world social engagement across arms, adjusting for baseline values and covariates.
- Mediation analyses: Test whether alliance mediates the effect of constraint-based prompts on transfer outcomes.
- Moderation analyses: Examine whether baseline loneliness, trust in AI, or other individual differences moderate the treatment effects.
- Handling of missing data: Multiple imputation or maximum-likelihood methods, with sensitivity analyses.
- Multiple comparison control: Pre-specified primary outcomes with hierarchical testing; secondary outcomes adjusted for multiple tests.

3.7 Measurement instruments and data sources
- Empathy: Multi-method (self-report scales; expert coding of empathy in transcripts; ESHCC where feasible; alignment with Concannon & Tomalin, 2023).
- Conversational depth: Transcript analytics; standardized coding rubrics for depth and perspective-taking.
- Real-world social engagement: EMA prompts; daily/weekly self-reports; external informants (e.g., peers, supervisors) where feasible.
- Alliance: DTA scales and WAI-SR-adapted items.
- AI over-reliance: Usage logs, prompt usage patterns, and measures of user autonomy in conversation.

3.8 Practical considerations and risk management
- Safety and privacy: Implement strong data governance; obtain informed consent for real-world data collection; ensure data encryption and compliant handling of sensitive information.
- Human-in-the-loop: Maintain human oversight to preserve warmth and authentic alliance signals; document fidelity to the constraint-based protocol.
- Cultural and individual differences: Adapt prompts to language, culture, and communication styles; plan sub-analyses by demographic or psychometric subgroups.
- Generalizability: Multi-site recruitment to improve external validity; consider stratified randomization to balance key covariates.

4) Design considerations and rationale (interpretation of evidence for design choices)

- Rationale for three-arm design
  - Arm A (standard AI) provides a direct comparator to test whether constraint-based, deliberate-difficulty prompts produce added benefits relative to typical AI prompting.
  - Arm B isolates the effect of deliberate-difficulty prompts within a constraint-based framework.
  - Arm C adds real-world transfer scaffolding to assess whether combining constraint-based design with transfer tasks yields incremental gains over the base constraint-based approach.
  This mirrors evidence that constrained, practice-focused prompts can improve outcomes (Koegel et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2022; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025) while noting that simple modality/interaction-type manipulations alone (as in Fang et al., 2025) do not reliably produce transfer.

- Outcome emphasis
  - The emphasis on multi-dimensional empathy and real-world engagement responds to measurement gaps identified in the literature (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023; Sanjeewa et al., 2024; Sanjeewa synthesis) and the need to demonstrate transferable social skills beyond in-session improvements (Weinstein et al., 2025; Koegel et al., 2025).

- Fidelity and safety governance
  - Given concerns about over-reliance, empathy authenticity, and potential for unsafe AI outputs, the protocol should enforce fidelity checks, safety guardrails, and human oversight (Gao et al., 2022; Hatem et al., 2023; Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025).

5) Implementation and reporting considerations

5.1 Measurement quality and standardization
- Adopt standardized empathy measurement where possible (e.g., ESHCC as a candidate) and supplement with established scales (IRI, EQ) and expert ratings to triangulate empathy constructs (Concannon & Tomalin, 2023; Sanjeewa et al., 2024).
- Develop robust conversational-depth metrics (topic maintenance, depth of reasoning, perspective-taking cues) with validated coding rubrics.

5.2 Transfer and ecological validity
- Incorporate real-world social engagement endpoints (EMA, social-activity logs, peer/teacher reports, or wearable-based proximity data) to capture transfer to day-to-day interactions, aligning with the transfer focus highlighted in Koegel et al. (ASD) and Fang et al. (general population) literature.

5.3 Alliance and “digital therapeutic alliance”
- Measure alliance with AI via DTA constructs and adapt WAI-SR-like items to AI-human collaboration contexts; examine mediation of empathy and real-world transfer by alliance strength (Cunningham et al., 2023/2024).

5.4 Ethics, safety, and transparency
- Pre-register the trial; publish a detailed intervention manual with prompt banks, progression criteria, and fidelity metrics; report safety incidents and data governance practices (Gao et al., 2022; Dempere et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2024).

5.5 Generalizability and scalability
- Consider multi-site recruitment, diverse populations, and cross-cultural adaptations; assess scalability of constraint-based protocols in real-world settings (Sethi & Jain, 2024; Jain & Sethi, 2024; Scherr et al., 2025).

6) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners

- Design and fidelity
  - Build a clearly documented constraint-based protocol with deliberate-difficulty prompts, including a progression plan, defined prompt templates, and explicit success criteria for advancing through levels.
  - Maintain a human-in-the-loop structure to supervise AI outputs, ensure empathy authenticity, and support alliance-building, in line with HITL principles (Stanford HAI; 2019).
- Measurement and evaluation
  - Use multi-method empathy metrics (self-report, expert coding, and third-party assessments) and standardized empathy scales.
  - Implement objective conversational-depth metrics and real-world social engagement measures to capture transfer.
  - Analyze alliance as a mediator; incorporate symmetric measures of warmth and genuineness in the digital context.
- Safety and ethics
  - Incorporate transparent disclosure of AI capabilities/limits; embed guardrails; ensure privacy-preserving data collection; monitor for over-reliance and disengagement risks.
- Practical deployment
  - Pilot the protocol in a controlled setting before scaling; iteratively refine the prompt-bank and progression logic to ensure user tolerance and sustained engagement.
- Reporting
  - Pre-register the trial; publish a comprehensive protocol; share data and materials where ethical; provide full fidelity reports and prompts used to enable replication.

7) Synthesis: what this means for the research question

- Direct evidence gap: There is no definitive, large-scale RCT showing that deliberate-difficulty prompts plus constraint-based AI workflows outperform standard AI in reducing AI over-reliance and in achieving durable transfer of social skills to real-world interactions. The closest evidence comes from ASD-focused constraint-based training (Koegel et al., 2025) and MI-focused constraint protocols (Meyer & Elsweiler, 2025).
- Direction for future work: A rigorously designed RCT with a three-arm design (standard AI; constraint-based deliberate-difficulty prompts; constraint-based with transfer scaffolding) and robust, multi-method outcomes is a plausible path to answering the question. Such a study should foreground empathy (multi-dimensional), conversational depth, and real-world social engagement, with alliance as a potential mediator and baseline individual-differences (loneliness, trust in AI) as moderators.
- Practical implications: If effective, constraint-based prompts and deliberate-difficulty workflows could reduce hollow ease, promote deeper social-cognitive engagement, and improve transfer to real-world social interactions—provided these protocols are implemented with fidelity, human-in-the-loop oversight, and transfer-focused scaffolds.

8) Concluding remarks

- The existing evidence underscores the potential of constraint-based, practice-focused AI interventions to improve empathic responding and, in some contexts, generalize to human interactions. However, robust, generalizable evidence for durable transfer to real-world social engagement, via deliberate-difficulty prompts and constraint-based AI workflows, remains nascent. A carefully designed, adequately powered RCT with explicit transfer outcomes, standardized empathy metrics, and fidelity reporting is warranted to determine whether such prompts and workflows can meaningfully reduce AI over-reliance and promote durable social-skill transfer.
