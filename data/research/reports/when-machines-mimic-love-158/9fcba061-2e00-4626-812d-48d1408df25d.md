# Impact of AI Companionship on Nonverbal Social Skills and Empathy Over Time: A Comprehensive Longitudinal Design

Executive summary
- This report synthesizes existing evidence and outlines a rigorous longitudinal design to assess whether AI companionship can shape nonverbal social skills and empathy over time, by combining validated survey scales with objective behavioral assessments (e.g., coded video interactions or simulated conversations).
- Key empirical baseline signals:
  - Active AI companionship can increase users’ perceived attachment to AI and perceived AI empathy in the short term, and can elevate motivation for entertainment-related AI use, but direct transfer to human nonverbal skills and real-world empathy remains unproven in those short, self-report–driven studies [Chandra et al., 2025; Weinstein et al., 2025].
  - Perceived listening quality and “feeling heard” are robustly linked to social connection and well-being in AI-mediated interactions, with mediation patterns that differ by context and presence, but longitudinal human-behavior transfer evidence is scarce in existing work [Weinstein et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023].
  - Validated empathy and nonverbal-skill measures exist (e.g., IRI, EQ, RMET, NVS) and psychometric syntheses indicate best practice is to triangulate self-report with objective behavioral data, not rely on a single modality [de Lima & Osório, 2021].
- Proposed longitudinal design embodies best practices: multi-wave measurement (e.g., baseline, multiple follow-ups across months), randomized or quasi-experimental AI exposure arms, validated empathy/nonverbal scales, and behavioral coding of nonverbal behaviors during AI interactions and subsequent human interactions.
- Actionable implications for researchers and practitioners include: preregistered hypotheses; integration of validated human-empathy scales with AI-based behavioral metrics; robust nonverbal-coding schemes (e.g., FACS–based, MEA/OpenPose synchrony) applied longitudinally; and explicit attention to ethical safeguards around privacy and data use.

1) Research question and rationale
- Research question: What is the impact of AI companionship on nonverbal social skills and empathy over time, using a longitudinal design that combines validated survey scales with behavioral assessments (e.g., coded video interactions or simulated conversations)?
- Rationale: AI companionship holds promise for providing accessible social interaction and practice in empathic language, but the extent to which sustained AI engagement recalibrates nonverbal social skills or human empathy remains uncertain. A longitudinal, multimodal design is needed to (a) track trajectories of self-reported empathy and nonverbal calibration, (b) quantify objective nonverbal changes, and (c) identify mechanisms and moderators of any transfer to human-human interactions. Foundational signals include short-term shifts in attachment and perceived AI empathy with active AI use, and robust evidence that perceived “feeling heard” mediates social outcomes in AI contexts, suggesting plausible pathways for longer-term change if measured with validated tools and behavioral metrics [Chandra et al., 2025; Weinstein et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023].

2) Landscape of evidence and key insights to inform the design
- Short-term AI companionship effects (self-report): A five-week longitudinal study (BU vs AU) found that active AI use increases perceived AI attachment, AI empathy, and entertainment-use motivation; however, attrition was nontrivial and effects were measured via weekly surveys rather than objective behavior with humans [Chandra et al., 2025].
- Mechanisms of AI companionship effects: Perceived listening quality and the feeling of being heard are robust mediators of social connection outcomes in AI contexts; “feeling heard” shows stronger mediation for loneliness reduction than conversational performance alone in some AI listening experiments, though longitudinal human-behavior transfer evidence remains limited [Weinstein et al., 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023].
- Validated empathy and nonverbal-skill measures: A large body of work documents validated scales for empathy (IRI, EQ, RMET, QCAE) and emphasizes combining self-report with observable behavior (e.g., nonverbal sensitivity, gaze, posture) to capture skill development over time; meta-analytic syntheses highlight the varying psychometrics across scales and contexts, underscoring the need for multi-method assessments in longitudinal AI work [de Lima & Osório, 2021].
- Nonverbal synchrony measurement: Automated and manual coding approaches (MEA, OpenPose, FACS-based cues) show convergent validity to some degree and can be used longitudinally to track dyadic nonverbal coordination and its relationship to affiliative outcomes; cross-wavelet coherence provides a time-frequency view of synchrony dynamics across sessions [Fujiwara et al., 2020; Dunbar et al., 2022; Ramseyer & Tschacher; Cao et al.; Noori et al. 2019].
- Ethically grounded and theoretically informed context: The six-component social intelligence framework (social perception, knowledge, memory, reasoning, creativity, and interaction) plus social interaction adds a blueprint for what to measure in AI companions, including nonverbal channels (Mathur et al., 2024). The literature also cautions about overclaiming AI empathy and highlights governance considerations (privacy, bias, trust) in longitudinal AI research [Mathur et al., 2024; Itzchakov et al., 2023; Shteynberg et al., 2024].

3) Design blueprint: a rigorous longitudinal study

3.1 Overall design
- Type: Longitudinal, multi-wave, randomized or quasi-experimental design with at least three to six measurement waves over 6–18 months.
- Arms:
  - High-empathy AI companion condition (text + voice + embodied cues if feasible; memory-enabled interactions; calibrated empathy prompts).
  - Control AI condition (neutral or low-empathy prompts; limited nonverbal signaling).
  - Optional human-interaction control (structured human-human sessions) to benchmark transfer to human social skills (if ethical/logistically feasible).
- Population: Adults diverse in age, gender identity, culture, AI familiarity, and baseline social functioning to maximize generalizability.

3.2 Measures (multi-method, longitudinal)
A. Validated self-report scales (administered at all waves)
- Empathy
  - Interpersonal Reactivity Index (IRI) – subscales: Perspective Taking, Empathic Concern, Fantasy, Personal Distress [Davis, 1980; 1983].
  - Empathy Quotient (EQ-60) or equivalent brief forms; consider using additional validated empathy scales (e.g., QCAE) to triangulate affective and cognitive components [de Lima & Osório, 2021].
- Social skills and social functioning
  - A validated social skills inventory or performance-based social functioning scale appropriate to adults; consider social perception/communication-skill measures.
- Loneliness and related well-being
  - UCLA Loneliness Scale or Three-Item Loneliness Scale to track loneliness trajectories as a distal outcome.
- Attitudes toward AI and autonomy
  - General AI attitudes; scales for perceived autonomy/agency in AI contexts.

B. Validated nonverbal-skill measures (intermittent or repeated)
- Nonverbal sensitivity and emotion recognition
  - Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (RMET) or Nonverbal Sensitivity (NVS) battery.
- Body language and expressivity
  - Use objective coding of body language or posture self-efficacy plus observer-rated nonverbal competence.

C. Behavioral assessments (administered at selected waves; coded video or simulated interactions)
- Nonverbal calibration during interactions
  - Gaze direction and duration toward interlocutor; postural alignment; gesture frequency and synchrony.
  - Prosody and timing (speech rate, pause intervals, turn-taking symmetry) during AI and human interactions.
- Empathic responsiveness
  - Coded responses to perceived emotions (e.g., reflective listening, acknowledgment, affective mirroring) in structured dialogues or simulated conversations.

D. Objective interaction data (AI system logs + multimodal cues)
- AI interaction metrics: duration, frequency, latency, memory recall of prior interactions; presence of simulated nonverbal cues (avatar gaze, facial expressions, voice prosody).
- Multimodal signals (optional, if resources permit): facial expression, voice prosody, gaze tracking, avatar motion.

E. Moderators/covariates
- Baseline loneliness, social anxiety, personality traits (Big Five), prior experience with AI, cultural background, education, and digital-literacy.

3.3 Behavioral coding and data quality
- Coding schemes: Adopt established frameworks (e.g., FACS for facial expressions, MEA or OpenPose for movement, RMET/NVS for nonverbal skills) and SPBCS-style behavior indicators for social presence, if feasible, ensuring alignment with the study’s aims (multimodal measurement of empathy and social skill) [Ekman & Friesen; Fujiwara et al.; Hayes et al.].
- Reliability: Train coders; report inter-rater reliability (Cohen’s Kappa and ICCs) across waves; calibrate coders periodically to prevent drift.
- Validation: Cross-validate automated digital-phenotype measures (gaze, posture, synchrony) against human-coded benchmarks on a subset of sessions.

4) Analytical plan

4.1 Primary analyses
- Growth curve modeling / linear mixed-effects models
  - Outcome trajectories: empathy (self-report and observed), nonverbal calibration metrics, loneliness.
  - Predictors: AI exposure intensity, AI condition (high vs low empathy), time, and time-by-condition interactions.
- Cross-lagged panel models
  - To examine directional relations between AI exposure, nonverbal calibration, and empathy across waves.

4.2 Mediation and moderation
- Mediation:
  - Test whether nonverbal calibration trajectories (e.g., gaze synchrony, posture alignment) mediate the effect of AI exposure on empathy outcomes.
  - Test whether perceived “feeling heard” or perceived social presence mediates effects on nonverbal calibration and empathy.
- Moderation:
  - Explore moderators such as baseline loneliness, gender identity, AI familiarity, embodiment (text-only vs. embodied avatar), and context (online vs in-person tasks).

4.3 Power, sample size, and data handling
- Power analysis: Tailored to detectable small-to-moderate effects in growth trajectories; anticipate attrition and plan for over-recruitment.
- Missing data: Use full-information maximum likelihood (FIML) or multiple imputation; sensitivity analyses for missing data patterns.

5) Mechanisms and theoretical integration

5.1 Mechanisms to test
- Parametric reductionism: AI constrains acceptable nonverbal expressions; test whether AI’s expression repertoire narrows user nonverbal options over time.
- Agency transference: Users may attribute AI agency; test whether higher AI agency correlations predict changes in user empathic behavior toward humans.
- Regulated expression: AI constraints may limit or shape expressive range; test whether changes in expressive repertoire mediate empathy outcomes.

5.2 Conceptual alignment with established theories
- Social intelligence framework (six core competencies plus social interaction) as a scaffold for selecting measurements (Mathur et al., 2024).
- Feeling-heard mediation and related constructs (Itzchakov et al., 2023; Roos et al., 2023) to understand how perception of AI empathy translates into behavior and social outcomes over time.
- Nonverbal synchrony methods (MEA, OpenPose, FACS) to quantify dyadic alignment and its relation to empathy and social functioning (Fujiwara et al., 2020; Dunbar et al., 2022).

6) Practical and ethical considerations

6.1 Practicalities
- Recruitment and retention: Design retention strategies for months-long studies; provide meaningful compensation and flexible scheduling.
- Platform heterogeneity: If multiple AI platforms are used, ensure standardized prompts and equivalent interaction contexts to enable fair comparisons.
- Data management: Secure data storage; de-identify video data; implement data governance aligned with privacy standards and ethics approvals.

6.2 Ethics and governance
- Informed consent for longitudinal video data and potential hyperscanning data if neural measures are included; ensure participants understand data sharing and potential secondary analyses.
- Privacy: Data minimization and robust encryption; clear policies about who has access to raw video, audio, and AI logs.
- Bias mitigation: Include diverse participants; pre-register analysis plans to prevent p-hacking; report robustness checks by subgroup.

7) Expected contributions and interpretations

7.1 Contributions
- Provides a rigorous, preregistered framework for longitudinal assessment of how AI companionship may shape nonverbal social skills and empathy, with explicit mediation pathways and moderation considerations.
- Delineates practical measurement bundles that combine validated human empathy scales with objective, automated nonverbal metrics, enabling robust cross-study comparability.

7.2 Interpretive cautions
- Given the heterogeneity of AI platforms and embodiments, findings may reflect idiosyncrasies of the AI design; robust replication across platforms is essential.
- The potential for negative spillovers (e.g., narrowed expressive repertoires, overreliance on AI cues) must be carefully monitored and reported.

8) Actionable recommendations for authors and practitioners

- Measurement strategy
  - Use multiple validated empathy scales (IRI, EQ, RMET/QCAE) at each wave to capture cognitive-affective and social behavior facets.
  - Include nonverbal-skill measures (NVS, RMET) and a robust battery of social skills measures to triangulate empathy outcomes.
  - Implement qualitative components (open-ended interviews) to contextualize quantitative trajectories.

- Behavioral assessment design
  - Adopt standardized video-interaction coding protocols (FACS-based facial expressions, MEA/OpenPose movement synchrony, gaze metrics, and turn-taking timing).
  - Develop or adapt a structured simulated-conversation task with AI partners to elicit consistent nonverbal responses and measure adaptability.

- Analysis and documentation
  - Pre-register analysis plans, specify modeling approaches (growth curves, mediation, cross-lagged paths), and publish code and data where permissible.
  - Report comprehensive effect sizes, confidence intervals, and model diagnostics to support meta-analytic synthesis.

- Ethics and governance
  - Implement strict privacy safeguards for longitudinal video data; provide participants with control over data scope and retention.
  - Address potential dependency effects and ensure safety nets for participants who rely heavily on AI companionship.
