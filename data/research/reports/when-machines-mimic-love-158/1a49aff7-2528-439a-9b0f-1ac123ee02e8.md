Causal Effects of Regular AI-Mediated Emotional-Support Chats on Real-World Social Outcomes: 12-Month Text-Only vs Embodied AI

Abstract
- The current evidence base suggests AI-mediated emotional-support chats can reduce depressive symptoms and psychological distress in broad samples, with effects moderated by modality (multimodal/generative AI, mobile delivery) and population characteristics. However, direct evidence linking regular AI chats to 12-month changes in real-world social skills, social anxiety, or relationship quality is sparse, and there is no solid causal comparison between text-only AI and embodied AI (robotic/haptic) modalities for these social-domain outcomes over 12 months (Li et al., 2023; Lau et al., 2025). Longitudinal, modality-comparative trials are needed to establish causal pathways, mechanisms (learning, affect regulation), and substitution vs. complement effects with real-world social experience (Volpato et al., 2025; Qin & Wang, 2024).
- Existing longitudinal work points to nuanced effects: AI-based virtual companionship frequency can reduce online social anxiety but may increase offline social anxiety; emotional expression mediates these associations; mindfulness buffers adverse effects (Xie & Wang, 2024). In short, AI can support certain affective processes without guaranteeing improvements in real-world social functioning, especially over longer horizons or across distinct modalities (text vs embodied) (Xie & Wang, 2024; Fang et al., 2025).
- This report synthesizes that evidence, identifies critical gaps for a 12-month adjudication of text-only vs embodied AI, and proposes a rigorous study design with validated social-outcome measures, causal modeling approaches, and practical guidance for researchers and developers.

1) Introduction: What is known and what remains uncertain
- Research question framing
  - Core question: What are the causal effects of regular AI-mediated emotional-support chats on real-world social skill development, social anxiety, and relationship quality over 12 months, comparing text-only AI with embodied AI (robotic/haptic) interactions?
  - Key challenge: Disentangling whether AI serves as a substitute for or a complement to real-world social experiences over a year, and determining whether embodiment adds value for social skills and relationship quality beyond text-based interfaces (Volpato et al., 2025; Qiu et al., 2024).
- Snapshot of the current evidence
  - Text- and voice-based AI interventions show modest to moderate benefits for depressive symptoms and distress (post-intervention), with effects more pronounced for multimodal/generative AI and mobile delivery, and larger in clinical/subclinical or older populations; effects on broader well-being are mixed (Li et al., 2023; Lau et al., 2025) [Li et al., 2023; Lau et al., 2025].
  - Longitudinal evidence on social-domain outcomes (social skills, social anxiety, relationship quality) is sparse. The best-available longitudinal work (Xie & Wang, 2024) links AI-mediated virtual companionship frequency to online/offline social anxiety differentially, with emotional expression as a mediator and mindfulness as a moderator; however, this work does not address 12-month trajectories or modality comparisons (text vs embodied AI) and uses two time points (approximately 4 months apart).
  - Embodiment literature indicates potential social-cue advantages of robot/haptic interfaces, but causal, long-term data comparing embodied vs text AI on social functioning are not yet established (Lim et al., 2019; Scoglio et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023).
- Conceptual scaffolding for mechanisms
  - Mechanisms: social learning and skill rehearsal (AI practice transfers to real-world social behaviors), affect regulation and emotion regulation, perceived social support and warmth/empathetic engagement from AI, and trust/presence in AI as contextual moderators (Makridakis, 2017; He et al., 2019; Volpato et al., 2025).
  - Moderators to consider: baseline social anxiety and skills, personality traits (e.g., attachment style, trust propensity), cultural context, platform/Delivery modality, degree of embodiment, and the AI’s perceived empathy and warmth (Volpato et al., 2025; Qin & Wang, 2024).

2) Quantitative findings that bear on the question (inline citations)
- General AI-chat interventions (mental health, broader social-emotional outcomes)
  - Depression and distress: AI-based conversational agents yield moderate improvements in depressive symptoms (g ≈ 0.64; 95% CI 0.17–1.12) and distress (g ≈ 0.70; 95% CI 0.18–1.22); no consistent significant benefit for overall well-being (g ≈ 0.32; 95% CI −0.13 to 0.78). Effects are larger for multimodal/generative AI integrated with mobile apps and in clinical/subclinical or elderly populations. (Li et al., 2023)
  - Practical implications: modality richness and platform integration matter for outcomes; effects on social functioning domains are not consistently reported in these syntheses (Li et al., 2023; Lau et al., 2025).
- Longitudinal social-outcome evidence and mechanisms
  - Online vs offline social anxiety in AI interactions: Higher frequency of AI companionship reduces online social anxiety but increases offline social anxiety; emotional expression mediates these links; mindfulness acts as a protective moderator. This two-wave study provides directional associations but not a 12-month causal trajectory, and it does not contrast text-only vs embodied AI (Xie & Wang, 2024).
- Embodiment and 12-month outcomes
  - There is no conclusive, published long-term (12-month) causal evidence directly comparing text-only AI to embodied AI for social skills, social anxiety, or relationship quality. The embodied AI literature presents plausible mechanisms (nonverbal cues, presence) that could influence social learning and affect regulation, but robust 12-month data remain lacking (Lim et al., 2019; Scoglio et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023).
- Cross-domain methodological considerations
  - Causal moderated mediation frameworks are available to decompose effects into direct, indirect (mediated by learning/affect regulation), and moderated pathways, with emphasis on longitudinal data and heterogeneity across individuals (Qin & Wang, 2024; moderate.mediation R package). This is highly relevant for designing and analyzing a 12-month study with multiple mediators and moderators.
- Substitution vs complement in social contexts
  - Theoretical work and some empirical work indicate AI can substitute for task-oriented human interactions but may complement social experiences in social contexts; conditioning by context, trust, and embodiment is critical (Qiu et al., 2024; Malfacini, 2025). This frames how a 12-month study should model real-world social engagement as an outcome.

3) Gaps and how they map to your 12-month, modality-comparative question
- Direct 12-month causal data for social skills, social anxiety, and relationship quality: Absent. Most trials focus on mood-related outcomes or short-term psychosocial indicators; no consistently reported long-term social-domain endpoints with modality contrasts (text vs embodied AI) (Li et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2025; Lau et al., 2025).
- Modality-specific causal evidence for embodiment: Absent. No robust long-term trials isolate embodied AI vs text-only AI on social skills or relationship quality; many embodied AI studies are exploratory or cross-sectional in naturalistic settings (Lim et al., 2019; Scoglio et al., 2019).
- Measurement validity for social outcomes in AI interventions: The psychometric quality of social-skills-related measures has known gaps in some contexts (Cordier et al., 2015); ensuring invariance and cross-cultural validity is crucial in multinational or culturally diverse samples (Cordier et al., 2015).
- Trust and socio-technical factors: Conceptual work emphasizes trust as a core moderator/mediator and highlights the need for longitudinal measures of trust in AI emotional support; this area is underdeveloped empirically (Volpato et al., 2025).

4) Proposed study design to answer the question (core elements)
- Overall design
  - A 12-month, multi-arm randomized controlled trial with at least three arms:
    1) Text-only AI-mediated emotional-support chats
    2) Embodied AI-mediated emotional-support interactions (robotic/haptic) with equivalent therapeutic content
    3) Control condition (e.g., waitlist or a standard-care social skills program)
  - Optional fourth arm: human-delivered emotional-support comparison to benchmark AI effects against human support.
  - Follow-up assessments at baseline, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months; consider an additional 18-month maintenance follow-up if feasible.
- Primary outcomes (direct social-domain endpoints)
  - Real-world social skill development: validated instruments such as the Social Skills Improvement System (SSIS), the HCSBS (Highly-Cited Social Skills Battery), or PKBS-2; include observer-rated or peer-rated components when possible.
  - Social anxiety: validated scales such as Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale (LSAS) or SPAI-C, with context-sensitive versions to capture online vs offline anxiety if feasible.
  - Relationship quality: dyadic measures when applicable (e.g., Dyadic Adjustment Scale, Quality of Relationships Inventory) and partner/close other reports where feasible.
- Mediators and moderators
  - Mediators: learning/social-cognitive skill acquisition (e.g., problem-solving in social contexts, perspective-taking), affect regulation (emotion regulation strategies, physiological arousal in social situations), perceived social connectedness with the AI, and perceived empathy from the AI.
  - Moderators: baseline social anxiety and skills; trait attachment; trust propensity; cultural background; embodiment intensity (presence, co-presence, tactile feedback); platform (messaging app vs dedicated interface); usage dose (frequency, duration).
- Mechanistic modeling
  - Use causal moderated mediation analyses to test whether changes in mediators (learning, affect regulation) explain AI-induced changes in social outcomes, and whether these pathways differ by modality (text vs embodied) and baseline characteristics (Qin & Wang, 2024; moderate.mediation package).
- Design considerations to guard against substitution risk
  - Include measures of real-world social exposure and opportunities to engage offline; model substitution effects explicitly (e.g., real-world social activity time as a covariate and potential mediator).
- Safety, ethics, and governance
  - Pre-registered protocols, data privacy protections, and escalation protocols for distress signals; safety oversight for embodied AI to mitigate physical-haptic risks and ensure safe interaction design.
- Sample and power
  - Estimate sample size to detect small-to-moderate effects on social outcomes with 12-month follow-up, accounting for attrition. Consider stratification by baseline social anxiety/skills to ensure sufficient subgroup power.
- Data and analysis plan
  - Mixed-effects longitudinal models for growth trajectories; cross-lagged panel models to assess temporal precedence among AI exposure, mediators, and social outcomes; structural equation modeling for mediation; sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding.
- Practical AI design implications
  - Embodiment design should emphasize behavioral congruence (empathy, authenticity, robustness) while carefully calibrating social presence to avoid over-reliance or misperceived intimacy (Volpato et al., 2025).
  - Ethical and trust considerations: assess and monitor trust in AI as a dynamic mediator/moderator; implement participatory auditing and privacy safeguards (Volpato et al., 2025; Li et al., 2023).

5) Measurement strategy and psychometrics
- Recommended social-outcome measures (with psychometric considerations)
  - Social skills: SSIS, HCSBS, PKBS-2 (reported as robust by psychometric reviews; ensure measurement invariance across waves and cultures) (Cordier et al., 2015).
  - Social anxiety: LSAS or SPAI with longitudinal validity; consider domain-specific adaptations for online/offline contexts as needed (Xie & Wang, 2024; Li et al., 2023).
  - Relationship quality: multi-informant scales such as Dyadic Adjustment Scale or QRI; include partner/close-other ratings to reduce mono-method bias (Li et al., 2023; Malfacini, 2025).
  - Mediators: validated emotion regulation scales (e.g., DERS), social-cognitive skill tasks, and perceived social support/warmth from the AI.
  - Trust and presence: develop or adopt AI-trust in emotional-support contexts measures; include social presence and perceived empathy scales for AI/embodied agents (Volpato et al., 2025).
- Methodological considerations
  - Ensure measurement invariance across time points and modalities; pre-register plans for testing measurement equivalence.
  - Include objective or behavioral proxies where feasible (e.g., observed social interactions, real-world social activity logs) to triangulate self-reports.

6) Statistical and analytic plan (high-level)
- Primary analyses
  - Compare neural- or cognition-grounded social outcomes across the three arms at 12 months using mixed-effects models, controlling for baseline scores.
  - Test mediation by learning/affect regulation, with AI modality as a moderator in a moderated mediation framework.
- Secondary analyses
  - Growth-curve analysis to model trajectories of social skills, social anxiety, and relationship quality over time; identify nonlinearities or plateaus.
  - Subgroup analyses by baseline social anxiety/skills, age, gender, culture, and trust propensity.
- Robustness and sensitivity
  - Pre-registration of all hypotheses and planned analyses; conduct sensitivity analyses for attrition, noncompliance, and potential unmeasured confounding (e.g., using causal mediation approaches described by Qin & Wang, 2024).
- Missing data
  - Use modern missing-data techniques (e.g., multiple imputation, full-information maximum likelihood) to handle longitudinal dropout and item nonresponse.

7) Practical and ethical considerations for researchers and practitioners
- Privacy, safety, and governance
  - Robust data governance, clear consent for longitudinal data collection, and transparent disclosure of AI capabilities and limitations.
  - Safety monitoring for distress or dysregulated affect; escalation to human professionals when appropriate.
- Equity and access
  - Ensure diverse samples (age, culture, language, socioeconomic status) to improve generalizability; consider language adaptation and cross-cultural validity of measures.
- Embodiment considerations
  - If employing embodied AI, standardize embodiment levels (e.g., identical to start with; test dose-response by embedding varying levels of nonverbal cues) to isolate modality effects.
- Stakeholder engagement
  - Involve users, clinicians, and ethicists in design and ongoing oversight to align AI behavior with social-support goals and to maintain trust.

8) Limitations and caveats
- The current evidence base does not provide direct 12-month causal estimates for social skills, social anxiety, or relationship quality, nor does it offer definitive modality contrasts between text-only and embodied AI for these outcomes (Li et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2025; Volpato et al., 2025).
- Embodiment effects on long-term social outcomes remain theoretically plausible but empirically untested at the required scale and duration; prior work is heterogeneous and often not modality-specific or longitudinal for social-domain endpoints (Lim et al., 2019; Scoglio et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023).
- Measurement challenges exist in social-skills domains, including measurement error and cross-cultural validity; careful psychometric planning is essential (Cordier et al., 2015).

9) Actionable insights for the author (summary recommendations)
- Design and implement a 12-month, three-arm randomized trial that directly compares text-only AI with embodied AI, plus a control condition, with primary outcomes in real-world social skills, social anxiety, and relationship quality.
- Include multiple waves (baseline, 3, 6, 9, 12 months) and consider a 18-month maintenance check to examine durability.
- Employ robust mediation/moderation analyses to test learning/affect regulation as mechanisms and embodiment/presence as moderators; use contemporary causal-mediation frameworks (Qin & Wang, 2024) and software (moderate.mediation in R) for analysis.
- Use validated, psychometrically strong social-functioning instruments (SSIS, HCSBS, PKBS-2; LSAS/SPAI for social anxiety; Dyadic Adjustment/Quality of Relationships Inventory for relationship quality), ensuring measurement invariance and cross-cultural validity where applicable (Cordier et al., 2015).
- Incorporate AI trust and social presence measures as key mediators/moderators to illuminate how long-term relationships with AI influence social functioning (Volpato et al., 2025).
- Plan for ethical safeguards, transparency, and governance, including privacy protections and escalation pathways for user distress or misalignment between AI support and real-world social needs (Volpato et al., 2025; Office of the Surgeon General, 2023).
- Prepare for substitution/complement analyses by collecting data on real-world social exposure and offline activity; the design should explicitly test whether AI usage displaces or augments real-world social engagement (Qiu et al., 2024).
- If embodied AI shows promise, pilot multiple embodiment levels to identify dose-response relationships and avoid confounded novelty effects; consider hybrid human–AI supervision to optimize engagement and safety (Lehtimaki et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023).
- Register hypotheses and analysis plans in advance; report effect sizes with confidence intervals and conduct sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounders.

10) Synthesis and conclusion
- The literature indicates a plausible causal chain in AI-mediated emotional-support contexts: AI interactions can influence emotional processing and social-cognition processes that, in turn, may affect real-world social outcomes. However, robust, long-term causal evidence for 12-month social skills, social anxiety, and relationship quality, specifically contrasting text-only versus embodied AI modalities, is currently lacking. The strongest existing longitudinal evidence points to context-specific effects on online vs offline social anxiety, with emotional expression and mindfulness as key mechanisms, but without an embodied-AI contrast or 12-month horizon (Xie & Wang, 2024).
- A rigorous 12-month, modality-comparative trial is warranted to determine whether embodied AI provides incremental benefits for social outcomes or whether AI acts as a substitute or complement to real-world social experiences. The proposed design emphasizes mediation by learning and affect regulation, moderation by embodiment/presence and individual characteristics, and direct measurement of social skills and relationship quality using validated instruments, along with rigorous causal modeling.
- The design should also attend to trust dynamics, safety, and governance, given the evolving nature of AI emotional support and societal concerns about reliance, privacy, and the quality of human relationships (Volpato et al., 2025; O’Neil, 2016; Zuboff, 2019).
