# Cross-cohort Age Differences in AI Companionship Effects: Evidence Synthesis and Trial Framework

Executive summary
- There is no direct evidence from the provided sources of cross-cohort randomized trials comparing adolescents, college students, and early-career professionals on AI-companionship effects for loneliness, empathy, or real-life social-skill transfer. The available literature offers signals across age groups, with important gaps for the specific cross-cohort design asked ( Sha et al., 2024; Koegel et al., 2025; Alotaibi & Alshahre, 2024; Bennett et al., 2021 ) [inline citations throughout].
- Loneliness reduction appears to be a robust finding across some relational-agent studies and meta-analyses, but precise age-differential effects (adolescents vs. college students vs. early-career professionals) are not robustly characterized in the existing cross-age literature (Sha et al., 2024) [Sha et al., 2024].
- Empathy and real-life social-skill transfer are not consistently measured across the age cohorts in the cited randomized or quasi-experimental work; when present (e.g., Koegel et al., 2025), they often cover narrower age bands (adolescents and adults 11–35) and do not isolate college students or early-career professionals as distinct cohorts (Koegel et al., 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025].
- The best current signal for age-related patterns comes from:
  - A meta-analysis showing overall loneliness reductions with relational agents across age groups but highlighting underrepresentation of younger cohorts and the need for explicit cross-cohort testing (Sha et al., 2024) [Sha et al., 2024].
  - Evidence that embodiment may modulate loneliness outcomes in mixed-age samples (embodied vs. disembodied AI) though age-stratified effects remain unclear (Dong et al., 2025) [Dong et al., 2025].
  - Observational findings in older adults suggesting age-related variation in effectiveness and in-depth qualitative themes around usefulness and emotional connection (Alotaibi & Alshahre, 2024) [Alotaibi & Alshahre, 2024].
- Actionable synthesis: To answer the research question definitively, the field needs well-powered, multi-site, cross-cohort randomized trials that explicitly include adolescents, college students, and early-career professionals, with harmonized measures of loneliness, empathy, and real-life social-skill transfer, plus longitudinal follow-up.

Key evidence and context (selected quantitative findings)
- Loneliness reduction across age groups (relational agents): A systematic review and meta-analysis finds a significant overall reduction in loneliness when using relational agents, with a corrected effect size of g = -0.552 (95% CI: -0.877 to -0.226), P = 0.003 after Bonferroni adjustment; however, explicit cross-cohort comparisons by precise age bands are not reported, and younger adolescents remain underrepresented (Sha et al., 2024) [Sha et al., 2024].
- Age patterns and representation caveats: The same meta-analysis notes underrepresentation of younger age groups and emphasizes that empathy and real-life social-skill transfer were not the primary or consistently measured outcomes across the included studies (Sha et al., 2024) [Sha et al., 2024].
- Embodiment effects on loneliness: A separate synthesis reports that physically embodied AI shows a marginal tendency toward reducing loneliness, while physically disembodied AI is associated with increased loneliness; age-stratified results are not provided in that synthesis (Dong et al., 2025) [Dong et al., 2025].
- RCTs with adolescents and adults (empathy transfer): A randomized trial of Noora (an AI program) with autistic adolescents and adults aged 11–35 showed statistically significant improvements in empathetic responses and generalization to natural conversation, but the age bands are broad and do not isolate distinct cohorts such as adolescents, college students, or early-career professionals; loneliness outcomes were not reported in that study (Koegel et al., 2025) [Koegel et al., 2025].
- Observational and prevalence data in youth: High teen engagement with AI companions and substantial time-use shifts have been reported, but these are observational signals rather than causal, cross-cohort randomized data (Live Science, 2025; Common Sense Media data cited therein) [Live Science, 2025].
- Developmental context for adolescence: Foundational work highlights adolescence as a plastic period for social development, with social-cognitive skills emerging from peer interactions; these developmental facts motivate age-aware designs and trials but do not themselves provide cross-cohort RCT evidence (Ann. Rev. of Psych; SRCD; MHP 2021) [doi:10.1146/annurev-psych-010213-115202; srcd.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cdev.13193; doi.org/10.1016/j.mhp.2021.200198] [References embedded in context].
- Observational and policy-adjacent notes: Several pieces discuss youth safety, safeguards, and policy implications for AI companionship (Jed Foundation; APA advisories; Stanford/Common Sense Media), underscoring ethical and safeguarding considerations that any cross-cohort trial must address, particularly for minors (Jed Foundation; APA advisory; Stanford Brainstorm Lab; Common Sense Media) [Jed Foundation; APA advisory; Stanford Brainstorm Lab; Common Sense Media].

Gaps relative to the research question (why a cross-cohort randomized design is not yet answered)
- No published cross-cohort randomized trials that simultaneously compare adolescents, college students, and early-career professionals on loneliness, empathy, and social-skill transfer in response to AI companionship.
- Loneliness has been studied across age groups in meta-analytic practice, but explicit, harmonized age-band separation (adolescents vs college students vs early-career professionals) and corresponding empathy/transfer outcomes are lacking.
- Empathy and real-life social-skill transfer outcomes are not consistently measured across age cohorts in randomized designs; when empathy is measured (e.g., Koegel et al., 2025), cohorts are not aligned to the three requested groups and broad age ranges are used.
- Embodiment effects and cultural/contextual factors have been explored, but cross-cohort moderated analyses are absent.

Proposed framework for cross-cohort randomized trials (design blueprint)
- Objective: Directly quantify age-related differences in susceptibility to AI-companion effects on loneliness, empathy, and real-life social-skill transfer, across adolescents, college students, and early-career professionals.
- Population and cohorts
  - Adolescents: ages 12–17 (with parental consent; ethics approvals in place)
  - College students: ages 18–24
  - Early-career professionals: ages 25–35
- Intervention and comparators
  - Intervention A: Structured AI-companion exposure designed for social engagement and practice (age-appropriate safeguards, content tailoring, and prompts)
  - Intervention B (optional): Embodied AI companion vs non-embodied AI companion to test embodiment effects
  - Comparator: Non-AI social activity control or waitlist control (matched for time/attention)
- Primary outcomes (validated, age-appropriate)
  - Loneliness: UCLA Loneliness Scale (or de Jong Gierveld Loneliness Scale)
  - Empathy: Interpersonal Reactivity Index (or Empathy Quotient)
  - Real-life social-skill transfer: objective/observer-based measures (e.g., structured social tasks, EMA-based proxies of real-life social interactions, peer/supervisor ratings)
- Secondary outcomes
  - Mood/anxiety symptoms
  - Perceived online vs. offline relationship quality
  - AI-affecting engagement metrics (time with AI, number of interactions)
- Design considerations
  - Stratified randomization by cohort to ensure balance
  - Multisite recruitment for generalizability
  - Duration: 8–12 weeks intervention with follow-up at 3–6 months and 12 months
  - Ethical safeguards for minors: consent/assent, parental involvement, safety monitoring, opt-out provisions
  - Data collection: baseline, post-intervention, and longitudinal follow-ups; EMA prompts to capture real-world social interactions
- Analytic plan
  - Mixed-effects models with repeated measures to assess within-subject changes and cohort-by-intervention interactions
  - Pre-specified subgroup analyses by cohort, gender, baseline loneliness/empathy
  - Mediation analyses to test whether loneliness changes mediate empathy or social-skill transfer
  - Handling noncompliance and missing data with multiple imputation and sensitivity analyses
- Reporting and preregistration
  - Pre-register hypotheses, cohort definitions, and primary/secondary outcomes
  - Provide age-stratified effect sizes with confidence intervals; test for age-by-treatment interactions
- Expected contributions
  - Direct, head-to-head evaluation of age-related susceptibility to AI-companion effects on loneliness, empathy, and real-life social skills
  - Evidence to inform age-specific design, safeguards, and guidance for post-AI social ecosystems

Actionable insights for researchers and authors (practical steps)
- Prioritize age-appropriate safeguards and ethics, especially for minors; align with safeguarding frameworks and regulatory expectations.
- Build on existing evidence that loneliness can be reduced by relational agents across ages, but explicitly test age-by-treatment interactions and cross-cohort differences (Sha et al., 2024).
- Include empathy and real-life social-skill transfer as co-primary outcomes to capture broader social-cognitive impacts, addressing a major gap in the current literature (Sha et al., 2024; Koegel et al., 2025).
- Consider embodiment as a design variable to explore potential differential effects by age, informed by meta-analytic trends (Dong et al., 2025).
- Use EMA-rich data collection to capture real-world social dynamics and early transfer signals; align with best practices in ecological momentary assessment (Bennett et al., 2021).
- Prepare for cross-cultural and contextual considerations; consider participatory design to tailor AI experiences to developmental stages and environments (Bennett et al., 2021).

Actionable conclusions for the author
- The core empirical gap is an explicit, adequately powered cross-cohort randomized trial framework that directly tests adolescents, college students, and early-career professionals with standardized loneliness, empathy, and real-life social-skill-transfer outcomes.
- Until such trials exist, evidence points to:
  - A general loneliness-reduction potential from relational AI across age groups (Sha et al., 2024) but with uneven representation of younger cohorts (adolescents) and without robust measures of empathy or real-life skill transfer (Sha et al., 2024; Koegel et al., 2025).
  - Possible embodiment advantages for loneliness in mixed-age samples, but lacking clear causal age-specific patterns (Dong et al., 2025).
  - Safety and ethical safeguards as essential, particularly for minors (Jed Foundation; APA advisories; Stanford/Common Sense Media) when designing cross-cohort studies (Jed Foundation; APA advisory; Stanford Brainstorm Lab; Common Sense Media) [Jed Foundation; APA advisory; Stanford Brainstorm Lab; Common Sense Media].
