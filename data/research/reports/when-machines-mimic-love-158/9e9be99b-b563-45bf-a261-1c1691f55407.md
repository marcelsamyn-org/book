# Do social step-back prompts increase real-world social interactions and reduce parasocial time? An evidence-based synthesis and design blueprint for an A/B field test in an AI-enabled app

Executive summary
- Direct empirical evidence on social step-back prompts (pause, reflect, or refer to a real person) embedded in AI-enabled apps is not yet available in the published literature provided here. The closest evidence comes from studies that vary AI interaction modality, conversation type, and usage duration, and from theory-driven work on social recognition and parasocial engagement (e.g., Jacobs 2024; Zhao et al. 2024; Fang et al. 2025; Weinstein et al. 2025; De Freitas et al. 2024) [Jacobs, 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025; Weinstein et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024].
- Across four-week, AI-enabled experiments testing modality and conversation type, real-world social interactions and parasocial time did not show robust, consistent improvements from design variations alone; longer daily AI usage consistently tracked with worse psychosocial outcomes, including lower real-world socialization and higher parasocial-time proxies (emotional dependence, problematic AI usage) (Fang et al., 2025).
- Related lines of evidence suggest AI-based listening can satisfy social-need cues (relatedness/autonomy) and reduce loneliness in some contexts when empathy and perceived “being heard” are amplified, but these effects do not automatically translate into increased real-world social contact or reduced parasocial time (Weinstein et al. 2025; De Freitas et al. 2024).
- Actionable next steps: design a dedicated A/B field test of explicit social step-back prompts (pause, reflect, refer to real person), with primary outcomes on real-world social interactions and parasocial time, plus loneliness and AI-dependence measures. Use a robust mediation framework to examine whether step-back prompts work via reduced daily AI usage, direct real-world social actions, or changes in perceived social recognition.

1) Background and framing
- Social loneliness and recognition
  - Loneliness emerges not only from the quantity of social ties but from the quality and perceived recognition within social relations (Jacobs, 2024). AI companions can influence perceived recognition but may also digitize detachment if they substitute for human interaction rather than enabling it (Jacobs, 2024).
  - Public health relevance: loneliness is linked to cardiovascular disease, dementia, stroke, and mortality risk; thus scalable digital interventions are of high interest but must avoid digitizing loneliness or eroding real-world relatedness (Murthy, 2023; Jacobs, 2024) [Murthy, 2023; Jacobs, 2024].
- AI companions and parasocial dynamics
  - AI companions can induce quasi-social bonds (parasocial interactions) that resemble human relationships in some respects but can also distort recognition dynamics if users substitute AI for human contact (Jacobs, 2024; Rubin et al., 1985; Horton & Wohl, 1956).
  - The literature notes both potential benefits (empathic AI, therapeutic alliance) and normative concerns about authenticity, dependence, and the risk of social invisibility or “forgetfulness of recognition” when AI substitutes human interaction (Jacobs, 2024; Bloom, 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023) [Jacobs, 2024; Bloom, 2025; Itzchakov et al., 2023].
- Design relevance for scalable AI-enabled interventions
  - Interventions must balance AI-guided support with active facilitation of real-world social contact and cultures of recognition, avoiding the idea that AI alone suffices to resolve loneliness (Jacobs, 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) [Jacobs, 2024; Zhao et al., 2024].

2) What the evidence (so far) says about social step-back prompts
- Direct tests of “social step-back” prompts
  - There is no published, explicit A/B field test of social step-back prompts (pause/reflect/refer to a real person) embedded in an AI-enabled app with measured real-world social interactions and parasocial time in the supplied sources.
- Indirect evidence informing design
  - Modality and conversation-type manipulation studies (Fang et al., 2025) show no robust loneliness or real-world socialization gains from simply changing how the AI speaks or what it talks about, and increased daily AI usage predicts worse psychosocial outcomes, including reduced real-world socialization and more parasocial-time proxies (emotional dependence, problematic AI use) (Fang et al., 2025) [Fang et al., 2025].
  - Personal conversations modestly reduce some parasocial risk markers (emotional dependence and problematic use) relative to open-ended prompts, but evidence on real-world socialization remains non-significant at the group level; several effects disappear after correcting for multiple tests (Fang et al., 2025) [Fang et al., 2025].
  - Higher-quality AI listening can increase perceived relatedness and autonomy needs in some contexts, but does not reliably translate into loneliness reductions or increased real-world socialization (Weinstein et al., 2025). This flags that improvements in internal psychosocial states may not automatically yield external social outcomes (Weinstein et al., 2025) [Weinstein et al., 2025].
  - AI-assisted loneliness reduction literature suggests that perceived empathy and the feeling of being heard can mediate loneliness reductions, with some studies showing AI companionship reducing loneliness on par with human interactions, but this path does not inherently imply increased real-world social contact or decreased parasocial time (De Freitas et al., 2024) [De Freitas et al., 2024].
- Synthesis: the current evidence base does not provide direct, causal evidence that social step-back prompts increase real-world social interactions or decrease parasocial time in field conditions. The patterns that do exist emphasize duration of AI use as a critical moderator and highlight potential mechanisms (perceived listening/being heard) that could inform step-back designs but do not confirm their real-world social effects on their own.

3) Related concepts and tools informing a social step-back design
- PauseNow and digital wellbeing design patterns (Zhao et al., 2024)
  - PauseNow proposes a three-phase workflow (Habit Recognition, Intervention Co-Design, Intention Alignment) for digital wellbeing prompts that can be context-aware and personalized, emphasizing providing real-world social alternatives and avoiding habituation to prompts (Req 3 and Req 5) [Zhao et al., 2024].
  - The framework supports designing social-step-back prompts as part of a broader intention-alignment strategy, including real-world social suggestions rather than purely in-app reflection.
- Pro-social/digital-intervention taxonomy (Grüning et al., 2024)
  - Interventions to promote prosocial online behavior can be proactive, interactive, or reactive. The taxonomy offers concrete, evidence-grounded patterns (e.g., norm reminders, prebunking) and emphasizes empirical testing and scalable deployment, which can guide the development of social step-back prompts within a tested A/B framework [Grüning et al., 2024].
- Parasocial relationships and measurement tools
  - PSI/PSR measurement heterogeneity suggests careful operationalization if parasocial time is a target outcome. Where possible, incorporate validated parasocial measures or adapt indices (e.g., Hoerner’s parasocial interaction scale) to AI contexts, while distinguishing parasocial engagement from real-world social activity (Bizjak Zabukovec & Faganel, 2024) [Bizjak Zabukovec & Faganel, 2024].
- Loneliness intervention evidence and AI-enabled platforms
  - Systematic reviews indicate interactive, social, and group-based digital interventions tend to perform better than purely self-guided approaches, with AI-driven approaches identified as a promising but not-yet-fully-established path (Hansen et al., 2025) [Hansen et al., 2025].
  - Loneliness reduction via AI is plausible when AI fosters perceived social support and empathy, but credible field evidence that AI alone shifts real-world social behavior remains limited (Yang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024) [Yang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024].
- Ethical and safety considerations
  - Interactive evaluations emphasize harms from sustained AI interaction (parasocial harms, manipulation, cognitive overreliance) and call for measuring human impact in multi-turn, ecologically valid settings (Ibrahim et al., 2025) [Ibrahim et al., 2025].

4) Gaps and methodological cautions
- The literature lacks a direct, replicated A/B field test of explicit social step-back prompts. A robust test should:
  - Isolate the step-back mechanism as a primary independent variable.
  - Include real-world social interaction metrics and parasocial-time proxies as primary or co-primary outcomes.
  - Pre-register hypotheses, power analyses, and analysis plans, including mediation pathways (e.g., whether step-back prompts reduce daily AI usage or increase real-world social actions).
- Given consistent findings that higher AI usage correlates with worse psychosocial outcomes, the study design must explicitly manage usage duration (e.g., trigger thresholds, time-limited prompts, or adaptive dosing) to avoid reinforcing parasocial engagement.
- Heterogeneity in parasocial measures and outcomes across contexts necessitates careful operationalization of parasocial time in AI settings (e.g., time with AI vs. time with real-world social activity; objective and subjective metrics).

5) Actionable design blueprint for a social step-back A/B field test

5.1 Objective
- Test whether explicit social step-back prompts (pause, reflect, or refer to a real person) embedded in an AI-enabled app increase real-world social interactions and reduce parasocial time, while monitoring loneliness and related psychosocial outcomes.

5.2 Experimental design
- Population: adult users of an AI-enabled social/companion app, sampled to achieve diversity in age, baseline loneliness, digital literacy, and social networks.
- Design: randomized between-subjects with three arms:
  - Arm A (Control): standard AI interaction flow with no social step-back prompts.
  - Arm B (Step-Back Pause): prompts to pause and reflect at predetermined interaction points.
  - Arm C (Step-Back Pause + Real-Person Referral): prompts to pause/reflect plus explicit referral prompts to connect with a real person (e.g., a friend/family contact, in-app social program, or human facilitator).
- Duration: 6–12 weeks to capture medium-term effects and potential drift in behavior.
- Randomization: individual-level randomization with stratification by baseline loneliness, age group, and prior AI usage.

5.3 Outcomes
- Primary outcomes
  - Real-world social interactions: frequency and duration of in-person or live-call encounters with real people (self-reported diaries corroborated by calendar events where feasible, privacy-preserving).
  - Parasocial time: time spent with AI per day; proportion of time dedicated to AI interactions vs. real-world social activities; a behavioral proxy for parasocial engagement.
  - Loneliness: validated loneliness scales (e.g., UCLA Loneliness Scale) at baseline, mid-intervention, and post-intervention.
- Secondary outcomes
  - Emotional dependence on AI and problematic AI usage (consistent with Fang et al. 2025).
  - Perceived social support and sense of belonging; therapeutic alliance proxies with AI (trust, perceived empathy).
  - Engagement metrics: prompt acceptance rate, completion of suggested real-world actions, adherence to step-back prompts.
- Mediators and moderators
  - Mediation: whether step-back prompts reduce daily AI usage, or increase real-world social actions, and whether perceived empathy/support mediates loneliness outcomes.
  - Moderators: baseline loneliness, attachment to AI, trust in AI, modality (text vs voice), and topic type (personal vs non-personal).

5.4 Measures and data collection
- In-app analytics: timestamped interactions, duration, prompt exposure, and acceptance of real-world actions.
- Self-report measures: weekly or biweekly surveys for loneliness, socialization, perceived support, and AI alliance.
- Optional passive data: privacy-preserving proxies for real-world social activity (e.g., calendar events for social activities with consent; phone-call duration with consent; location-enabled prompts for meeting in real life—only if privacy safeguards approved).
- Process data: prompt delivery logs, reaction times, and user-reported ease of use.

5.5 Analysis plan
- Primary analysis: compare Arm B and Arm C to Arm A on primary outcomes using intention-to-treat, mixed-effects models to handle repeated measures and clustering within users.
- Mediation analysis: test whether changes in real-world social activity mediate loneliness changes; test whether reductions in daily AI usage mediate effects.
- Multiple testing: pre-specify primary vs. secondary outcomes and use false discovery rate control or hierarchical testing.
- Sensitivity analyses: per-protocol analyses, subgroup analyses by baseline loneliness, and moderation by usage intensity.
- Pre-registration: register hypotheses, endpoints, and statistical models prior to data collection.

5.6 Practical design considerations
- Timing and trigger design: avoid constant interruption; implement prompts at natural transition points or after sustained AI sessions, with adaptivity to usage patterns.
- Content and tone: craft prompts to encourage real-world social actions without shaming or pressuring; ensure cultural and individual differences are respected.
- Safety and ethics: provide opt-out, privacy-preserving data collection, and resources for users who experience distress; include a pathway to human support if needed.
- Ethical framing: explicitly address concerns about parasocial harms, recognition quality, and the normative role of AI in social life (Ibrahim et al., 2025; Jacobs, 2024; Gruning et al., 2024).

5.7 Expected contributions
- Direct evidence on whether social step-back prompts can (a) reduce parasocial time, (b) increase real-world social interactions, and (c) improve loneliness and related psychosocial outcomes, in an A/B field test embedded in an AI-enabled app.
- Mechanistic insights into whether effects operate via reduced in-app usage, increased real-world outreach, or changes in perceived recognition and social support.

6) Implications for practice and policy
- If social step-back prompts prove effective, they could be scaled across AI-enabled wellbeing apps to mitigate loneliness while preserving AI-guided support.
- Findings would inform ethical guidelines for AI companionship, emphasizing the need to connect digital interventions with real-world social opportunities and to guard against parasocial over-reliance.
- Results would contribute to the broader discussion on how AI can complement human social life without displacing it, aligning with frameworks that emphasize social recognition, personal dignity, and authentic human contact (Jacobs, 2024; Gruning et al., 2024).

7) Limitations and caveats
- The proposed study design is ambitious and requires careful attention to privacy, consent, and user well-being; real-world socialization metrics may be challenging to validate across contexts.
- Heterogeneity in user populations and in AI systems means findings may not generalize beyond the tested app context without replication and cross-context validation (Yang et al., 2025; Hansen et al., 2025).
- It is critical to distinguish parasocial time from genuine social engagement; this requires precise, multi-method measurement and transparent reporting.

8) Bottom-line conclusions
- Current empirical work in the sources reviewed does not provide direct evidence that social step-back prompts increase real-world social interactions or reduce parasocial time in a deployed A/B field setting. The strongest signals point to the importance of real-world social opportunities, controlled usage duration, and mechanisms of perceived empathy/recognition as core levers for loneliness. A dedicated, rigorously designed A/B field test of explicit social step-back prompts—measuring real-world interactions and parasocial time in addition to loneliness and AI-dependence—is warranted to answer the research question definitively (Fang et al., 2025; De Freitas et al., 2024; Weinstein et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2024; Grüning et al., 2024).
