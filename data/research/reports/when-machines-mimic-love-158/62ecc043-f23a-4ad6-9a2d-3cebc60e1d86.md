# report.md

Calibration Interventions in AI-Assisted Social Decision-Making: Evidence on Active Recall/Reflective Prompts and “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-Style Calibration for Real-Life Conflicts

Executive summary
- Question addressed: Do active recall/reflective prompts and “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration interventions improve decision quality and reduce over-trust in AI-provided social advice during real-life conflicts?
- What the evidence in the literature shows (high level):
  - Active recall/reflective prompts (a broad family including cognitive forcing, probe-based prompting, reflective prompts, and similar strategies) consistently show potential to reduce overreliance and improve decision quality in AI-assisted tasks, though strength and generalizability vary by domain, task difficulty, and user characteristics. Examples include cognitive forcing functions that disrupt System 1 heuristics (Buçinca, Malaya, & Gajos, 2021), probe-based overreliance detection and personalization (Swaroop et al., 2025), and adaptive trust-calibration prompts (Okamura & Yamada, 2020) with notable trade-offs in usability and engagement (various sources cited inline).
  - “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration interventions (i.e., affective or reward-like trust signals) are not broadly evidenced in the core sources provided. The closest related work emphasizes transparency, confidence signaling, and metacognitive cues as calibration mechanisms (e.g., confidence displays, calibration labels, metacognitive signaling in AI) but not a dopamine-inspired labeling scheme with demonstrated field benefits in real-life conflicts (Gerke, 2023; Li et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2024).
  - Across real-life-like decision contexts (clinical, legal, negotiation, social conflict scenarios), results point to the importance of adaptive, context-aware calibration strategies that go beyond “more explanations” to actively engage users’ analytic processing, metacognition, and calibration of trust (various sources).
- Actionable takeaway for researchers/authors: When designing AI-assisted decision systems in social conflict contexts, prioritize calibration approaches that actively engage users (reflection, verification, and controlled friction) and pair them with transparent reliability signals. If exploring dopamine-like labeling, treat it as a potential augmentation to metacognitive calibration signals and validate in controlled experiments before deployment in real conflicts.

Scope and structure of the evidence base (key sources and their relevance)
- Active recall/reflective prompts and cognitive forcing functions
  - Buçinca, Malaya, & Gajos (2021) demonstrate that cognitive forcing functions (CFFs) such as checklists, diagnostic time-outs, and explicit prompts to consider alternatives reduce AI overreliance relative to simple XAI approaches and no-AI baselines. Moderation effects: benefits are larger for those higher in Need for Cognition; stronger CFFs yield greater calibration but can come at the cost of user satisfaction. This directly addresses decision quality and reliance in AI-assisted settings, though not all domains tested are “real-life conflicts” per se (Buçinca et al., 2021) [inline citation: (Buçinca, Malaya, & Gajos, 2021)].
  - Swaroop, Bučinca, Gajos, & Doshi-Velez (2025) extend active-recall-like intervention concepts with probe-based detection of overreliance and personalized AI assistance. After two probe questions, participants could be classified as overreliers with high accuracy (>90%), enabling rapid adaptation of AI support. Personalization benefited overreliant individuals; not-overreliants showed less sensitivity to prompting. This work demonstrates rapid, real-time calibration mechanisms with clear implications for real-time conflict contexts (Swaroop et al., 2025) [inline citation: (Swaroop et al., 2025)].
  - Drosos et al. (2025) investigate provocations as reflective prompts within AI-assisted knowledge work. Findings indicate provocations can stimulate reflective thinking and trust-related measures, with five UX dimensions shaping effectiveness. This provides a rigorous design-space for reflective prompts in decision workflows, though effect sizes are not specified in the cited excerpts (Drosos et al., 2025) [inline citation: (Drosos et al., 2025)].
  - Ma et al. (2023) examine cognitive–trust dynamics via correctness likelihood exploitation (CL-exploitation). Strategies that communicate and leverage human and AI correctness likelihood (Direct Display, Adaptive Workflow, Adaptive Recommendation) improve trust calibration and team performance in AI-assisted tasks; there is explicit evidence of improved calibration and outcomes, albeit in lab-like task settings (Ma et al., 2023) [inline citation: (Ma et al., 2023)].
  - Sakamoto et al. (2024) investigate reflective trust calibration in AI-driven diagnostic decision support. A calibrated trust prompt (calibration list) did not significantly improve physicians’ diagnostic accuracy in a small formative study, but calibration accuracy strongly predicted improved accuracy, highlighting that calibration quality matters. This points to reflective prompts as a promising mechanism but underscores the need for robust calibration design and larger samples (Sakamoto et al., 2024) [inline citation: (Sakamoto et al., 2024)].
- “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration interventions
  - Gerke et al. (2023) discuss nutrition-label-style labeling for AI/ML medical devices—advocating for dynamic, nutrition-label-like disclosures of data provenance, model limitations, performance, and update mechanisms. This represents a regulatory/labeling framework that could ground a dopamine-label-like calibration approach but does not itself test dopamine-like signals in decision tasks (Gerke, 2023) [inline citation: (Gerke, 2023)].
  - Li et al. (2025) on metacognitive sensitivity argue for AI confidence signaling and metacognitive indicators as calibration tools to improve human–AI decision making, including potential inversion scenarios where a less accurate AI with higher metacognitive signaling can outperform a more accurate but less discriminative AI. This framework suggests that a dopamine-label-like calibration signal could be designed as a calibrated, discriminative confidence signal, but there is no empirical “dopamine label” study reported in these sources (Li et al., 2025) [inline citation: (Li et al., 2025)].
  - The explicit, field-tested “Dopamine Nutrition Label” intervention does not appear in the core sources provided; most evidence centers on transparency signals (confidence, explanations) and calibration mechanisms, not neurochemical-inspired labeling. When exploring dopamine-like calibration, treat it as a signaling augmentation built atop calibrated confidence signals, validated in controlled experiments first (Gerke, 2023; Li et al., 2025).
- Real-life conflicts and social decision-making contexts
  - Okamura & Yamada (2020) show adaptive trust-calibration cues (TCCs) can recalibrate trust and improve discrimination (d’) in a drone-inspection task with fluctuating AI reliability; this is a concrete demonstration of adaptive cueing distinguishing when to trust AI vs. manual checks, albeit in a safety-technical domain rather than social conflict per se (Okamura & Yamada, 2020) [inline citation: (Okamura & Yamada, 2020)].
  - Lane et al. (2024) show narrative AI can boost alignment with AI recommendations and influence decision-making in field-like evaluation tasks; but narratives did not improve decision quality beyond non-narrative AI in some cases, highlighting complexities in how AI explanations influence trust and behavior (Lane et al., 2024).
  - Aligned work on trust calibration in human–AI collaboration across domains (e.g., McGrath et al., 2025; Mehta et al., 2024; Soto et al., 2023) emphasizes that calibration is multi-faceted (trust, metacognition, explanations, process visibility) and not reducible to a single prompt type or label.

Quantitative findings (key numbers and effect sizes) from the references (as reported in the sources)
- Bučinca et al. 2021: Significant reduction in AI overreliance with cognitive forcing functions; trade-off: strongest reductions came with lower subjective usability; NFC magnifies benefits. Exact effect sizes not provided in the summary here.
- Okamura & Yamada 2020: In a pothole-inspection task, adaptive TCCs produced significantly higher d’ (discriminability) than no-TCC condition; a group without TCCs did not alter behavior despite information about AI reliability. Sample: N=116; data publicly available (figshare).
- Swaroop et al. 2025: Probe-based overreliance estimation achieved >90% accuracy after two probes; personalization improved accuracy for overreliers; response-time improvements observed in Experiment 2; traits (Need for Cognition) correlated with overreliance.
- Chiang et al. 2024: Interactive devil’s advocate improved appropriate reliance in recidivism risk prediction tasks; effect sizes not specified here.
- Ma et al. 2023: Three CL-exploitation strategies (Direct Display, Adaptive Workflow, Adaptive Recommendation) increased trust calibration and team performance; no single numeric effect size reported in the provided summary.
- Li et al. 2025: Metacognitive signals (Type 2 confidence; metacognitive sensitivity) can boost joint decision performance; inversion conditions predicted where lower-accuracy AI with higher metacognitive sensitivity could outperform higher-accuracy AI with lower metacognition; quantitative metrics (e.g., AUC, meta-d’) discussed; exact numbers vary by study.
- Ariely et al. 2025 (RPE in education): Cohen’s Kappa > 0.8 with 40–60 exemplars in reflective prompt engineering; outperformed “multiple examples” prompts; implies strong alignment between AI prompts and human scoring rubrics.
- Lane et al. 2024: In field-like evaluation with screeners, AI-assisted conditions increased alignment with AI recommendations by about 19.4 percentage points; narrative AI increased alignment for rejection decisions; but overall decision quality advantage for narrative AI over black-box was not consistently observed.
- Sakamoto et al. 2024: Calibrated trust prompts did not significantly raise physicians’ diagnostic accuracy in a small formative study, but calibration accuracy strongly predicted improved diagnostic performance.
- Leichtmann et al. 2023: Explanations influence trust calibration; results depend on explanation class and domain; some explanations may increase trust without improving accuracy; design guidelines recommended.

Key domain-note on “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration
- Direct empirical evidence for a dopamine-nutrition-label-like intervention in real-world social decision contexts is not present in the cited sources. The closest analogs are:
  - Nutrition-label-style labeling for AI in medicine (Gerke, 2023) as a design/labeling framework.
  - Metacognitive signaling and calibrated confidence in Li et al. (2025) as a signaling framework that could underpin dopamine-like labeling if implemented as affective/valuation signals linked to reliability.
- Recommendation: If pursuing a dopamine-label approach, design experiments that compare:
  - Baseline AI confidence signals (Type 1) vs. augmented confidence signals that include discriminability (Type 2; metacognitive sensitivity) and risk/bidelity cues (calibration quality), and
  - Outcomes: decision quality, trust calibration metrics (ECE/diagonal calibration curves), and over-/under-reliance indicators in realistic conflict tasks.

Key actionable insights for authors and practitioners (distilled)
- Prioritize interactive calibration that requires user engagement
  - Implement cognitive forcing functions (checklists, diagnostic time-outs, explicit alternative ruling-out) at decision points to disrupt automatic AI-following and promote System 2 evaluation. Expect reductions in overreliance but be prepared for usability trade-offs; consider adaptive intensity based on NFC or engagement signals. (Bučinca et al., 2021)
- Leverage probe-based reliability assessment to personalize AI support
  - Use rapid probe questions to identify overreliance tendencies; tailor assistance accordingly (more verification prompts for high-overreliance individuals). Expect improvements in decision quality for those prone to overreliance; monitor for strategic changes in users’ AI-use patterns over time. (Swaroop et al., 2025)
- Explore reflective prompts and provocations as a design principle
  - Integrate reflective prompts (structured prompts, Socratic questioning, scenario reframing) to promote metacognition and reduce overtrust. Use standardized instruments to track reflective thinking and trust calibration; consider a multi-arm design to separate effects of prompts vs. explanations. (Drosos et al., 2025; Ariely et al., 2025)
- Ground “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration in a proven framework
  - Start with nutrition-label-style data provenance and reliability disclosures (Gerke, 2023) and augment with metacognitive signaling (Li et al., 2025). Validate whether dynamic, probabilistic confidence labels and discriminability signals reduce overtrust without inducing undertrust, using standard calibration metrics (ECE, calibration curves) and metacognitive measures (meta-d’, AUROC2).
- Design for real-life conflict contexts
  - Test in domains mirroring real conflicts: clinical decisions, negotiations, social risk judgments, and legal/ethical decision contexts. Use field-like experiments with ecological validity, and report both objective decision quality and trust calibration metrics.
- Measurement and reporting
  - Use standardized trust-calibration metrics (ECE, calibration curves), metacognitive metrics (meta-d’, AUROC2), and decision quality outcomes (accuracy, time-to-decision, etc.). Report effect sizes and CIs; preregister designs when possible.
- Ethical and usability considerations
  - Balance cognitive friction with user experience; ensure prompts do not induce fatigue or paralysis; tailor prompt intensity to user characteristics (NFC, expertise). Consider potential biases introduced by calibration signaling and ensure transparency about AI limitations.

Conclusions
- The collective evidence supports the premise that active recall/reflective prompts and adaptive calibration signals can influence trust calibration and decision quality in AI-assisted tasks, particularly in contexts where AI reliability varies or where social decisions are high-stakes. Cognitive forcing functions, probe-based overreliance detection, reflective provocations, and adaptive trust-calibration cues consistently demonstrate potential to reduce miscalibration and improve alignment between AI input and human judgment (Bučinca et al., 2021; Swaroop et al., 2025; Okamura & Yamada, 2020; Drosos et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2023).
- However, “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration interventions lack direct field evidence in the provided sources. Label-inspired, dopamine-like cues remain a plausible, but unproven, calibration approach in real-life social conflicts. Ground any such approach in calibrated confidence signaling and metacognitive signals; validate with rigorous experiments that report calibration metrics, decision quality, and user experience.
- Overall, the strongest actionable path is to deploy adaptive, reflective, and verification-oriented calibration strategies that directly engage user cognition and metacognition, paired with transparent reliability signals, in realistic conflict tasks. This approach aligns with the best-supported evidence across domains and yields actionable guidance for researchers and practitioners seeking to reduce over-trust while maintaining or improving decision quality in AI-assisted social decision-making.

Notes and caveats
- The literature collectively supports calibration-focused approaches that go beyond passive explanations, emphasizing reflective prompts, verification, and adaptive cueing as promising strategies to reduce overtrust and improve decision quality in AI-assisted social decision-making. However, none of the cited sources provide a single, definitive, real-world field study showing the universal superiority of active recall prompts or dopamine-label calibration across all conflict domains. Context matters: domain, task type, AI reliability, user expertise, and cognitive motivation modulate effectiveness.
- “Dopamine Nutrition Label”-style calibration, while conceptually appealing and aligned with labeling frameworks for AI interpretability and reliability, lacks direct empirical evidence in the provided sources for real-life conflict outcomes. Future work should test explicit dopamine-label-style cues in combination with reflective prompts in realistic conflict tasks to quantify added value and potential trade-offs in trust and decision quality.
