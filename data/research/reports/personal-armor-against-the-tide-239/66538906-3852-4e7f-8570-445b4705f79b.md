# Report: Can prompts that require active credibility evaluation improve calibration and reduce misjudgments in AI-assisted evidence synthesis?

Executive summary
- Across the literature, prompts that foreground credibility cues—such as explicit source citation, confidence rating, and bias assessment—are theoretically motivated to improve calibration and reduce misjudgments in AI-supported evidence synthesis. However, direct experimental evidence isolating such prompts as the sole intervention remains limited or absent in many sources; instead, existing work points to broader patterns: transparency, accountability, and human oversight are critical for trustworthy AI-assisted synthesis (Scotti et al., 2025; Eastaugh et al., 2025; Tem templin et al., 2025) [Scotti et al., 2025; Eastaugh et al., 2025; Templin et al., 2025].
- When credibility prompts are paired with robust reporting standards, training, and governance, there is accumulating evidence that calibration can improve and misjudgments can be reduced—though effect sizes are domain- and task-dependent and often depend on accompanying tooling (e.g., retrieval augmentation, uncertainty signaling, structured rubrics) (Clark et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Dang et al., 2025; Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Vereschak et al., 2024) [Clark et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Dang et al., 2025; Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Vereschak et al., 2024].
- Practical implications for authors: design credibility-promoting prompts as part of a structured, auditable workflow; couple prompts with explicit citation requirements, confidence signals, and bias checks; embed the prompts in modular, retrieval-augmented pipelines and evaluate them with calibration metrics (ECE, reliability curves) and misjudgment rates. The strongest gains are expected when credibility prompts are integrated with transparent reporting and human-in-the-loop validation (Scotti et al., 2025; Clark et al., 2025; Dang et al., 2025; Vereschak et al., 2024) [Scotti et al., 2025; Clark et al., 2025; Dang et al., 2025; Vereschak et al., 2024].

1. Introduction and research question
- Research question: Can prompts that require active credibility evaluation (cite sources, rate confidence, assess bias) improve calibration and reduce misjudgments when using AI for evidence synthesis?
- Rationale: Calibration (alignment between human trust/reliance and AI capability) is central to trustworthy AI-assisted evidence synthesis. Prompts that force active credibility evaluation are a natural design lever to improve transparency, traceability, and critical scrutiny of AI outputs, which in turn should support better calibration and fewer misjudgments if implemented within a robust reporting and training framework.

2. Context and evidence landscape (high-level synthesis)
- Adoption and transparency gaps in ML use for evidence synthesis
  - A 2017–2024 cross-venue analysis across Cochrane, Campbell, and Environmental Evidence found ML usage in only about 5% of syntheses explicitly reported; ML-enabled tools appeared in about 12% of studies, with ~90% of those not clarifying ML function use. Living reviews showed higher ML integration (≈15%) (Scotti et al., 2025) [Scotti et al., 2025].
  - These gaps align with a broader trust vulnerability: incomplete reporting undermines the ability of users to calibrate outputs or trust AI-assisted results, creating a theoretical rationale for prompts that enforce credibility evaluation and explicit reporting (Scotti et al., 2025).
- Systems-level features that accompany credibility prompts
  - The field emphasizes transparency standards (PRISMA and extensions MECCIR, ROSES) and the need for reporting improvements, including stopping rules, classifier validation, and translation methods; credibility prompts could function best when embedded in such reporting frameworks and training (Scotti et al., 2025; Eastaugh et al., 2025).
  - The emergence and proliferation of retrieval-augmented and uncertainty-aware, prompt-based approaches suggest a design space where credibility prompts (cite sources, rate confidence, assess bias) are integrated with retrieval and calibration mechanisms (Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Dang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020) [Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Dang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020].
- Empirical evidence on calibration and active credibility signals (select findings)
  - FAT* 2020: Showing AI confidence scores improves trust calibration on a case level but does not automatically improve joint decision performance; local explanations did not reliably improve calibration in some tasks (Zhang, Liao & Bellamy, 2020) [Zhang et al., 2020].
  - Subsequent experiments on human–AI collaboration indicate that explicit confidence signaling can improve calibration, yet gains depend on task-domain expertise, task difficulty, and whether the human can contribute domain knowledge to offset AI errors (Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Turner et al., 2020) [Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Turner et al., 2020].
  - Retrieval-augmented QA literature shows that uncertainty signaling (e.g., expression of uncertainty, probabilistic scores) can improve calibration, but miscalibration can still occur if retrieved passages mislead or if prompts fail to surface uncertainty adequately (Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Kadavath et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023) [Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Kadavath et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023].
  - Frameworks such as PROBAST+AI propose structured, signalling-based assessments (16 development questions; 18 evaluation questions) to judge bias and applicability of prediction-model studies; these signalling prompts can be adapted to evidence-synthesis prompts to enforce credibility judgments (Moons et al., 2025) [Moons et al., 2025].
- Open questions and gaps
  - Direct controlled experiments that test prompts explicitly requiring active credibility evaluation (cite sources, rate confidence, assess bias) in AI-assisted evidence synthesis remain scarce; most evidence is indirect, focusing on calibration mechanisms, explanations, uncertainty signaling, and governance frameworks (Clark et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Scotti et al., 2025; Templin et al., 2025).

3. Inline quantitative context and key takeaways (selected sources)
- Scotti et al. 2025 (Cochrane ESM)
  - Approx. ML adoption: explicit reporting ≈ 5%; ML-enabled tools ≈ 12%; living reviews ≈ 15% (Scotti et al., 2025).
  - Gaps: missing stopping rules, classifier validation, and translation methods hinder reproducibility and trust; the authors advocate better transparency, reporting standards, and user training to support responsible AI adoption (Scotti et al., 2025).
- Eastaugh et al. 2025 (CORE Information Retrieval Forum)
  - A large group of information specialists (n ≈ 131) discussed AI in evidence synthesis; AI “current uses” and “training wants/needs” highlighted the need for structure, ethics, and tooling to support credible use, not just automation; the forum emphasized credible use and tool suitability (Eastaugh et al., 2025).
- Clark et al. 2025 (Systematic Review)
  - Across searching, screening, data extraction, risk-of-bias assessment, AI performance is variable; miss rates for searching ranged 68–96% (median 91%); incorrect inclusions 0–29% (median 10%); incorrect exclusions 1–83% (median 28%); data extraction incorrect 4–31% (median 14%); ROB1 incorrect 10–56% (median 27%) (Clark et al., 2025). Overall, current evidence does not support GenAI use in evidence synthesis without human oversight; with proper design it can assist task-specific activities (Clark et al., 2025).
- Li et al. 2026 (arXiv)
  - Experiment 1: miscalibrated AI confidence harms reliance and decision efficacy; Experiment 2: surface calibration signals improve detection of miscalibration but can reduce trust in uncalibrated AI and may not improve decision efficacy; calibration is task and knowledge-bound (Li et al., 2026).
- Zhang, Liao, Bellamy 2020 (FAT*)
  - Confidence signals help calibration but do not guarantee improved joint decision performance; explanations’ impact on calibration is task/context dependent; local explanations may have limited effect on calibration; Why explanations can help understanding, but risks exist for novice users (Zhang et al., 2020).
- Additional practical frameworks and tools
  - PROBAST+AI (Moons et al., 2025) offers a structured signalling-question approach for bias/applicability assessment to be used in AI contexts; this framework can be adapted to credibility prompts in evidence synthesis.
  - The LibrAIry ROBOT test (Hervieux & Wheatley, 2020) provides a framework for evaluating reliability, objectivity, bias, ownership, and type of information (ROBOT) to guide credibility prompts.
  - RAISE guidance (Vereschak et al., 2024) and NICE position statements stress transparency, governance, and stakeholder engagement in AI-assisted evidence generation.
  - The Minnesota Compass guidance (2024) advocates structured credibility checks (cite sources, assess bias) and cross-verification with credible data sources, which can inform credibility-prompt design.
- These sources collectively support the plausibility—and the need for—credibility-focused prompts but highlight that credible, measurable calibration improvements require carefully designed experiments, robust metrics, and governance.

4. Synthesis of actionable design principles for credibility prompts
- Design principles (informed by the evidence base)
  - Enforce explicit source-citation for AI-generated claims and critical data points; prompt for bibliographic details (authors, title, venue, year, DOI/PMID) and verify provenance against primary sources where possible (Minnesota Compass; CRAAP adaptation; ROBOT) [Minnesota Compass 2024; SIFT/CRAAP; ROBOT].
  - Require explicit confidence ratings for outputs and claims, with justification that references the evidence quality and recency; avoid overreliance on confidence signals alone (Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2026).
  - Include bias assessment prompts that surface potential conflicts of interest, methodological limitations, and biases in sources or AI reasoning; prompt for missing viewpoints or counter-evidence (Dodge et al., 2019; Kulesza et al., 2013; PROBAST+AI framework) [Dodge et al., 2019; Kulesza et al., 2013; Moons et al., 2025].
  - Implement a hybrid explanations approach: combine global explanations on AI process with local, case-specific rationales for credibility signals (Dodge et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2020).
  - Pair credibility prompts with retrieval-grounded checks and uncertainty signaling (RA-QA, retrieval-augmented, and uncertainty-guided prompts) to anchor claims in verifiable evidence (Perez-Beltrachini & Lapata, 2025; Kadavath et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023).
  - Integrate a robust evaluation plan with calibration metrics (ECE, reliability curves, Brier scores) and misjudgment rates; preregister protocols and publish methods and artifacts to enable replication (Clark et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Scotti et al., 2025; RAISE).
- Experimental design guidance
  - Adopt randomized or within-subjects designs with multiple credibility-prompt variants (citation focus, confidence prompts, bias prompts, and combinations) and compare to a baseline with no explicit credibility prompts.
  - Use ground-truth evidence and expert judgments as benchmarks; measure calibration curves, Brier score, and misjudgment rates; collect user trust and cognitive-load measures.
  - Test across domains (health, policy, environment) to gauge generalizability; examine how domain expertise interacts with calibration gains (Clark et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026).
- Implementation and governance
  - Embed credibility prompts within auditable pipelines (e.g., PRISMA/ROSES-aligned outputs; PROBAST+AI signalling for bias in evidence) to ensure traceability; disclose prompts used, tool versions, and data handling decisions (Scotti et al., 2025; Moons et al., 2025; Templin et al., 2025).
  - Train users in credibility evaluation, bias awareness, and source verification to maximize calibration benefits and minimize under/over-trust (Li et al., 2026; Vereschak et al., 2024).
  - Provide tooling to support rapid verification and auditing, including provenance-tracing dashboards and bias flags, to facilitate reproducible evidence synthesis.

5. Limitations
- There is currently no universal, direct empirical demonstration that credibility prompts alone reliably improve calibration across all evidence-synthesis tasks. The best-supported conclusions come from a convergence of studies pointing to the importance of transparency, uncertainty signaling, human oversight, and structured quality appraisal (Clark et al., 2025; Scotti et al., 2025; Templin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020; Vereschak et al., 2024).
- Domain specificity matters: gains observed in one domain (e.g., clinical decision support, multi-omics) may not automatically generalize to all evidence-synthesis contexts; experiments should explicitly test cross-domain generalizability.
- Calibration is task- and knowledge-bound; miscalibration can persist even with credibility prompts if human users lack training or if the tool’s uncertainty is not well-calibrated (Li et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2020).

6. Conclusions
- The current body of work provides a strong theoretical and methodological basis for designing prompts that require active credibility evaluation (cite sources, rate confidence, assess bias) within AI-assisted evidence synthesis, and for evaluating such prompts with calibration-focused metrics. However, direct experimental evidence demonstrating robust, domain-agnostic calibration improvements from these prompts is not yet established in the literature reviewed here.
- The most promising path combines credibility prompts with:
  - explicit source-citation requirements,
  - calibrated confidence signaling,
  - bias/limitations assessment,
  - retrieval-grounded verification and transparent reporting,
  - and robust human-in-the-loop review, all within auditable governance frameworks (PRISMA/ROSES/RAISE/PROBAST+AI).
- Next steps: design and execute controlled experiments that test credibility prompts in evidence-synthesis tasks, measure calibration metrics (ECE, reliability curves, Brier score), misjudgment rates, and downstream decision quality, and report results in a preregistered, transparent manner.

Appendix: Suggested prompt design templates (for researchers)
- Source-citation prompt for each claim
  - For every AI-generated conclusion, list at least two credible sources with complete bibliographic details; provide a direct quote or a precise paraphrase; include a bibliographic citation trail and verify with primary sources when possible.
- Confidence prompt
  - For each conclusion, assign a numeric confidence (0–100%) and a brief justification that ties confidence to the quality and recency of the cited sources, the presence of conflicting evidence, and methodological limitations.
- Bias prompt
  - For each cited source or conclusion, identify potential biases (e.g., funding, population, design limitations) and discuss how these biases might affect the conclusion.
- Transparency prompt
  - Provide a short transparency note detailing the provenance of the AI outputs, the retrieval sources used, and any limitations or uncertainties identified.
- Calibration and auditing prompt
  - After generating outputs, produce a calibration summary comparing your confidence to any ground-truth or expert assessment, with a plan for human review where required.
