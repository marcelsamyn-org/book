Context resets in AI-assisted knowledge work: evidence synthesis and a concrete 4-week cross-over study design

Abstract
- The core question asks whether enforcing context resets after every AI interaction reduces over-reliance on the model and improves calibration compared with uninterrupted context in a 4-week field/lab cross-over study with knowledge workers.
- Direct empirical evidence for per-interaction context resets in this exact setting is not present in the cited sources. However, there is clear evidence that (a) trust calibration is dynamic and context-sensitive, (b) adaptive trust-calibration cues can improve calibration when miscalibration is detected, and (c) explanations alone have mixed effects on calibration and reliance. Taken together, these findings support a design in which context resets are treated as deliberate, adaptive recalibration opportunities that are complemented by transparency and user control, rather than as an indiscriminate clearing of all history (Okamura & Yamada, 2020; Duan, 2024; McGrath et al., 2025; Naiseh et al., 2022; Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Actionable outcome domains include: trust calibration metrics, over-reliance indicators, autonomy perceptions, and task performance, measured across two cross-over conditions (reset vs uninterrupted context) with appropriate washout and counterbalancing.

1) Introduction and research question framing
- Research question: In a 4-week field/lab cross-over study with knowledge workers, does enforcing context resets after every AI interaction reduce over-reliance on the model and improve calibration compared with uninterrupted context?
- Rationale: Trust in AI is dynamic and shaped by interaction history, environment, and system design. Intervention designs that actively manage trust—such as context-aware resets or recalibration prompts—offer a principled path to improve calibration and preserve autonomy in knowledge-work settings (CHAI-T principles; Duan, 2024; McGrath et al., 2025).
- Key pragmatic considerations: resets should be designed to preserve user autonomy and be accompanied by transparent reasoning, explanations, and user controls to override or challenge AI outputs when warranted (Duan, 2024; Naiseh et al., 2022; Okamura & Yamada, 2020).

Inline citations: (Duan, 2024); (McGrath et al., 2025); (Okamura & Yamada, 2020); (Naiseh et al., 2022)

2) Background: conceptual foundations and empirical anchors
- Trust and calibration in HAI
  - Trust is a dynamic construct comprising expectancy, subject, future actions, outcomes, and risk; calibration aligns trust with the AI’s actual reliability to support appropriate reliance (Mayer et al., 1995; Lee & See, 2004; Rotter, 1967; Rousseau, 1998). CHAI-T frames trust in collaborative human-AI teams as evolving through antecedents (human, technology, environment), mediators (processes, explanations, feedback), and outcomes (calibration, autonomy, performance) (McGrath et al., 2025). Inline: (McGrath et al., 2025); (Lee & See, 2004); (Mayer et al., 1995).
  - Empirical synthesis across 106 studies (Vaccaro et al., 2024) shows that human-AI combinations do not universally outperform the best single agent (human or AI) and that task type moderates outcomes; expectations about calibration must thus be task- and domain-specific. Inline: (Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Explanations and calibration
  - Explanations can improve understanding and trust in some settings but can also induce over-reliance, depending on class, job constraints, and user expertise. A hybrid global+local explanation approach often fares better than single-class explanations in some contexts, but findings are heterogeneous (Naiseh et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2009; Kulesza et al., 2013; Dodge et al., 2019). Inline: (Naiseh et al., 2022); (Lim et al., 2009); (Kulesza et al., 2013); (Dodge et al., 2019).
- Adaptive trust calibration cues
  - Adaptive trust calibration cues (TCCs) that prompt recalibration after detected miscalibration (over- or under-trust) can outperform continuous transparency in restoring calibration, as shown in an online study with a drone-inspection task (Okamura & Yamada, 2020). Inline: (Okamura & Yamada, 2020).
- Context management and cross-over considerations
  - Cross-over designs can be powerful to isolate within-subject differences in context management but require careful washouts to avoid carryover; broader evidence indicates trust calibration is sensitive to task context, workload, and domain-specific factors (Capili & Anastasi; Dwan et al. 2019; Capili & Anastasi 2024; Vaccaro et al., 2024). Inline: (Capili & Anastasi, 2024); (Dwan et al., 2019); (Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Relevance to knowledge workers
  - In knowledge-work contexts, context management, workflow integration, and autonomy are critical for sustained collaboration; context resets must be designed with minimal disruption and with explicit recalibration affordances (Gomez et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Mei et al., 2025).

Inline citations: (McGrath et al., 2025); (Vaccaro et al., 2024); (Naiseh et al., 2022); (Lim et al., 2009); (Kulesza et al., 2013); (Dodge et al., 2019); (Okamura & Yamada, 2020); (Capili & Anastasi, 2024); (Dwan et al., 2019); (Mei et al., 2025); (Li et al., 2025)

3) Evidence anchors most relevant to context resets
A. Adaptive trust-calibration cues (context-reset analogs)
- Okamura & Yamada (2020): Adaptive trust calibration cues (TCCs) delivered after detecting miscalibration prompt recalibration; adaptive cues (verbal cues most effective) improved calibration (higher d′) compared with continuous exposure to reliability signals alone. This supports using targeted recalibration prompts after AI interactions as a plausible mechanism to reduce over-trust in long-running use. Inline: (Okamura & Yamada, 2020).
- Implication for knowledge workers: a per-interaction recalibration prompt could be integrated into workflows as a lightweight, context-reset-like intervention, especially where AI reliability fluctuates or where error costs are high.

B. Dynamic trust and CHAI-T (trust as a process)
- Duan (2024) and McGrath et al. (2025) emphasize trust as evolving with interaction history and environment; CHAI-T proposes an active management of antecedents, mediators, and outcomes to avoid homogenization and over-trust; trust calibration is a critical objective in HAI collaboration. Inline: (Duan, 2024); (McGrath et al., 2025).

C. Calibration-focused design in XAI and knowledge-work contexts
- Naiseh et al. (2022) shows that XAI explanations interact with calibration, with some explanations contributing to over-trust when misaligned with constraints; design guidelines emphasize combining explanations with appropriate tasks and job constraints. Inline: (Naiseh et al., 2022).
- Mehrotra (2024) articulates a taxonomy of trust metrics and emphasizes adaptive vs static calibrations, along with cross-domain variations in trust, which informs how context resets might be differentially effective across domains.

D. Cross-domain syntheses on HAC and trust
- Vaccaro et al. (2024) synthesize evidence that HAC does not automatically outperform the best single agent; task characteristics and governance matter. This cautions against assuming resets alone will yield large performance gains; calibration and governance structures are essential. Inline: (Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Li et al. (2023) propose a Markovian trust-dynamics model (trust as a latent process) with per-trial context; this framework provides a quantitative lens to study how context resets (as shifts in context) alter trust states and reliance over time.

Inline citations: (Okamura & Yamada, 2020); (Duan, 2024); (McGrath et al., 2025); (Naiseh et al., 2022); (Mehrotra, 2024); (Vaccaro et al., 2024); (Li, 2023)

4) Proposed study design: a concrete 4-week cross-over protocol
Objective
- Test whether enforcing context resets after every AI interaction reduces over-reliance on the model and improves calibration, relative to uninterrupted context, in a 4-week field/lab cross-over with knowledge workers performing AI-assisted tasks.

Experimental design
- Design: Within-subject AB/BA cross-over.
- Conditions:
  - A (Uninterrupted context): Participants perform AI-assisted tasks with continuous context/history carried across interactions; users see AI outputs and explanations in a persistent thread.
  - B (Context resets after each interaction): After every AI interaction, context is reset, requiring re-establishment of relevant task context before the next AI prompt; adaptive recalibration prompts may accompany resets, but the core reset is the contextual clearing.
- Sequence: Randomized AB/BA with counterbalancing; washout period between phases of at least 1 week to minimize carryover, depending on task logistics.
- Participants: Knowledge workers across domains performing domain-relevant, decision-support tasks (examples: data analysis with AI assistance, document drafting with AI, research synthesis).
- Task design: A mix of knowledge-work tasks (analysis, synthesis, writing) with varying complexity and risk to ensure generalizability across contexts.

Interventions and manipulation details
- Primary manipulation: context reset policy (reset after every AI interaction vs no reset).
- Ancillary manipulations:
  - Explanations: two classes (local vs global; or a hybrid) to test interaction with resets; include confidence scores as a potential moderator.
  - Recalibration prompts: after resets, present succinct prompts encouraging verification, alternative viewpoints, or explicit uncertainty signaling.
- Rationale: resets function as an anti-habituation mechanism, potentially reducing homogenization and over-trust, but should be paired with calibration aids to avoid excessive cognitive load.

Outcome measures (quantitative and qualitative)
- Primary outcomes:
  - Calibration metrics: trust calibration measures (alignment between perceived AI reliability and observed AI performance); calibration curves; Brier-type scores; per-task calibration slopes.
  - Over-reliance indicators: frequency of following AI outputs without verification; rate of incorrect AI-driven decisions that persist after AI outputs are delivered; time spent verifying AI outputs.
- Secondary outcomes:
  - Task performance: accuracy, decision quality, error rates, time-to-decision.
  - Autonomy: perceived autonomy and agency via validated scales; perceived control over decision processes.
  - Cognitive load: NASA-TLX or similar scale; subjective workload after each week.
- Moderators and covariates:
  - Task type, domain, expertise, prior AI tool experience.
  - Explanation type, presence of confidence scores, and perceived transparency.
  - Sequence effects and potential learning.

Measurement instruments and data collection
- Behavioral data: interaction logs (AI outputs, human edits, verifications, overrides), timestamps, and context-reset events.
- Self-report: trust in automation scales, autonomy scales, cognitive load scales, calibration questionnaires.
- Performance data: ground-truth or expert-rated task outcomes.
- Qualitative data: post-task interviews or think-aloud transcripts to understand mental models and perceived impact of resets.

Data analysis plan
- Primary analyses: within-subject comparisons of calibration and over-reliance metrics between A and B, using mixed-effects models to account for repeated measures and potential period effects.
- Secondary analyses: interaction effects with task type, explanation class, and experience with AI. Examine carryover with residual confounds and include period as a fixed effect.
- Power considerations: a pilot study may be advisable to estimate within-subject effect sizes; cross-over designs typically increase power relative to parallel designs, but period effects and carryover must be accounted for in the model.
- Pre-registration: specify primary and secondary outcomes, analysis plan, and stopping rules prior to data collection.

Potential challenges and mitigations
- Cognitive load: resets may increase cognitive load if not designed carefully; mitigate with concise prompts and optional choose-your-depth of explanations.
- Carryover effects: implement a washout period and counterbalance sequences; monitor for lingering effects from the first period.
- Domain generalizability: use diverse tasks and multiple domains to assess generalizability; report domain-specific results to guide transferability.
- Ethical considerations: ensure transparency about the context-reset mechanism, preserve user autonomy, and provide override capabilities; protect participant privacy and data integrity.

5) Actionable insights for the author: practical design guidelines
- Treat context resets as an adaptive calibration mechanism, not a blunt force to erase history; combine with recalibration prompts and optional explanations to maintain user understanding.
- The Reset+Prompt design should be calibrated to workload and task risk; higher-stakes or higher-variance AI contexts may benefit more from resets with stronger calibration cues.
- Explanations: test different explanation modalities (local/global or hybrid) and align them with user expertise; calibrate the trade-off between cognitive load and calibration benefit.
- Transparency: pair resets with explicit uncertainty signaling and confidence in AI outputs to support calibration without overwhelming users.
- Measurement: collect multi-modal data (behavioral reliance, calibration metrics, task outcomes, autonomy/well-being measures) to understand the full effect of context resets.
- Generalize appropriately: include a plan for cross-domain generalization and specify domain-specific calibrations (e.g., data analytics vs. drafting vs. research synthesis).

Appendix: instruments and operational definitions (summary)
- Trust calibration: alignment between user trust and AI reliability; over-trust and under-trust are defined as deviations.
- Calibration metrics: calibration curves, Brier score, d′ (calibration sensitivity), trust-weighted accuracy.
- Over-reliance indicators: acceptance of AI outputs without verification; insufficient cross-checks or independent corroboration.
- Autonomy: perceived control over decision processes; measured with validated autonomy scales.
- Context resets: intervention condition defined as forced resetting of task context/history between AI interactions, potentially coupled with recalibration prompts and uncertainty signaling.
