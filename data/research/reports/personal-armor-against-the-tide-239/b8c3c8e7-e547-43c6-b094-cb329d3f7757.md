Executive summary
- There is no direct longitudinal evidence (6–12 months) that adherence to a “personal armor” protocol (discipline prompts, daily rituals, verify outputs) reduces AI dependency or preserves human-centered taste/judgment. The available literature provides a mix of theoretical frameworks, short-term experimental findings, and design guidance that can inform a longitudinal study, but none delivers the exact 6–12 month correlational data requested (several sources are descriptive, qualitative, or cross-sectional) (Agudo et al., 2024; Salloch & Eriksen, 2024; Vered et al., 2023; Goddard et al., 2011; NIST TEVV, 2022; Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Key quantitative anchors from related work:
  - Automation bias is robust across domains; explanations alone do not reliably eliminate bias and can sometimes increase it, though explanations can improve some performance metrics under certain conditions (Vered et al., 2023).
  - High DSS accuracy (80–90%) does not prevent automation bias; in some high-stakes contexts, clinicians override correct AI advice, illustrating the fragility of relying on accuracy alone (Goddard et al., 2011).
  - Concrete numeric findings exist in related areas: AI-assisted tasks can degrade human performance when AI outputs are incorrect (e.g., large reductions in diagnostic accuracy in ECG contexts when AI is wrong) and AI dependency can correlate with training, domain familiarity, and perceived AI benefits (Goddard et al., 2011; Bond et al. as cited in Abdelwanis et al., 2024).
  - Longitudinal, multi-domain evidence linking adherence to armor-like practices with reduced AI dependency is absent; existing work largely motivates, frames, or tests components of armor-like approaches (HITL AI coins, governance, TEVV) rather than delivering time-series correlations (NIST TEVV, 2022; Fragiadakis et al., 2024; Buijsman et al., 2025).
- Actionable directions for authors planning a 6–12 month study:
  - Ground the study in a clear decision-authority model (HITL vs AI2L) and define explicit armor-components (discipline prompts, daily rituals, verify-outputs) as observable workflow features.
  - Pre-register hypotheses and multi-metric outcomes that capture AI dependency trajectories, human-judgment preservation, and verification behaviors across time.
  - Use mixed designs (within- and between-subjects) and domain diversification to test moderation by task type, AI error rates, and user expertise.
  - Include both quantitative metrics (e.g., AI-dependency proxies, verification rates, expert-judgment scores) and qualitative assessments (trust, perceived autonomy, epistemic partnership) to map mechanisms.
- Core actionable insights for the author:
  - Favor a governance-and-teaching approach: embed explicit verification, failure-signaling, and accountability via audit trails; design daily rituals that promote reflective checking of AI outputs; ensure final authority rests with humans in high-stakes tasks.
  - Build an adaptable study design that can test prescriptive (armor as rules) vs supportive (armor as workflow scaffolds) deployment modes, given domain-specific needs.
  - Prepare for domain-specific variance: task type can qualitatively shift whether armor protocols reduce AI dependency or preserve judgment; plan for domain-stratified analyses.

1) Background and definitions
- Personal armor protocols = discipline prompts (structured prompts guiding critical evaluation), daily rituals (regular, repeated checks and reflection), and verify-outputs (mandatory verification and independent cross-checks before action).
- Research question framing: Over 6–12 months, does adherence to these armor protocols correlate with (a) reduced AI dependency and (b) preserved human-centered taste and judgment, compared with standard AI use?
- Foundational concepts (inline citations):
  - Human-in-the-loop (HITL) framing and co-reasoning emphasize meaningful human involvement and epistemic partnership with AI outputs, guiding how armor protocols might support human judgment (Salloch & Eriksen, 2024; Fragiadakis et al., 2024; NIST TEVV, 2022).
  - Automation bias (AB) arises when users over-rely on AI outputs; explanations do not reliably reduce AB and can alter performance, depending on context and task (Vered et al., 2023).
  - Validation frameworks (TEVV) encourage measurement-science approaches to AI, including context-specific metrics, testbeds, and continuous validation, all relevant to longitudinal armor testing (NIST TEVV, 2022).

2) Evidence landscape: what we know now (quantitative anchors and qualitative guidance)
- Direct longitudinal evidence for armor-like adherence is absent in the cited sources; the literature provides mechanisms, design principles, and short-term experimental findings that can inform a longitudinal design.
- Key quantitative anchors from the cited work:
  - AB literature (Goddard et al., 2011): DSS accuracy is typically high (80–90%), yet automation bias persists; observed instances include clinicians overriding correct AI recommendations in some cases (6% in one primary study cited by AB reviews) (Goddard et al., 2011).
  - AB interventions (Vered et al., 2023): Explanations and transparency can influence performance and trust variably; they did not consistently reduce AB; sometimes they reduced completion time and improved decision accuracy when AI advice was correct or incorrect, depending on task and explanation type.
  - Cross-domain AB synthesis (Vaccaro et al., 2024): Across 106 experiments, human–AI combinations often underperform the best single system (negative average effect size for combined performance) but can show augmentation relative to humans alone; synergy is not guaranteed and depends on task type and relative strengths of humans vs AI.
  - AB-related training/mitigation: The literature emphasizes training, explicit accountability, and interface design as mitigators; continuous validation is advocated but is underrepresented in practice (Goddard et al., 2011; Myllyaho et al., 2021; NIST TEVV guidance)).
- Longitudinal governance and HUM-AI collaboration:
  - HITL frameworks (Salloch & Eriksen, 2024; NIST TEVV, 2022; Fragiadakis et al., 2024; Buijsman et al., 2025) stress governance, human factors, auditability, and reflexivity as keys to durable, human-centered AI collaboration.

3) Synthesis: implications for a 6–12 month longitudinal armor study
- What to test, given the gaps:
  - Test whether adherence to a structured armor protocol reduces AI dependency over time (i.e., fewer AI-driven decisions, more independent validations, more overrides when AI is incorrect).
  - Test whether adherence preserves human-centered taste/judgment over time (as judged by domain experts, alignment with normative criteria, and resistance to homogenization of outputs).
  - Test how verification steps (VO) and discipline prompts interact with task type, AI error rate, and user expertise as moderators.
- Why these tests are grounded in the literature:
  - AB is mitigable via training, accountability, and guardrails; dialogue about failure modes and explainability is essential but not a universal fix (Goddard et al., 2011; Vered et al., 2023).
  - TEVV and governance patterns provide a framework for measuring durability, validation, and human factors over time (NIST TEVV, 2022; Fragiadakis et al., 2024).
  - Longitudinal autonomy literature emphasizes potential deskilling and authenticity risks if oversight erodes; the armor protocol should therefore include reflective practice and defeater mechanisms to protect domain competencies (Buijsman et al., 2025).

4) Proposed longitudinal study design (6–12 months)
- Design options: randomized controlled trial (attaching armor to an experimental arm) or a quasi-experimental longitudinal cohort with matched-controls.
- Population: knowledge workers/users of AI-assisted decision aids across diverse domains (healthcare, finance, education, design) to test domain generalizability.
- Arms:
  - Armor Protocol Arm: participants employ a defined armor protocol:
    - Discipline prompts: rule-based prompts to evaluate AI suggestions before acting.
    - Daily rituals: fixed routines for planning without AI, reflection on AI outputs, and end-of-day verification planning.
    - Verify outputs: mandatory verification steps, independent checks, and documentation of rationale.
  - Standard Use Arm: participants use AI outputs with no structured armor protocol beyond typical workflow.
- Duration and timepoints:
  - Baseline, Month 3, Month 6, Month 9, Month 12.
- Outcomes (primary and secondary):
  - AI Dependency Index (ADI): frequency of AI-driven decisions, time-to-decision, and rate of AI-driven final decisions without independent validation.
  - Judgment Preservation Score (JPS): expert-rated decision quality, alignment with domain standards, and demonstrated resistance to AI-induced homogenization.
  - Output Verification Rate (OVR): proportion of outputs subjected to formal verification steps.
  - Domain-specific metrics: task-appropriate performance, bias indicators, trust calibration, and perceived autonomy.
- Covariates: domain, task complexity, user expertise with AI, AI system accuracy/error rate, workload, and experience with prior AB training.
- Data collection methods:
  - System logs (prompt usage, AI outputs, edits, verifications).
  - Expert panel assessments at each interval.
  - Standardized trust and autonomy surveys.
  - Qualitative interviews or brief ethnographic notes to capture perceived epistemic partnership.
- Analysis plan:
  - Linear/mixed-effects models to examine trajectories of ADI and JPS as a function of armor-adherence scores, time, and domain, with random effects for participants.
  - Mediation analyses to test whether VO mediates the relationship between armor adherence and JPS.
  - Moderator analyses by task type, AI error rate, and domain.
  - Pre-registration of hypotheses and analysis plan; use intention-to-treat and per-protocol analyses.
- Preregistration and transparency:
  - Pre-register hypotheses, metrics, and analysis pipeline (including plans for interim analyses and stopping rules if safety or ethics concerns arise).
- Ethical and governance considerations:
  - Ensure informed consent, data privacy, safety monitoring, and domain-appropriate risk controls; incorporate TEVV-like evaluation to monitor bias and safety over time.

5) Operationalizing the armor protocol and measurement instruments
- Operational definitions:
  - Discipline prompts: mandatory prompts that require justification, cross-checking, and explicit action thresholds before accepting AI output.
  - Daily rituals: set of routine tasks performed daily to reflect on AI outputs, plan ahead without AI, and log verifications.
  - Verify outputs: structured verification steps (fact-checking, cross-source corroboration, alignment checks with normative criteria) documented per decision.
- Measurements:
  - ADI: composite index combining AI-output reliance, AI-dominated decisions, and need for AI justification in decisions.
  - OVR: frequency and depth of verification steps per task.
  - JPS: external expert panel scoring on decision quality, ethical alignment, and domain-specific taste.
  - VO and Rationale quality: qualitative coding of verification notes and defense of decisions.
- Data handling:
  - Version-controlled protocol, dashboards for adherence metrics, secure storage of logs and expert evaluations, and predefined data governance policies.

6) Expected contributions and interpretation
- If armor adherence correlates with reduced AI dependency and preserved human judgment, this would support structured, guardrail-rich workflows as a durable approach to human-centric AI collaboration, consistent with HITL governance principles and AB mitigation literature.
- If no correlation or negative correlation emerges, it would prompt refinement of armor components (more effective prompts, different verification paradigms, domain-adaptive rituals) and suggest that domain-specific tailoring and governance structure matter more than a universal protocol.

7) Limitations and caveats
- The absence of pre-existing longitudinal armor-data means the proposed study would generate new evidence; results will be contingent on domain, AI systems used, user training, and adherence fidelity.
- Domain heterogeneity requires careful stratification and possibly multiple parallel studies to avoid overgeneralizing.

8) Actionable insights for authors (summary)
- Build a robust, preregistered longitudinal study that explicitly defines armor components, metrics, and timepoints; embed governance and TEVV-like evaluation to ensure fidelity and safety.
- Use multi-method measurement (quantitative ADI/JPS/OVR plus qualitative trust/autonomy feedback) to illuminate mechanisms and address the AB literature’s calls for outcome-based measurement.
- Anticipate domain-specific moderation: plan for stratified analyses by domain and task type; calibrate expectations for synergy vs augmentation in line with meta-analytic findings (Vaccaro et al., 2024).
- Design for governance: include audit trails, explainability, and accountability to support interpretability and human oversight, aligning with HITL frameworks (Salloch & Eriksen, 2024; NIST TEVV, 2022).
