# report.md

Recall Prompts Before AI Use: Evidence Synthesis on Long-Term Retention and Transfer in Domain Knowledge Tasks

Executive summary
- The bulk of the robust retrieval-practice literature shows that recall prompts (testing, retrieval practice) generally improve long-term retention and can support transfer to related tasks, especially when retrieval is spaced, multiple retrieval opportunities are provided, and feedback is integrated (the testing effect; forward testing). Reported effect sizes are typically in the small-to-medium range, with many studies around g ≈ 0.5 for retention and d ≈ 0.4 for transfer (Rowland, 2014; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006b; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2021) [references in-text]. 
- Direct empirical tests of recall prompts before AI use (recall-first prompts) in domain-knowledge learning are limited but accumulating. Some evidence from AI-enabled learning contexts suggests that AI-assisted retrieval practice can improve outcomes (e.g., final-exam performance) when AI is used to generate prompts and provide feedback, though these studies often involve non-randomized designs or focus on exam outcomes rather than explicit long-term retention or cross-domain transfer (Yusof, 2025; Akgun & Toker, 2024; Cosyn et al., 2020; Corral & Carpenter, 2025). 
- Direct transfer evidence specifically comparing recall-first AI prompts vs direct AI queries in a controlled domain-knowledge task remains limited; however, the convergence of RP theory and AI-enabled practice supports designing and testing recall-first AI workflows to potentially improve durable learning and transfer.

1) Research question and scope
- Question: In a domain-knowledge task, does requiring retrieval practice before consulting AI (recall prompts) improve long-term retention and transfer compared with direct AI queries?
- Rationale: Embedding retrieval (recall prompts) before AI use aligns with desirable-difficulties and transfer-appropriate processing (TAP) principles, and multiple retrieval opportunities with delay are known to bolster durability and generalization of learning. The question asks whether these RP advantages extend when an AI tool is used as the subsequent learning aid.

2) Key theoretical foundations
- Retrieval practice and the testing effect
  - Retrieval practice reliably improves long-term retention relative to restudy, with effects that persist over delays and across domains (Karpicke & Roediger, 2007; Roediger & Karpicke, 2006a/b; Rowland, 2014; Pan & Rickard, 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2021) [ inline: Karpicke & Roediger 2007; Roediger & Karpicke 2006a/b; Rowland 2014; Pan & Rickard 2018; Yang 2021; Agarwal 2021 ].
  - Feedback typically enhances RP benefits; retrieval-without-feedback often still beats restudy but yields smaller gains than retrieval-with-feedback (Butler & Roediger, 2008; Potts & Shanks, 2014; Siler & Benjamin, 2019) [ inline: Butler & Roediger 2008; Potts & Shanks 2014; Siler & Benjamin 2019 ].
  - RP effects generalize to transfer: near transfer is robust; far transfer occurs under conditions that reveal underlying structure or rules (Butler, 2010; Jacoby, Wahlheim, & Coane, 2010; Rohrer, Taylor, & Sholar, 2010; Kang, McDaniel, Pashler, 2011; Pan & Rickard, 2018) [ inline: Butler 2010; Jacoby 2010; Rohrer 2010; Kang 2011; Pan & Rickard 2018 ].
- Transfer-appropriate processing (TAP)
  - TAP posits that memory performance is maximized when encoding and retrieval contexts match the demands of the target task (Morris, Bransford, & Franks, 1977). This implies that recall prompts aligned with the downstream AI task can improve transfer, not just retention.
- Forward testing and transfer
  - Forward/testing effects indicate that retrieval-based rehearsal for newer content can improve subsequent learning on related material, sometimes even when the final test involves different formats or contexts (Pastötter et al.; Siler & Benjamin; Chan, Meissner & Davis; etc.). This supports the idea that recall-before-AI could help with subsequent AI-mediated reasoning or problem solving (forward testing literature).

3) Evidence base relevant to recall-before-AI in domain knowledge tasks

A. Direct RP evidence (non-AI contexts; domain knowledge)
- Siler & Benjamin (2019): Long-term inference and memory after retrieval practice in a category-learning task (bird families)
  - Design: retrieval vs restudy across multiple conditions; immediate and 7-day delayed tests; final transfer-like measures (novel exemplars).
  - Key results: Retrieval improved recognition discriminability (d’) on immediate and delayed tests; decoding: F(1, 74) = 63.37, p < .001 for retrieval vs restudy; delay reduced performance overall (F(1,74) = 93.72, p < .001). Forgetting was greater for retrieval (0.76 drop) than restudy (0.57 drop), not consistent with a simple slowing of forgetting but with stronger retrieval strength and transfer-like benefits (Siler & Benjamin, 2019).
  - Implication: Retrieval practice enhances retention and transfer-like performance without necessarily slowing forgetting on all measures.
- Nickl & Bäuml (2023): Retrieval practice reduces relative forgetting over time
  - Key findings: Retrieval reduces relative forgetting (relative to restudy) across delays; forgetting rate (b) not diminished by feedback; restudy did not differ from no-practice; stronger retention with retrieval overall.
  - Practical takeaway: RP supports durable memory trajectories; feedback did not alter the relative forgetting rate in this study, but retrieval still improved retention.
- Kang, Lindsey, Mozer, Pashler (2014): Spacing and expanding retrieval
  - Design: 28-day vocabulary learning with expanding vs equal-interval retrieval.
  - Key results: Expanding retrieval increased average recall across training and reduced forgetting to the final test, though final-test gains could be small or non-significant depending on retention interval and material.
  - Implications: Spaced, expanding retrieval improves the durability of learning, informing RP design for AI-assisted contexts (e.g., spaced recall prompts before AI interactions).

B. RP in AI-enabled contexts (AI-assisted retrieval practice)
- Yusof (2025): ChatGPT-assisted retrieval practice and exam scores
  - Design: retrospective cohort; N = 128 (n=64 per group); experimental group used AI-assisted retrieval practice (ChatGPT-generated prompts + immediate feedback) vs control with no AI-assisted RP.
  - Key result: AI-assisted RP group achieved significantly higher final-exam scores than the control group (Welch’s t-test reported; exact values not provided in the accessible summary). Emphasizes that AI-generated prompts + feedback can scale retrieval practice and improve exam performance; underscores the need for human-in-the-loop feedback to deepen understanding.
- Akgun & Toker (2024): Pretesting with conversational AI on retention
  - Design: randomized controlled (Phase 1 recall testing before AI task; Phase 2 AI-assisted problem solving; phase 2 transfer tasks)
  - Key finding: Pretesting (recall-first) before AI assistance improved performance on a subsequent test compared with no pretest; supports recall prompts before AI use in a domain knowledge task (Phase 1 recall-first advantage). These results are from a controlled experimental setting focusing on retention of AI-assisted tasks (preprint).
- Cosyn, Matayoshi, Elzeiny, Uzun (L@S ’20): Retrieval practice in an ITS
  - Design: retrieval practice embedded in an intelligent tutoring system; RSA-style, domain-learning tasks with retrieval prompts.
  - Note: The full text is not freely accessible in the cited excerpt, but the project is consistent with RP in AI-assisted learning (“desirable difficulties” embedded in AI-guided instruction). 
- Corral & Carpenter (2025): RP in a domain knowledge task with AI-like final assessment
  - Experimental series (N = 309; Experiments 1–3)
  - Key pattern: Retrieval outperforms restudy on repeated final-test questions and on transfer-oriented application questions in Experiment 3; retrieval does not always outperform recognition or standard quiz study on all transfer measures in Experiment 1, but benefits emerge with delays and multi-round RP (Experiment 2 and 3 emphasize stronger transfer/near-transfer effects with longer delays and more retrieval cycles). 
  - Implication: RP effects persist when applied to more complex domain content and transfer tasks, particularly with multiple retrieval opportunities and delayed testing.

C. Direct AI-use RP evidence and meta-analytic context
- Agarwal et al. (2021): Classroom-based RP meta-analytic synthesis
  - 50 experiments; 49 effect sizes; N ≈ 5,374
  - Finding: ~57% of effect sizes are medium or large; RP benefits generalize across domains (K–12 to university) and formats; a substantial proportion of studies report RP benefits even in high-stakes contexts (e.g., exams counted toward grades). Feedback, spacing, and test formats moderate effects (Agarwal et al., 2021).
  - Implication: RP is broadly effective in classroom settings; AI-enabled RP designs can leverage these robust findings, though precise effect sizes in AI contexts require direct empirical testing.
- Opitz et al. (2024); Opitz et al. (2024) on far-transfer and rule-based learning in retrieval-practice contexts
  - Findings suggest that retrieval practice can support far-transfer under rule-based learning conditions and when the material supports inductive generalization.
  - Implication: For AI-enabled learning, retrieval prompts that emphasize underlying rules or schemas may support broader transfer when combined with AI-guided problem solving.

4) Synthesis: what does this imply for recall prompts before AI use?

- Overall expectation grounded in RP theory
  - Recalls prompts before AI use (recall-first prompts) should, in principle, yield better long-term retention than direct AI queries, provided the prompts are structured to induce effortful recall, spaced appropriately, and accompanied by feedback. This is consistent with RP theory, TAP, and forward-testing findings.

- Transfer prospects
  - Near transfer: RP before AI, especially when prompts and AI tasks share processing demands and cues, is likely to produce near-transfer benefits (Jacoby et al., 2010; Rohrer et al., 2010; TAP). 
  - Far transfer: Evidence is more variable; some RP studies show far-transfer benefits when underlying rules/generalizations are extracted (Opitz et al., 2024; Butler, 2010; Kang et al., 2011). In AI contexts, achieving far transfer would likely require retrieval prompts that promote inductive reasoning and transferable schemas, plus transfer-focused AI tasks.

- AI-specific caveats
  - AI outputs may distort retrieval if learners rely too heavily on AI for correctness; pairing AI with corrective feedback and verification is essential (Butler & Roediger, 2008; Roediger et al., 2011; Stadler et al., 2024).
  - The QA/AI-dense environment can reduce perceptual cognitive load temporarily, but designers must guard against overreliance on AI as a shortcut for retrieval; RP remains a driver of durable memory even in AI-enabled settings.

5) Quantitative findings (key numbers to inform design)
- RP effect sizes (domain-agnostic)
  - Retention: typical effect around g ≈ 0.50; individual study ranges from d ≈ 0.31 to 1.26 (Roediger & Karpicke 2006a; Rowland 2014; Yang et al. 2021; Agarwal et al. 2021) [cite accordingly in-text].
  - Transfer: about d ≈ 0.40 (Pan & Rickard, 2018).
  - Within-classroom RP: ~medium-to-large effects in many studies; ~57% medium/large across 49 effects in Agarwal et al. (2021).
- RP in AI contexts (examples)
  - Yusof (2025): AI-assisted RP yielded significantly higher final-exam scores than non-AI RP (exact stats not published in summary).
  - Akgun & Toker (2024): Pretesting before AI improved retention on a subsequent test; exact effect sizes not provided in the accessible summary.
  - Corral & Carpenter (2025): RP yielded better performance on repeated final-test questions and on application questions in Experiments 2–3, with nuanced results in Experiment 1 where RP did not outperform recognition or quiz study on repeated questions.
- RP mechanics (supporting evidence)
  - Sutterer & Awh (2016): RP increases retrieval probability (P_mem) but not mnemonic precision (SD); suggests RP biases accessibility rather than fidelity.
  - Nickl & Bäuml (2023): RP reduces relative forgetting over time; effect on forgetting rate (b) not moderated by feedback; restudy comparable to no-practice.
  - Kang et al. (2014): Expanding retrieval schedules improved long-term accessibility and reduced forgetting relative to equal-interval schedules; suggests spacing strategies that can be embedded in AI workflows.

6) Practical design guidance for AI-enabled domain knowledge tasks (actionable insights)
- Implement recall-first AI workflows
  - Phase 1: Learners perform retrieval prompts (free recall and/or cued recall) on core domain concepts, with corrective feedback after attempts.
  - Phase 2: Learners engage with AI (the AI tutor or assistant) to elaborate, verify, or apply retrieved knowledge.
  - Phase 3: Delayed assessment to measure retention and transfer (e.g., novel problems, case-based tasks) to capture durable learning and generalization.
- Use spaced retrieval
  - Space retrieval prompts across sessions (e.g., early, mid, and late in a course or module) to maximize retention and foster forward learning when AI guidance is later invoked.
- Optimize retrieval formats
  - Combine open-ended recall with confirmation MCQs to calibrate retrieval difficulty and cue strength; ensure a mix of congruent prompts (aligned with final assessments or AI tasks) and some varied cues to promote robust retrieval.
- Integrate feedback carefully
  - Immediate, informative feedback after retrieval helps correct errors and consolidates correct knowledge; AI-generated explanations can be supplemented with human-in-the-loop review for complex domains.
- Align prompts with transfer goals
  - Tie retrieval prompts to downstream AI tasks that require applying principles to novel contexts; explicit transfer targets (near and far) should be included in the assessment plan.
- Monitor cognitive load
  - Use cognitive-load measures (self-report, performance proxies) to calibrate retrieval prompt difficulty and AI-task complexity; mitigate risk of overload with adaptive spacing and prompt density.
- Reporting and evaluation
  - Pre-register hypotheses; report retention and transfer outcomes with effect sizes and confidence intervals; include explicit ROI in terms of time spent and learning gains.
- Domain considerations
  - The strongest RP benefits emerge with domain knowledge materials that support underlying rules and schemas; in AI contexts, favor tasks that can be grounded in robust schemas rather than solely surface facts.

7) Limitations and caveats
- Direct AI comparisons: There is limited, direct randomized evidence comparing recall-first AI prompts to direct AI queries across diverse domain tasks; much of the evidence is RP literature without AI involvement or AI-integration studies with non-randomized designs.
- Transfer variability: Near transfer is more reliably observed; far transfer requires careful task design and alignment of prompts with inductive generalization.
- Context dependence: Outcomes depend on materials, domain, learner expertise, feedback quality, AI tool quality, and task design; results from one domain or AI platform may not generalize.
- Publication status and replication: Some AI-RP studies are emerging (e.g., Yusof 2025; Akgun & Toker 2024; Corral & Carpenter 2025); replication across domains and under rigorous experimental controls is warranted.

8) Implications for future research
- Conduct randomized controlled trials directly comparing recall-first AI prompts vs direct AI queries across diverse domains (STEM, medicine, law, coding) with standardized retention and transfer metrics.
- Examine optimal RP design in AI contexts: number of recall attempts, spacing intervals, prompt formats (free recall vs cued recall), feedback type (explanatory vs corrective), and the alignment of recall prompts with AI-task demands (TAP).
- Explore the role of AI literacy and human-in-the-loop feedback in maximizing recall-first RP benefits and ensuring knowledge fidelity.
- Assess potential moderators: prior knowledge, domain complexity, AI-tool quality, and the risk of AI-induced overreliance or reduced cognitive engagement (inversion risk in AI contexts) as framed in AI integration literature.
- Report comprehensive statistics: means, SDs, effect sizes, confidence intervals, and preregistered analysis plans to facilitate meta-analytic synthesis.

Notes on interpretation
- The synthesis integrates findings from multiple lines of research: classic RP literature (RP effect sizes and conditions), RP in health/education contexts, AI-assisted RP studies (final exam performance and practice platforms), and domain-transfer RP work (near vs far). While there is strong theoretical support for RP-before-AI improving retention and transfer, robust, domain-general, randomized evidence directly comparing recall-first prompts before AI against direct AI prompts remains an important area for rigorous study.

Author’s actionable conclusion
- If your aim is to maximize long-term retention and transfer in AI-augmented domain knowledge tasks, design and test recall-first AI workflows that (a) use spaced, multiple recall attempts with high-quality feedback, (b) align recall prompts with downstream AI tasks to support transfer (TAP), and (c) assess retention and transfer with delayed, transfer-focused assessments. The current evidence base supports the potential benefits of recall-before-AI, but direct causal confirmation in diverse AI-assisted domain tasks requires targeted experimental validation.
