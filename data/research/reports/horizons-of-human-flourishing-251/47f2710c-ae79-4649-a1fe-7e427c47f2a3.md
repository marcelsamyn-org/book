# Automation-Induced Time Reallocation: Welfare and Meaning — A Comprehensive Longitudinal Research Agenda

Executive summary
- Automation generally reconfigures time use by substituting, augmenting, or enabling new tasks. The resulting time reallocation can influence welfare (income, health, well-being) and perceived meaning, particularly when time freed from routine tasks is redirected toward longer-horizon or meaning-rich activities. Key literature emphasizes substitution vs. complementarity, sectoral heterogeneity, and the moderating role of skills and task content (Filippi, Bannò, & Trento, 2023; Autor, 2015; MGI, 2017).
- Longitudinal evidence is still scarce on direct links from automation-driven time reallocation to perceived meaning and engagement in long-term projects. Several strands offer quantitative anchors:
  - Time-use and automation exposure show heterogeneous patterns across sectors and occupations; diffusion does not imply uniform welfare gains (Filippi et al., 2023; Giuntella et al., 2025).
  - AI exposure may shift time toward higher-skill, non-routine tasks when complemented by skills and supportive design; otherwise, substitution can reduce time for meaningful activities (Georgieff & Hyee, 2022; Pizzinelli et al., 2023).
  - Meaning in work relates to well-being and health in broad, multidimensional ways; robust measurement (e.g., Comprehensive Measure of Meaning) can help detect subtle shifts in meaning as time use changes (Hanson & VanderWeele, 2021).
- Actionable design implications include ground-truthing multi-level exposure (sector, occupation, tasks), capturing both objective time-use and subjective meaning, and employing longitudinal designs with mediation/moderation analyses to disentangle how automation reshapes welfare and purpose over time (Filippi et al., 2023; Hanson & VanderWeele, 2021).

1) Research question and motivation
- Core question: How does automation-induced time reallocation influence welfare and meaning? Specifically, can a longitudinal, sector-wide panel track time use, perceived meaning, and engagement in long-term projects as automation intensifies in a sector?
- Why it matters: In post-labor economics, automation changes not only incomes and employment probabilities but also how people experience meaning at work and invest their time. A robust, longitudinal design can reveal whether time freed by automation translates into more meaningful, long-horizon work or whether it erodes purpose due to surveillance, monotony, or misalignment with worker strengths (Filippi et al., 2023; Parker & Grote, 2019).

2) Theoretical framework
- Multilevel, task- and activity-centered view:
  - Automation operates across global-to-work-activity levels, altering tasks, time allocations, and the structure of work activities (Filippi, Bannò, & Trento, 2023).
  - Levels of analysis span global, international, regional, sector, occupation, and worker activities, with work-activities being central for time-use analyses.
- Substitution vs. complementarity:
  - Automation substitutes routine tasks in some contexts while complements non-routine, higher-skill work in others. Complementarity can enhance meaning if workers are engaged in problem-solving, creativity, and relationship-building tasks (Autor, 2015; Felten et al., 2021; Pizzinelli et al., 2023).
- Time-use and meaning linkages:
  - Time-use shifts interact with welfare and meaning via non-monetary channels such as leisure, health, autonomy, and engagement in long-term projects. Meaning, as measured by robust constructs, correlates with well-being and health outcomes (Hanson & VanderWeele, 2021; Steger, 2009; Cohen et al., 2016).
- Data- and design-relevant gaps:
  - The literature highlights a need for longitudinal designs that couple time-use data with measures of perceived meaning and long-term engagement, ideally with multi-level exposure metrics at sector/occupation levels (Filippi et al., 2023).

3) Outcomes and constructs to measure
- Time-use outcomes (primary):
  - Hours/minutes per week in core work tasks, automated tasks, learning/upskilling, collaboration, and long-term projects.
  - Time freed by automation and time reallocation toward higher-value or meaning-rich domains (e.g., problem solving, strategy, R&D).
- Welfare outcomes (secondary):
  - Financial welfare: earnings, income volatility, job security, benefits, consumption-related proxies.
  - Health and well-being: self-rated health, mental health proxies, stress/burnout indicators, general well-being.
  - Work-life balance and leisure: leisure time, sleep, social engagement, and overall life satisfaction.
- Meaning and engagement (central but latent in many datasets):
  - Perceived meaning in work, sense of purpose, and alignment with long-term goals.
  - Engagement in long-term projects: persistence, milestone progress, intrinsic motivation toward sustained initiatives.
  - Use of a robust measure of meaning, such as the Comprehensive Measure of Meaning (CMM), to capture coherence, significance, and direction of meaning in work (Hanson & VanderWeele, 2021).
- Automation exposure and task content:
  - Sector- and occupation-level automation exposure indices (e.g., AIOE, C-AIOE; ESCO-based Digital Competencies Indicator; GENOE task-based exposure) to map diffusion and complementarity (Felten et al., 2021; Pizzinelli et al., 2023; Septiandri et al., 2024; Benitez-Rueda & Parrado, 2024).
  - Task-level content shifts: proportion of tasks that are programmable, substitutable vs. augmentable, and changes in task composition over time.
- Moderators and mediators:
  - Moderators: gender, age/life cycle, education, occupation, sector, unionization, firm size, and policy context (upskilling programs, social protections).
  - Mediators: time reallocation mediating effects on welfare/meaning; job design resources (autonomy, feedback, skill use) as mediators for the meaning-welfare link.

4) Data sources and measurement tools (proposed integration)
- Time-use data:
  - Time-use diaries with high granularity (e.g., 10-minute slots) across days, using standardized instruments (e.g., MTUS/HETUS-compatible diaries; full diaries with location, co-presence, device use). Reference: Time-use diary literature and standardization efforts (UN DESA 2004; Sullivan, Gershuny, & Lamote de Grignon Perez, 2020).
  - Existing panel-time-use data to anchor baseline measures (e.g., ICPSR 9054: Time Use Longitudinal Panel Study, 1975–1981; 223 activity categories; four diaries per adult per year) for conceptual guidance on diary structure and longitudinal design (Juster, Hill, Stafford; ICPSR 9045) [ICPSR 9054].
- Meaning and engagement:
  - Comprehensive Measure of Meaning (CMM) to capture coherent, significant, and directional aspects of meaning in work and long-term projects (Hanson & VanderWeele, 2021).
  - Supplement with validated meaning/purpose scales and engagement/motivation instruments; include qualitative prompts to capture context around long-term projects.
- Welfare outcomes:
  - Objective and subjective welfare: earnings, job security, health outcomes, life satisfaction, burnout indicators; health proxies (self-rated health, mental health metrics).
- Automation exposure and task content:
  - AIOE and C-AIOE measures (Felten et al., 2021; Pizzinelli et al., 2023) and cross-walks with O*NET/ISOC-08; ESCO-based Digital Competencies Indicator (Lennon et al., 2023) for granular occupation- and sector-level exposure.
  - GENOE task-based exposure (Benitez-Rueda & Parrado, 2024) as a supplementary cross-check, and the IFR/Eurostat-based APR indicators (Valentini et al., 2023) for regional exposure patterns.
- Data integration and harmonization:
  - Use multi-country harmonization standards (HETUS, ICATUS) to enable cross-country and cross-sector comparisons; align with ATUS/MTUS frameworks for domestic U.S. data.
  - Link with external automation diffusion indicators (e.g., 1-, 5-, 10-year horizons for automation exposure; multi-source triangulation with BGT postings, AI-patent mappings, and synthetic AI survey methods where feasible).

5) Longitudinal study design (prospective plan)
- Unit of analysis: individuals nested within sectors and regions, tracked over multiple waves; optional sub-samples for high-exposure vs. low-exposure sectors.
- Design type: multi-wave longitudinal panel with annual or biannual data collection over 5–10 years, incorporating cross-sector variation in automation adoption trajectories and policy environments.
- Sample size and structure:
  - Target several thousand respondents per sector, spanning at least 8–12 sectors with varying automation exposure; ensure representation by gender, age, education, and income.
  - Oversample high-exposure occupations where complementarity is likely (to observe potential gains in meaning and long-term engagement) and low-exposure occupations to examine substitution risk.
- Measurement schedule:
  - Wave 1 (baseline): collect time-use diaries (4 diaries per respondent if feasible), CMM-based meaning measures, engagement in long-term projects, welfare indicators, and occupation-level automation exposure indices.
  - Waves 2–5: repeat diary collection and meaning/engagement measures; update automation exposure indices (AIOE, C-AIOE, ESCO-DCI) to reflect diffusion; capture policy/intervention context (training programs, scheduling reforms).
  - Optional: targeted supplementary modules on job design, autonomy, and perceived fairness to illuminate mediating mechanisms.
- Analytic plan:
  - Multi-level longitudinal models (linear mixed models, growth curve modeling) to estimate within-person changes in time use, welfare, and meaning as automation exposure evolves, with random intercepts/slopes for individuals and sectors.
  - Cross-lagged panel models to test reciprocal relations (time use ↔ meaning ↔ welfare) across waves; mediation analysis to test whether time reallocation mediates automation’s impact on welfare/meaning.
  - Difference-in-differences or instrumental-variable approaches around exogenous automation shocks or policy changes to identify causal effects on time use and meaning.
  - Moderation analyses by gender, education, and sector; heterogeneity analyses by exposure level (LE, HELC, HEHC; or AIOE/C-AIOE strata; GENOE-task exposures).
- Data quality and ethics:
  - Ensure privacy and data protection for diary data; implement attrition-mitigation strategies (incentives, regular engagement).
  - Pre-register analysis plans and provide open data/papers where permissible; establish data governance and governance checks for cross-country data sharing.

6) Analytical strategies and expected patterns
- Hypotheses (grounded in the literature):
  - H1: In sectors with strong automation complementarity (high C-AIOE or high ESCO-DCI alignment), time allocated to long-term, meaning-rich tasks will increase or be preserved, with neutral-to-positive welfare outcomes, compared to sectors with low complementarity.
  - H2: In sectors with predominantly substitutive automation (low complementarity), time reallocation will favor shorter-horizon or routine tasks, with potential negative welfare and meaning effects absent adequate retraining and redesign.
  - H3: Meaning and engagement will be positively associated with time spent on long-term projects and with higher job autonomy, skill use, and supportive work design; automation that preserves or enhances autonomy and enables long-horizon work will strengthen the meaning-welfare link.
  - H4: Policy interventions (retraining, flexible schedules, participatory change management) will moderate time-use and meaning dynamics, reducing negative welfare effects and promoting meaning in automated environments.
- Potential models:
  - Multilevel growth models for time-use trajectories; SEM for latent meaning constructs (CMM) with time-use as a mediator.
  - Cross-lagged panel models to examine the directionality between time use, meaning, and welfare across waves.
  - Mediation analyses with time reallocation as a mediator between automation exposure and welfare/meaning outcomes.
  - Moderation analyses to detect differential effects by sector, gender, education, age, and automation-exposure type.

7) Practical considerations and design choices
- Diary methodology:
  - Use validated time-use diary protocols (e.g., ATUS/HETUS-compatible diaries) with 2–4 diary days per wave to balance depth and respondent burden; incorporate 10-minute slot granularity where feasible (Sullivan et al., 2020; UNSD guidelines).
  - Integrate intention-to-participate instructions and prompts regarding meaning engagement; supplement with structured scales for meaning and engagement (e.g., CMM).
- Measurement validation:
  - Validate the meaning measure longitudinally; test measurement invariance across waves and across sectors; triangulate diary entries with self-reported meaning items and qualitative interviews.
- Data integration:
  - Link time-use data with automation-exposure indices (AIOE/C-AIOE; GENOE; ESCO-DCI; APR/Valentini) to assign exposure at the occupation/sector level; adjust for potential measurement error via sensitivity analyses.
- Cross-country coordination:
  - Harmonize data across countries with compatible classifications and time-use instruments; maintain common wave structure where possible to facilitate cross-country comparisons.
- Ethical and policy considerations:
  - Safeguard privacy for diary data; ensure informed consent covers longitudinal follow-up and cross-sector linking; share results with participants and relevant policy stakeholders.

8) Expected contributions and policy relevance
- The proposed longitudinal framework would fill notable gaps in the literature:
  - Direct measurement of time-use changes aligned with automation exposure at sector/occupation levels.
  - Explicit measurement of perceived meaning and engagement in long-term projects as automation evolves.
  - Robust testing of substitution vs. complementarity pathways and their welfare/meaning implications.
- Policy and design implications:
  - Evidence-based guidance on retraining, task redesign, and work design that preserves or enhances meaning while leveraging automation for efficiency.
  - Sector- and region-specific policy design to disseminate complementary capabilities (skills, autonomy, supportive design) that strengthen welfare and meaning under automation.

9) Limitations and potential challenges
- Causal inference challenges: although panel designs can support causal inferences, unobserved confounders and the timing of diffusion may complicate interpretation.
- Measurement complexity: meaning is a multi-faceted construct; ensuring validity across contexts requires careful instrument design and validation.
- Attrition: long-running panels face attrition; must implement robust retention strategies and missing-data handling.
- Data harmonization: cross-country data require careful harmonization of time-use diaries, exposure indices, and meaning measures.

10) Actionable insights for the author (concise checklist)
- Build a sector- and occupation-level longitudinal study incorporating:
  - Time-use diaries with high granularity and repeated waves.
  - Comprehensive meaning/engagement measures (CMM + supplementary scales).
  - Objective and subjective welfare indicators (income, health, well-being, burnout).
  - Multi-source automation exposure indices (AIOE/C-AIOE; ESCO-DCI; GENOE).
  - Moderators: gender, education, age, sector, and policy context.
- Design analyses to test mediation (time use → meaning → welfare/engagement) and moderation (sector, complementarity, exposure type).
- Ensure cross-country comparability with harmonized time-use data standards (HETUS/ICATUS) and standardized exposure metrics.
