# Exposure to Diverse AI Outputs and Calibration of Trust and Resilience to Misinformation: A Comprehensive Report and Research Blueprint

Executive summary
- Objective: Assess how exposure to diverse, non-aggregated AI outputs (simulations of multiple plausible answers) affects users’ calibration of trust and resilience to misinformation, over 6–12 months, across domains with varying stakes.
- State of evidence (cross-study synthesis):
  - Longitudinal trust calibration with diversified AI perspectives: Exposure to AI subsystems with differing accuracies and to multiple AI teammates can shape calibration dynamics. Notable findings include: (i) AI errors exert robust influence on trust adjustments and future decisions; (ii) subsystem-level signals are detectable by users; (iii) trust knowledge acquired during interaction guides future choices; (iv) data-point cues (fact-check cues) modulate trust; (v) individual differences (personality, cognitive load) modulate trust dynamics (Chen, 2022) [Chen, 2022].
  - Dynamic trust calibration mechanisms: A standardized trust-calibration indicator implemented via contextual bandits (CB) can dynamically decide when to rely on AI, yielding substantial performance gains (10–38% reward improvements) across three datasets and in high-stakes domains like disease diagnosis and criminal justice (Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024) [Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024].
  - Non-aggregated outputs in high-stakes diagnostics: Presenting physicians with non-aggregated AI outputs (top-10 differential lists) showed that calibration accuracy predicted diagnostic performance, though overall accuracy gains were not significant in a small sample (Sakamoto et al., 2024) [Sakamoto et al., 2024].
  - Cognitive forcing functions to reduce overreliance: Active Verification (requiring users to answer content-specific questions prior to accepting AI suggestions) significantly improves decision accuracy and reduces overreliance relative to other interventions in a controlled study (Doh, 2025) [Doh, 2025].
  - Broad evidence on misinformation and AI outputs: Scoping reviews and field experiments indicate AI can both contribute to misinformation diffusion and aid countermeasures (prebunking/debunking); effects on trust are domain- and context-dependent, with long-horizon evidence still scarce (Park & Nan, 2025) [Park & Nan, 2025].
  - Education and epistemic hygiene: Tools and curricula that promote exposure to diverse perspectives, critical evaluation, and transparency can support epistemic hygiene and calibrated trust over time (Vanderlaan et al., 2025) [Vanderlaan et al., 2025].
- Core takeaway: Existing evidence supports the plausibility that exposing users to diverse AI perspectives, when paired with uncertainty signaling, subsystem-level diagnostics, transparent data cues, and metacognitive prompts, can improve trust calibration and resilience to misinformation over time. However, longitudinal, cross-domain empirical data explicitly testing 6–12 months of such exposure across high- and low-stakes domains remain limited. A carefully designed longitudinal study, integrating diverse AI outputs with structured epistemic hygiene interventions, is warranted to answer the core question definitively (see a detailed study blueprint below).

1) Introduction: framing the research question
- Research question restated: How does exposure to diverse, non-aggregated AI outputs (simulations of multiple plausible answers) affect users’ calibration of trust and resilience to misinformation, over 6–12 months across domains with varying stakes?
- Key concepts:
  - Trust calibration: alignment between a user’s trust in AI outputs and the AI’s actual reliability; miscalibration can manifest as overtrust (automation bias) or undertrust (missed valuable input) (Chen, 2022; Henry & Santos Jr, 2024) [Chen, 2022; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024].
  - Diverse AI outputs (non-aggregated perspectives): exposures to multiple plausible answers generated by distinct AI subsystems or models, designed to surface domain-relevant disagreement, error modes, and reasoning processes (Chen, 2022; Sakamoto et al., 2024; Vanderlaan et al., 2025) [Chen, 2022; Sakamoto et al., 2024; Vanderlaan et al., 2025].
  - Resilience to misinformation: the degree to which users resist, detect, or debunk false or misleading AI outputs; moderated by exposure to diverse perspectives and by epistemic hygiene interventions (Park & Nan, 2025; Doh, 2025) [Park & Nan, 2025; Doh, 2025].
  - Stakes: high-stakes domains (health, justice, finance) versus low-stakes domains (information screening, everyday tasks); stakes influence calibration dynamics and susceptibility to misinformation (Chen, 2022; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024; Kostick-Quenet et al., 2024) [Chen, 2022; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024; Kostick-Quenet et al., 2024].

2) Synthesis of relevant findings (inline citations)
- Trust calibration under diversified AI perspectives:
  - Chen (2022): Longitudinal study indicates AI performance errors have outsized effects on trust adjustment; users can perceive subsystem-level performance given diverse AI outputs; training data cues influence trust; personality and cognitive load modulate trust dynamics (Chen, 2022) [Chen, 2022].
  - Sakamoto et al. (2024): Non-aggregated AI outputs (top-10 differential lists) enable calibration tasks; calibration accuracy predicts diagnostic performance (aOR 5.90); however, overall diagnostic accuracy did not significantly differ with the intervention in a small sample (Sakamoto et al., 2024) [Sakamoto et al., 2024].
- Dynamic, context-sensitive trust signals:
  - Henrique & Santos Jr (2024): Contextual Bandits and standardized trust-calibration measures yield substantial performance gains (10–38% reward improvements) across three datasets, including high-stakes domains; exposure to diverse AI inputs can be leveraged via context signals to optimize trust decisions (Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024) [Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024].
- Overreliance and cognitive forcing:
  - Doh (2025): Cognitive Forcing Functions, especially Active Verification, reduce overreliance and improve decision accuracy in AI-assisted tasks; trust dynamics vary with NFC, task complexity, and education (Doh, 2025) [Doh, 2025].
- Misinformation, prebunking, and debunking:
  - Park & Nan (2025): Scoping review finds both mitigation potential and mixed results; long-term, cross-domain studies are needed; prebunking and debunking show promise but evidence is not uniform (Park & Nan, 2025) [Park & Nan, 2025].
- Epistemic hygiene and education:
  - Vanderlaan et al. (2025): Educational toolkits demonstrating exposure to multiple perspectives and epistemic-hygiene practices; suggests long-term study designs to assess calibration and resilience across domains (Vanderlaan et al., 2025) [Vanderlaan et al., 2025].

3) Quantitative findings at a glance (selected, with caveats)
- Sakamoto et al., 2024 (clinical task with non-aggregated AI outputs):
  - Intervention vs control diagnostic accuracy: 41.5% vs 46% (not significant; P = .27; 95% CI -0.75 to 2.55) [Sakamoto et al., 2024].
  - Trust calibration accuracy: 61.5% overall; calibration accuracy significantly predicted diagnostic accuracy (aOR 5.90; 95% CI 2.93–12.46; P < .001) [Sakamoto et al., 2024].
- Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024 (CB-based trust calibration):
  - Reported 10%–38% improvements in reward metrics across three datasets when using the trust-calibration indicator (CB) in decision-making tasks, including high-stakes domains (disease diagnosis, criminal justice) [Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024].
- Chen, 2022 (longitudinal trust with diverse AI subsystems):
  - Qualitative emphasis: AI errors have large impact on trust; subsystem-level perception is possible; trust knowledge informs future decisions; data-point cues influence trust; personality and cognitive load modulate trust dynamics (Chen, 2022).
- Doh, 2025 (HASC/CFFs):
  - Active Verification yields the strongest improvements in accuracy and the largest reduction in overreliance versus alternatives; NFC, task complexity, and education moderate effects (Doh, 2025).
- Park & Nan, 2025 (misinformation scoping):
  - 24 empirical studies; AI-generated misinformation can reduce trust; prebunking/debunking show promise; long-term cross-domain evidence lacking (Park & Nan, 2025).
- Vanderlaan et al., 2025 (nurse education context):
  - Exposure to multiple perspectives and epistemic-hygiene practices; no longitudinal data reported; design principles for long-horizon studies (Vanderlaan et al., 2025).

4) Domains and stakes: how findings map to high- vs. low-stakes contexts
- High-stakes domains (health, law, finance, criminal justice):
  - Evidence suggests calibration and resilience are critical; dynamic trust signals and diverse perspectives can support calibrated reliance when combined with uncertainty signaling and fact-check cues (Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024; Sakamoto et al., 2024; Kostick-Quenet et al., 2024).
- Low-stakes domains (information screening, everyday tasks):
  - Evidence from misinformation studies indicates exposure to AI content can influence trust and engagement; prebunking or debunking can be effective but long-term transfer remains uncertain (Park & Nan, 2025).
- Cross-domain synthesis:
  - The strongest current signal is that calibrated trust and resilience require explicit signals about AI reliability, uncertainty, and rationale; exposing users to diverse outputs appears promising but needs rigorous longitudinal testing across multiple domains with clearly defined stakes (Chen, 2022; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024; Doh, 2025; Park & Nan, 2025).

5) Proposed longitudinal study blueprint (6–12 months)

A. Objective
- To empirically test whether exposure to diverse, non-aggregated AI outputs improves trust calibration and resilience to misinformation across high- and low-stakes domains over 6–12 months.

B. Design (multi-arm, longitudinal, cross-domain)
- Population: Adults stratified by expertise and domain interest; include professionals in at least two high-stakes domains (e.g., healthcare, finance/legal) and two low-stakes domains (e.g., information screening, consumer advice).
- Conditions:
  1) Diversity Outputs with uncertainty signals (non-aggregated, multiple plausible outputs; explicit uncertainty for each output; provenance cues).
  2) Aggregated Output (single synthesized output; standard reliability cues).
  3) Diversity Outputs without uncertainty signals (to test the additive value of uncertainty signaling).
  4) Control (no AI outputs; baseline task performance).
- Stakes design: Each domain contains high-stakes and low-stakes tasks, with clearly defined consequences (monetary, health, safety) to reflect domain variability.
- Duration and waves: Baseline, 3 months, 6 months, 9 months, 12 months (five waves).

C. Measures (per wave)
- Trust calibration metrics:
  - Calibration curves comparing confidence estimates to actual accuracy.
  - Brier score (probabilistic accuracy) for AI-output judgments.
  - Calibration distance metrics as defined in contextual-trust literature (e.g., G vs g with a standardized indicator).
- Misinformation resilience:
  - Susceptibility to misleading AI outputs; rate of belief revision after corrective information; persistence of corrected beliefs over time.
- Epistemic hygiene indicators:
  - Propensity to verify outputs, use of external sources, and willingness to challenge AI outputs.
- Cognitive and contextual moderators:
  - Need for Cognition (NFC), cognitive load, domain expertise, education level.
- Process variables:
  - Perceived transparency, usefulness of explanations, trust in the AI provider, and perceived provenance.
- Behavioral measures:
  - Task performance, reliance on AI outputs, time-to-decision, and error patterns.

D. Interventions and supports
- Training modules on interpreting non-aggregated outputs, uncertainty, and provenance.
- Metacognitive prompts at each wave to encourage justification and cross-checking.
- Retrieval-grounded outputs where feasible to anchor outputs in external sources.

E. Analysis plan
- Linear mixed-effects models or Bayesian hierarchical models to capture within-subject trajectories and cross-domain interactions.
- Mediation analyses to test whether uncertainty signaling and epistemic hygiene mediate the effect of diversity exposure on calibration and resilience.
- Pre-registered hypotheses and reporting to enable cross-study comparability.

F. Ethical and governance considerations
- IRB approval, informed consent, data privacy; transparent reporting of prompts, AI tools, and evaluation criteria; data sharing under open science practices where appropriate.

G. Anticipated outcomes and interpretation
- Hypotheses to test:
  - H1: Exposure to diverse AI outputs improves trust calibration stability over 6–12 months, especially when uncertainty signaling is included.
  - H2: Diversity exposure increases resilience to misinformation in high-stakes domains more than in low-stakes domains.
  - H3: Uncertainty signaling mediates the relationship between output diversity and improved calibration.
- Contingencies and risks:
  - Cognitive load may rise with diverse outputs; design interfaces to mitigate overload.
  - Longitudinal attrition; implement engagement strategies and retention incentives.

6) Practical recommendations for researchers and practitioners (actionable insights)
- Design for epistemic hygiene:
  - Surface multiple plausible outputs with explicit uncertainty and provenance; train users to verify and cross-check AI outputs.
- Use diverse AI perspectives:
  - Simulate multiple AI viewpoints across subsystems or models, ensuring varied reasoning styles and error profiles to surface domain-specific trust signals.
- Calibrate trust with measurable signals:
  - Include objective calibration metrics (calibration curves, Brier scores) alongside subjective trust scales; use standardized metrics to enable cross-study comparability.
- Domain-ststakes considerations:
  - Explicitly manipulate stakes to observe differential calibration and misinformation resilience; high-stakes domains should incorporate stronger verification prompts and oversight.
- Interventions to mitigate dependency:
  - Combine output diversity with cognitive forcing functions (e.g., Active Verification) or CB-based dynamic trust indicators to improve decision quality and reduce overreliance (Doh, 2025; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024).
- Reporting and preregistration:
  - Pre-register hypotheses, data collection plans, and analysis strategies; publish methods and materials for replication, including prompts, AI outputs, and evaluation rubrics.
- Governance and ethics:
  - Include provenance labeling, transparent data usage, and domain-specific ethical safeguards; adapt governance frameworks to the domain stakes and regulatory environment.

7) Key limitations and caveats
- Although multiple sources point to the potential benefits of exposure to diverse AI outputs for calibration and resilience, there is a paucity of longitudinal, cross-domain empirical data specifically testing 6–12 months of such exposure. The proposed design is thus a forward-looking blueprint informed by Chen (2022), Henrique & Santos Jr (2024), Sakamoto et al. (2024), Park & Nan (2025), and Doh (2025) [Chen, 2022; Henrique & Santos Jr, 2024; Sakamoto et al., 2024; Park & Nan, 2025; Doh, 2025].
- Generalizability across domains depends on stakes, domain conventions, and user populations; careful domain selection and stakeholder engagement are essential.
