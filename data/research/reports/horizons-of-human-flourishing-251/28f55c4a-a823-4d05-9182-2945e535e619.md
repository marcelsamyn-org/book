# report.md

Do accuracy nudges and inoculation messaging reduce susceptibility to AI-generated misinformation among habitual AI users, and how do effects vary with cognitive ability, prior skepticism, and task difficulty?

Executive summary
- Across a broad literature base, inoculation (prebunking) and accuracy nudges are two strands of misinformation-mitigation that can operate via different mechanisms (pre-exposure defenses vs. attention-shaping prompts). In general, prebunking shows robust, scalable effects on reducing susceptibility and improving discernment, while accuracy nudges tend to produce smaller, more conditions-dependent gains; combining approaches can yield stronger effects than either alone (Lu et al., 2023; Roozenbeek et al., 2022; Pennycook & Rand, 2022a).
- In AI-relevant contexts, evidence directly targeting AI-generated misinformation or habitual AI users is sparse. Existing AI-focused studies indicate that:
  - AI-generated content labels can modulate perceived credibility and may shift processing, but main effects are often modest and contingent on content type and domain (Li & Yang, 2024; Ansgariusson, 2024).
  - Forewarnings about AI hallucinations show effect sizes in the moderate range for reducing misinformation acceptance, with analytic thinking and thinking styles moderating effects in some studies (Hwang & Jeong 2025).
  - Debunking, inoculation, and multi-component approaches appear more effective when used together, particularly for AI-generated misinformation (Spearing et al., 2025).
- Moderators relevant to cognition and prior beliefs—such as cognitive ability, prior skepticism, and task difficulty—are variably reported across studies. Evidence suggests:
  - Cognitive ability and analytic thinking can strengthen or attenuate inoculation/nudge effects depending on the context (Ahmed 2021; Lu et al., 2023; Pennycook & Rand 2022).
  - Prior skepticism often interacts with intervention efficacy, sometimes enhancing gains for high-skeptic or high-susceptibility groups, though effects are domain- and modality-specific (Lu et al., 2023; Roozenbeek et al., 2022; Basol et al., 2020).
  - Task difficulty and content modality (text vs image/video; AI-generated vs human-generated) shape intervention effectiveness; effects tend to be stronger for simpler/transparent misinformation and can weaken under higher cognitive load or more deceptive formats (Pennycook & Rand 2022a; Li & Yang 2024; Ansgariusson 2024).
- Actionable implications for authors/researchers aiming to bolster AI-literacy and reduce AI-generated misinformation among habitual AI users:
  - Employ multi-component interventions (inoculation + accuracy nudges) with booster reinforcement to sustain effects over time.
  - Design content and delivery to reflect the cognitive profiles and skepticism levels of target populations; tailor interventions for habitual AI users who routinely encounter AI-generated content.
  - Include explicit measures of cognitive ability proxies, prior skepticism, and task difficulty as moderators to understand for whom interventions work best and under what conditions.
  - Test effectiveness across AI-related domains (deepfakes, AI-generated text, AI-curated feeds) and across languages/cultures to improve generalizability.
  - Report standardized effect sizes and provide pre-registered analysis plans to enable meta-analytic synthesis and cross-study comparisons.

Important inline citations: (Lu et al., 2023); (Roozenbeek et al., 2022); (Pennycook & Rand, 2022a); (Pennycook et al., 2020, 2021); (Li & Yang, 2024); (Spearing et al., 2025); (Ansgariusson, 2024); (Hwang & Jeong, 2025); (Basol et al., 2020)

1) Background and framing

- What accuracy nudges and inoculation messaging are
  - Accuracy nudges (prompts or labels that direct attention to accuracy) aim to shift cognitive processing toward veracity during information evaluation, thereby reducing sharing of misinformation (Pennycook & Rand, 2022a; 2022b; Epstein et al., 2021).
  - Inoculation (prebunking) pre-exposes people to weakened misinformation arguments plus refutations or warnings, aiming to build enduring resistance to future misinformation (McGuire, 1964; Roozenbeek et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023).
  - Debunking (post-exposure corrections) can reduce belief in misinformation but suffers from continued influence effects unless paired with prebunking (Ecker et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023).
- Relevance to AI misinformation
  - AI-generated misinformation introduces new challenges (hallucinations, synthetic media), with both supply-side (model faults, data issues) and demand-side (processing style, trust) dimensions. The literature advocates a layered approach: improve model governance and data quality while also arming users with inoculation and accuracy-promoting cues (Shao, 2025; Feuerriegel et al., 2023; Shin & Akhtar 2024; Roozenbeek et al., 2022).
  - Several AI-specific interventions exist (AIGC content labeling, inoculation-like forewarnings, and debunking) but robust, generalizable evidence—especially among habitual AI users—remains limited (Li & Yang, 2024; Ansgariusson, 2024; Spearing et al., 2025).

2) Scope and domain coverage of key interventions (with representative findings)

- Inoculation (general misinformation)
  - Roozenbeek et al. (Science Advances, 2022) show that a 15-minute inoculation game (Bad News) targeting manipulation techniques reduces susceptibility to real-world misinformation across six techniques, with large field-study validation on YouTube (n ≈ 22k). Effects persisted under booster reinforcement; cross-cultural replication observed in multiple languages (Basol et al., 2020; Roozenbeek et al., 2022). Effect sizes in individual technique domains typically ranged from small to moderate (e.g., d ≈ 0.15–0.24 per technique) for fake items; larger gains for the most susceptible participants.
  - Lu et al. (JMIR 2023) synthesize 42 studies (n ≈ 42,530) showing misinformation credibility reduction (d ≈ -0.36) and improvements in discernment (d ≈ 0.20–0.18 for various outcomes) with inoculation; durability varies with contexts and formats (pre-post designs tend to show stronger immediate effects; booster sessions enhance durability; climate vs other topics show variance) (Lu et al., 2023).
  - Ansgariusson (2024, master’s thesis) finds that AIGC labeling can modulate credibility of synthetic vs authentic images, with a moderation role for Bullshit Receptivity (BRS) — indicating individual differences in reflexive thinking influence inoculation-like effects on AI imagery.

- Accuracy nudges (without inoculation)
  - Pennycook & Rand (Nature Communications, 2022a) synthesize 20 experiments showing that accuracy prompts reduce sharing of false headlines by roughly 10% relative to control; effects are stronger for older, more reflective participants and less moderated by demographic variables; a dissociation exists between accuracy judgments and sharing, but nudges can align behavior with veracity by shifting attention (Pennycook & Rand, 2022a).
  - Fujimoto, Tanaka, and Inuzuka (Health Communication, 2025) show that an accuracy nudge alone reduces fake-headline sharing; effectiveness is enhanced when combined with a warning label; the pairing yields stronger effects than either alone, though the warning-alone condition does not gain enhanced effects when paired with the nudge (Fujimoto et al., 2025).
  - Li & Yang (JMIR Form Res, 2024) test AIGC labels as nudges; main effects of labeling are not significant on perceived accuracy, credibility, or sharing; however, information-type and content-category interactions exist (e.g., stronger perceived accuracy differences for accurate vs inaccurate content; for-profit vs not-for-profit categories). This suggests that labeling alone may be insufficient in AI contexts without broader inoculation or content-specific tailoring (Li & Yang, 2024).

- Debunking and multi-component approaches
  - Spearing et al. (2025) find that inoculation alone reduces trust in AI-generated misinformation but does not reduce its influence on reasoning; debunking reduces misinformation impact; only when inoculation and debunking are combined does misinformation influence disappear in the experiments (Experiment 2). This emphasizes the value of layered interventions for AI misinformation contexts (Spearing et al., 2025).
  - Cat Park (Ansgariusson, 2024) extends inoculation to multimodal AI imagery; inoculation reduces credibility of synthetic images (and increases credibility of authentic images) with a moderation signal from Bullshit Receptivity (BRS). It does not significantly alter perceived profundness for synthetic images, suggesting boundaries to inoculation effects in visual AI-misinformation domains.

3) Moderators and boundary conditions observed across studies

- Cognitive ability and analytic thinking
  - Ahmed (2021) reports that higher cognitive ability relates to greater skepticism toward social-media news; effects differ by deepfake modality and are stronger for lower cognitive ability when worry about deepfakes interacts with sharing. This pattern implies cognitive ability can moderate susceptibility and intervention efficacy, though direct moderator analyses in AI contexts are sparse (Ahmed, 2021).
  - Lu et al. (2023) identify cognitive engagement as a moderator in broader inoculation findings; older, more reflective participants show larger effects for accuracy nudges, suggesting cognitive ability-like proxies contribute to efficacy (Lu et al., 2023; Pennycook & Rand 2022a).
- Prior skepticism and belief priors
  - Inoculation effects tend to be larger for individuals with beliefs far from the truth or with higher susceptibility to misinformation, consistent across topics and contexts (Roozenbeek et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023).
  - In AI contexts, skepticism about AI and prior exposure to AI misinformation may shape responsiveness to inoculation and labeling (Shao, 2025; Shin & Akhtar 2024).
- Task difficulty and content modality
  - Task difficulty and cognitive load appear to affect intervention efficacy; higher difficulty often attenuates inoculation benefits unless booster reinforcement is provided (Pennycook & Rand 2022a; Lu et al., 2023).
  - Multimodal misinformation (images/videos) presents distinct challenges; Cat Park shows inoculation can reduce credibility of AI-generated imagery, but depth of processing (profiling of BRS) modulates effects (Ansgariusson, 2024; Neylan et al., 2023).

4) Implications for habitual AI users and AI-generated misinformation

- Do accuracy nudges and inoculation messaging reduce susceptibility among habitual AI users?
  - Indirect evidence suggests inoculation strategies generalize across misinformation domains and can be scaled (Roozenbeek et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023). However, direct evidence for habitual AI users facing AI-generated misinformation is sparse. Some AI-specific studies indicate labeling, forewarnings, and inoculation concepts can influence processing, but robust, domain-specific studies with habitual AI users are needed (Li & Yang, 2024; Ansgariusson, 2024; Spearing et al., 2025).
- How do effects vary with cognitive ability, prior skepticism, and task difficulty?
  - Across studies, higher cognitive ability/analytic thinking is associated with greater susceptibility resistance and stronger intervention effects in some contexts; prior skepticism often enhances inoculation/nudging effectiveness for mis/disinformation. Task difficulty moderates effects: higher difficulty reduces effectiveness of single interventions, but combinations (inoculation + debunking or inoculation + accuracy nudges) show stronger resilience even under tougher tasks (Pennycook & Rand 2022a; Lu et al., 2023; Spearing et al., 2025).
- Practical takeaways for AI-literacy initiatives
  - A layered approach is advisable: inoculation (prebunking) to build resistance, accuracy nudges to direct attention to veracity, and debunking to rectify exposure, supported by booster programs to sustain effects.
  - Adapt interventions to population characteristics: tailor inoculation content to cognitive-ability levels and existing skepticism; diversify content by modality (text, images, video) to address AI-generated misinformation’s multimodal nature.
  - Use booster strategies and repeated exposures to overcome decay and to sustain durable resistance, especially for habitual AI users exposed to AI-generated misinformation in dynamic AI ecosystems.

5) Recommendations for a concrete research plan (to address gaps)

- Objective
  - Do accuracy nudges and inoculation messaging reduce susceptibility to AI-generated misinformation among habitual AI users? How do effects vary with cognitive ability, prior skepticism, and task difficulty?
- Design
  - Factorial randomized design (2x2x2): Inoculation (present vs absent) × Accuracy nudge (present vs absent) × AI-content type (AI-generated misinformation vs non-AI misinformation) in a sample of habitual AI users.
  - Moderator measurements: cognitive ability (brief cognitive battery), prior skepticism toward AI/misinformation (validated scales), task difficulty (manipulated complexity of AI-generated content; images, video, or text; simple vs complex inference).
  - Outcomes: susceptibility (veracity judgments), sharing intentions, actual sharing or verification behaviors (where possible), credibility assessments for true vs false AI-generated content, and engagement metrics (time spent verifying, number of checks accessed).
  - Design considerations: include booster sessions (monthly, quarterly) to test durability; cross-cultural replication with diverse populations; multi-modal AI content (deepfakes, AI-generated text, synthetic audio).
- Analytic plan
  - Mixed-effects models to accommodate content-level random effects and participant-level random effects.
  - Report effect sizes (Cohen’s d or odds ratios), 95% CIs, and p-values for main effects and all interactions (including moderation terms: inoculation × cognitive ability, inoculation × skepticism, accuracy nudge × task difficulty).
  - Pre-register hypotheses and analysis code; share materials openly to enable replication.
- Practical plan (for implementation)
  - Develop modular inoculation content (text + short videos + interactive tasks) to be deployed in AI-enabled platforms (social feeds, AI assistants, search interfaces).
  - Integrate lightweight accuracy nudges (e.g., quick prompts before AI content, prompts to verify sources) that can be easily embedded in platforms.
  - Pilot in controlled lab-like settings followed by a field study on a platform with habitual AI usage (e.g., social media with AI-generated recommendations or AI-assisted content creation features).
- Evaluation and reporting
  - Transparent reporting of all effect sizes and confidence intervals; stratified analyses by cognitive ability and skepticism; robustness checks for different demographic groups and content types.
  - Clear articulation of limitations and boundary conditions, including potential cultural differences and platform-specific dynamics.
- Ethical considerations
  - Ensure ethical testing with careful de-biasing, avoid over-censorship or manipulation; debrief participants on AI-generated content risks and safeguards.

6) Practical implications for AI literacy practitioners and platforms

- If proven effective, implement multi-layer defenses in AI-rich information ecosystems:
  - Pre-exposure inoculation modules (gamified or video-based) taught at scale via public platforms, aligned with consensus messaging where appropriate.
  - On-platform accuracy nudges that direct attention toward veracity before users share AI-generated content.
  - Post-exposure debunking resources integrated into AI-assisted interfaces or after verification attempts.
- Booster scheduling and adaptive delivery
  - Implement booster inoculations at spaced intervals to sustain resilience; tailor booster frequency to user-skill profiles and content-domain risk.
- Measurement and accountability
  - Establish standardized metrics for AI misinformation susceptibility, including exposure to AI-generated misinformation and actual sharing/verification behavior; publish data to enable cross-study comparisons.

Notes and caveats
- A large portion of the evidence summarized above comes from diverse domains (health, climate, political misinformation, environmental misinformation) and from different target populations (general adults, students, online platform users, cross-cultural samples). Direct extrapolation to habitual AI users and to AI-generated misinformation requires dedicated, domain-specific experimental work.
- Where possible, we recommend using robust, preregistered designs with explicit moderator analyses (cognitive ability, prior skepticism, task difficulty) and reporting of effect sizes to facilitate cross-study synthesis and meta-analytic integration in AI-context misinformation research.

Appendix: shorthand glossary
- Inoculation/prebunking: pre-exposure defenses against misinformation by forewarning and refutational preemption.
- Accuracy nudges: prompts or cues that direct attention to accuracy during information processing and sharing decisions.
- Debunking: corrective information provided after exposure to misinformation.
- AI-generated misinformation (AIM): misinformation produced by AI systems (text, images, deepfakes, or other media) that may appear credible.
- Habitual AI users: individuals who routinely use AI tools in information-seeking, content creation, or decision-making contexts.
- Task difficulty: cognitive load or complexity of the misinformation task (e.g., ambiguous claims, multi-claim reasoning).
