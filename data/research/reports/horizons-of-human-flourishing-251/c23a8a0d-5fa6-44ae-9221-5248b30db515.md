Causal Effects of AI-enabled Collaborative Scaffolds on Relationship Quality, Trust, and Joint Problem-Solving in Cross-Disciplinary Teams over 12 Months: A Theory-to-Design Synthesis and Longitudinal Plan

Executive summary
- What the question asks: Do AI-enabled collaborative scaffolds—specifically shared mental models (SMMs), role rotation, and AI moderation—causally improve relationship quality, trust, and joint problem-solving success in cross-disciplinary teams over a 12-month horizon?
- What the current evidence suggests, at a high level:
  - SMMs and trust are central levers for effective human–AI teamwork, with stronger SMM alignment generally linked to better coordination and joint problem solving in mixed teams (Andrews et al., 2022; Schelble, 2023). However, direct, longitudinal, causal estimates for the bundle of scaffolds (SMMs, role rotation, AI moderation) in cross-disciplinary teams over 12 months are not yet established in the extant literature.
  - Trust dynamics in human–AI teams are nuanced: trust tends to depend on perceived trustworthiness and similarity to humans, and can vary by team composition (dyads vs. triads) and the presence of humans in the team (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024). This implies scaffold design should emphasize perceived AI trustworthiness and human–AI similarity to sustain relationship quality over time.
  - Several promising scaffolds and measurement tools exist (e.g., SMM measurement tools, trust scales, AI explainability features) that can be integrated into a longitudinal experimental design; but rigorous causal, longitudinal evidence for the specific trio of scaffolds in cross-disciplinary teams remains an open research agenda (Andrews et al., 2022; McGrath et al., 2025; Filho et al., 2022; Ulfert et al., 2023; Lane et al., 2024).
- Proposed path forward: A rigorous, longitudinal, multi-arm study (ideally randomized or quasi-experimental) over 12 months, with explicit operationalizations of the three scaffolds, rich measurement of SMMs/trust/relationship quality, and objective joint problem-solving outcomes. The plan leverages established measures (trust scales, SMM instruments) and contemporary AI-teaming frameworks to enable causal inferences about scaffolds’ effects over time (see Methods section).

1) Background and research question framing
- Core constructs:
  - Shared Mental Models (SMMs) / Shared cognition: systematic alignment of task understanding and team state that enables coordinated action (Andrews et al., 2022; DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus, 2010; Cannon-Bowers & Salas, 1998).
  - Trust in AI teammates: multi-dimensional (cognitive, affective) and affected by AI reliability, explainability, and perceived similarity to human teammates (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024; Hancock et al., 2013; Glikson & Woolley, 2020).
  - Relationship quality and joint problem solving: relational ease, psychological safety, coordination quality, and successful collaborative outcomes on joint tasks.
- Why cross-disciplinary teams and 12 months?
  - Cross-disciplinary contexts introduce domain heterogeneity in mental models and communication norms; longitudinal dynamics are essential to understand the formation, stabilization, or decay of SMMs and trust, and how they translate into joint problem-solving success over time (Andrews et al., 2022; Schelble, 2023; Ulfert et al., 2023; Lane et al., 2024).
- Research question stated for this report:
  - What is the causal effect of AI-enabled collaborative scaffolds (shared mental models, role rotation, AI moderation) on relationship quality, trust, and joint problem-solving success in cross-disciplinary teams over 12 months?

2) What we know from the evidence landscape (key findings and gaps)
- Shared Mental Models in Human-AI Teams (theory and measurement challenges)
  - A synthesized theory indicates SMMs predict coordination and performance in human–AI teams, but the field lacks consistent terminology and standardized measures for Human-AI SMMs, complicating causal inference and longitudinal designs (Andrews et al., 2022).
  - Measurement: multiple approaches exist (content, structure, emergence), with no universally accepted SMM metric for H-AI contexts; design considerations emphasize bi-directional MM formation and the role of explainable AI (Andrews et al., 2022; DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus, 2010).
- Longitudinal evidence and scaffolds
  - Schelble (2023) reports three interrelated studies in human–AI teams:
    - Study 1: SMM development in HH vs. H-A teams; explicit shared goals and action-related communication accelerate SMM development; trust toward AI is weaker in AI-dominant compositions; SMM similarity can be inconsistent in H-A contexts.
    - Study 2: Information-sharing attributes (including explainability and SA updates) strongly influence attitudes toward AI and perceived team cognition.
    - Study 3: AI memory-enabled SA, and later AI participation in discussions, improve SMMs, SA, and resilience; role/agency features and structured AI participation support team performance.
  - These findings support plausible causal pathways from scaffolds to SMMs to trust and joint problem solving, but they stop short of a 12-month, cross-disciplinary causal estimate for the three scaffolds bundled together, and they do not test role rotation or AI moderation as explicit interventions in real cross-disciplinary teams across a year (Schelble, 2023).
- Trust dynamics in H-A teams and dyad vs triad configurations
  - Georganta & Ulfert (2024) show trust differences by team composition: dyads experience lower trust in AI teammates when AI is present, mediated by perceived trustworthiness and similarity; triads show no difference in team trust by composition. This implies scaffold effectiveness may depend on team size and human presence and underscores the need to tailor scaffolds to composition (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024).
- Measurement tools of trust and SMM
  - Validated trust scales exist for AI contexts (TIAS and S-TIAS) with strong reliability, suggesting trust can be measured frequently in longitudinal designs, including cross-cultural contexts (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - SMM measurement tools exist (TMMI; 11-item instrument for SMM and Complementary Mental Models; sport-domain validation) that could be adapted to AI contexts with careful validation; cross-domain generalizability and longitudinal reliability remain open (Filho et al., 2022).
- Gaps that motivate the proposed longitudinal study
  - No published 12-month causal estimates for the joint bundle of scaffolds (SMM, role rotation, AI moderation) in cross-disciplinary teams.
  - No standardized, cross-domain SMM instrument validated for AI-enabled cross-disciplinary contexts with repeated measures over time.
  - Need for multi-method, longitudinal designs that combine trust, relationship quality, SMMs, and joint problem solving with objective performance metrics.

3) Conceptual framework and causal pathways to test (proposed)
- Core causal chain (proposed):
  - AI-enabled scaffolds (SMM alignment facilitation, role rotation, AI moderation) → formation/strengthening of human–AI SMMs and cognitive alignment (SMM/CMM/SMM similarity) → improvements in relationship quality and trust (including trust in AI) → enhanced joint problem-solving performance and coordination over time.
- Mediators and moderators:
  - Mediators: SMM alignment (Human–AI) and trust development; perhaps AI transparency as a moderator of SMM formation.
  - Moderators: team size (dyad vs triad), domain diversity, prior collaboration with AI, perceived AI reliability, and cultural/domain context.
- Measurement targets:
  - SMMs: use adapted SMM instruments (or a combination of DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus measurement approaches and TMMI), with attention to content, structure, and emergence in H-A contexts.
  - Trust: use TIAS/S-TIAS (short form for frequent measurements) plus Mayer et al. trust dimensions (ability, benevolence, integrity) for triangulation (McGrath et al., 2025; Mayer et al., 1995).
  - Relationship quality: scales capturing interpersonal trust, cohesion, psychological safety, and perceived collaboration quality.
  - Joint problem solving: objective performance metrics and process indicators (coordination quality, information sharing, time-to-solution, solution quality).

4) Proposed longitudinal study design (to estimate causal effects over 12 months)
- Design type
  - Multi-arm randomized or quasi-experimental longitudinal study with cross-disciplinary teams.
  - Arms:
    - A) Shared mental models scaffolds (explicit alignment sessions, joint MM artifacts, AI-facilitated MM prompts).
    - B) Role rotation scaffolds (systematic rotation of roles with AI support; defined agency levels for AI).
    - C) AI moderation scaffolds (AI-facilitated moderation to balance participation and mediating conflicts).
    - D) Combined scaffolds (A+B+C).
    - E) Control condition (standard AI integration without explicit scaffold features).
- Participants and setting
  - Cross-disciplinary teams of 4–6 members plus an AI teammate or AI-enabled scaffold; teams stratified by composition (2-person vs 3-person teams) and domain diversity.
  - Realistic cross-disciplinary tasks that require integration across domains (e.g., design/problem-solving tasks with multiple knowledge domains).
- Timeframe and measurements
  - Timepoints: baseline (pre-intervention), 3 months, 6 months, 9 months, 12 months.
  - Primary outcomes: relationship quality, trust (in AI and in the team), and joint problem-solving success.
  - Mediators: SMM alignment (human–AI), perceived AI transparency, AI explainability, SA updates.
  - Secondary: AI task performance metrics, coordination indicators, and qualitative process data (interviews or diary entries).
- Data collection methods
  - Quantitative: validated scales (TIAS/S-TIAS; trust in AI; relationship quality; SMM proxies; joint problem-solving metrics).
  - Qualitative: semi-structured interviews to uncover mechanisms of SMM formation, trust development, and coordination practices.
  - Behavioral logs: collaboration platform data, transcripts, and task-artifact metrics to quantify information sharing and coordination.
- Analytic plan
  - Primary method: longitudinal, multilevel structural equation modeling (ML-SEM) or cross-lagged panel models to test causal sequences (scaffolds → SMM alignment → trust/relationship quality → joint problem solving).
  - Mediation analysis: test whether SMM alignment mediates the scaffold → trust/relationship quality → joint problem solving path; bootstrap confidence intervals for indirect effects.
  - Moderation analysis: test whether effects differ by team size (dyad vs triad), domain diversity, and initial trust baseline.
  - Robustness: sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding; alternative lag structures (e.g., 2–3 month lags) to check directional effects.
- Measurement instruments (recommended and grounded in the literature)
  - Trust
    - TIAS (Trust in Automation Scale) and S-TIAS (short form) for frequent assessments; additional trust components based on Mayer et al. (1995) may be included for triangulation (McGrath et al., 2025; Mayer et al., 1995).
  - Relationship quality
    - Validated teamwork/trust and cohesion scales; psychological safety scales adapted for AI contexts.
  - SMM alignment
    - Adaptations of established SMM instruments (e.g., DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus 2010 approaches; TMMI 11-item instrument by Filho et al. 2022; potentially combine SMM and CMM perspectives) with AI-context adaptations.
  - Joint problem solving
    - Objective task metrics (time to solution, quality scores, number of iterations) plus process metrics (coordination quality, info-sharing frequency).
  - SA and explainability
    - SA proxies or objective SA estimators; measures of perceived AI explanations and transparency.
- Practical considerations
  - Standardization and terminology: adopt consistent H-AI SMM terminology and align measures with existing scales; plan to document operational definitions carefully (Andrews et al., 2022; DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus, 2010; ARLIS 2021).
  - Ethics and governance: ensure ethical AI use, human oversight, and clear decision rights in scaffolding designs (Shneiderman 2020a; Crandall et al. 2018; Rahwan et al. 2019).
  - Cross-domain validity: pilot-test instruments in multiple domains to assess cross-disciplinary generalizability; address potential cultural differences (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024; Lane et al., 2024).
  - Pre-registration: pre-register hypotheses, analysis plans, and mediation/moderation models to strengthen causal claims and replication potential (Fragiadakis et al. 2025; Andrews et al. 2022).

5) Alternative designs and considerations
- If full randomization is logistically challenging, consider stepped-wedge or wait-list designs to introduce scaffolds in a staggered fashion, allowing within-team comparisons over time and preserving ethical considerations for scaffold deployment.
- In contexts where cross-disciplinary teams are embedded in organizations, quasi-experimental designs (difference-in-differences) could be employed if randomization is not feasible, though this requires careful control of confounding variables (task complexity, domain maturity, etc.).
- Mixed-methods integration: synthesize quantitative trajectories with qualitative accounts to interpret mechanisms, especially when SMM formation or trust shifts are rapid or non-linear.

6) Expected outcomes and interpretation
- If the hypotheses hold, we expect:
  - Scaffold exposure → increases in SMM alignment (human–AI) over time.
  - Increased SMM alignment → increases in relationship quality and trust (in AI and in human teammates) over time.
  - Higher trust and relationship quality → improved joint problem-solving success (faster, higher-quality solutions, better coordination).
  - Mediation: SMM alignment mediates the scaffold effect on trust and joint problem solving; stronger mediation as time progresses (12 months).
- Potential caveats:
  - In dyads, AI trust dynamics may be more fragile; scaffolds may need to be stronger or more explicit to sustain trust (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024).
  - The heterogeneity of cross-disciplinary domains may lead to differential scaffold efficacy; analyses should account for domain-specific moderating effects (Lane et al., 2024).

7) Actionable insights for authors and practitioners (practical takeaways)
- Ground your study in a robust SMM framework and ensure measurement alignment:
  - Use established trust scales for AI contexts (TIAS/S-TIAS) to track trust trajectories; supplement with multi-item relationship quality scales.
  - Adapt SMM measures to human–AI contexts, leveraging TMMI or DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus measurement approaches, with explicit AI-context validation.
- Design scaffolds with explicit, observable features:
  - Shared mental models scaffolds: joint task representations, shared planning artifacts, AI-explainable rationale for decisions.
  - Role rotation scaffolds: explicit rotation schedules with AI-assisted facilitation and documentation of role experiences.
  - AI moderation scaffolds: structured facilitation for turn-taking, conflict resolution, and information flow.
- Plan for longitudinal analysis from the outset:
  - Pre-register hypotheses and analytic plans; adopt cross-lagged panel designs and mediation analyses.
  - Ensure data collection at 0, 3, 6, 9, and 12 months; collect qualitative data to illuminate mechanisms.
- Emphasize cross-disciplinary generalizability and governance:
  - Design the study to include multiple domains (e.g., engineering, life sciences, design) and ensure AI system transparency and ethical governance are central to scaffold design.
- Reporting and dissemination:
  - Report effect sizes and confidence intervals for scaffold effects; report mediation effects and conditional effects by domain composition.
  - Share data and materials when possible to enable replication and meta-analysis as the field builds causal causal evidence.

Note on scope and interpretation
- The present plan integrates findings from theoretical reviews (SMM in human–AI teams), empirical longitudinal work in human–AI teams where available (Schelble, 2023), trust in AI studies (Georganta & Ulfert, 2024; McGrath et al., 2025), and measurement advances (TIAS/S-TIAS; TMMI) to design a rigorous 12-month causal study of AI-enabled scaffolds in cross-disciplinary teams.
- The literature to date does not provide a completed 12-month causal estimate for the bundled scaffolds in cross-disciplinary teams. The proposed design is intentionally ambitious and aims to fill this gap with robust causal inferences, while recognizing measurement challenges and domain-specific considerations.

Appendix: representative measurement options and scales (for planning)
- Trust in AI
  - TIAS (Trust in Automation Scale) and S-TIAS (short form) for frequent measurements (McGrath et al., 2025).
  - Mayer, Davis, & Schoorman (1995) trust model for theoretical grounding (ability, benevolence, integrity).
- Relationship quality and team dynamics
  - Scales for interpersonal trust, cohesion, psychological safety, and teamwork climate (adapted to AI contexts as needed).
- Shared mental models
  - SMM instruments (DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus 2010; Johnson et al.; TMMI by Filho et al. 2022) with adaptations for AI contexts.
- SMM measurement approaches
  - Content-based elicitation, structure-based measures (e.g., network representations), and emergence/consensus indicators (DeChurch & Mesmer-Magnus, 2010).

Limitations and caveats
- The field lacks a single, universally validated Human-AI SMM instrument with demonstrated longitudinal reliability; instrument adaptation and validation are required for cross-disciplinary AI-enabled contexts.
- Achieving a clean causal estimate over 12 months requires rigorous experimental or quasi-experimental designs and careful control of confounds (domain diversity, prior AI exposure, task complexity).
- Generalizability across domains and cultures should be explicitly planned for, with multi-site or multi-domain designs to ensure external validity.
