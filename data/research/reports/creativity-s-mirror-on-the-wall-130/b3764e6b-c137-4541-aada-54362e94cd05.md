# report.md

AI-assisted ideation workflows: influence on collaboration dynamics, trust, attribution, ownership, and protocols to preserve healthy relationships while maximizing creative outcomes

Executive summary
- Across domains, AI-assisted ideation reshapes collaboration by shifting credit flows, decision rights, and human-in-the-loop requirements. Trust and psychological safety emerge as central levers for sustaining healthy relationships and high creative yield (Pan et al., 2025; Nols et al., 2023; Jarvenpaa & Selander, 2023) [Pan et al., 2025; Nols et al., 2023; Jarvenpaa & Selander, 2023].
- Evidence from diverse contexts indicates that: AI literacy and perceived AI value predict trust in AI teammates; perceived AI accuracy calibrates trust and collaboration; dispersion of trust can be buffered by psychological safety; and explicit attribution/ownership protocols are critical to maintaining motivation and fairness (Pan et al., 2025; Dubious domain syntheses: Nols et al., 2023; Polimetla et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024) [Pan et al., 2025; Nols et al., 2023; Polimetla et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024].
- Design and governance implications center on: explicit credit flows, bounded AI autonomy, explainability and data provenance, human-in-the-loop quality checks, domain-aware AI literacy, and SCARF-based relationship management to sustain collaboration and creativity (Choi et al.; McGrath et al.; Endsley; Leichtmann et al.) [SCARF literature; McGrath et al., 2025; Leichtmann et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024].
- Actionable takeaway for authors/designers: implement multi-dimensional attribution schemas, explicit decision rights, transparent AI rationales, and AI-involvement disclosures; design for adaptive trust calibration and human agency across iterations and domains (He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Liu, 2025; Chen et al., 2025) [He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Liu, 2025; Chen et al., 2025].

1) Introduction: why AI-assisted ideation changes team dynamics
- AI agents can act as team members or co-creators in ideation workflows, altering how ideas are generated, evaluated, and owned. This reframing has direct consequences for collaboration dynamics, attribution norms, and relationship health (Pan et al., 2025; Ulfert et al., 2023) [Pan et al., 2025; Ulfert et al., 2023].
- Foundational concepts frame trust as a multi-level construct in human-AI teams (individual, team, inter-agent). Trust supports psychological safety, information sharing, and cooperative behavior, all of which underpin creative collaboration with AI (Pan et al., 2025; De Jong et al., 2016; Jarvenpaa & Selander, 2023) [Pan et al., 2025; De Jong et al., 2016; Jarvenpaa & Selander, 2023].
- The boundary between human and AI contributions is increasingly blurred; explicit norms for credit, decision rights, and accountability are required to preserve healthy relationships while maximizing ideation outcomes (Fiore & Wiltshire, 2016; Kozlowski et al., 2015) [Fiore & Wiltshire, 2016; Kozlowski et al., 2015].

2) What the evidence suggests about collaboration dynamics, trust, attribution, and ownership
- Collaboration dynamics
  - AI literacy and perceived AI value strongly predict trust in GAI teammates, which in turn shapes collaboration quality and willingness to co-create (Pan et al., 2025) [Pan et al., 2025].
  - Perceived AI accuracy is a critical driver of trust; high accuracy boosts trust but must be managed with transparency to avoid complacency (Pan et al., 2025) [Pan et al., 2025].
  - A paradox emerges: higher AI literacy can coincide with hesitancy about broader AI adoption due to elevated expectations and concerns about future shifts (Pan et al., 2025) [Pan et al., 2025].
  - Across domains, teams benefit from explicit scaffolding, bounded autonomy for AI, and role clarity to prevent over-reliance or misalignment (Kozlowski et al., 2015; Endsley, 2023) [Kozlowski et al., 2015; Endsley, 2023].
- Trust and psychological safety
  - Trust dispersion (differences in individual trust in AI within a team) can hinder consensus; psychological safety moderates this dispersion, enabling divergent viewpoints to enrich decision-making if safe to speak up (Nols et al., 2023) [Nols et al., 2023].
  - Psychological safety buffers negative effects of trust dispersion, supporting creative exploration and critical evaluation of AI outputs (Nols et al., 2023) [Nols et al., 2023].
- Attribution and ownership
  - Attribution in AI-assisted ideation is not binary; nine notable frameworks and attribution experiments show that AI contributions receive less credit than equivalent human contributions, suggesting the need for granular credit taxonomy and explicit AI involvement disclosures (He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Hutson, 2025) [He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Hutson, 2025].
  - Multi-dimensional attribution frameworks (type, amount, initiative) better reflect the nuanced contributions of AI versus humans and align with domain-specific governance and policy contexts (He et al., 2025; Hutson, 2025; Xu et al., 2024) [He et al., 2025; Hutson, 2025; Xu et al., 2024].
- Ownership perceptions and psychological ownership
  - Psychological ownership in AI-enabled co-creations is multi-dimensional (self-identity, self-efficacy, autonomy, territoriality, accountability). Designs that surface AI contributions, promote control, and acknowledge domain-specific data provenance support stronger ownership perceptions (Xu et al., 2024) [Xu et al., 2024].
  - Experimental work in co-creative domains (music, design, writing) shows hybrid authorship is common; explicit governance and explicit credit flows mitigate ownership confusion and maintain collaboration quality (Liu, 2025; He et al., 2025; Polimetla et al., 2025) [Liu, 2025; He et al., 2025; Polimetla et al., 2025].
- Domain-level patterns and cross-domain implications
  - Education, healthcare, software, and creative domains show domain-specific trust dynamics; in education, AI trust can lag, whereas in professional design contexts, AI can heighten creativity when roles are clearly defined (Lin, 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Liu, 2025) [Lin, 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Liu, 2025].
  - Across domains, transparent AI explanations, data provenance, and explicit credit flows consistently support trust and collaboration quality (Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; He et al., 2025) [Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; He et al., 2025].

3) Protocols and governance patterns that preserve healthy relationships while maximizing creative outcomes
- Explicit attribution and ownership protocols
  - Move beyond binary AI/human credit to multi-dimensional attributions that reflect contribution type, amount, and initiative; disclose AI involvement; document provenance and data sources; establish governance around credit flow and decision rights (He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Polimetla et al., 2025) [He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Polimetla et al., 2025].
- Bounded AI autonomy with human-in-the-loop oversight
  - Implement task scaffolding, bounded AI autonomy, and escalation paths; ensure final decision rights reside with humans in ideation outcomes (Kozlowski et al., 2015; O’Neill et al., 2022; Endsley, 2023) [Kozlowski et al., 2015; O’Neill et al., 2022; Endsley, 2023].
- Transparent AI rationale and data provenance
  - Provide explainable rationales and provenance signals for AI suggestions; surface training data hints when relevant; support model identification to aid accountability and trust (Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; Technical attribution literature) [Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; Technical Approaches].
- Psychological safety and trust-building practices
  - Integrate SCARF-based governance to address status, certainty, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness; implement trust-calibration rituals and reflective sessions to sustain collaboration as AI capabilities evolve (Dyson, 2025; Nols et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023) [Dyson, 2025; Nols et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023].
- Domain-aware education and literacy
  - Tailor AI literacy programs to domain needs; emphasize task-relevant explanations and real-time rationales rather than broad AI-education to enhance performance and trust in ideation contexts (Leichtmann et al., 2023; Lin, 2024; Siau & Wang, 2018) [Leichtmann et al., 2023; Lin, 2024; Siau & Wang, 2018].
- Governance across the AI lifecycle
  - Structural (organization), procedural (processes), and relational (trust, collaboration) practices should be synchronized; lifecycle governance should cover design, execution, monitoring, and evaluation (Papagiannidis et al., 2025) [Papagiannidis et al., 2025].
- Prototyping and experimentation
  - Use open, reproducible platforms and cross-domain pilots to validate attribution and governance patterns (Liu, 2025; He et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025) [Liu, 2025; He et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025].

4) Actionable insights for authors designing AI-assisted ideation workflows
- Build multi-dimensional attribution into the workflow
  - Implement taxonomies that capture contribution type, amount, and initiative; surface AI rationale and mode of involvement; provide verifiable provenance trails (He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024).
- Design for adaptive trust calibration
  - Incorporate trust-calibration cues, dynamic reliability indicators, and environment-sensitive explanations to align user trust with AI capability across moments of uncertainty (Okamura & Yamada; Lebiere et al.; Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025) [Okamura et al., 2020; Lebiere et al., 2021; Leichtmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025].
- Preserve human agency and ownership
  - Maintain explicit final decision rights with override options; ensure humans remain responsible for final outputs; structure hybrid authorship norms that reflect both human and AI contributions (McGuire et al., 2024; Hutson, 2025; He et al., 2025).
- Integrate SCARF-informed governance
  - Apply SCARF-based strategies to manage status, certainty, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness during AI-enabled ideation; implement stakeholder-specific governance artifacts, including onboarding, reviews, and health checks (Dyson, 2025; Rock, 2008).
- Domain-specific design patterns
  - In education and healthcare, emphasize explainability, safety, and explicit human oversight; in software and creative domains, emphasize credit flows and creative autonomy to sustain motivation and originality (Lin, 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Liu, 2025; Xu et al., 2024).
- Measurement and research agenda
  - Develop instruments to capture perceived ownership, trust calibration, attribution fairness, and relationship health in AI-assisted ideation across domains; employ longitudinal designs to track trust evolution and ownership perceptions (Doh, 2025; Polimetla et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; McGrath et al., 2025).

5) Domain-specific implications at a glance
- Education
  - Trust and safety concerns; calibrate AI reliance with explainable outputs; ensure human-in-the-loop quality to preserve teaching authority and ownership of ideas (Lin, 2024; Leichtmann et al., 2023) [Lin, 2024; Leichtmann et al., 2023].
- Healthcare
  - Emphasize transparency, data provenance, and accountability; maintain human oversight to safeguard patient safety and professional responsibility (Chen et al., 2025; Ammelling et al., 2025) [Chen et al., 2025; Ammelling et al., 2025].
- Software and design teams
  - Licit attribution frameworks and robust credit flows; bounded AI autonomy with explicit decision rights; psychological safety supports iterative ideation and risk-taking (Pan et al., 2025; Liu, 2025) [Pan et al., 2025; Liu, 2025].
- Creative domains (art, writing, music)
  - Co-creation and hybrid authorship become common; multi-dimensional attribution schemas and explicit ownership discussions reduce friction and support flow of credit (He et al., 2025; Hutson, 2025; Xu et al., 2024) [He et al., 2025; Hutson, 2025; Xu et al., 2024].
- Public policy and governance
  - Responsible AI governance, lifecycle oversight, and multi-stakeholder approaches help ensure fairness, transparency, and accountability in AI-assisted ideation across sectors (Papagiannidis et al., 2025; Krakówski et al., 2025; Leichtmann et al., 2023) [Papagiannidis et al., 2025; Polimetla et al., 2025; Leichtmann et al., 2023].

6) Limitations and scope
- Many sources are conceptual or domain-specific case studies with limited generalizable effect sizes. Where quantitative results exist (e.g., trust calibration cues, attribution experiments), effects are context-bound and require replication across domains and tasks (Okamura et al., 2020; Leichtmann et al., 2023; He et al., 2025) [Okamura et al., 2020; Leichtmann et al., 2023; He et al., 2025].
- Generalizability caution: trust dynamics, attribution norms, and ownership perceptions vary by domain, culture, team composition, and AI capabilities; designs should be domain-adapted and validated with domain-specific measures (Lin, 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024) [Lin, 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024].

7) Conclusion
- AI-assisted ideation workflows offer substantial creative potential but introduce complex social dynamics around trust, attribution, and ownership. The literature converges on several practical design and governance strategies: explicit, nuanced attribution; bounded AI autonomy with robust human-in-the-loop; transparent AI reasoning and data provenance; and SCARF-informed governance to sustain healthy relationships and high creative outcomes (Pan et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Liu, 2025; Dyson, 2025; Papagiannidis et al., 2025) [Pan et al., 2025; He et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Liu, 2025; Dyson, 2025; Papagiannidis et al., 2025].
- Practical guidance for authors: embed measurement and governance into the design process, test across domains, and iterate on attribution models and credit flows to preserve relationships while maximizing creative outcomes.
