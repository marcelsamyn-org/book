Diversity-Prompting in AI Outputs: Originality, Fixation, and Coherence Trade-offs

Executive summary
- Question: Does prompting AI to produce a more diverse set of outputs (higher diversity sampling) increase originality and reduce intervention-induced fixation, and what are the practical costs to coherence or usefulness?
- Core synthesis: Across diverse lines of evidence, increasing diversity in prompts or decoding can enhance originality and broaden the design/concept space, but effects on fixation are context-dependent and can introduce costs to coherence, usefulness, and task performance. The strongest empirical gaps arise when diversity is implemented naively (e.g., simply exposing users to many AI outputs without workflow safeguards). Calibrated, prompt-design-aware diversity strategies—paired with structured evaluation and human-in-the-loop checks—tend to yield more favorable originality/variation outcomes while containing coherence costs.
- Practical consequences for authors and practitioners: Do not assume “more diversity” automatically yields better outcomes. Use diversity-promoting prompts and decoding methods with explicit workflows to reduce fixation, and pair them with evaluation frameworks that consider both originality and usefulness (coherence, factuality, applicability). Monitor cognitive load, bias, and task-specific constraints, and employ human-in-the-loop curation where originality and coherence must both be maintained.
- Guiding takeaway: Calibrated, diversity-aware prompts and decoding strategies—especially those with explicit prompts for expansion, convergence design steps, and robust evaluation—are the most promising path for increasing originality while preserving usefulness in knowledge-work tasks.

1) Background, definitions, and framing
- Core concepts
  - Originality: the novelty or novelty-to-usefulness balance in outputs, often conceptualized as divergent thinking or expansion beyond familiar patterns.
  - Design fixation / fixation: the tendency to anchor ideation to exemplars, briefs, or familiar patterns, reducing design space exploration.
  - Coherence / usefulness: task-relevance, factual alignment, and applicability of outputs to real-world workflows.
- Why diversity matters
  - In theory, increasing diversity in prompts or outputs can broaden conceptual space and invite unconventional approaches, potentially boosting originality and reducing fixation. However, empirical evidence shows that naive exposure to AI-generated content can induce fixation on AI outputs or exemplars if prompts are not designed to promote genuine expansion (CHI 2024; egg-task studies; design-heuristics literature) [Wadinambiarachchi et al., CHI 2024; Leahy et al., 2018; Jansson & Smith, 1991].

2) Evidence on AI-assisted ideation, prompts, and fixation
- The CHI 2024 study on AI-generated imagery for ideation
  - Design task: ideation for a chatbot avatar; exposure to AI-generated imagery vs none or Google Image Search.
  - Key findings: AI-generated imagery increased design fixation relative to other conditions; participants in the AI-supported group produced fewer ideas, with lower fluency, variety, and originality than a baseline without AI support; fixation displacement was observed, where focus shifted onto the AI outputs [Wadinambiarachchi et al., CHI 2024].
  - Qualitative insight: prompt-design strategies and response strategies materially influence the effectiveness of AI in ideation, suggesting that diverse prompts must be embedded within thoughtful workflows to mitigate fixation [Wadinambiarachchi et al., CHI 2024].
- Egg-task study (divergent thinking and AI vs humans)
  - Task framing: an egg drop problem with 10 conceptual categories; measures include fluency, flexibility, fixation score, expansivity (expansion outside dominant categories).
  - Core findings: AI (ChatGPT-4o) showed higher productivity (fluency) but fixation patterns similar to humans; AI outputs clustered in fixation categories and exhibited limited genuine originality or expansion without targeted prompting; humans remained better at distinguishing true originality from conventional ideas; substantial portion of AI ideas remained fixation-biased within dominant categories; expansion ideas resided in non-dominant categories but were generated less frequently without targeted prompts [Egg Task study; associated theoretical framing].
  - Implications: high fluency does not equate to high originality; to achieve genuine originality, interventions that promote conceptual expansion outside fixation categories are needed, alongside metacognitive and inhibitory-control processes [Egg Task study; triadic cognitive models].
- Design-fixation literature
  - Foundational work documents fixation as a driver of restricted ideation space, with factors modulating fixation including modality, fidelity, novelty, exposure time, and proximity to the problem (Jansson & Smith, Vasconcelos & Crilly, Youmans & Arciszewski, et al.) [Jansson & Smith, 1991; Vasconcelos & Crilly, 2016; Youmans & Arciszewski, 2014].
  - Mixed effects across studies: exposure to exemplars or stimuli can either open conceptual space or reinforce fixation, depending on task, prompts, and interaction design [Vasconcelos & Crilly, 2016; Chiou et al., 2023].
- Diversity sampling and co-ideation frameworks
  - Conceptual work in diversity sampling emphasizes that a diverse bank of prompts or exemplars can help improve downstream performance when applied via a deliberate, three-step workflow (diverse sample → LLM solves on the sample → lift solution to full data) and evaluated via diversity-aware metrics (AggWasted, IsNew) [Diversity sampling frameworks; AggWasted/IsNew; coreset paradigm].
  - Empirical claims indicate baselines often underperform compared to curated diversity samplers, with baselines lagging by notable margins (6%–61% worse in aggregated metrics on diversity benchmarks) and with higher time costs than the proposed method; however, the measured diversity is input-space diversity, not directly a measure of output originality in all tasks [Diversity Samplers paper].
- Top-H decoding (bounded-entropy approach)
  - Top-H reframes decoding as an entropy-constrained minimum-divergence problem (ECMD) and shows equivalence to entropy-constrained mass maximization (ECMM); NP-hardness motivates a greedy Top-H algorithm that balances creativity and coherence by adaptively bounding entropy as generation proceeds.
  - Empirical findings: Top-H outperforms min-p by up to 25.63% on creative-writing benchmarks; robust on reasoning/QA datasets (GPQA, GSM8K, MT-Bench); LLM-as-judge evaluations show maintained coherence at higher temperatures; the method integrates easily with existing pipelines and code is available.
  - Implications for diversity/prompts: Top-H demonstrates that distribution-wide, adaptive constraining of sampling space—rather than fixed probability thresholds—can yield higher originality without sacrificing coherence. This is a strong pointer that calibrated, distribution-aware diversity strategies can mitigate fixation while preserving usefulness [Baghaei Potraghloo et al., 2025].

3) Synthesis: does higher diversity sampling increase originality and reduce fixation?
- Evidence points toward nuanced conclusions:
  - Direct, naive diversity exposure (e.g., AI-generated imagery or random diversity prompts without workflow guards) can increase fixation or reduce originality in ideation tasks, as seen in the CHI 2024 study and egg-task findings. In those setups, AI prompts without carefully designed workflows tended to anchor ideation and reduce expansion into non-dominant categories [Wadinambiarachchi et al., CHI 2024; Egg Task study].
  - When designing for higher diversity with explicit expansion goals and structured workflows, diversity-promoting prompts can enhance originality and broaden the ideation space, particularly when guided by metacognitive strategies (inhibitory control, metacognition) and when prompts explicitly target expansion beyond dominant categories. Literature emphasizing design heuristics and structured divergence supports this: using prompts that diversify problem framing and concept types can reduce fixation and improve originality (Leahy et al., 2018; Jansson & Smith, 1991; design heuristics as catalysts for ongoing ideation) [Leahy et al., 2018; Jansson & Smith, 1991].
  - The diversity-sampling literature (sequence-based diversity or verbalized distributions) suggests that a bank of diverse prompts or prompts-derived knowledge can improve downstream solution quality if the diversity is well-aligned with downstream tasks and constrained by evaluation frameworks (AggWasted, IsNew, distributional alignment). In practical terms, sampling diversity must be coupled with external evaluation, synthesis steps, and domain constraints to translate diversity into real originality and usefulness [Diversity Samplers; Verbalize/Sequence-based distribution approaches; KS_S concept].
  - Coherence costs: Across several studies, increased diversity can impose higher cognitive load, reduce immediate coherence, or require post-generation synthesis to maintain usefulness. The balance is task- and domain-dependent; higher diversity often requires structured curation and convergence steps to produce a single usable product, particularly for knowledge-work tasks with high stakes for accuracy and coherence (Harvey 2013; Sattele & Ortiz 2025; Chen et al., 2018/2025) [Harvey, 2013; Sattele & Ortiz, 2025; Chen et al., 2018/2025].
- Bottom line: higher diversity sampling has the potential to increase originality and reduce fixation—but only when implemented via calibrated prompts, adaptive decoding, and structured workflows that promote expansion while maintaining coherence. Naive exposure to diverse AI outputs is more likely to increase fixation or reduce usefulness, particularly in knowledge-work contexts where coherence and applicability matter.

4) Practical costs to coherence or usefulness (as evidenced)
- Coherence and usefulness trade-offs are a recurring theme:
  - Naive AI-inspired diversity can lead to fixation on AI outputs or exemplars and can reduce fluency, variety, and originality in ideation tasks (CHI 2024; egg-task results) [Wadinambiarachchi et al., CHI 2024; Egg Task study].
  - Diversity-enhancing methods (Top-H, ACS, ACS-like adaptive decoding) report improved diversity/originality with preserved or only modest reductions in coherence, especially when entropy budgets are carefully tuned and evaluated with human judgments (Top-H; Adaptive Decoding; ACS) [Baghaei Potraghloo et al., 2025; Arias et al., 2024].
  - MAUVE vs human judgments: automatic diversity metrics (MAUVE) may misalign with what humans perceive as useful; thus, diversity gains must be paired with human-in-the-loop evaluation for real-world usefulness in knowledge-work tasks (ACS literature; descriptive caution) [Arias et al., 2024; Pillutla et al., 2021].
  - For healthcare/regulated domains, bias or fairness considerations intersect with diversity strategies; a rigorous bias-detection and lifecycle approach (data diversity, bias frames, PROBAST-like assessments) is recommended to ensure that diversity-driven outputs do not introduce new disparities or unsafe results (Hasanzadeh et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2024) [Hasanzadeh et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2024].
- Practical costs in different decoding modalities
  - Min-p: marginal computational overhead; robust at higher temperatures; straightforward to implement; supported by open-source tooling and community adoption (Min-p; Hugging Face integration). However, for some domains, it may still produce less diverse outputs than more sophisticated distribution-expressing methods (Sequence/Verbalize) depending on the task and prompt design [Min-p paper; Top-H decoding literature].
  - Top-H: NP-hard ECMM problem with greedy approximation; modest computational overhead and straightforward integration; best results in creative writing tasks with robust coherence in QA contexts; depends on careful tuning of the entropy budget α and per-step dynamics [Top-H Decoding, 2025].
  - ACS: adaptive contrastive/uncertainty-guided decoding that aims to maximize diversity while maintaining coherence; performance hinges on the chosen divergence penalties and entropy-like surrogates; some MAUVE-human misalignment concerns exist; generally provides a stronger diversity/coherence balance than fixed baselines in open-ended generation [Arias et al., 2024; CS literature].
  - PEFT-based diversity (semantic diversity prompts) increases output diversity without accuracy loss or latency increase in tested tasks, suggesting a family of approach with favorable coherence/usefulness trade-offs when carefully tuned [Mai & Carson-Berndsen, 2024].
- Domain-specific notes
  - In design and ideation domains, diversity that increases semantic breadth while allowing human-guided convergence improves originality when prompts encourage multi-valued problem framing; otherwise, the risk of fixation and diminished usefulness remains if prompts anchor the AI to narrow directions. Design heuristics and staged prompting can help mitigate this [Leahy et al., 2018; Sattele & Ortiz, 2025].

5) Quantitative synthesis: what to report in your work
- When evaluating diversity-promoting prompts or decoding strategies, report:
  - Originality/variety metrics: e.g., design-expansivity, semantic novelty, category-expansion (where applicable).
  - Fixation proxies: rate of ideas within fixation categories vs expansion, or measures of anchor reliance in prompts.
  - Coherence and usefulness: human judgments on coherence, factual accuracy, task usefulness; automated proxies (fluency, consistency) with caveats about MAUVE alignment.
  - Cognitive/workload indicators: NASA-TLX-like load indices or task-demand measures to assess whether increased diversity adds cognitive burden.
  - Computational costs: runtime/latency, additional overhead per token, and scaling behavior for different model sizes.
  - Bias/fairness considerations: lifecycle bias detection, representation diversity, and alignment with regulatory expectations (e.g., PRISMA/PROBAST-style checks where relevant).
- Suggested reporting formats:
  - Multi-metric dashboards combining diversity, fixation, coherence, usefulness, and workload.
  - Task-specific case studies that illustrate trade-offs in a realistic workflow (e.g., ideation-to-concept synthesis in design; creative writing with fidelity constraints).
  - A data-rich appendix with per-task results, hyperparameters, and prompts used to enable replication.

6) Actionable recommendations for authors and practitioners
- Adopt a calibrated, diversity-aware workflow
  - Do not rely solely on raw output counts or simple diversity metrics. Use a diversity-aware or distribution-aware prompting approach that promotes conceptual expansion while preserving convergence to task goals.
  - Combine divergent prompts with structured convergence steps (synthesis prompts, evaluation gates) to convert expanded ideation into coherent, usable outputs.
- Use distribution-expressive prompting where appropriate
  - When feasible, use Sequence/Verbalize distribution approaches to capture and operationalize target diversity beyond simple token-level randomness; tie these prompts to downstream exploration and lifting steps.
- Leverage guided or guided-MI interactions
  - If possible, employ mixed-initiative co-creative patterns where humans steer, critique, and curate AI outputs to prevent fixation, especially in professional knowledge-work contexts.
  - Design prompts that explicitly request exploration of alternative problem frames, constraints, and design directions rather than direct imitation of exemplars.
- Monitor bias and fairness
  - Integrate lifecycle bias detection and mitigation early in the pipeline; ensure diversity strategies do not amplify existing disparities, particularly in domain settings with sensitive or regulated content (e.g., healthcare, law, public policy).
- Evaluate with a balanced, human-centric approach
  - Use human judgments as primary indicators of usefulness and originality, with automatic metrics serving as supplementary signals (recognizing that MAUVE-like metrics may misalign with human judgments in open-ended tasks).
- Plan for domain-specific constraints
  - In high-stakes domains, favor conservative diversity budgets that preserve coherence and factual grounding; in exploratory or ideation-driven tasks, allow broader diversity with explicit convergence steps to obtain practical outputs.
- Build reproducible experiments
  - Document prompts, prompts-decomposition strategies, entropy budgets, and evaluation protocols; share prompts and prompts-design guidelines to enable cross-task replication and meta-analyses.

7) Limitations and caveats
- The evidence base is heterogeneous: some studies show fixation risks with naïve AI-assisted diversity, while others show clear gains with calibrated or structured approaches; the domain, task, and workflow design materially influence results.
- Many studies rely on proxy metrics (diversity, expansion, MAUVE) or human judgments; ensure multi-faceted evaluation and cross-validation across domains and model families.
- The generalizability to all knowledge-work tasks remains an empirical question; researchers should validate in their own domain contexts with task-relevant measures.
- The value of diversity must be weighed against potential costs to coherence, trust, safety, and regulatory compliance; bias, fairness, and explainability considerations should be integrated into the evaluation framework.

9) Final takeaways for authors
- Diversity alone is not a guarantee of originality or reduced fixation; use calibrated, adaptive methods and structured workflows to promote genuine conceptual expansion.
- Implement diversity prompts as components of broader ideation pipelines that include explicit convergence, evaluation, and synthesis steps.
- Use human-centered evaluation as a core component of any diversity-prompting workflow; combine with multi-maceted automatic metrics and bias/fairness checks.
- Favor reproducibility: document prompts, decoding budgets, and evaluation methodologies; share resources where possible to enable broader cross-task validation.
- When applying to knowledge-work domains with high coherence requirements (e.g., legal, medical, safety-critical), start with conservative diversity budgets and incorporate robust verification steps.
