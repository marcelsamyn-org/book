Executive summary
- Across a constellation of controlled and semi-controlled divergent-thinking studies, there is converging evidence that AI-first ideation can alter the tempo and quality of outputs, but the direction and durability of effects are context-dependent.
- Short-term fluency (idea throughput) is not consistently higher under AI-first conditions; several studies find AI prompts do not boost immediate idea production and may even introduce fixation or anchoring that constrains variety in the moment (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2025). Other work finds that AI-first can accelerate initial ideation in some settings, but benefits to long-run novelty are not universal and often hinge on timing and prompts (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).
- Long-run novelty (the emergence of rare or highly novel ideas after an initial AI-assisted phase) shows mixed patterns: early AI exposure can seed higher originality/diversity in subsequent unaided phases in some designs, but other studies report semantic convergence and increased anchoring that may dampen late novelty (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).
- Cognitive mediators discussed across sources include fixation/desynchronization from initial AI prompts, perspective-shifting capacity, working-memory load, and general cognitive-load dynamics. Direct measurements of working memory load, fixation, and perspective-shifting are variably reported, but theoretical and empirical links point to these as plausible mediators of any AI-first vs human-first trade-off (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2025; Takeuchi et al., 2020; Pauszek, 2023; Beaty & Silvia, 2012).
- Actionable implication: if the goal is to maximize enduring novelty, design AI-first ideation with staged prompts that promote perspective-shifts and explicit exploration beyond initial AI anchors; monitor and manage fixation via input diversification and prompting strategies; consider measuring cognitive-load and attention as mediators to understand when AI accelerates throughput at the expense of depth.

Introduction: the AI-first vs human-first trade-off in divergent thinking
- The research question asks whether initiating ideation with AI yields higher short-term fluency but lower long-run novelty relative to human-led ideation, and which cognitive mediators explain this trade-off (working memory load, fixation, perspective-shifting).
- The literature suggests a nuanced pattern:
  - Short-term throughput is not uniformly boosted by AI-first prompts, and AI can introduce anchoring or fixation that constrains immediate exploration (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).
  - Long-run novelty can be enhanced by early AI exposure in some designs, but there is also evidence of semantic convergence and reduced elaboration that may temper late-novelty potential (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).
  - Mediators that plausibly shape these dynamics include fixation (design- and task-related), perspective-shifting/exploration, and working-memory/cognitive-load dynamics, consistent with broader cognitive-neuroscience findings about executive control, DMN/ECN balance, and cognitive load in divergent thinking (Takeuchi et al., 2020; Beaty & Silvia, 2012; Pauszek, 2023; Gong, 2023; Takeuchi et al., 2020).
- Inline citations: (Hubert, Awa, & Zabelina, 2024); (Baltà-Salvador, Brasó-Vives, & Peña, 2025); (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024); (Hou et al., 2025); (Fan et al., 2025); (Byrge, Guzik, & Gilde, 2025); (Beaty & Silvia, 2012); (Takeuchi et al., 2020); (Pauszek, 2023); (Gong, 2023)

Integrated synthesis: what the evidence says about the core question
1) Do AI-first ideation conditions yield higher short-term fluency?
- Mixed evidence:
  - Some converging findings show no clear boost to immediate fluency under AI-assisted ideation; in visual-design tasks, AI assistance did not reduce throughput and sometimes was associated with more constrained exploration due to fixation, rather than a throughput boost (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024).
  - In broader DT tasks with AI-assisted prompts, early AI prompts can accelerate certain creative dimensions (e.g., creativity in prompt-driven tasks), but this often accompanies anchoring to AI-derived ideas rather than wholesale increases in fluency per se (Fan et al., 2025).
  - In a large, end-to-end CPS-like setting with AI vs human teams, AI entries achieved strong performance and high evaluative ratings, but the metric set did not isolate short-term fluency vs long-run novelty in a time-resolved way (Byrge et al., 2025).
- Takeaway: AI-first does not demonstrably guarantee higher short-term fluency across task types; effects are task- and design- dependent, with fixation/anchoring as a potential mechanism reducing immediate exploratory throughput in some contexts (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).

2) Do AI-first ideation conditions yield lower long-run novelty?
- Mixed-to-supportive patterns:
  - Some studies suggest early AI exposure seeds higher originality/diversity in subsequent unaided phases, indicating possible uplift in long-run novelty when AI is used to initiate the ideation process (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025).
  - Other work shows semantic convergence and reduced elaboration during AI-assisted phases, implying potential erosion of long-run novelty or at least a constraint on divergent expansion during AI-supported periods (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025).
  - In group brainstorming with neural markers, flexible mindset (ECN/DLPFC) relative to imitation (IFG) predicted creativity; strong fixation or imitation tendencies could hinder long-run novelty in AI-assisted contexts if prompts bias toward familiar patterns (Pick et al., 2024).
- Takeaway: AI-first ideation can either bolster or dampen long-run novelty depending on prompting strategy, the degree of exploration encouraged, and the degree to which AI anchors or diversifies thought across rounds (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Pick et al., 2024; Byrge et al., 2025).

3) What cognitive mediators explain the trade-off?
- Working memory load and executive control:
  - Evidence from neuroscience and psychology links working-memory capacity and executive control to creativity, including divergent thinking; higher WM support can correlate with greater originality under certain conditions, but not universally (Takeuchi et al., 2020; Gong, 2023; Beaty & Silvia, 2012; Pino et al., 2024).
  - In AI-enabled ideation, AI prompts could either offload cognitive load (through retrieval of external information) or impose additional processing demands (integration, filtering), thereby shaping fluency and novelty trajectories (Pergantis et al., 2025; Takeuchi et al., 2020).
- Fixation/attention and perspective-shifting:
  - Design fixation (anchoring to AI prompts or exemplars) and the associated reduction in exploratory breadth can reduce short-term novelty and hinder long-run expansion unless prompts explicitly encourage perspective-shifting (Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2025; Beaty & Silvia, 2012).
  - Eye-tracking frameworks suggest fixation duration, dwell on AI prompts vs. human prompts, and transitions between perspectives predict cognitive load and ideation strategy, offering a concrete set of mediators to test in AI-first contexts (Pauszek, 2023; Fan et al., 2025).
- Perspective-shifting and network dynamics:
  - The DMN/ECN balance (generation vs evaluation) and regional activations in the brain relate to how novel ideas emerge under varying WM demands; AI prompts that stimulate or dampen these networks could meaningfully alter fluency and novelty trajectories (Takeuchi et al., 2020; Gong, 2023; Beaty & Silvia, 2012).
- Taken together: WM load, fixation/attention, and perspective-shifting are plausible mediators that could explain when AI-first ideation increases short-term throughput but either harms or helps long-run novelty, depending on task structure, prompts, and participant/expert differences (Takeuchi et al., 2020; Pauszek, 2023; Beaty & Silvia, 2012; Fan et al., 2025).

Limitations and caveats
- There is no single, clean experimental design that conclusively demonstrates “AI-first yields higher short-term fluency but lower long-run novelty” across all divergent-thinking tasks. The evidence is heterogeneous, spanning DT tasks in text, design, and group settings, with varying AI tools, prompt regimes, and measurement horizons.
- Mediators are variably measured; some studies rely on proxy indicators (e.g., fixation, cognitive load proxies, semantic convergence) rather than direct WM metrics or explicit perspective-shifting measurements in AI-driven ideation.
- Most studies have domain-specific constraints (design tasks, CPS-like tasks, or constrained laboratory DT tasks). Generalization to broader, real-world ideation contexts requires careful replication with standardized metrics.

Actionable insights for authors and designers (practical guidelines)
- If aiming to maximize long-run novelty with AI:
  - Introduce AI prompts early to seed idea space, then deliberately insert pivot prompts and perspective-shifting tasks in later rounds to counteract anchoring and promote divergent expansion.
  - Use prompts designed to diversify semantic spaces rather than converge on a narrow meaning space; actively encourage cross-domain or multi-stakeholder viewpoints.
  - Implement scaffolds that require re-framing problems, challenging assumptions, or evaluating ideas from alternative frames to engage perspective-shifting processes.
- To manage short-run fluency without sacrificing depth:
  - Pair AI prompts with structured interaction patterns that promote active exploration rather than passive acceptance (e.g., collaborative critique cycles, prompt-refinement tasks, or adversarial prompts that challenge AI-provided notions).
- Mediator-centric measurement plan for future studies:
  - Include direct working-memory proxies (dual-task paradigms, WM capacity assessments) and objective cognitive-load measures (pupilometry, NASA-TLX) during ideation sessions.
  - Integrate eye-tracking to quantify fixation on AI prompts vs. human prompts and to gauge perspective-shifting via scanpaths across a set of AOIs representing problem frames.
  - Analyze longitudinal outputs across multiple ideation rounds to quantify the emergence of rare or high-novelty outputs and relate these to measured mediators.
- Design considerations for AI-system developers:
  - Create AI prompting strategies that explicitly encourage perspective-shifting and exploration of dissimilar idea spaces, including prompts that require reinterpretation of outputs from multiple viewpoints.
  - Provide tools to monitor and manage fixation risk (e.g., prompts that deliberately rotate focus across different prompts or problem representations, or UI features that nudge users toward less-explored idea spaces).
  - Consider memory-augmentation or structured reasoning prompts that balance speed with depth, reducing the risk of superficial convergence while preserving throughput.
- Experimental design blueprint to test the core question (alin to the current evidence base):
  - Design: Randomized crossover or multi-arm between-subjects study with AI-first and human-first conditions; use a standardized divergent-thinking battery (e.g., AUT, CT, Divergent Associations Task) with cross-task comparability.
  - Measures: Short-term fluency (idea count in the initial phase), originality/elaboration, and a longitudinal novelty index (emergence of rare/expanded ideas across later rounds); mediators measured via eye-tracking (fixation metrics, AOI dwell), cognitive-load proxies (NASA-TLX, pupillometry), and perspective-shifting indicators (prompt-based framing changes, category-switch metrics).
  - Analyses: Multilevel modeling to capture trial- and participant-level variation; mediation analyses to quantify indirect effects of WM load, fixation, and perspective-shifting on the fluency–novelty trajectory; moderation analyses for expertise, domain, and prompt types.
  - Pre-registration and reporting: preregister hypotheses on AI-first vs human-first effects, specify mediator models, report effect sizes and confidence intervals for both direct and indirect paths.

Conclusions
- The literature supports a nuanced view: AI-first ideation can alter both the speed and depth of creative ideation, but there is no universal rule that AI-first yields higher short-term fluency and lower long-run novelty. Outcomes depend on task domain, AI prompting strategy, timing, and the cognitive processes engaged during ideation.
- Plausible mediators—working-memory load, fixation/attention, and perspective-shifting—likely govern these dynamics, but direct empirical evidence tying these mediators to AI-first versus human-first trajectories remains to be consistently demonstrated across tasks.
- For researchers and designers aiming to harness AI for creative ideation, the most robust path forward is to design experiments that explicitly manipulate AI exposure timing, encourage perspective-shifting, and measure mediators with time-resolved and domain-relevant metrics. This enables a principled understanding of when AI accelerates ideation and when it may inadvertently constrain long-run novelty, along with practical countermeasures to optimize both throughput and depth.
