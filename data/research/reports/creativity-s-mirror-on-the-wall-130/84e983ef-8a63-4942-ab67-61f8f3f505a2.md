Diversity-Promoting Workflows for Cross-Domain Knowledge Linkage: Evidence, Gaps, and Actionable Guidelines

Executive summary
- Purpose and core claim: Across a range of cross-domain settings, evidence suggests that diversity-promoting workflows—those that integrate heterogeneous domains, data sources, models, and problem framings—tend to strengthen cross-domain knowledge linkage and transfer, relative to more homogeneous or single-domain AI workflows. However, the strength and granularity of the evidence vary by context and by whether quantitative cross-domain knowledge-network metrics are reported.
- What the evidence covers (and what it does not):
  - Boundary-spanning tools and practices (Pershina et al., 2019; Dionne & Carlile, 2024; Deng & Chi, 2015): Qualitative and process-focused findings show boundary-spanning artifacts and spaces as mediators of cross-domain integration, often improving alignment and joint enactment across domains, but without quantitative knowledge-network metrics.
  - Cross-domain transfer with diversity across sources (TJAPL; Gong et al., 2024; DAEA; Cantador et al., 2015; BRIDGE; MDGFM): Quantitative gains are reported in several concrete settings:
    - TJAPL (Xu et al., 2024): Transfer-learning gains in cross-domain sequential recommendation when using multi-source signals, with components TD-APL, CD-UAPL, CD-LAPL contributing in ablations; the approach significantly outperforms baselines across three cross-domain datasets.
    - IIoT cross-domain transfer (Gong et al., 2024): Diversity-promoting configurations (multi-source groups with heterogeneous energy sources) produce distinct cross-domain transfer profiles across AoI, queue length, and energy consumption; results vary by configuration and learning method, indicating diversity interacts with learning dynamics to shape cross-domain outcomes.
    - Cross-domain KG adaptations (DAEA, Yang et al., 2025): Multi-source KG selection plus domain-adaptive loss yields substantial improvements in entity-alignment metrics (Hits@1) on real-world KGs (e.g., +29.94% on DOREMUS, +5.64% on AGROLD).
    - Graph foundation models and theory (MDGFM; BRIDGE; BRIDGE ablations): Theoretical guarantees and ablation results point to topology-alignment and domain-invariant representations as levers for cross-domain generalization, with empirical gains across diverse datasets and tasks.
  - What remains largely qualitative or missing: explicit, standardized, cross-domain knowledge-network metrics (e.g., betweenness centrality, modularity, cross-domain edge density) and controlled, side-by-side comparisons of diversity-promoting workflows versus standard AI-assisted workflows on those metrics are not uniformly reported across all sources.
- Actionable takeaway: To rigorously establish the value of diversity-promoting workflows for cross-domain knowledge linkage, practitioners should (a) define and measure knowledge-network metrics that capture cross-domain connectivity and topology, (b) design cross-domain transfer tasks that directly evaluate transferability across domains, and (c) run controlled ablations that separate the contributions of data diversity, model diversity, and boundary-work artifacts.

Structured evidence and synthesis by theme

1) Boundary-spanning artifacts and diversity across domains
- Key claim: Boundary-spanning tools (digital and non-digital artifacts) function as knowledge bridges that enable alignment and integration of divergent domains, and they support iterative, cross-domain prototyping and transformation (Pershina et al., 2019; Dionne & Carlile, 2024).
- Evidence type and limits:
  - Pershina et al. (2019) offer qualitative, nested case-study evidence about boundary-spanning tools and iterative cross-domain collaboration in digital serious games development; no quantitative metrics are reported.
  - Dionne & Carlile (2024) articulate a pragmatic cycle of knowledge work (exploring, complementing, mapping, modeling) and emphasize the role of temporary spaces in fostering cross-domain knowledge-building; no quantitative metrics are provided.
- Relevance to the research question: The boundary-spanning approach provides a plausible mechanism by which diversity-promoting workflows increase cross-domain connectivity, but quantitative benchmarking against standard AI workflows is not established in these works.

2) Cross-domain transfer with multiple source domains
- TJAPL (Xu et al., 2024): Cross-domain sequential recommendation with multi-source signals
  - Setup: Target-domain APL; cross-domain user APL; cross-domain local APL; multi-source data with several source domains.
  - Results: TJAPL significantly outperforms 13 baselines across three cross-domain datasets; ablation analyses demonstrate contributions of CD-UAPL and CD-LAPL; components TD-APL, CD-UAPL, CD-LAPL enable richer cross-domain signal extraction.
  - Interpretation: Diversity-aware, multi-source-domain transfer improves cross-domain linkage and transfer performance in a sequential-recommendation setting, illustrating tangible gains from diversity in data sources and transfer components (Xu et al., 2024).
- Gong et al. (2024): Cross-domain knowledge transfer in heterogeneous IIoT
  - Setup: Three equipment groups with diverse energy sources; warm-start policies; cross-domain transfer across varying learning methods (GP, GS, MS, All groups).
  - Metrics: AoI, queue length, grid energy consumption, battery energy consumption; results show group-specific improvements/trade-offs and dependence on learning method and diversity configuration.
  - Interpretation: Diversity-aware configurations modulate cross-domain transfer performance and resource-use trade-offs; component interactions (group composition, learning method) shape transfer outcomes in a realistic, heterogeneous system context.
- Cantador et al. (2015) Cross-domain recommender frameworks
  - Conceptual framing: Cross-domain knowledge transfer via knowledge aggregation or knowledge linkage; data overlap scenarios across domains (user/item overlaps) enable cross-domain enhancement of target-domain recommendations.
  - Relevance: Provides a baseline framing for why multi-domain data can improve cross-domain knowledge linkage in recommender-like tasks.

3) Real-world transfer in knowledge graphs (multisource and domain adaptation)
- DAEA: Domain Adaptation for cross-domain KG entity alignment (Yang et al., 2025)
  - Setup: Multi-source KGs; select sources with minimal domain-distance to target; domain-adaptive loss; seed pairs; BERT-based integration for cross-KG alignment.
  - Metrics: Hits@1 improvements on DOREMUS (+29.94%) and AGROLD (+5.64%) relative to SOTA.
  - Interpretation: Diversity-aware source selection plus domain-adaptation improves cross-domain linkage in real KGs; outputs translate to stronger cross-domain alignment and transfer capability.
- KG-MFEND (Fu et al., 2023) and KG integration approaches
  - Setup: Multi-domain fake-news detection with knowledge-graph-based integration; uses knowledge graphs to fuse multi-domain knowledge and mitigate noise.
  - Evidence: Demonstrates cross-domain generalization through KG integration and advanced embedding strategies, but quantitative cross-domain-transfer metrics are not explicit in the accessible summary.
- BRIDGE (BRIDGE, 2025) and spectral generalization
  - Core: Multi-domain pre-training with domain-invariant alignment and a mixture-of-experts prompting strategy; spectral regularization provides a theoretical bound on cross-domain transfer/generalization.
  - Evidence: Ablations show the importance of alignment, MoE prompting, and spectral regularization; empirical gains across six datasets for cross-domain transfer tasks (node/graph classification), including few-shot settings. Some numbers appear in reviewer materials (e.g., zero-shot vs text-enabled variants) and ablations, but full numerical detail is in the ICML 2025 OpenReview context.
- MDGFM: Multi-domain Graph Foundation Models (Yang et al., 2025)
  - Core idea: A topology-aligned pre-training and downstream adaptation framework with Graph Structure Learning (GSL) and domain-adaptive prompting; theoretical guarantees on topology alignment and domain generalization; experiments on both homophilic and heterophilic graphs show robustness and transfer benefits.
  - Note: The work emphasizes theory and experiments across diverse graph domains but provides qualitative and quantitative results that support cross-domain transfer with topology-aware learning.

4) Boundary-spanning, organizational and design-level insights
- Deng & Chi (2015): Boundary-spanning in IS support networks improves productivity and experiential learning; hierarchy of boundary-spanning roles and network position influence outcomes; external boundary exposure matters.
- Carlson/Long et al. (2013) review on brokers and boundary spanners: Boundary-spanning roles facilitate cross-group knowledge transfer and trust, but entail costs (workload, bottlenecks) and depend on network structure; practical governance matters.
- Lai et al. (2022) design frameworks that combine Function-Behavior-Structure (FBS) with Knowledge Graphs to support cross-domain design; qualitative feasibility in a single example but demonstrates cross-domain integration pathways and transfer-oriented tooling.

5) Gaps and methodological considerations
- Consistent quantitative metrics: Across sources, there is a lack of standardized knowledge-network metrics that capture cross-domain connectivity (e.g., betweenness centrality, modularity, cross-domain edge density) in the context of diversity-promoting workflows. Where metrics exist (Hits@k, AoI, transfer accuracy, etc.), they are task- or domain-specific and not uniformly translatable into a single cross-domain benchmark.
- Direct comparators: Few works provide direct head-to-head comparisons between diversity-promoting workflows and standard AI-assisted workflows on a standardized cross-domain knowledge-network metric suite.
- Domain breadth and generalization: Many studies are domain-specific (healthcare, IIoT, KG alignment, etc.); generalizability to a broad set of domains and to truly long-horizon, creativity-driven cross-domain tasks remains an empirical gap.

Implications for design, measurement, and practice

Practical design patterns to promote cross-domain knowledge linkage
- Build diversity into workflows:
  - Use multi-source data, multi-domain models, and boundary artifacts to stimulate cross-domain signal flow.
  - Promote boundary-spanning roles or agents that actively connect disparate domains, with governance to prevent overload (Deng & Chi, 2015; Long et al., 2013).
- Create artifacts and spaces that support integration:
  - Boundary-spanning artifacts (digital and non-digital) and iterative cycles (ideation → prototyping → testing) to surface cross-domain linkages (Pershina et al., 2019; Dionne & Carlile, 2024).
  - Temporary collaboration spaces (hackathons, workshops, etc.) to catalyze cross-domain ideation and reduce domain friction (Dionne & Carlile, 2024).
- Leverage domain adaptation and multi-source integration:
  - Use source-domain selection to minimize negative transfer and apply domain-adaptive objectives to reduce distributional gaps (Yang et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024).
  - Leverage graph-based topology alignment and structure-learning to preserve cross-domain signal while filtering noise (MDGFM, BRIDGE; DAEA).

Measurement plan for diversity-promoting cross-domain workflows
- Knowledge-network metrics (define and report consistently):
  - Topology: cross-domain edge density, modularity, average path length across domain clusters, bridging centrality of cross-domain nodes, and domain-specific clustering coefficients.
  - Connectivity dynamics: rate of cross-domain link formation, cross-domain motif counts, cross-domain propagation speed.
  - Alignment quality: domain-invariant representation similarity, cross-domain transferability scores, and domain-adaptation gap.
- Cross-domain transfer tasks:
  - Define explicit transfer tasks across multiple domains (e.g., cross-domain item/connection prediction, cross-domain QA, cross-domain control/policy transfer, few-shot cross-domain adaptation).
  - Report transfer performance (accuracy, F1, AUC) across domain pairs, plus learning-efficiency measures (time-to-target, iterations to convergence).
- Ablations and controls:
  - Remove or alter boundaries artifacts, data diversity sets, or domain-adapters to quantify each component’s contribution to cross-domain linkage and transfer.
  - Compare joint multi-domain pre-training vs sequential transfer to assess the effect of temporal integration on topology alignment and transfer gains.

Actionable research agenda (short to medium horizon)
- Develop a standardized cross-domain knowledge-network benchmark:
  - Curate a set of domain pairs (or triplets) with defined domain boundaries, shared concepts, and domain-specific vocabularies; specify tasks that require cross-domain linking and transfer.
  - Specify a metrics suite that captures topology, transfer performance, and interpretability.
- Conduct controlled experiments across multiple domains:
  - Compare diversity-promoting workflows (multi-source signals, boundary artifacts, topology-aware training, MoE prompting, etc.) to standard AI workflows on the benchmark.
  - Include ablations to identify which diversity components drive improvements.
- Publish reusable artifacts:
  - Datasets, KG schemas, model implementations, evaluation scripts, and a leaderboard to enable replication and cross-study comparison.
- Integrate with theory:
  - Ground results in boundary-spanning and knowledge-integration theory (Carlile; Long; Pershina) to interpret causal pathways from diversity-promoting practices to cross-domain connectivity.

Limitations to keep in mind
- Heterogeneity of domains and metrics: The evidence spans many domains (education, IIoT, KG alignment, design) with domain-specific metrics. There is no single, universally accepted cross-domain knowledge-network metric; results are often task- or domain-specific.
- Direct AI-workflow vs diversity-promoting workflow comparisons are not uniformly present across the literature; where they exist (e.g., TJAPL, DAEA, MDGFM), they are context-specific demonstrations rather than broad, domain-agnostic conclusions.
- Some works report significant gains; others provide qualitative mechanisms or qualitative guidance. When synthesizing, treat the strongest conclusions as domain-contextual and the generalizability as an open empirical question.

Concluding synthesis
- The assembled evidence points to a convergent conclusion: diversity-promoting, cross-domain workflows—operationalized through boundary-spanning artifacts, multi-source data integration, domain-adaptive learning, and topology-aware representation learning—can produce stronger cross-domain linkage and transfer in many settings. The clearest, most robust quantitative gains are reported in multi-domain or cross-domain transfer benchmarks with explicit source-domain diversity (TJAPL, DAEA, MDGFM BRIDGE style work) and in diverse, real-world KG contexts (DAEA). However, a rigorous, standardized, cross-domain benchmark with uniform knowledge-network metrics is still a work in progress, which limits generalizable, cross-domain claims across all domains and tasks.
- Actionable next steps for researchers and practitioners:
  - Define and publish a standardized cross-domain knowledge-network metric suite and a cross-domain transfer benchmark.
  - Conduct controlled experiments comparing diversity-promoting workflows to standard AI workflows across multiple domains and tasks, reporting topology metrics, transfer metrics, and ablations.
  - Build and share pipelines, data, KG schemas, and evaluation scripts to enable replication and cross-study comparisons.
  - Ground evaluations in theory from boundary-spanning and knowledge-integration literatures, ensuring that measurement captures both structural connectivity and substantive transfer outcomes.
