# report.md

Executive summary
- Based on the gathered evidence, repeated AI-assisted ideation in short- to medium-term contexts does not inherently degrade non-AI creativity in the immediate term and can even enhance certain creativity metrics when used with design guidance and reflection. However, several studies document unintended consequences such as semantic convergence, design fixation, or automation bias if AI is used passively or without safeguards (e.g., friction or structured reflection). Key patterns emerge around the importance of deliberate friction, reflective practices, and with-design designs to sustain long-run skill development (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Rana et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Tholander & Jonsson, 2023; Zhou et al., 2025).
- A robust longitudinal field study addressing the research question should: (a) systematically manipulate friction/reflection and with-design practices; (b) track non-AI creativity trajectories alongside AI-assisted outputs across multiple waves; (c) employ multi-dimensional creativity measures (fluency, flexibility, originality, novelty, semantic divergence, product conformance); and (d) incorporate qualitative analyses of interaction patterns, ownership, and ethical/governance considerations (Rana et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025; van Lancker, 2025).
- Actionable takeaway for authors: embed friction/reflection and with-design practices from the outset, monitor AI dependence and autonomy, and design longitudinal, mixed-methods protocols that capture both non-AI creativity and long-run skill development. Use progressive prompting, structured critique, and explicit ownership/ethics discussions to preserve human agency while benefiting from AI-assisted ideation (Li et al., 2025; Rana et al., 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025).

1) Introduction and research question framing
- Core question: In a short- to medium-term longitudinal field study, does repeated AI-assisted ideation degrade non-AI creativity unless paired with deliberate friction or structured reflection, and can with-design practices sustain long-run skill development?
- Rationale: Existing evidence shows that AI can sustain or enhance short- to mid-term creativity when used with pedagogical guidance, while also risking homogenization, automation bias, or reduced deep learning if used passively. Long-run effects on non-AI creativity and skill development require longitudinal, intervention-based designs (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Rana et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Tholander & Jonsson, 2023).
- Key concepts and mechanisms to examine:
  - Non-AI creativity: multi-dimensional measures capturing divergent/convergent outcomes, transfer to non-AI tasks, and durability of skills (Dean et al.; Guilford; Torrance lineage in the cited literature) (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Hadas & Hershkovitz, 2025).
  - AI-assisted ideation: AI as co-creator or co-inquirer; potential for design fixation or semantic convergence without countermeasures (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Wang et al., 2025).
  - With-design practices: deliberate integration of AI across design thinking stages with ethical reasoning, bias screening, and reflective critique to preserve human agency and support durable skill growth (Rana et al., 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025).
  - Friction/reflection: deliberate, proximate countermeasures designed to sustain cognitive engagement, critical evaluation, and ownership (Van Lancker, 2025; Rana et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Tholander & Jonsson, 2023).

2) Evidence landscape: what we know about AI-assisted ideation and creativity
- Short- to medium-term effects (experimental and classroom studies):
  - AI assistance can preserve or boost core creativity metrics (fluency, flexibility, originality) in some contexts, but AI-induced semantic convergence or fixation can occur if usage is passive. Early AI exposure can have lasting effects on originality/diversity across tasks (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025).
  - Group AI-assisted ideation may yield higher novelty but lower workability; AI outputs often resemble existing products and show reduced textual elaboration; interactions with AI tend to be passive rather than collaborative (ADIC2024/115; Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2025).
  - Mixed findings exist across domains: AI can enhance creativity and concentration in short-term design tasks, with strong concentration–creativity correlations; however, risks include automation bias and diminished deep engagement if friction/reflection are not embedded (Wang et al., 2025; Rana et al., 2025; Lively & Hutson, 2024; Fisher et al., 2025).
- Design implications and countermeasures:
  - Progressive prompting, structured critique, reflective practice, and ethical/governance considerations are repeatedly identified as mechanisms to sustain or enhance long-run skill development in AI-augmented design work (Li et al., 2025; Rana et al., 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025; Ye et al., 2025).
  - Friction-based countermeasures and with-design practices are emphasized as essential to sustaining human agency, particularly in contexts with repeated AI involvement (Van Lancker, 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025; Tholander & Jonsson, 2023).
- Notable cross-domain patterns:
  - AI-driven productivity boosts can lead to larger total outputs but may reduce per-unit novelty over time if not counterbalanced; diffusion of AI usage can broaden participation but potentially dilute individual novelty (Zhou et al., 2025; Doshi & Hauser, 2024).
  - In education, GenAI can augment creativity and critical thinking when scaffolded, yet ethical concerns require governance and reflective practice to preserve authentic human agency (Rana et al., 2025; Marrone et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025).

3) Conceptual model for a short- to medium-term longitudinal field study
- Hypotheses (operationalization):
  - H1: Repeated AI-assisted ideation without friction/reflection degrades non-AI creativity over waves, relative to a friction/reflection condition or to non-AI controls.
  - H2: With-design practices (structured integration of AI across design-thinking stages with reflective critique and ethical considerations) mitigate decay or even enhance non-AI creativity trajectories.
  - H3: Friction/reflection will moderate the AI–creativity relationship by sustaining autonomy, critical evaluation, and ownership, thereby preserving non-AI creativity across waves.
  - H4: Early AI exposure has lasting positive effects on originality/diversity across later unaided tasks when combined with deliberate with-design practices (consistent with findings that early AI exposure can shape later creativity) (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2025; Rana et al., 2025).
- Population and sampling:
  - Target: Undergraduates and early-career designers across design domains, plus practicing designers in field contexts to improve generalizability.
  - Sample size: Several cohorts, with at least 40–60 participants per condition to enable mixed-effects analyses; stratify by domain/expertise to examine moderation by prior experience (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2025; Fisher et al., 2025).
- Design and arms (factorial or multi-arm):
  - Arm A: AI-assisted ideation with friction/reflection (structured prompts, mandatory critique, reflective journaling, ethical checks).
  - Arm B: AI-assisted ideation with with-design practices (guided integration across design-thinking stages; emphasis on ownership and critique).
  - Arm C: AI-assisted ideation with minimal friction (passive AI use; limited reflection).
  - Arm D: Non-AI ideation control (no AI assistance) to establish a non-AI creativity baseline.
  - Optional cross-over: rotate between arms to control for order effects.
- Waves and timing:
  - 6–12 months total, with waves every 4–8 weeks depending on task cadence (e.g., design-project cycles); include a post-intervention follow-up after a washout period (3–6 months) to assess retention/transfer.
- Tasks and domains:
  - Design ideation tasks across multiple domains (product, graphic, service, UX) to test generalizability; include both divergent ideation tasks and convergent evaluation tasks.
- Measurements (multi-method; aligned with the literature):
  - Creativity metrics: fluency, flexibility, originality (Guilford/Torrance tradition); semantic divergence; novelty; product conformance; cross-context transfer to non-AI tasks.
  - Non-AI creativity trajectories: independent, unaided outputs across waves; evaluation by external raters blinded to condition.
  - AI engagement metrics: proportion of AI-generated content used in final artifacts; depth of collaboration; prompting strategies; ownership attributions.
  - Friction/reflection indicators: frequency, duration, and quality of reflective prompts; compliance with critique protocols; ethics/bias checks.
  - Agency and autonomy: validated scales plus qualitative interviews on perceived autonomy, trust, and governance.
  - Cognitive load and engagement: optional EEG or proxy measures (if feasible) or validated cognitive-load questionnaires; NASA-TLX as useful reference (Wang et al., 2025; Wadinambiarachchi et al., 2024).
- Data analysis plan:
  - Longitudinal mixed-effects models to capture trajectories of non-AI creativity across waves and to test interaction effects with friction/with-design conditions.
  - Mediation analyses to test whether friction/reflection and with-design practices mediate the AI–creativity relationship.
  - Moderation analyses to examine domain, expertise, and baseline creativity as potential moderators.
  - Qualitative analysis: thematic coding of interaction patterns, reflective journals, and interviews to elucidate mechanisms (ownership, autonomy, engagement, bias awareness).
- Governance, ethics, and transparency:
  - Pre-register hypotheses, analytic plans, and measurement schemes.
  - Ensure robust data provenance, bias checks, and Indigenous data sovereignty considerations as applicable; emphasize ethics and epistemic vigilance in AI use (Rana, 2024; Baker & Hawn, 2022; Vhatkar et al., 2024).
  - Obtain appropriate institutional approvals; ensure participants understand AI usage, data handling, and authorship attribution.

4) Measurement and instrumentation: core metrics
- Non-AI creativity metrics (primary outcome for the question):
  - Fluency (number of ideas generated unaided).
  - Flexibility (number of distinct conceptual categories or classes).
  - Originality (statistical infrequency; judged by expert raters).
  - Novelty and relevance to user needs; semantic divergence (lexical/semantic distance across ideas).
  - Product conformance or practicality (alignment with user needs and feasibility).
  - Transferability: performance on non-AI tasks or later contexts without AI prompts.
- AI engagement metrics:
  - Proportion of final artifacts that incorporate AI-generated content.
  - Depth of collaboration: extent of human refinement, critique, and re-framing.
  - Prompt sophistication and prompt design patterns (progressive prompting usage; refinement prompts).
- Friction/reflection and with-design indicators:
  - Frequency and quality of reflective prompts completed after each wave.
  - Time delays or constraint-based prompts implemented (friction intensity).
  - Ethics/bias checks completed in each wave.
- Agency and ethical governance:
  - Perceived autonomy, trust in AI, and perceived authorship/ownership.
  - Epistemic vigilance indicators (bias detection, source validation, provenance documentation).

5) Practical design recommendations for authors
- From the literature, there is clear support for with-design approaches and friction/reflection to sustain long-run human agency in AI-augmented ideation. Implement these as core components of the study design (Rana et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Chompunuch & Lubart, 2025; Ye et al., 2025; van Lancker, 2025).
- Use progressive prompting as a scalable intervention to maintain metacognition and prevent cognitive offloading, while tracking whether metacognitive gains translate into durable non-AI creativity (Li et al., 2025).
- Emphasize ethical governance and epistemic literacy across waves; integrate bias checks, data provenance, and authorship attribution into the design of each wave and artifact (Rana, 2024; Bos culture).
- Prioritize active human-human collaboration patterns (team-based critique, diverse stakeholder input) to counter homogenization and enhance durable skill growth (Marrone et al., 2024; Ra et al., 2025; Carlgren et al., 2016).
- Report results with comprehensive statistics (means, SDs, effect sizes, F-values, p-values) for each wave, plus correlations between cognitive/physiological proxies and creativity outcomes, to support cross-study synthesis (Wang et al., 2025; Hadas & Hershkovitz, 2025).
- Ensure that the longitudinal design remains ecologically valid by embedding tasks in realistic design contexts and by including a variety of domains to test generalizability (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Zhou et al., 2025).

6) Limitations and caveats
- The majority of available evidence is either cross-sectional, short-term, or domain-specific. Long-run trajectories of non-AI creativity under repeated AI-assisted ideation require carefully designed longitudinal studies with explicit friction/reflection and with-design interventions (Baltà-Salvador et al., 2026; Rana et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025; Tholander & Jonsson, 2023; Ye et al., 2025).
- Effects may be domain-specific; results from design tasks may not generalize to writing, coding, or other creative domains without adaptation (Fisher et al., 2025; Doshi & Hauser, 2024; Jiang, 2025).
- Ethical considerations and governance are critical; ensure ongoing monitoring of bias, authorship, data provenance, and Indigenous data sovereignty considerations as applicable (Baker & Hawn, 2022; Rana, 2024).

7) Actionable insights for the author: a concise checklist
- Experimental design:
  - Include friction/reflection and with-design arms in every wave; consider a 2×2×2 design: AI assistance (yes/no) × friction/reflection (yes/no) × with-design practices (yes/no).
  - Use cross-over or parallel-group designs to control for order effects and participant heterogeneity.
- Measurements:
  - Use a multi-dimensional creativity rubric (fluency, flexibility, originality, semantic divergence, novelty) across waves.
  - Include non-AI creativity tasks to detect transfer and durability of non-AI capabilities.
  - Collect AI engagement metrics and reflection quality indicators.
- Analysis:
  - Plan for mixed-effects modeling to capture within-person trajectories and between-group differences.
  - Pre-register hypotheses and analysis plan; document dropout, missing data handling, and sensitivity analyses.
- Ethics and governance:
  - Build in ongoing ethics training and governance checks; document authorship attribution for AI-generated contributions.
- Reporting:
  - Report inline statistics per wave and summarize long-run trajectories; provide qualitative insights into interaction patterns and mechanisms (ownership, agency, reflection efficacy).
