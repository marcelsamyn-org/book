AI-use metrics mediating tutoring condition to long-term retention/transfer: evidence landscape and research blueprint
Report for authors and reviewers
(report.md)

Introduction
This report synthesizes the gathered contexts to address the research question:
- Which AI-use metrics (for example, frequency of offloading, reflective prompts engagement, auto-summarization usage) mediate the relationship between tutoring condition and long-term retention/transfer, and do explicit memory-goal guardrails moderate these mediation paths?

Key message
- Across the available literature, direct empirical mediation analyses that quantify specific AI-use metrics as mediators between tutoring condition and long-term retention or transfer are largely absent. Long-term gains have been demonstrated for certain AI-enabled tutoring systems (especially under sustained, theory-aligned design), but mediation paths by the specific AI-use metrics named and moderation by memory-goal guardrails have not been routinely tested. Plausible mediation channels exist in theory and adjacent evidence (adaptive features, immediate feedback, reflective practice, and cautious engagement with AI outputs), and guardrails are repeatedly recommended as design levers to shape learning trajectories. The practical task is to design studies that explicitly measure the proposed AI-use metrics, test mediation models, and examine guardrails as moderators, preferably with multi-year follow-ups and across domains.

1) Evidence landscape: what we know and what we don’t
- Direct mediation evidence on AI-use metrics is not reported in the cited systematic reviews focusing on ITS in K-12 settings (Létourneau et al., 2025). These reviews document generally positive ITS effects and highlight core active features (immediate feedback, guided practice, adaptivity) but stop short of mediation analyses using offloading, reflective prompts, or auto-summarization as mediators (Létourneau et al., 2025) [cite: Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Long-term retention and transfer in ITS contexts show promise in high-school settings with sustained implementation (Cognitive Tutor Algebra I; CTAI) with an effect around +0.20 standard deviations in high schools in the second year, though middle-school effects were non-significant (Pane et al.; summarized by Létourneau et al., 2025) [Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Guardrails (memory-goal framing) are discussed as an ethical/design imperative in AI tutoring but are not empirically tested as moderators of mediation pathways in the cited ITS literature (Létourneau et al., 2025; Bastani et al., 2025) [Bastani et al., 2025; Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Related mechanistic work outside K-12 ITS offers concrete mediators and guardrail concepts:
  - Extraheric AI and higher-order thinking: interaction strategies (explanation prompts, questioning, scenarios) and evaluations aligned with germane load can influence higher-order learning outcomes (Yatani et al., 2024) [Yatani et al., 2024].
  - Metacognition and self-regulated learning: metacognitive prompts, reflective engagement, and discourse analytics provide concrete AI-use metrics that could mediate learning outcomes, though empirical mediation evidence remains to be pre-registered or tested (Goldberg et al.; Graf et al.; Hu chapters) [Goldberg et al.; Hu chapters referenced in Yatani et al. 2024].
  - Guardrails-based design patterns: guardrails to constrain or guide AI use—such as prompts that promote multiple perspectives, transparency, and safety—are conceptually advocated but not consistently backed by mediation analyses (Yatani et al., 2024; Bastani et al., 2025) [Bastani et al., 2025; Yatani et al., 2024].
- In additional AI-use contexts (language learning, writing, STEM), evidence points to both benefits and risks of AI-assisted learning, and to the critical role of prompt design, reflective practices, and retrieval-based strategies for durable learning (Barshay; Barshay et al.; Barshay 2025; Harvard physics AI-tutor study; 2023 BJET reviews) [Barshay 2025; Harvard Physics AI-tutor; 2023 BJET].

2) Plausible AI-use mediators (operationalizable candidates)
The literature provides a coherent causal logic for several AI-use metrics as potential mediators between tutoring condition and long-term outcomes. Although empirical mediation estimates are not consistently reported, these candidates are well-supported by theory and adjacent empirical findings:

- Offloading frequency and cognitive reliance on AI
  - Rationale: Externalizing cognitive work to AI changes cognitive-load distribution and may alter depth of processing, self-regulation, and problem-solving quality. Risks include reduced engagement with underlying concepts and retrieval practices (Risko, 2016; Barshay/Barshay et al.; Bastani et al. 2025). Evidence across contexts indicates that unguided offloading can harm transfer, whereas guardrails may curb this tendency (Bastani et al., 2025).
  - Mediation role: Tutoring condition that increases or reduces offloading could indirectly affect long-term retention/transfer via changes in cognitive engagement and retrieval practice.
- Reflective prompts engagement (metacognition)
  - Rationale: Reflective prompts and metacognitive engagement support regulation of learning, self-explanation, and deeper processing (Chi 2000; Bisra et al. 2018; Yatani et al., 2024). When integrated with AI tutoring, reflective prompts can promote germane load and better transfer, especially if prompts are design-optimized and progression-appropriate (Yatani et al., 2024; Goldberg et al.).
  - Mediation role: Engagement with reflective prompts could bridge tutoring condition to durable learning outcomes via enhanced encoding, elaboration, and retrieval practice.
- Auto-summarization usage
  - Rationale: AI-generated summaries may reduce cognitive load but risk truncating essential reasoning if used in place of active processing; evidence on summarization in education suggests benefits when used to support retrieval and organization without bypassing critical reasoning (Yatani et al.; Barshay; Colloquy around summarization in AI-assisted learning contexts). 
  - Mediation role: Properly integrated summarization can facilitate encoding and retrieval; misuse can undermine transfer if it substitutes for active problem solving.
- AI-driven predictive analytics, feedback quality, and adaptive pacing
  - Rationale: Analytics that forecast performance and inform guidance align with instructional theory; immediate feedback and adaptive sequencing are central features of effective ITS (Létourneau et al.; Honebein & Reigeluth).
  - Mediation role: The way analytics, feedback, and pacing steer practice could mediate short-term gains and, with the right guardrails and follow-up practice, longer-term retention/transfer.
- Metacognitive strategy deployment and scaffolding intensity
  - Rationale: Metacognitive scaffolding (macro/micro strategies) and explicit scaffolding sequences are core mechanisms in effective ITS designs; these strategies produce robust learning when paired with reflective engagement (Goldberg et al.; Graesser et al. DENDROGRAM/DeepTutor-inspired scaffolding).
  - Mediation role: The degree and pattern of scaffolding could mediate the tutoring effect on long-term outcomes by shaping the learner’s autonomy and retrieval practice.

3) Moderation by memory-goal guardrails: conceptual grounds
- Guardrails in AI tutoring generally aim to safeguard memory, retrieval practice, and deep processing while preserving learner autonomy and agency. While guardrails are advocated and sometimes implemented (for privacy, safety, or “one-step-at-a-time” prompts in guardrail studies), direct empirical moderation effects on mediation pathways (memory-goal guardrails moderating AI-use mediation to retention/transfer) are not consistently reported in the current sources. Theoretical rationales suggest guardrails could:
  - Strengthen the positive mediation through reflective engagement and retrieval practice by explicitly tethering AI usage to memory goals.
  - Weaken negative mediation through offloading by imposing constraints on when and how AI assistance can be used, thereby preserving opportunity for independent retrieval and elaboration.
  - Improve transfer by ensuring memory-relevant encoding strategies are practiced and retrievable in new contexts.
- Given the current evidence, explicit memory-goal guardrails remain an important but empirically under-evaluated moderator in this domain. They should be integrated into experimental designs to test moderated mediation paths.

4) Quantitative findings and indicative patterns (where available)
- Long-term outcomes with sustained ITS use show moderate durable gains in some contexts (e.g., CTAI high schools; ~+0.20 SD in second year; but less consistent in other contexts) [Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025].
- Guardrails tend to improve short-term learning outcomes and transfer in some guardrail-enabled studies (e.g., PNAS Bastani et al. 2025; Harvard physics study) though not universally; retention outcomes beyond immediate post-tests remain less clearly quantified in these sources.
- There is consistent evidence that offloading and reliance on AI can undermine higher-order thinking and long-term retention, particularly when guardrails are weak or absent (Gerlich 2025; Barshay 2025; Lin et al. 2023; Yatani et al. 2024). 

5) Gaps and methodological suggestions
- Direct mediation analyses with specific AI-use metrics (offloading, reflective prompts, auto-summarization) as mediators between tutoring condition and retention/transfer are not widely reported. Future work should pre-register mediation models and use robust longitudinal data.
- Memory-goal guardrails as moderators of mediation paths are not widely tested. Experimental manipulation with guardrails as a design condition is recommended.
- Measurement standards for AI-use metrics should be standardized, including:
  - Offloading frequency and context (task-level logs, prompts, and outcomes)
  - Reflective prompts engagement (response depth, time-on-task, quality of reasoning)
  - Auto-summarization usage (frequency, context, reliance)
  - Guardrail fidelity and learner perception of guardrails
- Analytical recommendations:
  - Use longitudinal mediation models (SEM, causal mediation or PROCESS-based moderated mediation) with multi-level structure to handle classroom or school clustering.
  - Pre-register hypotheses, report indirect effects with CIs, and provide model fit indices.
  - Consider multi-domain replication to assess boundary conditions (domain specificity, grade level, student prior knowledge).

6) Actionable insights for authors (practical guidance)
- Design mediation-focused studies
  - Define and operationalize AI-use metrics (M1–M3) with precise, replicable definitions. Collect system logs and, where feasible, learner-generated evidence of reflective engagement.
  - Establish long-term outcomes (retention and transfer) beyond post-intervention assessments, with follow-ups at multiple time points (e.g., 6–12 months post-intervention).
  - Pre-register mediation and moderated mediation hypotheses; outline analytic plans for multiple mediators and potential moderator effects (guardrails).
- Guardrails as experimental conditions
  - Implement explicit memory-goal guardrails as a randomized factor (present vs absent, varying degrees of strictness) and test moderation of mediation pathways.
  - Consider guardrail designs that balance scaffolding with learner autonomy, including retrieval-practice prompts, spaced-repetition scheduling, and explicit memory targets.
- Domains and contexts
  - Replicate across domains (math, language, science) and across school contexts (K–12 and higher education) to assess boundary conditions and generalizability.
- Reporting
  - Provide effect sizes for direct and indirect effects, along with confidence intervals.
  - Include a detailed data dictionary and a transparent analytic protocol to enable replication and meta-analytic synthesis.

8) Final takeaways for authors and practitioners
- There is a plausible, theory-backed set of AI-use mediators (offloading, reflective prompts, summarization, adaptive feedback) that could explain how tutoring conditions translate into long-term outcomes, but empirical mediation/moderation analyses are not yet commonplace in ITS literature.
- Explicit memory-goal guardrails are widely advocated as protective and performance-enhancing design features; however, rigorous mediation/moderation evidence is still needed.
- The research design you adopt should prioritize explicit measurement of AI-use mediators, long-term outcomes, and guardrail moderation, ideally with preregistration and multi-domain replication to contribute to a coherent cumulative science of AI-enabled tutoring.

Appendix: suggested study blueprint (concise)
- Design: Randomized controlled design with at least two tutoring conditions (AI-enabled, non-AI baseline) and with/without memory-goal guardrails; longitudinal follow-up across 6–12 months.
- Mediators: AI-use metrics including offloading frequency, reflective prompts engagement, auto-summarization usage; additional mediators may include analytics-driven adaptive pacing and feedback richness.
- Outcomes: Retention and transfer on domain-relevant tasks.
- Moderators: Memory-goal guardrails; possibly learner characteristics (prior knowledge, motivation) as covariates.
- Analyses: Mediation and moderated mediation with robust bootstrap CIs; multi-level modeling to account for clustering; preregistration.

Note: The above synthesis emphasizes the need for explicit mediation testing and guardrail moderation in AI tutoring research. It builds on the cross-domain evidence and provides a concrete, implementable path for researchers seeking to quantify how AI-use metrics mediate tutoring effects on durable learning, and how explicit memory-goal guardrails may alter those pathways.
