# report.md

Trajectory of hippocampus–PFC–DMN–FPN connectivity across four timepoints in an preregistered longitudinal MRI study of AI-offloading with guardrails vs control, with modulation by explicit memory-goal instructions and episodic-memory task content

Executive summary
- Large-scale networks relevant to memory, cognition, and cognitive control are not monolithic. The DMN comprises subsystems with distinct mnemonic roles; the PMN (parietal memory network) and AT network (anterior temporal network) show parallel hippocampal links along an anterior–posterior (head/body vs tail) axis [Zheng et al., 2021]. This motivates a preregistered longitudinal design that tracks hippocampus–DMN and hippocampus–FPN connectivity by hippocampal subdivision (anterior vs posterior) and with explicit attention to DMN subcomponents and PMN involvement across baseline, mid, end, and post-follow-up.
- Task context matters: explicit memory-goal instructions and episodic-memory content are predicted to modulate both DMN and FPN coupling with the hippocampus, potentially shifting the balance between internal mentation (DMN) and externally oriented control (FPN) over time [Dixon et al., 2014; Spreng et al., 2010; Westphal et al., 2017]. Task-related modulation of DMN connectivity during mnemonic tasks, and its interaction with PFC/FPN, provides a principled scaffold for the proposed study’s hypotheses and analyses [Li et al., 2015; Thakral et al., 2020].
- A preregistered longitudinal plan can test whether AI-offloading with guardrails fosters distinct connectivity trajectories relative to a control condition, and whether memory-goal and episodic-content manipulations magnify or dampen those trajectories. Baseline-to-follow-up changes should be modeled with time as a fixed effect, condition as a between-subject factor, and instruction/content as within-subject modulators, using robust longitudinal network methods (e.g., mlVAR, CLPN, GIMME) as preregistered options [Freichel et al., 2024].

1) Scientific motivation and core hypotheses
- Core idea: Memory-guided cognition engages hippocampal connections with DMN subsystems (e.g., PCC, mPFC, angular gyrus) and with frontoparietal control networks. Under AI-offloading with guardrails, the reliance on internal mnemonic strategies may be altered, leading to measurable trajectories in hippocampus–DMN–FPN connectivity across four timepoints (baseline, mid, end, post-follow-up) [Zheng et al., 2021; Spreng et al., 2010].
- Hypotheses
  - H1 (Baseline stability with drift): At baseline, hippocampus–DMN and hippocampus–FPN connections show standard mnemonic–control coupling patterns, with anterior hippocampus preferentially coupling to DMN nodes and posterior hippocampus showing stronger ties to PMN-like parietal regions; over time, this pattern drifts as subjects engage with AI-offloading guardrails or control conditions [Zheng et al., 2021; Mancuso et al., 2022].
  - H2 (Memory-goal instructions bias toward PMN/FPN): Explicit memory-goal instructions will bias connectivity toward posterior hippocampus–PMN pathways during episodic-memory blocks, with enhanced tail–PMN coupling and concomitant modulation of head/body–DMN coupling relative to instruction-absent blocks [Westphal et al., 2017; Zheng et al., 2021].
  - H3 (Episodic-content modulates trajectories): Rich episodic content will preferentially recruit posterior hippocampus–PMN pathways and co-activate tail–PMN with FPN regions during memory-guided tasks; schematic or generalized content will shift toward anterior hippocampus–DMN coupling, consistent with anterior mnemonic representations and DMN involvement [1, 6, 7, 18; Zheng et al., 2021].
  - H4 (AI-offloading guardrails vs control): Guardrails will alter the trajectory by shifting the balance between internal memory processes and externally guided control, potentially accelerating or delaying the shift toward network segregation/integration patterns typifying mnemonic-control tasks (tested via dynamic connectivity and modularity metrics) [Freichel et al., 2024/2025; Westphal et al., 2017; Spreng et al., 2010].

2) Study design (overview)
- Design type: preregistered longitudinal MRI study with four assessment points: baseline, mid-follow-up, end of follow-up, and post-follow-up. Randomized assignment to AI-offloading with guardrails vs control.
- Task design and memory context
  - Resting-state scans at each timepoint to quantify intrinsic connectivity drift.
  - Task-based fMRI incorporating:
    - Explicit memory-goal instruction blocks (e.g., retrieve with a defined retrieval goal; encode with a target for later recollection).
    - Episodic-memory tasks with content manipulations (episodic-rich vs schematic/semantic cues).
  - Optional: episodic-memory tasks designed to vary event details vs gist content to map posterior–anterior hippocampal contributions (anterior hippocampus for schematic content; posterior hippocampus for detailed recollection) [1, 6, 7, 18].
- Regions of interest (ROIs) and networks
  - Hippocampus subdivided into head/body (anterior) and tail (posterior) to reflect DMN–PMN distinctions; seed with standard hippocampal subregions based on anatomical segmentation.
  - DMN nodes: posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), angular gyrus (AG).
  - PMN components (parietal memory network) including angular gyrus/precuneus portions, and posterior medial parietal regions.
  - AT network components (anterior temporal memory system) as contextual anchors for anterior hippocampus links.
  - Frontoparietal network (FPN) nodes: dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC), inferior parietal lobule (IPL), inferior frontal gyrus (IFG).
  - Rationale: This composition aligns with posterior/anterior hippocampal gradients and with DMN vs PMN subdivisions as described in the literature (Zheng et al., 2021; Mancuso et al., 2022; Spreng et al., 2010).
- Sample and power
  - Target sample: 80–120 participants total (40–60 per condition) to provide adequate power for timepoint-by-condition interactions in ROI-to-ROI and network-level metrics, accounting for longitudinal follow-up dropouts and motion/artifact exclusion.
  - Rationale: Longitudinal MRI with four timepoints and multiple task conditions benefits from moderate-to-large samples for stable mixed-effects modeling; prior preregistration guidelines emphasize planning for power to detect time-by-condition interactions in longitudinal network analyses [Freichel et al., 2024/2025].
- Data acquisition considerations
  - MRI hardware and protocol harmonization (same scanner or harmonized sequences across sites if multi-site) to minimize drift.
  - Preprocessing with motion/physiology regressed; robust denoising and artifact checks.
  - High-resolution hippocampal segmentation to distinguish head/body vs tail.

3) Measures and metrics (connectivity targets and analyses)
- Functional connectivity (static ROI-to-ROI)
  - Compute Fisher z-transformed correlations between ROI time courses for:
    - Hippocampus head/body with DMN nodes (PCC, mPFC, AG)
    - Hippocampus tail with PMN nodes (precuneus, posterior parietal regions; PMN proxies)
    - Hippocampus with FPN nodes (DLPFC, IPL, IFG)
    - DMN with FPN nodes (DLPFC, IPL) to assess coupling/anticoupling dynamics
- Effective connectivity and directionality
  - Seed-based psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analyses for task blocks (memory-goal vs neutral) to examine context-dependent changes in hippocampus–DMN–FPN coupling.
  - Exploratory, preregistered directional models (DCM/dc-DCM or DCM variants) for hypothesis-driven directionality between hippocampus and DMN/FPN during encoding vs retrieval epochs.
- Dynamic functional connectivity (dFC)
  - Sliding-window or state-based analyses to capture time-resolved network states; quantify state dwell time, transition probabilities, and modularity over time; examine how these relate to timepoint, condition, instruction, and content.
- Modularity and network topology
  - Compute global modularity and module assignments to quantify segregation vs integration across timepoints; test whether memory-goal instruction and episodic content shift modular structure and inter-network coupling trajectories (as per Westphal et al., 2017; Freichel et al., 2024/2025).
- Behavioral outcomes
  - Memory performance measures (e.g., recall accuracy, recollection confidence, context memory) and learning metrics under guardrails vs control; correlate with connectivity trajectories to map brain–behavior links.
- Analytic plan and preregistration
  - Predefine primary connectivity contrasts: baseline vs mid vs end vs post for hippocampus–DMN and hippocampus–FPN connectivity by hippocampal subdivision, plus interactions with instruction/content.
  - Predefine secondary analyses: PMN connectivity with tail hippocampus; AT network connectivity with head/body hippocampus; DMN–FPN interactions.
  - Predefine analytic models: linear mixed-effects (LME) or hierarchical Bayesian models for ROI connectivity as a function of timepoint, condition, instruction, and content; preregister alternative models (mlVAR, CLPN, GIMME) as sensitivity checks per preregistration guidelines [Freichel et al., 2024/2025].
  - Multiple-comparison corrections: specify FDR or Bonferroni corrections for ROI-to-ROI edges and network metrics; plan to report effect sizes (Cohen's d or standardized beta) with 95% CIs.

4) How the evidence informs the expected trajectories and modulations
- Hippocampus–DMN vs hippocampus–PMN organization
  - Anterior hippocampus shows stronger DMN coupling; posterior tail hippocampus shows stronger PMN coupling; this anterior–posterior gradient maps onto task deactivation patterns and boundaries between DMN and PMN networks [Zheng et al., 2021].
- Task-driven modulation of DMN and memory networks
  - DMN engagement is not simply de-activated during tasks; mnemonic processing engages DMN subsystems and cross-talk with PFC; memory-goal instructions can shift DMN–FPN coupling and hippocampal coupling patterns (task-dependent modulation; meta patterns observed in task-based and resting-state contexts) [Mancuso et al., 2022; Spreng et al., 2010; Dixon et al., 2014; Li et al., 2015].
- AI offloading and guardrails as modulation of network balance
  - Guardrails may alter reliance on external guidance vs memory; this could modulate hippocampus–DMN–FPN dynamics by pushing the network toward more external control (FPN) engagement or by reinforcing internal mnemonic strategies (DMN) depending on task stage and content [Freichel et al., 2024/2025; Thakral et al., 2020; Dave et al., 2022].
- Temporal dynamics
  - Longitudinal trajectories could show:
    - Baseline stability in core mnemonic circuits with gradual drift toward greater segregation or integration depending on instruction/content and guardrail exposure.
    - Mid-to-end follow-up changes reflecting strategy adaptation and learning depth under guardrails, possibly with stronger tail–PMN coupling during episodic blocks and more head/body–DMN coupling during schematic tasks.
    - Post-follow-up patterns reflecting consolidation, rebalancing, or persistent guardrail-induced network reconfiguration.
- The potential for dissociations
  - Distinct patterns may emerge for encoding vs retrieval phases, with encoding showing DMN suppression more pronounced in some contexts and retrieval showing enhanced hippocampal–DMN coupling when episodic detail is emphasized. This dissociation aligns with the broader literature on DMN involvement in mnemonic construction and internal mentation [Raichle et al., 2001; Shulman et al., 1997; Jeong et al., 2015].

5) Actionable insights, testable hypotheses, and study-design recommendations
- Testable hypotheses to preregister
  - H1: In the AI-offloading with guardrails condition, tail hippocampus–PMN connectivity shows greater timepoint-specific modulation during episodic-memory blocks than head/body hippocampus–DMN coupling, relative to control.
  - H2: Explicit memory-goal instructions will increase tail–PMN coupling during episodic memory tasks, with concurrent decreases in head/body–DMN coupling during encoding blocks; these effects are more pronounced at mid/end follow-ups.
  - H3: Episodic-memory content richness will correlate with posterior hippocampus–PMN connectivity strength and with FPN engagement during memory tasks, with guardrails amplifying or attenuating these correlations depending on instruction conditions.
  - H4: Guardrails modulate the trajectory of global network modularity (in a direction consistent with a shift toward either greater segregation or greater integration) and alter DMN–FPN coupling trajectories across timepoints, compared with control.
- Design and analysis recommendations
  - Use four timepoints (baseline, mid, end, post) with standardized scanning parameters to support robust longitudinal modeling.
  - Include both resting-state and task-based fMRI, with tasks designed to manipulate explicit memory-goal instructions and episodic-memory content.
  - Implement precise ROI/seed definitions for hippocampus subregions, DMN hubs, PMN nodes, AT network components, and FPN nodes; report both ROI-to-ROI and ROI-to-network results.
  - Pre-register a primary analytic framework (e.g., mlVAR or CLPN) with defined lag structure, random effects, and planned model comparisons; include secondary analyses with alternative models as robustness checks.
  - Plan for mediation and moderation analyses to connect connectivity trajectories with behavioral memory outcomes and guardrail-related performance metrics.
  - Include sensitivity analyses for parcellation choices (which DMN/PMN/AT/FP nodes are used) to ensure robustness of network interpretations.
  - Data-quality safeguards: motion scrubbing, physiological noise modeling, and cross-timepoint reliability checks; consider harmonization if multi-site data are included.
- Practical considerations
  - Sample size planning should account for dropout across four timepoints; consider an over-recruitment strategy and intent-to-treat analyses where feasible.
  - Blinding and preregistration: maintain preregistered analysis plans and document any deviations with justification to ensure transparency and mitigate analytic flexibility.
  - Behavioral anchors: collect memory performance metrics and learning depth proxies (e.g., episodic richness, strategy use) to link neural trajectories to cognitive outcomes.
- Translational implications
  - If guardrails reliably shift hippocampus–DMN–FPN trajectories during memory tasks, this would inform cognitive augmentation design for AI-assisted cognition. Clear links between memory-goal instruction, task content, and network dynamics would guide the development of guardrails that optimize memory-support without compromising executive-control integrity.

6) Limitations and caveats
- Heterogeneity and generalizability: The proposed design emphasizes cross-subject random effects; individual differences in network topology and hippocampal subfield anatomy can shape trajectories. Findings should be interpreted with respect to group-level patterns and subject-specific architectures.
- Causality: Observational longitudinal FC/effective connectivity cannot establish causality; complementary causal probes (e.g., targeted stimulation, perturbation studies) could help, but the primary design remains correlational.
- Parcellation dependence: Network boundaries (DMN, PMN, AT, FPN) and ROI seeds depend on atlas choice. Robust preregistration includes robustness checks across parcellations to ensure replicability of trajectory interpretations [Bijsterbosch et al., 2020; Woo et al., 2014].
- Task design confounds: Repeated exposure to memory tasks can induce learning or fatigue; the preregistration should include counterbalancing, task variants, and modeling of practice effects.
- Guardrail implementation fidelity: The behavioral and neural effects of AI guardrails depend on their design; ensure precise documentation and standardization of guardrail procedures to enable reproducibility.

7) Implementation plan and key deliverables
- Pre-registration
  - Formally preregister the four timepoints, between-subject factor (AI-offload with guardrails vs control), within-subject factors (instruction type; episodic-content richness), and the primary connectivity metrics (hippocampus head/body vs tail with DMN and PMN; tail hippocampus with FPN) plus modularity metrics.
  - Pre-register the primary model (mlVAR or CLPN as the default; GIMME as a sensitivity check) and the planned ROI definitions; specify regression models and correction methods.
  - Pre-register primary contrasts and planned mediation analyses with behavioral outcomes (memory performance, learning depth).
- Data collection
  - Acquire resting-state and task-based fMRI at baseline, mid, end, and post-follow-up; ensure motor/eye movement controls for task blocks; maintain consistent task timing and stimuli across timepoints.
- Analysis pipeline
  - Predefine ROI time series extraction, FC/EC estimation, dFC/M modularity calculations, and mixed-effects models; implement robustness checks with alternate atlases.
  - Predefine result reporting formats, including connectivity trajectories by timepoint, condition, instruction, and content, plus linkages to behavior.
- Reporting
  - Publish preregistered protocol and analysis scripts; commit to sharing anonymized data and code when permissible.

9) Concluding note
- The literature consistently supports a nuanced, task-context–dependent, and organization-wide view of memory-related connectivity: anterior hippocampus tends to align with DMN while posterior hippocampus aligns with PMN; DMN–FPN interactions reflect goal-directed control; memory-goal instructions and episodic-memory content can shift these couplings in predictable directions. A preregistered longitudinal MRI study that explicitly manipulates AI-offloading guardrails, memory-goal instructions, and episodic-memory content, while measuring both resting-state and task-based connectivity across four timepoints and employing robust longitudinal network analyses, stands to test these principled predictions. The integration of precise hippocampal subdivision analyses with DMN/PMN/FPN network metrics will yield actionable insights for understanding how AI-assisted cognition interfaces with human memory networks over time.
