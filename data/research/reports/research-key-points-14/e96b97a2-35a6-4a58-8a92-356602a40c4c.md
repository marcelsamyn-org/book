Within-course randomized controlled trial: three arms (Laddered Adaptive ITS, Traditional Instructor-Led, Static AI)
Implications for long-run retention, transfer (delayed post-tests and novel-context tasks), and cross-site replication across domains

Executive summary
- Across a broad evidence base, adaptive instructional designs show small overall effects when aggregating diverse educational interventions, with targeted learning gains more evident than broad, cross-domain advantages. Specifically, a meta-analysis of adaptive educational games found:
  - Overall adaptive vs non-adaptive effect: g = 0.11 (p = 0.26), not a substantial overall advantage (Liu et al., 2020) [in the same body of work that informs expectations for ITS-like adaptivity in real classrooms]. Inline: (Liu et al., 2020).
  - Target outcomes: learning g = 0.39 (p < 0.001); engagement g = 0.41 (p = 0.13); game performance g = −0.27 (p = 0.04) — suggesting adaptivity can boost learning but may involve trade-offs on certain metrics; heterogeneity is not fully explained by adaptivity design alone (Liu et al., 2020). Inline: (Liu et al., 2020).
  - Moderator insight: design variability in adaptivity did not explain most cross-study heterogeneity; other factors (randomization quality, game genre, gameplay length) likely matter more (Liu et al., 2020). Inline: (Liu et al., 2020).
  - Publication bias: potential bias in this literature, urging preregistration and comprehensive reporting in replication efforts (Liu et al., 2020). Inline: (Liu et al., 2020).

- Direct evidence for the exact three-arm, within-course, multi-site, long-run retention/transfer question remains limited. However, several strands of evidence inform expectations, design decisions, and replication strategies:
  - Long-run retention and transfer signals in ITS literature are context-sensitive and domain-specific. In large, domain-focused ITS deployments (e.g., Cognitive Tutor Algebra I), evidence for durable, cross-year retention/transfer exists under sustained, domain-aligned implementation, with high-school contexts showing more robust effects (+≈0.20 SD in second year) and middle-school contexts showing directionally positive but non-significant effects (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025). Inline: (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025).
  - Cross-site replication across domains is not consistently demonstrated; generalizability requires explicit multi-site, cross-domain trials with standardized retention and transfer measures (Létourneau et al., 2025). Inline: (Létourneau et al., 2025).
  - In related within-course designs, laddered/adaptive sequencing sometimes yields gains within the ITS task domain but not consistently in terms of long-run transfer; several studies emphasize the importance of immediate feedback, guided practice, and principled adaptivity aligned to instructional theory (Honebein & Reigeluth; Létourneau et al., 2025). Inline: (Honebein & Reigulth; Létourneau et al., 2025).

- Related experimental evidence that informs hypotheses about long-run outcomes and cross-site replication:
  - Interleaving literature ( Nemeth, Osterberg, & Lipowsky, 2025) shows that interleaved schedules (random or adaptive) outperform blocked practice for immediate and delayed tests in a visual-category-learning domain, but adaptive interleaving does not outperform random interleaving. This suggests that sophisticated adaptivity may not routinely outperform simpler robust scheduling in promoting delayed retention/transfer across domains. Inline: (Nemeth et al., 2025).
  - Transfer in working-memory and cognitive-training literature suggests strong near transfer but weak far transfer; methodological rigor and preregistration are critical to credible conclusions about cross-domain generalization (Melby-Lervåg et al., 2016; Soveri et al., 2017; Rodas et al., 2024; Syed et al., 2024). Inline: (Melby-Lervåg et al., 2016; Soveri et al., 2017; Rodas et al., 2024; Syed et al., 2024).
  - In education-specific adaptive-trial contexts outside K–12 ITS, adaptive designs in trials can improve efficiency but require careful planning, particularly when clusters are few or ICC is high (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024). Inline: (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024).

- Implications for your three-arm within-course trial
  - Expectation: modest overall advantages for learning with laddered adaptive ITS relative to traditional and static AI arms, with variability across sites and domains; robust long-run retention/transfer is not guaranteed and will depend on explicit measurement of delayed post-tests and novel-context transfer tasks.
  - Replication: rigorous, multi-site replication across domains is critical to establish generalizability; plan standardized transfer tasks and delayed post-tests, and pre-register hypotheses and analysis plans (Létourneau et al., 2025).
  - Measurement emphasis: explicit, domain-spanning transfer tasks and multiple delayed post-tests are essential to quantify cross-domain generalization and durability of learning.

Key quantitative benchmarks from the literature to inform study design and power planning
- Adaptive vs non-adaptive effects (overall): small effect (g ≈ 0.11; p = .26) across studies; but targeted learning gains can be substantial (g ≈ 0.39; p < .001) when the outcome is learning per se (Liu et al., 2020). Inline: (Liu et al., 2020).
- Long-run retention/transfer exemplars:
  - CTAI algebra-longitudinal results: approximately +0.20 SD advantage in the second year in high schools under sustained, domain-aligned implementation (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025). Inline: (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025).
  - Transfer and generalization signals in cognitive-training literature: near transfer more robust than far transfer; potential publication bias and requirement for rigorous preregistration (Melby-Lervåg et al., 2016; Soveri et al., 2017; Rodas et al., 2024; Syed et al., 2024). Inline: (Melby-Lervåg et al., 2016; Soveri et al., 2017; Rodas et al., 2024; Syed et al., 2024).
- Interleaving scheduling (domain-general):
  - Interleaved vs blocked scheduling: both random interleaved and adaptive interleaved improve learning vs blocked immediately and after a delay; no clear advantage of adaptive interleaving over random interleaving. Domain: visual category learning. Inline: (Nemeth et al., 2025).

- Adaptive trial design in clinical/implementation contexts:
  - Adaptive designs can increase efficiency but come with risks of reduced power when clusters are few or ICC is high; arm-dropping decisions must be carefully calibrated (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024). Inline: (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024).

Synthesis: what to expect in a three-arm within-course RCT
- Primary outcomes: long-run retention (delayed post-tests) and transfer (novel-context tasks) across multiple sites and domains.
- Expected patterns (based on converging evidence):
  - Laddered Adaptive ITS will likely show improvements in learning outcomes similar to prior adaptive ITS findings, but cross-site variability is expected; long-run retention/transfer requires explicit delayed-post-test design and domain-appropriate transfer tasks (Liu et al., 2020; Pane et al., 2013/2014; Létourneau et al., 2025).
  - Traditional Instructor-Led may show weaker learning gains and variable retention/transfer depending on instructor effectiveness and domain alignment.
  - Static AI could produce moderate gains if content aligns with learner needs, but adaptive elements are missing, making long-run transfer potentially weaker unless transfer-focused measures are embedded.
  - Cross-site replication is uncertain; expect domain and site interactions to be substantial; plan hierarchical models to quantify site/domain moderation (Létourneau et al., 2025).
- Measurement strategy should explicitly target:
  - Delayed post-tests at multiple time points to map retention curves.
  - Novel-context transfer tasks across domains to probe generalization beyond practiced problems.
  - Domain-specific rubrics for transfer tasks to enable cross-site comparability.

Actionable design heuristics and recommendations for your study
1) Measurement design
   - Primary outcomes: delayed post-tests (retention) and novel-context transfer tasks across domains.
   - Include immediate learning outcomes as secondary measures to contextualize the trajectory from learning to retention.
   - Use standardized, domain-appropriate rubrics for transfer tasks; include multiple tasks per domain to enable robust effect estimation.
   - Pre-register all outcomes, measurement instruments, scoring rubrics, and analysis plans (including planned moderators like site and domain).

2) Multi-site design and generalizability
   - Implement at least 2–4 sites across diverse domains (e.g., STEM, humanities, language arts) and multiple class types to probe cross-domain robustness.
   - Collect site-level covariates (instructor experience, class size, baseline proficiency, tech access) for hierarchical analyses.

3) Intervention design and arms
   - Laddered Adaptive ITS: define mastery criteria, laddered progression, adaptive sequencing rules grounded in instructional theory; document calibration and responsiveness across sites.
   - Traditional Instructor-Led: standard, non-adaptive, instructor-guided delivery with fixed pacing.
   - Static AI: non-adaptive AI-enabled instruction with fixed content and pacing.
   - Include fidelity monitoring to ensure consistent delivery across sites and arms.

4) Analysis plan
   - Use multilevel/hierarchical models to accommodate site and domain nesting; pre-specify site-by-arm interaction tests.
   - Plan for missing data via modern missing-data approaches (e.g., multiple imputation or full-information maximum likelihood) and conduct sensitivity analyses.
   - Report effect sizes by outcome, site, and domain; include confidence intervals and heterogeneity metrics to support cross-site synthesis.
   - Consider Bayesian methods for interim monitoring if adaptive designs are used, with pre-specified decision rules that minimize the risk of erroneous arm dropping (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024).

5) Reproducibility and transparency
   - Pre-register the protocol (including PICO, outcomes, and analysis plans) and publish null results where relevant.
   - Document all adaptivity design choices (e.g., progression criteria, termination conditions) to facilitate replication and future meta-analytic synthesis.
   - Plan a meta-analytic plan in advance to enable integration with wider evidence on retention/transfer across sites and domains (Harrer et al., 2023; Cochrane guidelines).

6) Ethical and equity considerations
   - Monitor differential effects by subgroups (demographics, prior knowledge) and report subgroup analyses transparently.
   - Ensure transparency on potential biases (publication bias concerns in adaptive education literature) and apply preregistration and robust reporting standards.

7) Practicalities and feasibility
   - If using adaptive designs with few clusters (sites), conduct pre-trial simulations to calibrate power, type I error, and arm-dropping rules under plausible intra-cluster correlations (ICC).
   - Be cautious about over-optimistic power gains in contexts with high clustering; design for adequate clusters per site (Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024).

Limitations and caveats
- Direct empirical results for a three-arm Laddered Adaptive ITS vs Traditional Instructor-Led vs Static AI within-course RCTs across multiple sites are not yet established in the provided literature. Most evidence is indirect (meta-analytic, single-domain ITS deployments, or two-arm designs) and emphasizes heterogeneity, domain sensitivity, and the need for rigorous replication (Liu et al., 2020; Létourneau et al., 2025; Nemeth et al., 2025; Ben-Eltriki et al., 2024).
- Cross-domain generalizability remains a central uncertainty; robust conclusions require multi-domain replication with harmonized transfer tasks and retention tests (Létourneau et al., 2025).

Concluding remarks
- The proposed three-arm within-course trial has potential to illuminate long-run retention and transfer across sites and domains, but success hinges on deliberate measurement and analysis choices aimed at capturing and explaining cross-site heterogeneity.
- The strongest signals for durable retention/transfer in the literature come from sustained, domain-aligned ITS deployments and carefully designed transfer tasks; abstraction from these patterns to a multi-domain, multi-site ITS trial should be accompanied by rigorous preregistration, standardized measures, and a robust meta-analytic plan to support cumulative evidence across sites and domains (Pane et al.; Létourneau et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2020).
